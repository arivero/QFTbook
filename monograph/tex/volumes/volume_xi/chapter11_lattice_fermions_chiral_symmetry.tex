\chapter{Lattice Fermions And Chiral Symmetry}
\label{ch:constructive-lattice-fermions-chiral-symmetry}

\ConventionsEuclidean

Fermions on a lattice require two separate pieces of structure.  The
variables integrated in the Euclidean functional integral are generators of a
finite Grassmann algebra, so the finite-cutoff integral is a Berezin
functional rather than a Borel measure.  The fields reconstructed after
Osterwalder--Schrader positivity are operator-valued distributions satisfying
graded locality.  The purpose of this chapter is to state the finite
algebraic regulator precisely, to identify where chiral symmetry is lost or
modified at nonzero lattice spacing, and to record the reflection-positive
fermion constructions that can serve as honest nonperturbative cutoffs.

\section{Finite Lattice Grassmann Integral}

Let \(\Lambda\subset a\mathbb Z^D\) be a finite lattice with a chosen ordering
of the pairs \((x,\alpha)\), where \(x\in\Lambda\) and
\(\alpha=1,\ldots,N_s\) is a spinor, flavor, and internal index.  The
fermionic field variables are odd generators
\[
  \psi_\alpha(x),\qquad \bar\psi_\alpha(x)
\]
of the complex Grassmann algebra
\(\bigwedge(\mathbb C^{2N_s|\Lambda|})\).  They are not points of a
configuration space.  A lattice fermion action quadratic in the fields is
specified by a finite matrix \(D\) with entries
\[
  D^{\alpha\beta}_{xy}:\mathbb C^{N_s|\Lambda|}
  \longrightarrow
  \mathbb C^{N_s|\Lambda|},
\]
and is written
\[
  S_D(\bar\psi,\psi)
  =
  \sum_{x,y\in\Lambda}
  \bar\psi_\alpha(x)D^{\alpha\beta}_{xy}\psi_\beta(y).
\]
The Berezin functional is normalized by
\[
  \int
  \prod_{(x,\alpha)}d\bar\psi_\alpha(x)d\psi_\alpha(x)\,
  \prod_{(x,\alpha)}\bar\psi_\alpha(x)\psi_\alpha(x)
  =
  1 .
\]
Changing this ordering changes both numerator and denominator by the same
sign in normalized correlation functions, but a fixed ordering must be
chosen before signs in reflection positivity or anomalies are meaningful.

\paragraph{Finite Berezin Gaussian identities.}
Let \(D\) be an invertible \(N\times N\) complex matrix and write
\[
  S_D=\sum_{i,j=1}^N\bar\psi_iD_{ij}\psi_j .
\]
With the Berezin ordering fixed above,
\[
  Z_D=
  \int\prod_i d\bar\psi_i\,d\psi_i\,e^{-S_D}
  =
  \det D .
\]
For every \(i,j\),
\[
  \frac{1}{Z_D}
  \int\prod_k d\bar\psi_k\,d\psi_k\,
  \bar\psi_j\psi_i\,e^{-S_D}
  =
  (D^{-1})_{ij}.
\]
More generally, for equal numbers of \(\psi\)'s and \(\bar\psi\)'s,
the normalized expectation is the determinant of the matrix of two-point
functions, with the sign fixed by the displayed order of the insertions.

Introduce independent odd sources \(\eta_i,\bar\eta_i\).  Completing the
square is an identity in the finite Grassmann algebra:
\[
  -\bar\psi D\psi+\bar\eta\psi+\bar\psi\eta
  =
  -(\bar\psi-\bar\eta D^{-1})D(\psi-D^{-1}\eta)
  +
  \bar\eta D^{-1}\eta .
\]
Berezin translation invariance gives
\[
  \int d\bar\psi\,d\psi\,
  e^{-\bar\psi D\psi+\bar\eta\psi+\bar\psi\eta}
  =
  \det D\,
  e^{\bar\eta D^{-1}\eta}.
\]
Differentiating with respect to \(\eta_i\) and \(\bar\eta_j\), using left
Grassmann derivatives in the fixed order, gives the two-point identity.
Higher identities follow by differentiating the same generating functional;
the exponential is quadratic in sources, so the coefficient of a monomial is
the corresponding determinant.

\section{Naive Dirac Operator And Doublers}

Let \(\gamma_\mu\), \(\mu=1,\ldots,D\), be Hermitian Euclidean gamma matrices,
\[
  \{\gamma_\mu,\gamma_\nu\}=2\delta_{\mu\nu}\mathbf 1 .
\]
On a periodic lattice, the naive massive Dirac operator is
\[
  D_{\rm naive}(p)
  =
  m\mathbf 1+\frac{\ii}{a}\sum_{\mu=1}^D\gamma_\mu\sin(ap_\mu),
  \qquad
  p_\mu\in(-\pi/a,\pi/a].
\]
For \(m\neq0\) it is invertible and
\begin{equation}
  D_{\rm naive}(p)^{-1}
  =
  \frac{
    m\mathbf 1-\frac{\ii}{a}\sum_\mu\gamma_\mu\sin(ap_\mu)}
  {m^2+a^{-2}\sum_\mu\sin^2(ap_\mu)} .
  \label{eq:lattice-naive-propagator-symbol}
\end{equation}
The massless symbol vanishes at every corner of the Brillouin zone:
\[
  p_\mu=\frac{\pi}{a}\epsilon_\mu,\qquad
  \epsilon_\mu\in\{0,1\}.
\]
The existence of these \(2^D\) zeros is the elementary doubling phenomenon.
The next proposition is a precise finite-dimensional version of the
topological obstruction in the translation-invariant nearest-continuum
setting.

\begin{proposition}[Brillouin-torus index cancellation]
\label{prop:lattice-fermion-brillouin-index-cancellation}
Let \(f=(f_1,\ldots,f_D):\mathbb T^D\to\mathbb R^D\) be a smooth periodic
map whose zeros are isolated and nondegenerate.  Consider the massless
translation-invariant Dirac symbol
\[
  D_f(p)=\ii\sum_{\mu=1}^D\gamma_\mu f_\mu(p).
\]
The chirality sign of a zero \(p_*\) is
\[
  \chi(p_*)=\operatorname{sign}\det
  \left(\frac{\partial f_\mu}{\partial p_\nu}(p_*)\right).
\]
Then
\[
  \sum_{f(p_*)=0}\chi(p_*)=0 .
\]
For \(f_\mu(p)=a^{-1}\sin(ap_\mu)\), the zero at the corner
\(\epsilon\in\{0,1\}^D\) has
\[
  \chi_\epsilon=(-1)^{\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_D},
\]
so the \(2^D\) naive species split equally between the two chirality signs.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a regular value \(y\in\mathbb R^D\) of \(f\), define
\[
  I(y)=\sum_{f(p)=y}
  \operatorname{sign}\det
  \left(\frac{\partial f_\mu}{\partial p_\nu}(p)\right).
\]
The set of regular values is open and dense by Sard's theorem.  On a
connected component of regular values, \(I(y)\) is locally constant: if
\(y(s)\) is a small path of regular values and \(f(p_0)=y(0)\), the inverse
function theorem gives a unique smooth branch \(p(s)\) with
\(f(p(s))=y(s)\), and the sign of the nonzero Jacobian determinant cannot
change along the branch.  No signed zero can appear or disappear while the
value remains regular.

Since \(f(\mathbb T^D)\) is compact, choose \(y_\infty\) outside the image.
Then \(I(y_\infty)=0\).  Connect \(0\) to \(y_\infty\) by a piecewise smooth
path and perturb the path, keeping its endpoints fixed, so that it meets the
critical-value set only at isolated crossing parameters.  At such a crossing,
the standard local normal form for a birth--death critical point creates or
annihilates a pair of simple preimages with opposite Jacobian signs; hence
the signed count does not change.  Therefore \(I(0)=I(y_\infty)=0\).  This
is the degree-zero statement for a map from the torus to \(\mathbb R^D\).

For the naive symbol,
\[
  \frac{\partial f_\mu}{\partial p_\nu}
  =
  \delta_{\mu\nu}\cos(ap_\mu),
\]
and at \(p_\mu=\pi\epsilon_\mu/a\) the determinant is
\((-1)^{\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_D}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proposition proves the topological core of doubling for symbols of the
form \(D_f=\ii\gamma\cdot f\).  The full Nielsen--Ninomiya theorem allows a
broader class of local, translation-invariant, chirally symmetric lattice
Dirac operators.  Its proof is the same degree-theoretic obstruction after
one constructs the appropriate normalized vector field on the Brillouin
torus.  The hypotheses matter: dropping exact lattice chiral symmetry,
ultralocality, or the finite-component Dirac symbol changes the conclusion.
\end{remark}

\section{Wilson Fermions}

Wilson's construction changes the massless symbol by adding a spin-scalar
lattice Laplacian:
\begin{equation}
  D_W(p)
  =
  m\mathbf 1
  +\frac{\ii}{a}\sum_{\mu=1}^D\gamma_\mu\sin(ap_\mu)
  +\frac{r}{a}\sum_{\mu=1}^D(1-\cos(ap_\mu))\mathbf 1 ,
  \qquad r>0 .
  \label{eq:lattice-wilson-dirac-symbol}
\end{equation}
In position space, with gauge links \(U_\mu(x)\) in a unitary
representation of the gauge group,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  (D_W\psi)(x)
  &=
  (m+rDa^{-1})\psi(x)                                      \\
  &\quad
  -\frac1{2a}\sum_\mu
  \Bigl[
    (r-\gamma_\mu)U_\mu(x)\psi(x+a\hat\mu)
    +(r+\gamma_\mu)U_\mu(x-a\hat\mu)^{-1}\psi(x-a\hat\mu)
  \Bigr].
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:lattice-wilson-dirac-position}
\end{equation}
The Wilson term is gauge covariant and local at fixed \(a\).  It is not
chirally symmetric: when \(D\) is even and
\(\gamma_5=\ii^{D/2}\gamma_1\cdots\gamma_D\), the scalar Wilson term does
not anticommute with \(\gamma_5\).

\paragraph{Wilson lifting of the naive species.}
Let \(p=p_\epsilon+k\), where
\[
  (p_\epsilon)_\mu=\frac{\pi}{a}\epsilon_\mu,\qquad
  \epsilon_\mu\in\{0,1\},
\]
and \(ak_\mu\) is small.  Put
\[
  n_\epsilon=\epsilon_1+\cdots+\epsilon_D .
\]
Then the Wilson symbol has the expansion
\[
  D_W(p_\epsilon+k)
  =
  \left(m+\frac{2rn_\epsilon}{a}\right)\mathbf 1
  +\ii\sum_\mu(-1)^{\epsilon_\mu}\gamma_\mu k_\mu
  +O(a|k|^2).
\]
Thus the physical corner \(n_\epsilon=0\) has mass \(m\), while every
doubler corner has mass \(m+2rn_\epsilon/a\), which diverges in lattice
units as \(a\to0\) when \(m\) is held in the scaling window.

For \(p_\mu=\pi\epsilon_\mu/a+k_\mu\),
\[
  \sin(ap_\mu)=(-1)^{\epsilon_\mu}ak_\mu+O(a^3|k_\mu|^3),
\]
and
\[
  1-\cos(ap_\mu)
  =
  1-(-1)^{\epsilon_\mu}\cos(ak_\mu)
  =
  2\epsilon_\mu+O(a^2k_\mu^2).
\]
Substitution in \eqref{eq:lattice-wilson-dirac-symbol} gives the displayed
expansion.
The term \(2rn_\epsilon/a\) is present exactly at corners with
\(n_\epsilon>0\).  Thus the Wilson term distinguishes the physical corner
from the doubler corners by a regulator-scale mass shift while preserving the
linear Dirac behavior of the physical corner.

\section{Mid-link Reflection Positivity}

Reflection positivity for fermions is a statement about an antilinear
anti-automorphism of the Grassmann algebra and about the sign of the factor
coupling the two halves of the lattice.  It is not automatic from positivity
of a determinant.  We record the finite algebraic mechanism because this is
where many informal discussions lose the distinction between a Berezin
functional and a measure.

Let the reflection plane be the mid-link plane
\[
  x_0=\frac a2 .
\]
Let
\[
  \Lambda_+=\{x\in\Lambda:x_0\geq a\},
  \qquad
  \Lambda_-=\{x\in\Lambda:x_0\leq0\},
\]
and let \(\vartheta(t,\mathbf x)=(a-t,\mathbf x)\).  Choose the Euclidean
time gamma matrix so that \(\gamma_0^\dagger=\gamma_0\) and
\(\gamma_0^2=\mathbf 1\).  The reflection \(\Theta\) is defined on
positive-time generators by
\[
  \Theta\bigl(\psi_\alpha(x)\bigr)
  =
  (\gamma_0)_\beta{}^\alpha\bar\psi_\beta(\vartheta x),
  \qquad
  \Theta\bigl(\bar\psi_\alpha(x)\bigr)
  =
  (\gamma_0)_\alpha{}^\beta\psi_\beta(\vartheta x),
\]
extended antilinearly and with reversed order,
\[
  \Theta(AB)=\Theta(B)\Theta(A).
\]

Figure~\ref{fig:lattice-fermion-midlink-reflection} records the midlink
reflection used in the lattice-fermion reflection-positivity argument and the
induced exchange of fermion and antifermion variables under \(\Theta\).

\begin{definition}[Fermionic reflection-positive crossing factor]
\label{def:lattice-fermion-rp-crossing-factor}
Let \(\mathcal A_+\) be the Grassmann algebra generated by positive-time
variables.  A crossing factor is reflection-positive if it can be written,
with a fixed ordering of the odd generators \(F_1,\ldots,F_M\in\mathcal A_+\),
as
\[
  B_\times
  =
  \prod_{j=1}^M\bigl(1+c_j\Theta(F_j)F_j\bigr),
  \qquad c_j\geq0 .
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Finite Grassmann reflection-positivity criterion]
\label{prop:lattice-fermion-finite-rp-criterion}
Suppose a finite fermion functional has the form
\[
  \vev{A}
  =
  \frac1Z
  \int d\mu_-\,d\mu_+\,
  \Theta(e^{-S_+})\,e^{-S_+}\,B_\times\,A ,
\]
where \(S_+\in\mathcal A_+\), \(B_\times\) is a reflection-positive crossing
factor in the sense of Definition~\ref{def:lattice-fermion-rp-crossing-factor},
and \(Z=\vev{1}>0\).  Then
\[
  \vev{\Theta(O)O}\geq0
\]
for every \(O\in\mathcal A_+\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
  \Psi=e^{-S_+}O .
\]
Expanding the crossing factor gives
\[
  B_\times=
  \sum_{I\subset\{1,\ldots,M\}}
  c_I\,\Theta(F_I)F_I,
  \qquad
  c_I=\prod_{j\in I}c_j\geq0,
\]
where the ordered monomial \(F_I\) includes the sign convention needed to
put \(\Theta(F_I)F_I\) in reflected-positive order.  Therefore
\[
  \int d\mu_-\,d\mu_+\,
  \Theta(\Psi)\,B_\times\,\Psi
  =
  \sum_I c_I
  \int d\mu_-\,d\mu_+\,
  \Theta(F_I\Psi)\,F_I\Psi .
\]
For each \(I\), the last integral extracts the top coefficient of a
Grassmann monomial together with its reflected complex conjugate.  In the
ordered basis of \(\mathcal A_+\), it is a sum of absolute squares of the
coefficients of \(F_I\Psi\).  Hence every summand is nonnegative, and the
finite sum is nonnegative.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Free nearest-neighbor fermion: only the mid-link cut]
\label{prop:lattice-fermion-midlink-free-rp}
For the free nearest-neighbor Dirac action with Hermitian Euclidean gamma
matrices and reflection \(x_0\mapsto a-x_0\), the hopping terms crossing the
plane \(x_0=a/2\) can be written as a reflection-positive crossing factor.
The site reflection \(x_0\mapsto -x_0\) and the diagonal reflection
generated by \(e_0+e_1\) fail already on one-fermion two-point tests for
generic spinors.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the mid-link reflection, the only cross-plane nearest-neighbor terms are
the links between \(x_0=0\) and \(x_0=a\).  In a basis diagonalizing the
spin reflection pairing, the cross-plane part of the Boltzmann factor is a
finite product of factors
\[
  1+c\,\Theta(\chi_j)\chi_j,
  \qquad c>0,
\]
where each \(\chi_j\) is one of the positive-time boundary Grassmann
generators or its reflected spinor linear combination.  Nilpotence makes the
exponential of each bilinear equal to \(1+c\Theta(\chi_j)\chi_j\).  Thus
Definition~\ref{def:lattice-fermion-rp-crossing-factor} applies, and
Proposition~\ref{prop:lattice-fermion-finite-rp-criterion} proves reflection
positivity.

The failure of the other cuts can be seen without a general theorem.  For a
one-particle positive-side test \(O=\zeta^\alpha\psi_\alpha(x)\), the
reflected two-point function is obtained from
\eqref{eq:lattice-naive-propagator-symbol}.  For a site reflection in the
time direction, the odd part of the \(\sin p_0\) numerator integrates to
zero and the remaining term contains
\[
  m\,\zeta^\dagger\gamma_0\zeta
  \int_{-\pi/a}^{\pi/a}
  \frac{\cos(2p_0x_0)\,\dd p_0}
       {m^2+a^{-2}\sin^2(ap_0)+B(\mathbf p)} ,
\]
where \(B(\mathbf p)\geq0\) is independent of \(p_0\).  The Hermitian form
\(\zeta^\dagger\gamma_0\zeta\) is indefinite, so the expression cannot be
nonnegative for all spinors.  For the diagonal reflection, the analogous
test produces the indefinite Hermitian form
\(\zeta^\dagger(\gamma_0+\gamma_1)\zeta\) after the symmetric momentum
integral.  Hence neither reflection gives OS positivity on the one-fermion
subspace.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Wilson mid-link positivity at \(r=1\)]
\label{cor:lattice-wilson-midlink-rp}
For the gauge-covariant Wilson operator
\eqref{eq:lattice-wilson-dirac-position} with \(r=1\), unitary gauge links,
and the mid-link time reflection, the cross-plane Wilson hopping term is a
reflection-positive crossing factor after diagonalizing the positive
semidefinite projectors
\[
  P_\pm=\frac12(1\pm\gamma_0).
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The time-direction part of the Wilson action contains the cross-plane terms
\[
  -\frac1a\bar\psi(0,\mathbf x)P_+U_0(0,\mathbf x)\psi(a,\mathbf x)
  -
  \frac1a\bar\psi(a,\mathbf x)P_-U_0(0,\mathbf x)^{-1}\psi(0,\mathbf x),
\]
with all spin, color, and spatial indices suppressed.  Reflection sends the
second term to the first, with the unitary link reflected to its inverse.
Choose orthonormal bases of the ranges of \(P_+\) and \(P_-\).  In these
bases the two crossing bilinears are a finite sum of terms
\[
  c_j\,\Theta(F_j)F_j,\qquad c_j=a^{-1}>0,
\]
where the link matrix is absorbed into the positive-side linear combination
\(F_j\).  Nilpotence again turns the exponential of each bilinear into
\(1+c_j\Theta(F_j)F_j\).  Hence
Definition~\ref{def:lattice-fermion-rp-crossing-factor} and
Proposition~\ref{prop:lattice-fermion-finite-rp-criterion} apply.
\end{proof}

\section{Staggered Fermions And A Reflection-positive Thirring Coupling}

In two dimensions choose \(\gamma_0=\sigma_3\), \(\gamma_1=\sigma_1\).
The staggered one-component field \(\eta(x),\bar\eta(x)\) has kinetic matrix
\[
  M(x,x+a\hat\mu)=\frac{1}{2a}\eta_\mu(x),
  \qquad
  M(x,x-a\hat\mu)=-\frac{1}{2a}\eta_\mu(x-a\hat\mu),
\]
with
\[
  \eta_0(x)=1,\qquad \eta_1(x)=(-1)^{x_0/a}.
\]
The action is
\[
  S_{\rm stag}
  =
  \sum_{x,y}\bar\eta(x)M(x,y)\eta(y)
  +
  m\sum_x\bar\eta(x)\eta(x).
\]
The mid-link reflection acts by
\[
  \Theta(\eta(x))=(-1)^{x_1/a}\bar\eta(\vartheta x),
  \qquad
  \Theta(\bar\eta(x))=(-1)^{x_1/a}\eta(\vartheta x),
\]
with order reversal.

\begin{proposition}[Staggered Thirring reflection positivity]
\label{prop:lattice-staggered-thirring-rp}
Add the nearest-neighbor four-fermion interaction
\[
  \Delta S
  =
  U\sum_{x,\mu}
  \bar\eta(x)\eta(x+a\hat\mu)\bar\eta(x+a\hat\mu)\eta(x).
\]
For \(U\geq0\), the two-dimensional staggered fermion functional with action
\(S_{\rm stag}+\Delta S\) is reflection positive for the mid-link time
reflection.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The free staggered kinetic and mass terms have the mid-link crossing form
already described for the nearest-neighbor fermion functional: all terms
strictly on the positive half-lattice are placed in \(S_+\), their reflected
partners are placed in \(\Theta(S_+)\), and the hopping terms crossing the
reflection plane have a positive crossing-factor expansion.

It remains to check the quartic interaction at the reflection plane.  Links
entirely inside one half again belong to \(S_+\) or to \(\Theta(S_+)\).  The
only interaction terms crossing the plane are, for \(x\) on the positive
boundary,
\[
  U\,\bar\eta(\vartheta x)\eta(x)\bar\eta(x)\eta(\vartheta x).
\]
Using the displayed reflection rule and the order reversal, this monomial is
the reflected-positive bilinear in the even positive-side generator
\(F_x=\eta(x)\bar\eta(x)\).  More explicitly, with the fixed ordering of the
positive generators,
\[
  e^{-\Delta S_{\times}}
  =
  \prod_{x\in P_+}
  \left(
    1+U\,\Theta(\eta(x)\bar\eta(x))\,\eta(x)\bar\eta(x)
  \right),
\]
because the square of each Grassmann quartic monomial vanishes.  The
different boundary links involve distinct Grassmann generators, so the finite
product expands into a sum
\(\sum_I U^{|I|}\Theta(F_I)F_I\) with nonnegative coefficients precisely when
\(U\geq0\).  The full Boltzmann factor is therefore in the form required by
Definition~\ref{def:lattice-fermion-rp-crossing-factor}, and the finite
reflection-positivity criterion,
Proposition~\ref{prop:lattice-fermion-finite-rp-criterion}, applies.
\end{proof}

\section{Ginsparg--Wilson Relation}

The Wilson term removes doublers by breaking the naive chiral symmetry.
Ginsparg--Wilson fermions replace the naive anticommutation relation by a
finite-cutoff identity.  A lattice Dirac operator \(D\) satisfies the
Ginsparg--Wilson relation with lattice spacing \(a\) if
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_5D+D\gamma_5=aD\gamma_5D .
  \label{eq:lattice-ginsparg-wilson}
\end{equation}
Assume also \(\gamma_5\)-Hermiticity,
\[
  D^\dagger=\gamma_5D\gamma_5 .
\]

\begin{proposition}[Exact lattice chiral symmetry and the finite Berezinian]
\label{prop:lattice-gw-chiral-jacobian}
Let \(D\) satisfy \eqref{eq:lattice-ginsparg-wilson} and
\(\gamma_5\)-Hermiticity.  The finite action
\[
  S=\bar\psi D\psi
\]
is invariant under the infinitesimal transformation
\[
  \delta\psi=\varepsilon\,\gamma_5(1-aD)\psi,
  \qquad
  \delta\bar\psi=\varepsilon\,\bar\psi\gamma_5 .
\]
If the finite spinor space has \(\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5=0\), the Berezin
measure transforms by
\[
  d\bar\psi'\,d\psi'
  =
  \exp\!\left(
    -2\varepsilon\,
    \operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5\left(1-\frac a2D\right)
    +O(\varepsilon^2)
  \right)
  d\bar\psi\,d\psi .
\]
The integer
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{index}(D)
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5\left(1-\frac a2D\right)
  \label{eq:lattice-gw-index}
\end{equation}
is the finite-cutoff index associated with the Ginsparg--Wilson operator.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The variation of the action is
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \delta S
  &=
  \varepsilon\,\bar\psi\gamma_5D\psi
  +
  \varepsilon\,\bar\psi D\gamma_5(1-aD)\psi        \\
  &=
  \varepsilon\,\bar\psi
  \bigl(\gamma_5D+D\gamma_5-aD\gamma_5D\bigr)\psi
  =
  0 .
\end{aligned}
\]
For odd variables, the Berezin measure transforms with the inverse
determinant.  Therefore
\[
  d\psi'
  =
  \det\bigl(1+\varepsilon\gamma_5(1-aD)\bigr)^{-1}d\psi,
  \qquad
  d\bar\psi'
  =
  \det(1+\varepsilon\gamma_5)^{-1}d\bar\psi .
\]
Taking logarithms gives
\[
  \log J
  =
  -\varepsilon\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5(1-aD)
  -\varepsilon\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5
  +O(\varepsilon^2).
\]
When \(\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5=0\), this becomes
\[
  \log J
  =
  \varepsilon a\,\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5D
  =
  -2\varepsilon\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5
  \left(1-\frac a2D\right),
\]
which is the displayed formula.

The integrality of the trace follows from a finite spectral argument.  Define
\[
  \widehat\gamma_5=\gamma_5(1-aD).
\]
\(\gamma_5\)-Hermiticity gives
\[
  \widehat\gamma_5^\dagger
  =
  (1-aD^\dagger)\gamma_5
  =
  (1-a\gamma_5D\gamma_5)\gamma_5
  =
  \gamma_5(1-aD)
  =
  \widehat\gamma_5 .
\]
Multiplying the Ginsparg--Wilson relation by \(\gamma_5\) on the left gives
\[
  D+\gamma_5D\gamma_5=a\gamma_5D\gamma_5D,
\]
and therefore
\[
  \widehat\gamma_5^2
  =
  (1-a\gamma_5D\gamma_5)(1-aD)
  =
  1 .
\]
Thus \(\widehat\gamma_5\) is a Hermitian involution on the finite spinor
space.  Its eigenvalues are \(\pm1\), so
\(\operatorname{Tr}\widehat\gamma_5=\dim E_+-\dim E_-\).  Since
\(\operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5=0\), the finite spinor space is even-dimensional,
and \(\dim E_+-\dim E_-\) is even.  Finally,
\[
  \operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5\left(1-\frac a2D\right)
  =
  \frac12\operatorname{Tr}(\gamma_5+\widehat\gamma_5)
  =
  \frac12\operatorname{Tr}\widehat\gamma_5
  \in\mathbb Z .
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Overlap Operator}

Let \(D_W\) be the Wilson operator with a negative mass parameter in the
usual overlap window, and define the Hermitian kernel
\[
  H_W=\gamma_5(D_W-m_0/a).
\]
Assume \(H_W\) has no zero eigenvalues.  Let
\[
  \epsilon(H_W)=\operatorname{sign}(H_W)
  =
  H_W(H_W^2)^{-1/2}.
\]
The overlap operator is
\begin{equation}
  D_{\rm ov}
  =
  \frac1a\left(\mathbf 1+\gamma_5\epsilon(H_W)\right).
  \label{eq:lattice-overlap-operator}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Overlap operator satisfies Ginsparg--Wilson.}
If \(H_W=H_W^\dagger\) has no zero modes, then
\(D_{\rm ov}\) satisfies
\[
  \gamma_5D_{\rm ov}+D_{\rm ov}\gamma_5
  =
  aD_{\rm ov}\gamma_5D_{\rm ov}.
\]

Put
\[
  V=\gamma_5\epsilon(H_W).
\]
Since \(\epsilon(H_W)^\dagger=\epsilon(H_W)\) and
\(\epsilon(H_W)^2=\mathbf 1\), one has
\[
  V^\dagger=\epsilon(H_W)\gamma_5,\qquad
  V^\dagger V=\mathbf 1 .
\]
Moreover \(V^\dagger=\gamma_5V\gamma_5\).  Thus
\[
  D_{\rm ov}=\frac1a(1+V),
  \qquad
  D_{\rm ov}^\dagger=\gamma_5D_{\rm ov}\gamma_5 .
\]
Unitarity of \(V\) gives
\[
  D_{\rm ov}+D_{\rm ov}^\dagger
  =
  \frac1a(2+V+V^\dagger)
  =
  aD_{\rm ov}^\dagger D_{\rm ov}.
\]
Multiplying this identity on the left by \(\gamma_5\) and using
\(D_{\rm ov}^\dagger=\gamma_5D_{\rm ov}\gamma_5\) gives the
Ginsparg--Wilson relation.

\paragraph{Overlap index as spectral asymmetry.}
For the overlap operator,
\[
  \operatorname{index}(D_{\rm ov})
  =
  -\frac12\operatorname{Tr}\epsilon(H_W).
\]

Using \eqref{eq:lattice-overlap-operator},
\[
  \gamma_5\left(1-\frac a2D_{\rm ov}\right)
  =
  \gamma_5\left(1-\frac12(1+\gamma_5\epsilon(H_W))\right)
  =
  \frac12\gamma_5-\frac12\epsilon(H_W).
\]
The finite spinor-lattice trace of \(\gamma_5\) vanishes, hence
\[
  \operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5\left(1-\frac a2D_{\rm ov}\right)
  =
  -\frac12\operatorname{Tr}\epsilon(H_W).
\]
The left-hand side is the Ginsparg--Wilson lattice index written as a finite
trace, so the equality identifies the index with the spectral asymmetry of
the Hermitian Wilson kernel \(H_W\).

\section{Domain-wall Fermions And The Overlap Limit}

Domain-wall fermions realize the same chiral mechanism by adding a finite
fifth lattice direction \(s=1,\ldots,L_s\) with a mass profile whose sign
changes across the wall.  The low-energy modes are localized near opposite
walls and have opposite chirality.  At finite \(L_s\) their overlap gives a
residual mass.  In the limit \(L_s\to\infty\), after integrating out the
bulk fifth-dimensional modes, the effective four-dimensional operator is the
overlap operator \eqref{eq:lattice-overlap-operator}.  Thus domain-wall
fermions and overlap fermions should not be viewed as two unrelated
solutions: one is a local higher-dimensional regulator whose infinite-wall
limit produces the four-dimensional Ginsparg--Wilson operator.

The price paid by overlap fermions is that \(D_{\rm ov}\) is not ultralocal.
Locality means exponential decay of the kernel,
\[
  \|D_{\rm ov}(x,y)\|\leq C e^{-c|x-y|/a}.
\]
This is a theorem only under hypotheses excluding near-zero modes of
\(H_W\), for instance an admissibility condition on the gauge plaquettes or
a spectral-gap estimate in the gauge ensemble.  Without such an estimate,
the formula \eqref{eq:lattice-overlap-operator} still defines a finite
matrix when \(H_W\) is invertible, but it does not by itself prove locality
of the regulator.

\section{Continuum Ledger}

A lattice fermion construction used as a nonperturbative QFT regulator must
specify the following data.
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{finite Grassmann algebra and Berezin ordering},\\
  \text{Dirac operator, gauge covariance, spin structure, and boundary
  conditions},\\
  \text{reflection positivity or a replacement Hilbert-space construction},\\
  \text{chiral symmetry, anomaly, and index convention},\\
  \text{locality estimates uniform enough for a scaling limit},\\
  \text{operator regularization and renormalization separate from the
  cutoff action}.
\end{gathered}
\]
For chiral gauge theories, the fermion determinant or Pfaffian is a section
of a determinant line over the finite lattice gauge-field space.  A
nonperturbative definition requires a gauge-invariant, local, and
reflection-compatible choice of phase.  Anomaly cancellation is the local
cohomological condition that makes such a choice possible perturbatively; it
is not by itself a complete nonperturbative construction of the interacting
continuum theory.

\begin{openproblem}[Chiral gauge lattice]
\label{op:chiral-gauge-lattice}
Construct four-dimensional interacting chiral gauge theories on the lattice
with locality, reflection positivity or an equivalent reconstruction route,
exact gauge invariance, anomaly cancellation, and a continuum limit with the
desired local operator algebra.
\end{openproblem}
