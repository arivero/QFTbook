\chapter{Stochastic Quantization and Singular SPDE}
\label{ch:constructive-stochastic-quantization-singular-spde}

\ConventionsEuclidean

Stochastic quantization constructs Euclidean QFT data as the invariant law of
a Markov process on fields.  The auxiliary time of the process is not
Lorentzian time and is not an extra physical dimension.  Its purpose is to
replace a formal functional integral by a dynamical construction whose
stationary distribution can sometimes be proved to exist.

In singular dimensions the stochastic equation itself must be renormalized.
The counterterms are local coordinates in the regulated stochastic drift and
must be fixed before the cutoff is removed.  The resulting invariant measure
still has to be checked against the OS hypotheses before it becomes a
Lorentzian QFT by reconstruction.

\section{Finite-Dimensional Langevin Identity}

Let \(S:\R^n\to\R\) be smooth and confining enough that
\[
  Z=\int_{\R^n}\ee^{-S(x)}\,\dd x<\infty .
\]
The overdamped Langevin equation
\begin{equation}
  \dd X_t=-\nabla S(X_t)\,\dd t+\sqrt2\,\dd B_t
  \label{eq:finite-dimensional-langevin}
\end{equation}
has generator
\[
  Lf=-\nabla S\cdot\nabla f+\Delta f .
\]
For smooth compactly supported \(f,g\),
\begin{equation}
  \int (Lf)g\,\ee^{-S}\dd x
  =
  -\int \nabla f\cdot\nabla g\,\ee^{-S}\dd x .
  \label{eq:finite-dimensional-dirichlet-form}
\end{equation}
Therefore \(L\) is symmetric in \(L^2(\ee^{-S}\dd x)\), and the probability
measure \(Z^{-1}\ee^{-S}\dd x\) is invariant.

\begin{proof}
Integrating the drift term by parts gives
\[
  \int (-\partial_iS)(\partial_i f)g\,\ee^{-S}\dd x
  =
  \int \partial_i(\ee^{-S})(\partial_i f)g\,\dd x .
\]
Adding the Laplacian contribution and integrating by parts once more,
\[
  \int
  \bigl[\partial_i(\ee^{-S})(\partial_i f)g
        +(\partial_i^2 f)g\ee^{-S}\bigr]\dd x
  =
  -\int (\partial_i f)(\partial_i g)\ee^{-S}\dd x .
\]
Summing over \(i\) gives \eqref{eq:finite-dimensional-dirichlet-form}.  With
\(g=1\), the right-hand side vanishes, so the adjoint generator annihilates
\(\ee^{-S}\dd x\); this is invariance.
\end{proof}

\section{Regularized Field Equation}

Let \(M\) be a compact Euclidean spatial manifold, let \(C_\Lambda\) be a
smooth ultraviolet cutoff covariance on \(M\), and let \(\xi_\Lambda\) be
white noise in stochastic time with spatial covariance chosen so that the
linear equation has invariant Gaussian law \(\mu_{C_\Lambda}\).  A
regularized scalar \(\Phi^4\) Langevin equation has the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau\phi_\Lambda
  =
  -C_\Lambda^{-1}\phi_\Lambda
  -\lambda_\Lambda\phi_\Lambda^3
  +c_\Lambda\phi_\Lambda
  +\xi_\Lambda .
  \label{eq:regularized-spde-gradient-flow}
\end{equation}
The drift is the negative \(L^2\)-gradient of the regulated action
\begin{equation}
  S_\Lambda(\phi)
  =
  \frac12(\phi,C_\Lambda^{-1}\phi)
  +
  \int_M
  \left[
    \frac{\lambda_\Lambda}{4}\phi^4
    -\frac{c_\Lambda}{2}\phi^2
  \right]\dd^dx .
  \label{eq:regularized-spde-action}
\end{equation}
At finite \(\Lambda\), the argument of the previous section applies on the
finite-dimensional cutoff field space.  Thus
\[
  Z_\Lambda^{-1}\ee^{-S_\Lambda(\phi)}D\phi
\]
is invariant.  The constructive question is whether the laws of
\(\phi_\Lambda\), with tuned local coordinates, converge as
\(\Lambda\to\infty\).

\section{Renormalized Products}

For the Gaussian free field in spatial dimension \(d\geq2\), \(\phi(x)\) is
a distribution.  Products in the drift are therefore defined through
renormalized local functionals.  If
\(\phi_\epsilon=\rho_\epsilon\ast\phi\) and
\[
  C_\epsilon=\mathbb E[\phi_\epsilon(x)^2],
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  :\!\phi_\epsilon^3\!:
  =
  \phi_\epsilon^3-3C_\epsilon\phi_\epsilon .
  \label{eq:spde-cubic-wick-product}
\end{equation}
The coefficient \(3\) is not conventional: it counts the possible
contractions of two of the three fields.  The same convention gives
\[
  :\!\phi_\epsilon^4\!:
  =
  \phi_\epsilon^4-6C_\epsilon\phi_\epsilon^2+3C_\epsilon^2 .
\]

\section{Stationary Stochastic Convolution and Wick Powers}

The first analytic object in the Da Prato--Debussche construction is the
stationary solution of the linear stochastic heat equation.  We record the
Fourier construction because it fixes the normalization of the noise and
proves, in a Sobolev form, the existence of the Wick powers used later.

Let \(\mathbb T^2=\R^2/(2\pi\mathbb Z)^2\), and let
\(\{e_k\}_{k\in\mathbb Z^2}\) be the orthonormal Fourier basis of
\(L^2(\mathbb T^2)\), normalized so that
\(-\Delta e_k=|k|^2e_k\).  The spacetime white noise in the stochastic
quantization equation is normalized by
\[
  \mathbb E[\xi(\tau,x)\xi(\tau',y)]
  =
  2\,\delta(\tau-\tau')\delta(x-y).
\]
Equivalently, in Fourier coordinates,
\begin{equation}
  \dd X_k(\tau)
  =
  -(|k|^2+m^2)X_k(\tau)\dd\tau+\sqrt2\,\dd B_k(\tau),
  \qquad X_{-k}=\overline{X_k},
  \label{eq:spde-fourier-ou-mode}
\end{equation}
where the complex Brownian motions are paired so that the reconstructed
field is real.

\begin{lemma}[Stationary covariance of the linear SPDE]
\label{lem:spde-stationary-ou-covariance}
The stationary solution of \eqref{eq:spde-fourier-ou-mode} satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E[X_k\overline{X_\ell}]
  =
  \frac{\delta_{k\ell}}{|k|^2+m^2}.
  \label{eq:spde-stationary-fourier-covariance}
\end{equation}
Consequently \(X(\tau,\cdot)\) has the massive Gaussian free-field law with
covariance \((-\Delta+m^2)^{-1}\), and
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E\|X(\tau)\|_{H^{-s}(\mathbb T^2)}^2
  =
  \sum_{k\in\mathbb Z^2}
  \frac{(1+|k|^2)^{-s}}{|k|^2+m^2}
  <\infty
  \qquad(s>0).
  \label{eq:spde-ou-negative-sobolev-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For the one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck equation
\(\dd Z_t=-aZ_t\dd t+\sqrt2\,\dd B_t\), stationarity gives
\[
  Z_t=\sqrt2\int_{-\infty}^{t}e^{-a(t-r)}\,\dd B_r,
  \qquad
  \mathbb E|Z_t|^2
  =
  2\int_0^\infty e^{-2au}\,\dd u
  =
  a^{-1}.
\]
Taking \(a=|k|^2+m^2\) gives
\eqref{eq:spde-stationary-fourier-covariance}.  The Sobolev expectation is
the displayed Fourier sum.  In two dimensions the large-\(|k|\) summand is
comparable to \(|k|^{-2-2s}\), whose lattice sum converges exactly when
\(s>0\).
\end{proof}

Let \(P_N\) be the Fourier projection to \(|k|\leq N\), set \(X_N=P_NX\),
and write
\[
  C_N(x-y)=\mathbb E[X_N(x)X_N(y)].
\]
The Wick powers are
\[
  :\!X_N^2\!:=X_N^2-C_N(0),\qquad
  :\!X_N^3\!:=X_N^3-3C_N(0)X_N .
\]

\begin{lemma}[Two-dimensional massive-convolution bound]
\label{lem:spde-massive-convolution-bound}
Let
\[
  g(k)=\frac{1}{1+|k|^2},\qquad
  L(k)=1+\log(2+|k|),
  \qquad k\in\mathbb Z^2 .
\]
For every integer \(n\geq1\) there is \(C_n<\infty\) such that the
\(n\)-fold lattice convolution satisfies
\begin{equation}
  g^{*n}(q)
  \leq
  C_n\,\frac{L(q)^{n-1}}{1+|q|^2},
  \qquad q\in\mathbb Z^2 .
  \label{eq:spde-lattice-convolution-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The case \(n=1\) is the definition.  The induction step is the elementary
estimate
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{p\in\mathbb Z^2}
  \frac{L(p)^a}{(1+|p|^2)(1+|q-p|^2)}
  \leq
  C_a\,\frac{L(q)^{a+1}}{1+|q|^2},
  \qquad a\geq0 .
  \label{eq:spde-convolution-induction-estimate}
\end{equation}
For \(|q|\leq4\) the left side is finite and the estimate is absorbed into
the constant.  Assume \(|q|>4\).  Split the sum into three regions:
\[
  A=\{|p|\leq |q|/2\},\qquad
  B=\{|q-p|\leq |q|/2\},\qquad
  C=(A\cup B)^c .
\]
On \(A\), \(1+|q-p|^2\geq c(1+|q|^2)\), and
\[
  \sum_{|p|\leq |q|/2}\frac{L(p)^a}{1+|p|^2}
  \leq
  C_a L(q)^{a+1},
\]
because the two-dimensional shell of radius \(r\) contributes
\(\lesssim r^{-1}(1+\log r)^a\).  This proves the desired bound on \(A\).
The region \(B\) is identical after replacing \(r=q-p\): then
\(|p|\geq |q|/2\), \(L(p)\leq C L(q)\), and the remaining
\(\sum_{|r|\leq |q|/2}(1+|r|^2)^{-1}\) contributes one additional
logarithm.
On \(C\), both \(|p|\) and \(|q-p|\) are \(\geq |q|/2\).  Decompose \(C\)
into dyadic annuli \(|p|\sim 2^j|q|\) and
\(|q-p|\sim 2^k|q|\).  The number of lattice points in a two-dimensional
dyadic annulus of radius \(R\) is \(O(R^2)\), while the summand is bounded
by \(C L(p)^a/(|p|^2|q-p|^2)\).  Summing first over the smaller of the two
annuli and then over the larger gives
\[
  \sum_{p\in C}
  \frac{L(p)^a}{(1+|p|^2)(1+|q-p|^2)}
  \leq
  C_a\frac{L(q)^a}{1+|q|^2}.
\]
Combining the three regions proves
\eqref{eq:spde-convolution-induction-estimate}.  Applying it with
\(a=n-1\) to \(g*g^{*n}\) proves
\eqref{eq:spde-lattice-convolution-bound} by induction.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Smeared Wick powers of the two-dimensional stochastic convolution]
\label{prop:spde-two-dimensional-wick-powers}
For every \(n\geq1\) and every \(f\in C^\infty(\mathbb T^2)\), the random
variables
\[
  :\!X_N^n\!:(f)=\int_{\mathbb T^2}f(x):\!X_N(x)^n\!:\,\dd^2x
\]
converge in \(L^2(\Omega)\) as \(N\to\infty\).  Moreover, for every
\(s>0\), the fields \(:\!X_N^n\!:\) converge in
\(L^2(\Omega;H^{-s}(\mathbb T^2))\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Wick product covariance is the finite-dimensional Gaussian identity
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E\!\left[
    :\!X_N(x)^n\!:\,:\!X_M(y)^n\!:
  \right]
  =
  n!\,C_{N,M}(x-y)^n,
  \qquad
  C_{N,M}(x-y)=\mathbb E[X_N(x)X_M(y)] .
  \label{eq:spde-wick-power-covariance}
\end{equation}
This follows from the generating function
\(\exp(tX_N-\frac12t^2C_N(0))\) and differentiating \(n\) times in each
variable.  Hence
\[
  \mathbb E\left[
    :\!X_N^n\!:(f)\,:\!X_M^n\!:(f)
  \right]
  =
  n!\int_{\mathbb T^2\times\mathbb T^2}
  f(x)f(y)\,C_{N,M}(x-y)^n\,\dd^2x\,\dd^2y .
\]
The massive two-dimensional covariance has the local form
\[
  C(x)=-\frac{1}{2\pi}\log |x|+O(1)
  \qquad (x\to0),
\]
and powers of \(\log |x|\) are locally integrable in two dimensions:
\[
  \int_0^r \rho\,|\log \rho|^n\,\dd\rho<\infty .
\]
The kernels \(C_{N,M}\) converge to \(C\) away from the diagonal, and the
standard Fourier cutoff bounds are dominated by an integrable logarithmic
majorant.  Dominated convergence therefore proves the \(L^2\)-Cauchy
property for every smeared Wick power.

For the Sobolev statement, use the dual norm
\[
  \|u\|_{H^{-s}}^2
  =
  \sum_{q\in\mathbb Z^2}
  (1+|q|^2)^{-s}|\widehat u(q)|^2 .
\]
Applying \eqref{eq:spde-wick-power-covariance} to Fourier test functions
shows that
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E|\widehat{:\!X_N^n\!:}(q)|^2
  =
  n!\!
  \sum_{\substack{k_1+\cdots+k_n=q\\ |k_i|\leq N}}
  \prod_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{|k_i|^2+m^2}.
  \label{eq:spde-wick-fourier-variance-convolution}
\end{equation}
The right side is bounded by
\(C_{n,m}L(q)^{n-1}(1+|q|^2)^{-1}\) by
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-massive-convolution-bound}.  Therefore
\[
  \sum_{q\in\mathbb Z^2}
  (1+|q|^2)^{-s}
  \mathbb E|\widehat{:\!X_N^n\!:}(q)|^2
  \leq
  C_{n,m}
  \sum_{q\in\mathbb Z^2}
  \frac{L(q)^{n-1}}{(1+|q|^2)^{1+s}}<\infty
\]
for every \(s>0\).  For fixed \(q\), the cutoff variances and mixed
covariances converge by dominated convergence of the convolution sums.
The same convolution bound dominates
\(\mathbb E|\widehat{:\!X_N^n\!:}(q)
          -\widehat{:\!X_M^n\!:}(q)|^2\), up to a harmless constant, so
dominated convergence over \(q\) proves convergence in
\(L^2(\Omega;H^{-s})\).  In particular the stochastic input needed by the
Sobolev DPD fixed point below is available for any prescribed
\(0<\kappa<1/4\) after taking \(s=\kappa\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Heat-kernel smoothing estimate]
\label{prop:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}
Let \(A=\Delta-m^2\) on \(\mathbb T^d\).  For \(r\in\R\), \(\theta\geq0\),
and \(t>0\),
\begin{equation}
  \|e^{tA}u\|_{H^{r+2\theta}}
  \leq
  C_\theta t^{-\theta}e^{-m^2t/2}\|u\|_{H^r}.
  \label{eq:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}
\end{equation}
Consequently, if \(F\in L^\infty([0,T];H^r)\), then
\begin{equation}
  \left\|
    \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}F(s)\,\dd s
  \right\|_{H^{r+2\theta}}
  \leq
  C_{\theta,T}\|F\|_{L^\infty([0,T];H^r)}
  \quad(0\leq\theta<1).
  \label{eq:spde-duhamel-smoothing}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In Fourier coordinates,
\[
  \|e^{tA}u\|_{H^{r+2\theta}}^2
  =
  \sum_k
  (1+|k|^2)^{r+2\theta}
  e^{-2t(|k|^2+m^2)}
  |\widehat u(k)|^2 .
\]
The elementary bound
\[
  (1+|k|^2)^{2\theta}e^{-t(|k|^2+m^2)}
  \leq C_\theta t^{-2\theta}e^{-m^2t/2}
\]
gives \eqref{eq:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}.  The Duhamel estimate follows
by integrating
\((t-s)^{-\theta}\) over \(0<s<t\), which is finite exactly for
\(\theta<1\).
\end{proof}

\section{Da Prato--Debussche in Two Dimensions}

On a two-dimensional torus, consider the stochastic quantization equation
for the Wick-ordered measure
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau\Phi
  =
  \Delta\Phi-m^2\Phi-\lambda:\!\Phi^3\!:+\xi .
  \label{eq:dpd-phi-four-two}
\end{equation}
Let \(X\) solve the stationary linear equation
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau X=(\Delta-m^2)X+\xi .
  \label{eq:ornstein-uhlenbeck-field}
\end{equation}
The field \(X(\tau,\cdot)\) has the massive Gaussian free-field law on the
spatial torus and is almost surely a spatial distribution of regularity
\(-\kappa\) for every \(\kappa>0\).  Write
\[
  \Phi=X+Y.
\]
Then the nonlinear equation for \(Y\) is
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau Y
  =
  (\Delta-m^2)Y
  -\lambda\bigl(
    Y^3+3Y^2X+3Y:\!X^2\!:+:\!X^3\!:
  \bigr).
  \label{eq:dpd-remainder-equation}
\end{equation}
In two dimensions the products in
\eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation} are well-defined once
\(:\!X^2\!:\) and \(:\!X^3\!:\) are constructed as Wick powers, because the
unknown \(Y\) gains positive parabolic regularity from the heat operator.

\begin{lemma}[Sobolev products for the DPD fixed point]
\label{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products}
Let \(0<\kappa<1/4\) and set \(\beta=1+2\kappa\).  On \(\mathbb T^2\),
there is \(C_\kappa<\infty\) such that
\begin{align}
  \|uv\|_{H^\beta}
  &\leq C_\kappa\|u\|_{H^\beta}\|v\|_{H^\beta},
  \label{eq:spde-hbeta-algebra}\\
  \|uv\|_{H^{-\kappa}}
  &\leq C_\kappa\|u\|_{H^\beta}\|v\|_{H^{-\kappa}},
  \label{eq:spde-hbeta-negative-multiplier}
\end{align}
for \(u,v\) in the indicated spaces.  Consequently, if
\(\mathbb X=(X,X^{(2)},X^{(3)})\in C([0,T];H^{-\kappa})^3\) and
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)
  =
  Y^3+3Y^2X+3YX^{(2)}+X^{(3)},
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity}
\end{equation}
then \(\mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)\in C([0,T];H^{-\kappa})\) for every
\(Y\in C([0,T];H^\beta)\).  We write
\[
  \|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}
  =
  \max_{1\leq j\leq3}\|X^{(j)}\|_{C([0,T];H^{-\kappa})},
  \qquad X^{(1)}=X,
\]
and for
\(\|Y\|_{C_TH^\beta},\|Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}\leq R\),
\begin{equation}
  \|\mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)-\mathcal N(Z,\mathbb X)\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}
  \leq
  C_{\kappa,R}\bigl(1+\|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}\bigr)
  \|Y-Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}.
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity-lipschitz}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We recall the dyadic proof because this is the exact multiplication input in
the fixed point.  Let \(\Delta_j\) be a Littlewood--Paley partition on the
torus and \(S_j=\sum_{i<j}\Delta_i\).  Bony's decomposition writes
\[
  uv=u\prec v+u\circ v+u\succ v,
  \qquad
  u\prec v=\sum_j S_{j-1}u\,\Delta_jv ,
\]
with \(u\circ v=\sum_{|i-j|\leq1}\Delta_i u\,\Delta_jv\).  Bernstein's
inequality in two dimensions gives
\begin{equation}
  \|\Delta_j u\|_{L^\infty}\leq C2^{j(1-\beta)}\|u\|_{H^\beta}
  \qquad(\beta>1),
  \label{eq:spde-bernstein-hbeta-linfty-block}
\end{equation}
and summing in \(j\) gives \(H^\beta\hookrightarrow L^\infty\).  The
paraproduct estimates following from the almost-disjoint frequency supports
are
\begin{equation}
  \|u\prec v\|_{H^s}+\|u\succ v\|_{H^s}
  \leq C\|u\|_{L^\infty}\|v\|_{H^s}
       +C\|v\|_{L^\infty}\|u\|_{H^s},
\end{equation}
and, for the resonant term,
\begin{equation}
  \|u\circ v\|_{H^{s+t-1}}
  \leq C\|u\|_{H^s}\|v\|_{H^t}
  \quad\text{when }s+t>1 .
  \label{eq:spde-resonant-product-estimate}
\end{equation}
Indeed, \(\Delta_\ell(u\prec v)\) only receives terms with
\(|j-\ell|\leq O(1)\), so
\[
  2^{\ell s}\|\Delta_\ell(u\prec v)\|_{L^2}
  \leq
  C\|u\|_{L^\infty}
  \sum_{|j-\ell|\leq O(1)}2^{js}\|\Delta_jv\|_{L^2},
\]
and square summation in \(\ell\) gives the first paraproduct bound; the
bound for \(u\succ v\) is identical.  For \(u\circ v\), the product
\(\Delta_i u\,\Delta_j v\) with \(|i-j|\leq1\) has frequencies
\(\lesssim 2^j\).  Bernstein and Cauchy--Schwarz give
\(\|\Delta_i u\,\Delta_jv\|_{L^2}\leq
C2^{j(1-s-t)}a_ib_j\), where
\(a_j=2^{js}\|\Delta_j u\|_{L^2}\) and
\(b_j=2^{jt}\|\Delta_jv\|_{L^2}\).  Multiplication by the
\(H^{s+t-1}\) weight cancels the factor \(2^{j(1-s-t)}\), and the condition
\(s+t>1\) makes the finite-overlap high-frequency convolution summable.
Taking \(s=t=\beta\) gives the algebra estimate
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-algebra}, since \(2\beta-1>\beta\).  Taking
\(s=\beta\) and \(t=-\kappa\) gives
\(\beta-\kappa>1\), so the resonant term lands in
\(H^{\beta-\kappa-1}\).  Because \(\beta-\kappa-1=\kappa>-\kappa\), this
term is in \(H^{-\kappa}\), and the two paraproduct terms are controlled by
the \(L^\infty\) embedding of \(H^\beta\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-negative-multiplier}.

The bounds on \(\mathcal N\) follow by applying
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-algebra} and
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-negative-multiplier} term by term:
\[
  \|Y^3\|_{H^\beta}\leq C\|Y\|_{H^\beta}^3,
  \quad
  \|Y^2X\|_{H^{-\kappa}}\leq C\|Y\|_{H^\beta}^2\|X\|_{H^{-\kappa}},
\]
and similarly for \(YX^{(2)}\).  For the Lipschitz estimate, use
\[
  Y^3-Z^3=(Y-Z)(Y^2+YZ+Z^2),
  \qquad
  Y^2X-Z^2X=(Y-Z)(Y+Z)X,
\]
and the same two product estimates.  This gives
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity-lipschitz}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Deterministic DPD local fixed point in Sobolev coordinates]
\label{thm:spde-dpd-deterministic-local-fixed-point}
Let \(0<\kappa<1/4\), \(\beta=1+2\kappa\), \(m^2>0\), \(\lambda>0\), and
\[
  \mathbb X=(X,X^{(2)},X^{(3)})\in C([0,T_0];H^{-\kappa}(\mathbb T^2))^3 .
\]
Set
\[
  M_{\mathbb X}
  =
  \max_{1\leq j\leq3}
  \|X^{(j)}\|_{C([0,T_0];H^{-\kappa})},
  \qquad X^{(1)}=X .
\]
For every \(Y_0\in H^\beta(\mathbb T^2)\) there is
\(T\in(0,T_0]\), depending only on
\(\|Y_0\|_{H^\beta}\), \(\lambda\), \(m\), \(\kappa\), and \(M_{\mathbb X}\),
such that the mild equation
\begin{equation}
  Y(t)
  =
  e^{t(\Delta-m^2)}Y_0
  -
  \lambda\int_0^t
  e^{(t-s)(\Delta-m^2)}
  \mathcal N(Y(s),\mathbb X(s))\,\dd s
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-mild-fixed-point}
\end{equation}
has a unique solution \(Y\in C([0,T];H^\beta)\).  The solution depends
locally Lipschitz continuously on \(Y_0\) and on \(\mathbb X\) in these
norms.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(C_T H^r=C([0,T];H^r)\) and put \(A=\Delta-m^2\).  Define
\[
  (\Psi Y)(t)
  =
  e^{tA}Y_0
  -
  \lambda\int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\mathcal N(Y(s),\mathbb X(s))\,\dd s .
\]
The forcing belongs to \(C_T H^{-\kappa}\) by
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products}.  To estimate the Duhamel term in
\(H^\beta\), use Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing} with
\[
  r=-\kappa,\qquad
  r+2\theta=\beta,\qquad
  \theta=\frac{\beta+\kappa}{2}=\frac{1+3\kappa}{2}.
\]
Since \(\kappa<1/4\), \(\theta<1\).  Hence
\begin{equation}
  \left\|
    \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}F(s)\,\dd s
  \right\|_{C_TH^\beta}
  \leq
  C_{\kappa,m}T^{1-\theta}\|F\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}} .
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-duhamel-time-gain}
\end{equation}
Choose \(R=2C\|Y_0\|_{H^\beta}+1\), where \(C\) bounds the heat semigroup
on \(H^\beta\).  Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products} gives, for
\(\|Y\|_{C_TH^\beta}\leq R\),
\[
  \|\mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}
  \leq
  C_{\kappa,R}\bigl(1+\|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}\bigr).
\]
By \eqref{eq:spde-dpd-duhamel-time-gain}, \(T\) can be chosen so small that
\(\Psi\) maps the closed ball \(B_R\subset C_TH^\beta\) into itself.
For \(Y,Z\in B_R\), equations
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity-lipschitz} and
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-duhamel-time-gain} give
\[
  \|\Psi Y-\Psi Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}
  \leq
  |\lambda|C_{\kappa,R,m}
  T^{1-\theta}
  \bigl(1+\|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}\bigr)
  \|Y-Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}.
\]
Reducing \(T\) once more makes the coefficient \(<1\).  Banach's fixed-point
theorem gives a unique fixed point in \(B_R\).  The same contraction
estimate applied to two sets of data gives local Lipschitz dependence,
after taking the smaller of the two local existence times.
\end{proof}

This theorem is deliberately weaker than the sharp Da Prato--Debussche
theorem in Holder--Besov spaces, but it proves the exact deterministic
logic: enhanced noise first makes every term in the nonlinearity meaningful,
then heat smoothing supplies a positive time power, and the local solution
is obtained by contraction.  The remaining global and invariant-measure
steps require estimates beyond this local theorem.

\begin{proposition}[Smooth enhanced-noise energy estimate]
\label{prop:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate}
Let \(X_1,X_2,X_3\) be smooth real functions on
\([0,T]\times\mathbb T^2\), let \(m^2>0\), \(\lambda>0\), and let \(Y\) be
a smooth solution of
\begin{equation}
  \partial_tY
  =
  (\Delta-m^2)Y
  -\lambda\bigl(Y^3+3Y^2X_1+3YX_2+X_3\bigr).
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-equation}
\end{equation}
Then, for a numerical constant \(C\),
\begin{align}
  \frac12\frac{d}{dt}\|Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2
  &+
  \|\nabla Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2
  +m^2\|Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2
  +\frac{\lambda}{4}\|Y(t)\|_{L^4}^4
  \notag\\
  &\leq
  C\lambda
  \left(
    \|X_1(t)\|_{L^4}^4
    +\|X_2(t)\|_{L^2}^2
    +\|X_3(t)\|_{L^{4/3}}^{4/3}
  \right).
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate}
\end{align}
Consequently no finite-time blowup can occur in a smooth Galerkin
approximation whose enhanced noise norms on the right-hand side stay
integrable on compact stochastic-time intervals.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Multiply \eqref{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-equation} by \(Y\) and integrate
over the torus.  Periodicity gives
\[
  \int_{\mathbb T^2}Y\Delta Y\,\dd^2x
  =
  -\|\nabla Y\|_{L^2}^2 .
\]
Thus
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \frac12\frac{d}{dt}\|Y\|_{L^2}^2
  &+
  \|\nabla Y\|_{L^2}^2
  +m^2\|Y\|_{L^2}^2
  +\lambda\|Y\|_{L^4}^4 \\
  &=
  -3\lambda\int Y^3X_1
  -3\lambda\int Y^2X_2
  -\lambda\int YX_3 .
\end{aligned}
\]
Young's inequality with conjugate exponents \((4/3,4)\), \((2,2)\), and
\((4,4/3)\) gives pointwise bounds
\[
\begin{aligned}
  3|Y|^3|X_1|
  &\leq \frac14 |Y|^4+C|X_1|^4,\\
  3|Y|^2|X_2|
  &\leq \frac14 |Y|^4+C|X_2|^2,\\
  |Y||X_3|
  &\leq \frac14 |Y|^4+C|X_3|^{4/3}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Substituting these three estimates into the energy identity and retaining
the remaining positive \(\lambda\|Y\|_{L^4}^4/4\) proves
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate}.  Integrating the inequality in
time bounds \(\|Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2\), the accumulated \(H^1\) norm, and the
accumulated \(L^4\) norm by the initial \(L^2\) norm and the three enhanced
noise norms.  A smooth Galerkin solution can be continued as long as its
finite-dimensional norm is finite; the displayed bound prevents blowup on
intervals where the right-hand side is integrable.
\end{proof}

The rough Da Prato--Debussche global argument replaces the smooth norms in
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate} by Besov or
paracontrolled norms of the enhanced noise.  The algebra is the same: the
quartic term absorbs powers of \(Y\), and all remaining terms are functions
only of the enhanced stochastic convolution on the time interval under
consideration.  The analytic work is to justify this calculation by
approximation and to prove integrability of the rough enhanced-noise norms.

\begin{proposition}[Passing invariance through a cutoff limit]
\label{prop:spde-invariant-measure-limit}
Let \(E\) be a Polish space, let \(P_t^{(n)}\) and \(P_t\) be Markov
semigroups on bounded continuous functions on \(E\), and let
\(\mu_n\) be an invariant probability measure for \(P_t^{(n)}\).  Suppose
\(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\) weakly and that, for every bounded continuous
function \(f\) and every fixed \(t\geq0\),
\[
  P_t^{(n)}f\longrightarrow P_tf
  \quad\hbox{uniformly on compact subsets of }E .
\]
Assume also that \(\{\mu_n\}\) is tight and that the convergence above is
uniform on a compact set whose \(\mu_n\)-mass is arbitrarily close to one,
uniformly in \(n\).  Then \(\mu\) is invariant for \(P_t\):
\[
  \int_E P_tf\,\dd\mu=\int_E f\,\dd\mu .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(f\in C_b(E)\), \(t\geq0\), and \(\eta>0\).  By tightness, choose a
compact set \(K\subset E\) with \(\mu_n(K)\geq1-\eta\) for all sufficiently
large \(n\).  Then
\[
  \left|
    \int P_t^{(n)}f\,\dd\mu_n
    -
    \int P_tf\,\dd\mu_n
  \right|
  \leq
  \sup_{x\in K}|P_t^{(n)}f(x)-P_tf(x)|
  +2\|f\|_\infty\eta ,
\]
which tends to at most \(2\|f\|_\infty\eta\).  Letting \(\eta\downarrow0\)
gives
\[
  \int P_t^{(n)}f\,\dd\mu_n
  -
  \int P_tf\,\dd\mu_n
  \longrightarrow0 .
\]
Since \(P_tf\) is bounded and continuous, weak convergence of \(\mu_n\)
gives \(\int P_tf\,\dd\mu_n\to\int P_tf\,\dd\mu\).  Invariance of
\(\mu_n\) gives \(\int P_t^{(n)}f\,\dd\mu_n=\int f\,\dd\mu_n\), and weak
convergence also gives \(\int f\,\dd\mu_n\to\int f\,\dd\mu\).  Combining
these limits proves the assertion.
\end{proof}

For singular SPDE, the hypotheses of
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-invariant-measure-limit} are not automatic.
They require tightness of the stationary cutoff laws in the chosen field
topology, convergence of the renormalized solution maps on high-probability
compact sets of enhanced noises and initial data, and continuity of the
limiting Markov semigroup on the same topology.  This is the exact
invariant-law step that sits between cutoff Langevin stationarity and a
Euclidean measure for QFT.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Da Prato--Debussche solution mechanism]
\label{thm:dpd-solution-mechanism}
Fix \(m^2>0\), \(\lambda>0\), and a finite spatial torus in dimension two.
For almost every realization of the enhanced noise
\[
  \mathbb X=(X,:\!X^2\!:,:\!X^3\!:),
\]
the remainder equation \eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation} has local
pathwise solutions in a positive-regularity function space, and the positive
quartic drift supplies a priori bounds that extend the Markov process
globally.  The invariant law is the Wick-ordered \(\Phi^4_2\) Euclidean
measure on the torus.
\end{quotedtheorem}

This Da Prato--Debussche theorem is quoted here as a precise SPDE theorem
boundary, not as a monograph-proved result.  The quoted source is the
Da Prato--Debussche stochastic-quantization construction for strong
solutions.  The mechanism is as follows.
The mild form of \eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation} is
\[
  Y(\tau)
  =
  e^{\tau(\Delta-m^2)}Y(0)
  -
  \lambda\int_0^\tau
  e^{(\tau-s)(\Delta-m^2)}
  \mathcal N(Y(s),\mathbb X(s))\,\dd s ,
\]
where \(\mathcal N\) is the cubic expression in
\eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation}.  Parabolic Schauder estimates increase
spatial regularity by two derivatives relative to the forcing; the Sobolev
version is Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}, while the
sharp Da Prato--Debussche proof uses the corresponding parabolic
Besov--Holder estimates.  Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-two-dimensional-wick-powers}
constructs the Wick powers in a Sobolev topology; the sharper statement used
in the fixed point places \(:\!X^2\!:\) and \(:\!X^3\!:\) in negative
Holder/Besov spaces of arbitrarily small negative regularity.
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-dpd-deterministic-local-fixed-point} give a
self-contained Sobolev version of the local fixed-point argument.  The sharp
proof upgrades these Sobolev exponents to the scale-invariant parabolic
Besov--Holder spaces.  Multiplying the equation by \(Y\), integrating over
space, and using the sign of
\(\lambda Y^4\) gives an energy inequality with random lower-order forcing;
that estimate prevents blowup and gives global solutions.  The invariant law
is identified by applying the finite-cutoff Langevin identity and proving
tightness plus convergence of the enhanced noise.  In the final standard of
this monograph, these Schauder, multiplication, energy, tightness, and
invariant-law identification estimates are proof obligations, not facts
accepted merely because they are standard in the SPDE literature.

\section{\texorpdfstring{Regularity Structures and \(\Phi^4_3\)}{Regularity Structures and Phi-four-three}}

In three spatial dimensions the decomposition \(\Phi=X+Y\) is not
sufficient: \(X\) has regularity \(-\frac12-\kappa\), and the products
generated by the cubic drift fall below the classical multiplication
threshold.  Regularity structures supply the missing local expansion
calculus.

\begin{definition}[Regularity structure]
\label{def:regularity-structure}
A regularity structure is a triple \((A,T,G)\), where \(A\subset\R\) is a
locally finite set of homogeneities bounded below, \(T=\bigoplus_{\alpha\in
A}T_\alpha\) is a graded model space, and \(G\) is a structure group acting
on \(T\) such that for \(\tau\in T_\alpha\),
\[
  \Gamma\tau-\tau\in\bigoplus_{\beta<\alpha}T_\beta
  \qquad(\Gamma\in G).
\]
A model on spacetime assigns to every point \(z\) a linear map
\(\Pi_z:T\to\mathcal D'(\R^{1+d})\) and to every pair \(z,z'\) an element
\(\Gamma_{zz'}\in G\), satisfying
\[
  \Pi_z=\Pi_{z'}\Gamma_{z'z},\qquad
  \Gamma_{zz'}\Gamma_{z'z''}=\Gamma_{zz''},
\]
together with analytic bounds compatible with the homogeneities.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Parabolic model and modelled-distribution norms]
\label{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}
Let \(\mathfrak s=(2,1,\ldots,1)\) be the parabolic scaling on
\(\R^{1+d}\), let \(|\mathfrak s|=d+2\), and set
\[
  \|(\tau,x)\|_{\mathfrak s}
  =
  |\tau|^{1/2}+|x_1|+\cdots+|x_d|.
\]
For \(0<\delta\leq1\), \(z\in\R^{1+d}\), and
\(\varphi\in C_c^r(\R^{1+d})\), define
\[
  \varphi_z^\delta(w)
  =
  \delta^{-|\mathfrak s|}
  \varphi\bigl(\delta^{-2}(w_0-z_0),
               \delta^{-1}(w_1-z_1),\ldots,
               \delta^{-1}(w_d-z_d)\bigr).
\]
Let \(\mathcal B_r\) be the set of such test functions supported in the unit
\(\mathfrak s\)-ball and satisfying \(\|\varphi\|_{C^r}\leq1\).  Fix a
compact set \(K\) and \(\gamma>0\).  The model seminorm below \(\gamma\) is
finite when
\[
  \|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}
  =
  \sup_{\substack{z\in K,\ \alpha<\gamma\\
                  \tau\in T_\alpha,\ \|\tau\|_\alpha=1\\
                  \varphi\in\mathcal B_r,\ 0<\delta\leq1}}
  \delta^{-\alpha}
  \left|\langle\Pi_z\tau,\varphi_z^\delta\rangle\right|
  <\infty
\]
and
\[
  \|\Gamma\|_{K;\gamma}
  =
  \sup_{\substack{z,z'\in K,\ 0<\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq1\\
                  \beta<\alpha<\gamma,\ \tau\in T_\alpha,\ \|\tau\|_\alpha=1}}
  \frac{\|(\Gamma_{zz'}\tau)_\beta\|_\beta}
       {\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\alpha-\beta}}
  <\infty .
\]
Here \((\cdot)_\beta\) denotes projection to \(T_\beta\).  A modelled
distribution of regularity \(\gamma\) on \(K\) is a function
\[
  F:K\longrightarrow T_{<\gamma}:=\bigoplus_{\alpha<\gamma}T_\alpha
\]
with finite norm
\[
  \|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  =
  \sup_{z\in K,\ \alpha<\gamma}\|F_\alpha(z)\|_\alpha
  +
  \sup_{\substack{z,z'\in K,\ 0<\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq1\\ \alpha<\gamma}}
  \frac{\|F_\alpha(z)-(\Gamma_{zz'}F(z'))_\alpha\|_\alpha}
       {\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\gamma-\alpha}} .
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Coherence of a modelled local germ]
\label{lem:spde-modelled-germ-coherence}
Let \(F\) be a modelled distribution in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}, and set
\(\zeta_z=\Pi_zF(z)\).  If \(z,z'\in K\),
\(\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq\delta\leq1\), and
\(\varphi\in\mathcal B_r\), then
\[
  \left|
    \left\langle \zeta_z-\zeta_{z'},\varphi_z^\delta\right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  C_K\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\,\delta^\gamma .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The model identity gives
\[
  \Pi_{z'}F(z')=\Pi_z\Gamma_{zz'}F(z') ,
\]
hence
\[
  \zeta_z-\zeta_{z'}
  =
  \Pi_z\bigl(F(z)-\Gamma_{zz'}F(z')\bigr).
\]
Decompose the vector in parentheses into homogeneous components.  For each
\(\alpha<\gamma\), the modelled-distribution norm gives
\[
  \|(F(z)-\Gamma_{zz'}F(z'))_\alpha\|_\alpha
  \leq
  \|F\|_{\gamma;K}\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\gamma-\alpha}.
\]
The model bound gives
\[
  \left|
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z (F(z)-\Gamma_{zz'}F(z'))_\alpha,
    \varphi_z^\delta
  \right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  \|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^\alpha\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\gamma-\alpha}.
\]
Since \(\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq\delta\) and the set
\(\{\alpha\in A:\alpha<\gamma\}\) is finite on compact homogeneity windows,
the sum over \(\alpha\) is bounded by the displayed constant times
\(\delta^\gamma\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Compact finite-sector reconstruction theorem]
\label{thm:spde-compact-reconstruction}
Assume that the set \(A_{<\gamma}=\{\alpha\in A:\alpha<\gamma\}\) is finite,
assume \(0<\gamma<|\mathfrak s|\),
let
\[
  r>\max\{-\min A_{<\gamma},\,\gamma\},
\]
and let the model seminorms of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms} be finite on
a compact parabolic neighborhood of \(K\).  For every modelled distribution
\(F\) of regularity \(\gamma>0\) on that neighborhood, there is a unique
distribution \(\mathcal RF\) on the interior of \(K\) such that, for every
\(\varphi\in\mathcal B_r\), \(z\in K\), and \(0<\delta\leq1\) with
\(\operatorname{supp}\varphi_z^\delta\subset K\),
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    \left\langle
      \mathcal RF-\Pi_zF(z),\varphi_z^\delta
    \right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  C_K
  (1+\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma})(1+\|\Gamma\|_{K;\gamma})
  \|F\|_{\gamma;K}\,\delta^\gamma .
  \label{eq:spde-reconstruction-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose a compactly supported parabolic multiresolution system of class
\(C^r\): scaling functions \(\varphi_y^0\) at scale \(1\), wavelets
\(\psi_y^n\) at scale \(2^{-n}\), \(y\in\Lambda_n\), with vanishing moments
against all parabolic polynomials of degree \(<r\), finite overlap at each
scale, and the expansion formula
\[
  \eta
  =
  \sum_{y\in\Lambda_0}\langle\eta,\varphi_y^0\rangle\varphi_y^0
  +
  \sum_{n\geq0}\sum_{y\in\Lambda_n}
  \langle\eta,\psi_y^n\rangle\psi_y^n
\]
for every test function \(\eta\) supported in the interior of \(K\).  The
wavelets are \(L^2\)-normalized, so
\(\psi_y^n=2^{n|\mathfrak s|/2}\psi_y^{2^{-n}}\) in the notation of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}.

Define a distribution by prescribing its coefficients
\[
  \langle\mathcal RF,\varphi_y^0\rangle
  =
  \langle\Pi_yF(y),\varphi_y^0\rangle,\qquad
  \langle\mathcal RF,\psi_y^n\rangle
  =
  \langle\Pi_yF(y),\psi_y^n\rangle .
\]
The coefficient estimate for \(\psi_y^n\) is
\[
  |\langle\Pi_yF(y),\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  2^{-n(\alpha_0+|\mathfrak s|/2)},
  \qquad
  \alpha_0=\min A_{<\gamma}.
\]
For a \(C^r\) test function \(\eta\), the wavelet moment cancellation gives
\[
  |\langle\eta,\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C_\eta 2^{-n(r+|\mathfrak s|/2)} .
\]
Only \(O(2^{n|\mathfrak s|})\) wavelets at scale \(2^{-n}\) meet
\(\operatorname{supp}\eta\), hence the absolute value of the scale-\(n\)
contribution is bounded by
\[
  C_\eta\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  2^{n|\mathfrak s|}
  2^{-n(\alpha_0+|\mathfrak s|/2)}
  2^{-n(r+|\mathfrak s|/2)}
  =
  C_\eta\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}2^{-n(\alpha_0+r)} .
\]
The hypothesis \(r>-\alpha_0\) makes the series converge.  Thus
\(\mathcal RF\) is a well-defined distribution.

It remains to prove the local estimate.  Fix \(z\), \(\delta\), and
\(\varphi_z^\delta\).  Put \(\ell_n=2^{-n}\).  The coefficient of
\(\mathcal RF-\Pi_zF(z)\) against a wavelet \(\psi_y^n\) equals
\[
  \langle\Pi_yF(y)-\Pi_zF(z),\psi_y^n\rangle .
\]
If \(\psi_y^n\) meets the support of \(\varphi_z^\delta\), then
\(\|y-z\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq C(\ell_n+\delta)\).  Split the wavelet expansion
into coarse scales \(\ell_n\geq\delta\) and fine scales \(\ell_n<\delta\).

At a coarse scale, \(\|y-z\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq C\ell_n\), so
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-modelled-germ-coherence}, tested at scale \(\ell_n\),
gives
\[
  |\langle\Pi_yF(y)-\Pi_zF(z),\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \ell_n^{\gamma+|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
The \(r\) vanishing moments of \(\psi_y^n\) and the \(C^r\) bound of
\(\varphi_z^\delta\) give
\[
  |\langle\varphi_z^\delta,\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\,\delta^r\ell_n^{-r-|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
Only a bounded number of coarse-scale wavelets meet the support of
\(\varphi_z^\delta\).  Therefore the coarse contribution is bounded by
\[
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\delta^\gamma ,
\]
because
\[
  \sum_{\ell_n\geq\delta}\delta^r\ell_n^{\gamma-r}
  \leq
  C\delta^\gamma
\]
when \(r>\gamma\).
The scale-one scaling functions are handled in the same coarse block except
that no moment cancellation is needed: their coefficients are bounded and
their pairing with \(\varphi_z^\delta\) is \(O(\delta^{|\mathfrak s|})\),
which is \(O(\delta^\gamma)\) under the hypothesis \(\gamma<|\mathfrak s|\).

At a fine scale, write the modelled-distribution difference by homogeneity:
\[
  \langle\mathcal RF-\Pi_zF(z),\psi_y^n\rangle
  =
  \sum_{\alpha<\gamma}
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z\bigl(\Gamma_{zy}F(y)-F(z)\bigr)_\alpha,\psi_y^n
  \right\rangle .
\]
For wavelets meeting the support of \(\varphi_z^\delta\),
\(\|y-z\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq C\delta\).  The \(\alpha\)-component of the
modelled-distribution difference is therefore bounded by
\[
  C\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\delta^{\gamma-\alpha}.
\]
Testing the corresponding model component at wavelet scale \(\ell_n\) gives
\[
  \left|
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z\bigl(\Gamma_{zy}F(y)-F(z)\bigr)_\alpha,\psi_y^n
  \right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^{\gamma-\alpha}\ell_n^{\alpha+|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
The wavelet moment estimate in the opposite scale regime gives
\[
  |\langle\varphi_z^\delta,\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\,\delta^{-|\mathfrak s|-r}\ell_n^{r+|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
There are \(O((\delta/\ell_n)^{|\mathfrak s|})\) fine-scale wavelets meeting
the support of \(\varphi_z^\delta\).  The scale-\(n\) contribution of a fixed
homogeneity \(\alpha\) is thus bounded by
\[
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^{\gamma-\alpha-r}\ell_n^{\alpha+r}.
\]
Since \(r>-\alpha\), the sum over \(\ell_n<\delta\) is bounded by
\[
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^{\gamma-\alpha-r}\delta^{\alpha+r}
  =
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\delta^\gamma .
\]
There are only finitely many \(\alpha<\gamma\), so summing the homogeneities
proves \eqref{eq:spde-reconstruction-bound}.

For uniqueness, let \(U\) be the difference of two distributions satisfying
\eqref{eq:spde-reconstruction-bound}.  Choose a non-negative
\(\chi\in C_c^r\) with integral one.  For any test function \(\eta\) with
support in the interior of \(K\),
\[
  \langle U,\eta\rangle
  =
  \lim_{\delta\downarrow0}
  \int \eta(z)\langle U,\chi_z^\delta\rangle\,\dd z .
\]
The bound \(|\langle U,\chi_z^\delta\rangle|\leq C\delta^\gamma\) and
\(\gamma>0\) force the limit to vanish.  Hence \(U=0\).
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-compact-reconstruction} supplies the reconstruction
component of the finite-sector theory used by the local \(\Phi^4_3\)
regularity structure.  The still-open part for dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) is not
the reconstruction step itself; it is the construction of the
BPHZ-renormalized random model with uniform analytic bounds, the convergence
of those models as the mollifier is removed, and the nonlinear fixed-point
argument in the corresponding modelled-distribution spaces.

For dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\), the symbols are generated by the noise \(\Xi\), the
abstract integration map \(\mathcal I\), multiplication, and polynomials in
the spacetime coordinates.  Negative-homogeneity trees encode precisely the
subdivergent stochastic diagrams.  Renormalization acts on these trees before
the reconstruction theorem is applied.

\section{BPHZ Renormalization of the Dynamic Equation}

For the normalization
\[
  \partial_\tau\Phi
  =
  \Delta\Phi-m^2\Phi-\lambda\Phi^3+\xi ,
\]
the renormalized cutoff equation in the BPHZ regularity-structure convention
has the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau\Phi_\epsilon
  =
  \Delta\Phi_\epsilon-m^2\Phi_\epsilon
  -\lambda\Phi_\epsilon^3
  +
  \bigl(3\lambda C_{1,\epsilon}
        -9\lambda^2 C_{2,\epsilon}
        +c_{\rm fin}\bigr)\Phi_\epsilon
  +\xi_\epsilon .
  \label{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde}
\end{equation}
Here \(C_{1,\epsilon}\) is the one-loop contraction already visible in
\(:\!\Phi^3\!:\), \(C_{2,\epsilon}\) is the two-loop local contraction
arising from the negative tree with two cubic vertices, and \(c_{\rm fin}\)
is the finite mass coordinate.  Different smooth mollifiers or subtraction
schemes change \(C_{1,\epsilon}\), \(C_{2,\epsilon}\), and \(c_{\rm fin}\)
separately, but not the statement that only the local linear drift and
vacuum-energy coordinates are needed in this superrenormalizable model.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Renormalized dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) theorem package]
\label{thm:renormalized-phi-four-three-spde-datum}
Let the spatial manifold be a finite three-dimensional torus, let
\(\xi_\epsilon=\rho_\epsilon\ast\xi\) be a smooth space-time mollification
of white noise, and fix the normalization of
\eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde}.  In the BPHZ
regularity-structure chart there are local constants
\(C_{1,\epsilon}\), \(C_{2,\epsilon}\), and a finite mass coordinate
\(c_{\rm fin}\), such that the following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item For each fixed \(\epsilon>0\) and smooth initial field, the cutoff
  equation \eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde} has a unique smooth
  local solution.  The solution map is Markovian and depends continuously on
  the initial field and on the smooth noise.
  \item After enhancing the mollified noise by its BPHZ-renormalized negative
  trees, the cutoff solutions converge in probability, on compact
  stochastic-time intervals before explosion and in negative spatial Holder
  or Besov topologies, to a limit independent of the mollifier up to finite
  local coordinate changes.
  \item The positive quartic drift, together with the renormalized mass
  coordinate, gives global solutions and invariant probability measures for
  the limiting Markov process in the massive phase.  Starting the cutoff
  equation in its finite-cutoff Gibbs law gives stationary solutions, and
  every limiting stationary law is invariant for the limiting Markov
  semigroup.
  \item In a common finite-volume regulator and renormalization chart, this
  invariant law has the same Schwinger moments as the constructive
  \(\Phi^4_3\) Euclidean measure with the same renormalized mass coordinate.
  Equivalently, after the field and mass normalizations are matched, the
  SPDE stationary construction and the constructive Euclidean construction
  determine the same OS input hierarchy.
\end{enumerate}
\end{quotedtheorem}

This renormalized \(\Phi^4_3\) SPDE theorem is a quoted theorem-boundary for
the regularity-structures construction, not a proof completed here.  The
quoted source lineage is Hairer's regularity-structures construction and the
Hairer--Mourrat--Weber treatments of dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) renormalization.
The four clauses are logically distinct.  Local cutoff well-posedness is an
ordinary smooth parabolic theorem.  Renormalized convergence is the singular
SPDE theorem: one first builds the canonical model from the mollified noise;
its negative tree components diverge as \(\epsilon\downarrow0\); the BPHZ
character subtracts the expectations of all divergent negative subtrees,
producing a renormalized model satisfying uniform analytic bounds; compactness
and Kolmogorov estimates then give convergence of the model.

The fixed-point problem for the SPDE is then solved in the space of modelled
distributions.  The contraction uses parabolic Schauder estimates for the
abstract integration map and local Lipschitz bounds for the cubic
nonlinearity in the regularity-structure product.  Reconstructing the fixed
point gives the distribution-valued solution.  The explicit local term in
\eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde} is the image, in the concrete
equation, of the BPHZ action on the negative trees.  Invariant measures are
identified by finite-cutoff stationarity, tightness, and convergence of
stationary solutions.  Identification with the constructive Euclidean
\(\Phi^4_3\) measure is still another step: both constructions must be placed
in the same finite-volume cutoff chart, the mass and field coordinates must be
matched, and equality of the limiting Schwinger moments must be proved.  Each
sentence in this paragraph names a theorem-level estimate; none is to be
treated as proved merely by being quoted.

The theorem-boundary statements still left in this chapter concern the
global Da Prato--Debussche construction of two-dimensional stochastic
quantization and the BPHZ-renormalized construction of the dynamic
\(\Phi^4_3\) model.\footnote{See G.~Da Prato and A.~Debussche, ``Strong
solutions to the stochastic quantization equations,'' \emph{Ann. Probab.}
31 (2003); M.~Hairer, ``A theory of regularity structures,''
\emph{Invent. Math.} 198 (2014); and the Hairer--Mourrat--Weber
regularity-structures treatment of \(\Phi^4_3\) renormalization.  For recent
singular-SPDE work in gauge contexts, see A.~Chandra, I.~Chevyrev,
M.~Hairer, and H.~Shen, ``Stochastic quantisation of Yang--Mills,''
Invent. Math. \textbf{230} (2022) 1025--1096.}  The compact finite-sector
reconstruction theorem has been proved above because its proof is a local
analytic argument once the model bounds and modelled-distribution bounds are
available.  The cited works identify deeper analytic machinery that remains
to be reconstructed in this monograph; they do not by themselves settle the
monograph's proof obligations.

\begin{openproblem}[Self-contained singular-SPDE proof stack]
\label{op:self-contained-singular-spde-proof-stack}
Develop self-contained monograph proofs of the quoted SPDE results used
above.  At minimum this requires: construction and convergence of Wick
powers for the stochastic convolution; the parabolic Schauder and Besov or
Holder multiplication estimates used in the Da Prato--Debussche fixed point;
the global energy estimate and invariant-law identification for
\(\Phi^4_2\); construction of the BPHZ-renormalized model for dynamic
\(\Phi^4_3\); convergence of the renormalized models; solution of the
fixed-point problem in modelled distributions; identification of the local
counterterms in the concrete cutoff equation; and the passage from stationary
SPDE solutions to OS data.  Until these arguments are supplied, the quoted
theorem blocks are documented proof boundaries, not authority claims.
\end{openproblem}

\section{Paracontrolled and RG Routes}

Regularity structures are not the only analytic language for singular SPDE.
Paracontrolled distributions decompose products using Bony's paraproduct
\[
  fg=f\prec g+f\circ g+f\succ g,
\]
and control the resonant product \(f\circ g\) by enhancing the noise with the
few ill-defined resonant objects needed by the equation.  For \(\Phi^4_3\),
this route uses the same local counterterm content as
\eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde}, expressed in Fourier/Besov
language rather than in a tree Hopf algebra.

Kupiainen-style RG treats the stochastic convolution and nonlinear response
by scale integration.  It is closer in form to the constructive RG of
Chapter~\ref{ch:constructive-rigorous-renormalization-group}.  The common
structure is that no finite truncation of diagrams is being trusted by
itself: the theorem estimates the infinite irrelevant tail in a norm and
shows convergence of the renormalized limit.

\section{Invariant Measure and OS Data}

If the renormalized Markov process exists and is ergodic with invariant
measure \(\mu\), the Euclidean Schwinger functions are
\begin{equation}
  S_n(f_1,\ldots,f_n)
  =
  \int \Phi(f_1)\cdots\Phi(f_n)\,\dd\mu(\Phi).
  \label{eq:spde-invariant-measure-schwinger}
\end{equation}
This is not yet a complete QFT construction.  One must verify:
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{Euclidean covariance},\qquad
  \text{reflection positivity},\\
  \text{regularity and OS growth bounds},\qquad
  \text{clustering or phase decomposition}.
\end{gathered}
\]
Stochastic quantization is especially powerful because it gives a dynamic
construction of the measure and a precise algebraic account of stochastic
counterterms.  Reflection positivity, however, is a property of the
Euclidean field measure under physical time reflection; it is not automatic
from Markov positivity in the auxiliary stochastic time.

\begin{remark}[Gauge theories and BV compatibility]
\label{rem:spde-gauge-bv-compatibility}
For gauge theories, stochastic quantization must be reconciled with gauge
symmetry, gauge fixing, and the BV master equation.  A stochastic evolution
for gauge potentials before quotienting is not by itself a construction of a
gauge-invariant local QFT.  The constructive target is a measure or
observable theory for gauge-invariant fields, or a gauge-fixed/BV
construction whose quantum master equation and positivity replacement are
controlled.  Two- and three-dimensional Yang--Mills SPDE programs provide
important laboratories; four-dimensional Yang--Mills remains a separate open
problem.
\end{remark}

\begin{openproblem}[SPDE-to-Wightman construction]
\label{op:spde-to-wightman-construction}
Starting from a renormalized singular SPDE construction of an interacting
Euclidean field, prove the OS hypotheses, including the linear growth
condition needed for reconstruction where applicable, identify the resulting
Wightman distributions, and compare the stochastic counterterms with
Wilsonian local coordinates in a common renormalization chart.
\end{openproblem}
