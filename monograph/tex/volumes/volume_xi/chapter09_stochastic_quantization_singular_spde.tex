\chapter{Stochastic Quantization and Singular SPDE}
\label{ch:constructive-stochastic-quantization-singular-spde}

\ConventionsEuclidean

Stochastic quantization constructs Euclidean QFT data as the invariant law of
a Markov process on fields.  The auxiliary time of the process is not
Lorentzian time and is not an extra physical dimension.  Its purpose is to
replace a formal functional integral by a dynamical construction whose
stationary distribution can sometimes be proved to exist.

In singular dimensions the stochastic equation itself must be renormalized.
The counterterms are local coordinates in the regulated stochastic drift and
must be fixed before the cutoff is removed.  The resulting invariant measure
still has to be checked against the OS hypotheses before it becomes a
Lorentzian QFT by reconstruction.

\section{Finite-Dimensional Langevin Identity}

Let \(S:\R^n\to\R\) be smooth and confining enough that
\[
  Z=\int_{\R^n}\ee^{-S(x)}\,\dd x<\infty .
\]
The overdamped Langevin equation
\begin{equation}
  \dd X_t=-\nabla S(X_t)\,\dd t+\sqrt2\,\dd B_t
  \label{eq:finite-dimensional-langevin}
\end{equation}
has generator
\[
  Lf=-\nabla S\cdot\nabla f+\Delta f .
\]
For smooth compactly supported \(f,g\),
\begin{equation}
  \int (Lf)g\,\ee^{-S}\dd x
  =
  -\int \nabla f\cdot\nabla g\,\ee^{-S}\dd x .
  \label{eq:finite-dimensional-dirichlet-form}
\end{equation}
Therefore \(L\) is symmetric in \(L^2(\ee^{-S}\dd x)\), and the probability
measure \(Z^{-1}\ee^{-S}\dd x\) is invariant.

\begin{proof}
Integrating the drift term by parts gives
\[
  \int (-\partial_iS)(\partial_i f)g\,\ee^{-S}\dd x
  =
  \int \partial_i(\ee^{-S})(\partial_i f)g\,\dd x .
\]
Adding the Laplacian contribution and integrating by parts once more,
\[
  \int
  \bigl[\partial_i(\ee^{-S})(\partial_i f)g
        +(\partial_i^2 f)g\ee^{-S}\bigr]\dd x
  =
  -\int (\partial_i f)(\partial_i g)\ee^{-S}\dd x .
\]
Summing over \(i\) gives \eqref{eq:finite-dimensional-dirichlet-form}.  With
\(g=1\), the right-hand side vanishes, so the adjoint generator annihilates
\(\ee^{-S}\dd x\); this is invariance.
\end{proof}

\section{Regularized Field Equation}

Let \(M\) be a compact Euclidean spatial manifold, let \(C_\Lambda\) be a
smooth ultraviolet cutoff covariance on \(M\), and let \(\xi_\Lambda\) be
white noise in stochastic time with spatial covariance chosen so that the
linear equation has invariant Gaussian law \(\mu_{C_\Lambda}\).  A
regularized scalar \(\Phi^4\) Langevin equation has the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau\phi_\Lambda
  =
  -C_\Lambda^{-1}\phi_\Lambda
  -\lambda_\Lambda\phi_\Lambda^3
  +c_\Lambda\phi_\Lambda
  +\xi_\Lambda .
  \label{eq:regularized-spde-gradient-flow}
\end{equation}
The drift is the negative \(L^2\)-gradient of the regulated action
\begin{equation}
  S_\Lambda(\phi)
  =
  \frac12(\phi,C_\Lambda^{-1}\phi)
  +
  \int_M
  \left[
    \frac{\lambda_\Lambda}{4}\phi^4
    -\frac{c_\Lambda}{2}\phi^2
  \right]\dd^dx .
  \label{eq:regularized-spde-action}
\end{equation}
At finite \(\Lambda\), the argument of the previous section applies on the
finite-dimensional cutoff field space.  Thus
\[
  Z_\Lambda^{-1}\ee^{-S_\Lambda(\phi)}D\phi
\]
is invariant.  The constructive question is whether the laws of
\(\phi_\Lambda\), with tuned local coordinates, converge as
\(\Lambda\to\infty\).

\section{Renormalized Products}

For the Gaussian free field in spatial dimension \(d\geq2\), \(\phi(x)\) is
a distribution.  Products in the drift are therefore defined through
renormalized local functionals.  If
\(\phi_\epsilon=\rho_\epsilon\ast\phi\) and
\[
  C_\epsilon=\mathbb E[\phi_\epsilon(x)^2],
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  :\!\phi_\epsilon^3\!:
  =
  \phi_\epsilon^3-3C_\epsilon\phi_\epsilon .
  \label{eq:spde-cubic-wick-product}
\end{equation}
The coefficient \(3\) is not conventional: it counts the possible
contractions of two of the three fields.  The same convention gives
\[
  :\!\phi_\epsilon^4\!:
  =
  \phi_\epsilon^4-6C_\epsilon\phi_\epsilon^2+3C_\epsilon^2 .
\]

\section{Stationary Stochastic Convolution and Wick Powers}

The first analytic object in the Da Prato--Debussche construction is the
stationary solution of the linear stochastic heat equation.  We record the
Fourier construction because it fixes the normalization of the noise and
proves, in a Sobolev form, the existence of the Wick powers used later.

Let \(\mathbb T^2=\R^2/(2\pi\mathbb Z)^2\), and let
\(\{e_k\}_{k\in\mathbb Z^2}\) be the orthonormal Fourier basis of
\(L^2(\mathbb T^2)\), normalized so that
\(-\Delta e_k=|k|^2e_k\).  The spacetime white noise in the stochastic
quantization equation is normalized by
\[
  \mathbb E[\xi(\tau,x)\xi(\tau',y)]
  =
  2\,\delta(\tau-\tau')\delta(x-y).
\]
Equivalently, in Fourier coordinates,
\begin{equation}
  \dd X_k(\tau)
  =
  -(|k|^2+m^2)X_k(\tau)\dd\tau+\sqrt2\,\dd B_k(\tau),
  \qquad X_{-k}=\overline{X_k},
  \label{eq:spde-fourier-ou-mode}
\end{equation}
where the complex Brownian motions are paired so that the reconstructed
field is real.

\begin{lemma}[Stationary covariance of the linear SPDE]
\label{lem:spde-stationary-ou-covariance}
The stationary solution of \eqref{eq:spde-fourier-ou-mode} satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E[X_k\overline{X_\ell}]
  =
  \frac{\delta_{k\ell}}{|k|^2+m^2}.
  \label{eq:spde-stationary-fourier-covariance}
\end{equation}
Consequently \(X(\tau,\cdot)\) has the massive Gaussian free-field law with
covariance \((-\Delta+m^2)^{-1}\), and
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E\|X(\tau)\|_{H^{-s}(\mathbb T^2)}^2
  =
  \sum_{k\in\mathbb Z^2}
  \frac{(1+|k|^2)^{-s}}{|k|^2+m^2}
  <\infty
  \qquad(s>0).
  \label{eq:spde-ou-negative-sobolev-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For the one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck equation
\(\dd Z_t=-aZ_t\dd t+\sqrt2\,\dd B_t\), stationarity gives
\[
  Z_t=\sqrt2\int_{-\infty}^{t}e^{-a(t-r)}\,\dd B_r,
  \qquad
  \mathbb E|Z_t|^2
  =
  2\int_0^\infty e^{-2au}\,\dd u
  =
  a^{-1}.
\]
Taking \(a=|k|^2+m^2\) gives
\eqref{eq:spde-stationary-fourier-covariance}.  The Sobolev expectation is
the displayed Fourier sum.  In two dimensions the large-\(|k|\) summand is
comparable to \(|k|^{-2-2s}\), whose lattice sum converges exactly when
\(s>0\).
\end{proof}

Let \(P_N\) be the Fourier projection to \(|k|\leq N\), set \(X_N=P_NX\),
and write
\[
  C_N(x-y)=\mathbb E[X_N(x)X_N(y)].
\]
The Wick powers are
\[
  :\!X_N^2\!:=X_N^2-C_N(0),\qquad
  :\!X_N^3\!:=X_N^3-3C_N(0)X_N .
\]

\begin{lemma}[Two-dimensional massive-convolution bound]
\label{lem:spde-massive-convolution-bound}
Let
\[
  g(k)=\frac{1}{1+|k|^2},\qquad
  L(k)=1+\log(2+|k|),
  \qquad k\in\mathbb Z^2 .
\]
For every integer \(n\geq1\) there is \(C_n<\infty\) such that the
\(n\)-fold lattice convolution satisfies
\begin{equation}
  g^{*n}(q)
  \leq
  C_n\,\frac{L(q)^{n-1}}{1+|q|^2},
  \qquad q\in\mathbb Z^2 .
  \label{eq:spde-lattice-convolution-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The case \(n=1\) is the definition.  The induction step is the elementary
estimate
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{p\in\mathbb Z^2}
  \frac{L(p)^a}{(1+|p|^2)(1+|q-p|^2)}
  \leq
  C_a\,\frac{L(q)^{a+1}}{1+|q|^2},
  \qquad a\geq0 .
  \label{eq:spde-convolution-induction-estimate}
\end{equation}
For \(|q|\leq4\) the left side is finite and the estimate is absorbed into
the constant.  Assume \(|q|>4\).  Split the sum into three regions:
\[
  A=\{|p|\leq |q|/2\},\qquad
  B=\{|q-p|\leq |q|/2\},\qquad
  C=(A\cup B)^c .
\]
On \(A\), \(1+|q-p|^2\geq c(1+|q|^2)\), and
\[
  \sum_{|p|\leq |q|/2}\frac{L(p)^a}{1+|p|^2}
  \leq
  C_a L(q)^{a+1},
\]
because the two-dimensional shell of radius \(r\) contributes
\(\lesssim r^{-1}(1+\log r)^a\).  This proves the desired bound on \(A\).
The region \(B\) is identical after replacing \(r=q-p\): then
\(|p|\geq |q|/2\), \(L(p)\leq C L(q)\), and the remaining
\(\sum_{|r|\leq |q|/2}(1+|r|^2)^{-1}\) contributes one additional
logarithm.
On \(C\), both \(|p|\) and \(|q-p|\) are \(\geq |q|/2\).  Decompose \(C\)
into dyadic annuli \(|p|\sim 2^j|q|\) and
\(|q-p|\sim 2^k|q|\).  The number of lattice points in a two-dimensional
dyadic annulus of radius \(R\) is \(O(R^2)\), while the summand is bounded
by \(C L(p)^a/(|p|^2|q-p|^2)\).  Summing first over the smaller of the two
annuli and then over the larger gives
\[
  \sum_{p\in C}
  \frac{L(p)^a}{(1+|p|^2)(1+|q-p|^2)}
  \leq
  C_a\frac{L(q)^a}{1+|q|^2}.
\]
Combining the three regions proves
\eqref{eq:spde-convolution-induction-estimate}.  Applying it with
\(a=n-1\) to \(g*g^{*n}\) proves
\eqref{eq:spde-lattice-convolution-bound} by induction.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Smeared Wick powers of the two-dimensional stochastic convolution]
\label{prop:spde-two-dimensional-wick-powers}
For every \(n\geq1\) and every \(f\in C^\infty(\mathbb T^2)\), the random
variables
\[
  :\!X_N^n\!:(f)=\int_{\mathbb T^2}f(x):\!X_N(x)^n\!:\,\dd^2x
\]
converge in \(L^2(\Omega)\) as \(N\to\infty\).  Moreover, for every
\(s>0\), the fields \(:\!X_N^n\!:\) converge in
\(L^2(\Omega;H^{-s}(\mathbb T^2))\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Wick product covariance is the finite-dimensional Gaussian identity
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E\!\left[
    :\!X_N(x)^n\!:\,:\!X_M(y)^n\!:
  \right]
  =
  n!\,C_{N,M}(x-y)^n,
  \qquad
  C_{N,M}(x-y)=\mathbb E[X_N(x)X_M(y)] .
  \label{eq:spde-wick-power-covariance}
\end{equation}
This follows from the generating function
\(\exp(tX_N-\frac12t^2C_N(0))\) and differentiating \(n\) times in each
variable.  Hence
\[
  \mathbb E\left[
    :\!X_N^n\!:(f)\,:\!X_M^n\!:(f)
  \right]
  =
  n!\int_{\mathbb T^2\times\mathbb T^2}
  f(x)f(y)\,C_{N,M}(x-y)^n\,\dd^2x\,\dd^2y .
\]
The massive two-dimensional covariance has the local form
\[
  C(x)=-\frac{1}{2\pi}\log |x|+O(1)
  \qquad (x\to0),
\]
and powers of \(\log |x|\) are locally integrable in two dimensions:
\[
  \int_0^r \rho\,|\log \rho|^n\,\dd\rho<\infty .
\]
The kernels \(C_{N,M}\) converge to \(C\) away from the diagonal, and the
standard Fourier cutoff bounds are dominated by an integrable logarithmic
majorant.  Dominated convergence therefore proves the \(L^2\)-Cauchy
property for every smeared Wick power.

For the Sobolev statement, use the dual norm
\[
  \|u\|_{H^{-s}}^2
  =
  \sum_{q\in\mathbb Z^2}
  (1+|q|^2)^{-s}|\widehat u(q)|^2 .
\]
Applying \eqref{eq:spde-wick-power-covariance} to Fourier test functions
shows that
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E|\widehat{:\!X_N^n\!:}(q)|^2
  =
  n!\!
  \sum_{\substack{k_1+\cdots+k_n=q\\ |k_i|\leq N}}
  \prod_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{|k_i|^2+m^2}.
  \label{eq:spde-wick-fourier-variance-convolution}
\end{equation}
The right side is bounded by
\(C_{n,m}L(q)^{n-1}(1+|q|^2)^{-1}\) by
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-massive-convolution-bound}.  Therefore
\[
  \sum_{q\in\mathbb Z^2}
  (1+|q|^2)^{-s}
  \mathbb E|\widehat{:\!X_N^n\!:}(q)|^2
  \leq
  C_{n,m}
  \sum_{q\in\mathbb Z^2}
  \frac{L(q)^{n-1}}{(1+|q|^2)^{1+s}}<\infty
\]
for every \(s>0\).  For fixed \(q\), the cutoff variances and mixed
covariances converge by dominated convergence of the convolution sums.
The same convolution bound dominates
\(\mathbb E|\widehat{:\!X_N^n\!:}(q)
          -\widehat{:\!X_M^n\!:}(q)|^2\), up to a harmless constant, so
dominated convergence over \(q\) proves convergence in
\(L^2(\Omega;H^{-s})\).  In particular the stochastic input needed by the
Sobolev DPD fixed point below is available for any prescribed
\(0<\kappa<1/4\) after taking \(s=\kappa\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Heat-kernel smoothing estimate]
\label{prop:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}
Let \(A=\Delta-m^2\) on \(\mathbb T^d\).  For \(r\in\R\), \(\theta\geq0\),
and \(t>0\),
\begin{equation}
  \|e^{tA}u\|_{H^{r+2\theta}}
  \leq
  C_\theta t^{-\theta}e^{-m^2t/2}\|u\|_{H^r}.
  \label{eq:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}
\end{equation}
Consequently, if \(F\in L^\infty([0,T];H^r)\), then
\begin{equation}
  \left\|
    \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}F(s)\,\dd s
  \right\|_{H^{r+2\theta}}
  \leq
  C_{\theta,T}\|F\|_{L^\infty([0,T];H^r)}
  \quad(0\leq\theta<1).
  \label{eq:spde-duhamel-smoothing}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In Fourier coordinates,
\[
  \|e^{tA}u\|_{H^{r+2\theta}}^2
  =
  \sum_k
  (1+|k|^2)^{r+2\theta}
  e^{-2t(|k|^2+m^2)}
  |\widehat u(k)|^2 .
\]
The elementary bound
\[
  (1+|k|^2)^{2\theta}e^{-t(|k|^2+m^2)}
  \leq C_\theta t^{-2\theta}e^{-m^2t/2}
\]
gives \eqref{eq:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}.  The Duhamel estimate follows
by integrating
\((t-s)^{-\theta}\) over \(0<s<t\), which is finite exactly for
\(\theta<1\).
\end{proof}

\section{Da Prato--Debussche in Two Dimensions}

On a two-dimensional torus, consider the stochastic quantization equation
for the Wick-ordered measure
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau\Phi
  =
  \Delta\Phi-m^2\Phi-\lambda:\!\Phi^3\!:+\xi .
  \label{eq:dpd-phi-four-two}
\end{equation}
Let \(X\) solve the stationary linear equation
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau X=(\Delta-m^2)X+\xi .
  \label{eq:ornstein-uhlenbeck-field}
\end{equation}
The field \(X(\tau,\cdot)\) has the massive Gaussian free-field law on the
spatial torus and is almost surely a spatial distribution of regularity
\(-\kappa\) for every \(\kappa>0\).  Write
\[
  \Phi=X+Y.
\]
Then the nonlinear equation for \(Y\) is
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau Y
  =
  (\Delta-m^2)Y
  -\lambda\bigl(
    Y^3+3Y^2X+3Y:\!X^2\!:+:\!X^3\!:
  \bigr).
  \label{eq:dpd-remainder-equation}
\end{equation}
In two dimensions the products in
\eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation} are well-defined once
\(:\!X^2\!:\) and \(:\!X^3\!:\) are constructed as Wick powers, because the
unknown \(Y\) gains positive parabolic regularity from the heat operator.

\begin{lemma}[Sobolev products for the DPD fixed point]
\label{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products}
Let \(0<\kappa<1/4\) and set \(\beta=1+2\kappa\).  On \(\mathbb T^2\),
there is \(C_\kappa<\infty\) such that
\begin{align}
  \|uv\|_{H^\beta}
  &\leq C_\kappa\|u\|_{H^\beta}\|v\|_{H^\beta},
  \label{eq:spde-hbeta-algebra}\\
  \|uv\|_{H^{-\kappa}}
  &\leq C_\kappa\|u\|_{H^\beta}\|v\|_{H^{-\kappa}},
  \label{eq:spde-hbeta-negative-multiplier}
\end{align}
for \(u,v\) in the indicated spaces.  Consequently, if
\(\mathbb X=(X,X^{(2)},X^{(3)})\in C([0,T];H^{-\kappa})^3\) and
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)
  =
  Y^3+3Y^2X+3YX^{(2)}+X^{(3)},
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity}
\end{equation}
then \(\mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)\in C([0,T];H^{-\kappa})\) for every
\(Y\in C([0,T];H^\beta)\).  We write
\[
  \|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}
  =
  \max_{1\leq j\leq3}\|X^{(j)}\|_{C([0,T];H^{-\kappa})},
  \qquad X^{(1)}=X,
\]
and for
\(\|Y\|_{C_TH^\beta},\|Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}\leq R\),
\begin{equation}
  \|\mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)-\mathcal N(Z,\mathbb X)\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}
  \leq
  C_{\kappa,R}\bigl(1+\|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}\bigr)
  \|Y-Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}.
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity-lipschitz}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We recall the dyadic proof because this is the exact multiplication input in
the fixed point.  Let \(\Delta_j\) be a Littlewood--Paley partition on the
torus and \(S_j=\sum_{i<j}\Delta_i\).  Bony's decomposition writes
\[
  uv=u\prec v+u\circ v+u\succ v,
  \qquad
  u\prec v=\sum_j S_{j-1}u\,\Delta_jv ,
\]
with \(u\circ v=\sum_{|i-j|\leq1}\Delta_i u\,\Delta_jv\).  Bernstein's
inequality in two dimensions gives
\begin{equation}
  \|\Delta_j u\|_{L^\infty}\leq C2^{j(1-\beta)}\|u\|_{H^\beta}
  \qquad(\beta>1),
  \label{eq:spde-bernstein-hbeta-linfty-block}
\end{equation}
and summing in \(j\) gives \(H^\beta\hookrightarrow L^\infty\).  The
paraproduct estimates following from the almost-disjoint frequency supports
are
\begin{equation}
  \|u\prec v\|_{H^s}+\|u\succ v\|_{H^s}
  \leq C\|u\|_{L^\infty}\|v\|_{H^s}
       +C\|v\|_{L^\infty}\|u\|_{H^s},
\end{equation}
and, for the resonant term,
\begin{equation}
  \|u\circ v\|_{H^{s+t-1}}
  \leq C\|u\|_{H^s}\|v\|_{H^t}
  \quad\text{when }s+t>1 .
  \label{eq:spde-resonant-product-estimate}
\end{equation}
Indeed, \(\Delta_\ell(u\prec v)\) only receives terms with
\(|j-\ell|\leq O(1)\), so
\[
  2^{\ell s}\|\Delta_\ell(u\prec v)\|_{L^2}
  \leq
  C\|u\|_{L^\infty}
  \sum_{|j-\ell|\leq O(1)}2^{js}\|\Delta_jv\|_{L^2},
\]
and square summation in \(\ell\) gives the first paraproduct bound; the
bound for \(u\succ v\) is identical.  For \(u\circ v\), the product
\(\Delta_i u\,\Delta_j v\) with \(|i-j|\leq1\) has frequencies
\(\lesssim 2^j\).  Bernstein and Cauchy--Schwarz give
\(\|\Delta_i u\,\Delta_jv\|_{L^2}\leq
C2^{j(1-s-t)}a_ib_j\), where
\(a_j=2^{js}\|\Delta_j u\|_{L^2}\) and
\(b_j=2^{jt}\|\Delta_jv\|_{L^2}\).  Multiplication by the
\(H^{s+t-1}\) weight cancels the factor \(2^{j(1-s-t)}\), and the condition
\(s+t>1\) makes the finite-overlap high-frequency convolution summable.
Taking \(s=t=\beta\) gives the algebra estimate
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-algebra}, since \(2\beta-1>\beta\).  Taking
\(s=\beta\) and \(t=-\kappa\) gives
\(\beta-\kappa>1\), so the resonant term lands in
\(H^{\beta-\kappa-1}\).  Because \(\beta-\kappa-1=\kappa>-\kappa\), this
term is in \(H^{-\kappa}\), and the two paraproduct terms are controlled by
the \(L^\infty\) embedding of \(H^\beta\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-negative-multiplier}.

The bounds on \(\mathcal N\) follow by applying
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-algebra} and
\eqref{eq:spde-hbeta-negative-multiplier} term by term:
\[
  \|Y^3\|_{H^\beta}\leq C\|Y\|_{H^\beta}^3,
  \quad
  \|Y^2X\|_{H^{-\kappa}}\leq C\|Y\|_{H^\beta}^2\|X\|_{H^{-\kappa}},
\]
and similarly for \(YX^{(2)}\).  For the Lipschitz estimate, use
\[
  Y^3-Z^3=(Y-Z)(Y^2+YZ+Z^2),
  \qquad
  Y^2X-Z^2X=(Y-Z)(Y+Z)X,
\]
and the same two product estimates.  This gives
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity-lipschitz}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Deterministic DPD local fixed point in Sobolev coordinates]
\label{thm:spde-dpd-deterministic-local-fixed-point}
Let \(0<\kappa<1/4\), \(\beta=1+2\kappa\), \(m^2>0\), \(\lambda>0\), and
\[
  \mathbb X=(X,X^{(2)},X^{(3)})\in C([0,T_0];H^{-\kappa}(\mathbb T^2))^3 .
\]
Set
\[
  M_{\mathbb X}
  =
  \max_{1\leq j\leq3}
  \|X^{(j)}\|_{C([0,T_0];H^{-\kappa})},
  \qquad X^{(1)}=X .
\]
For every \(Y_0\in H^\beta(\mathbb T^2)\) there is
\(T\in(0,T_0]\), depending only on
\(\|Y_0\|_{H^\beta}\), \(\lambda\), \(m\), \(\kappa\), and \(M_{\mathbb X}\),
such that the mild equation
\begin{equation}
  Y(t)
  =
  e^{t(\Delta-m^2)}Y_0
  -
  \lambda\int_0^t
  e^{(t-s)(\Delta-m^2)}
  \mathcal N(Y(s),\mathbb X(s))\,\dd s
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-mild-fixed-point}
\end{equation}
has a unique solution \(Y\in C([0,T];H^\beta)\).  The solution depends
locally Lipschitz continuously on \(Y_0\) and on \(\mathbb X\) in these
norms.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(C_T H^r=C([0,T];H^r)\) and put \(A=\Delta-m^2\).  Define
\[
  (\Psi Y)(t)
  =
  e^{tA}Y_0
  -
  \lambda\int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}\mathcal N(Y(s),\mathbb X(s))\,\dd s .
\]
The forcing belongs to \(C_T H^{-\kappa}\) by
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products}.  To estimate the Duhamel term in
\(H^\beta\), use Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing} with
\[
  r=-\kappa,\qquad
  r+2\theta=\beta,\qquad
  \theta=\frac{\beta+\kappa}{2}=\frac{1+3\kappa}{2}.
\]
Since \(\kappa<1/4\), \(\theta<1\).  Hence
\begin{equation}
  \left\|
    \int_0^t e^{(t-s)A}F(s)\,\dd s
  \right\|_{C_TH^\beta}
  \leq
  C_{\kappa,m}T^{1-\theta}\|F\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}} .
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-duhamel-time-gain}
\end{equation}
Choose \(R=2C\|Y_0\|_{H^\beta}+1\), where \(C\) bounds the heat semigroup
on \(H^\beta\).  Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products} gives, for
\(\|Y\|_{C_TH^\beta}\leq R\),
\[
  \|\mathcal N(Y,\mathbb X)\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}
  \leq
  C_{\kappa,R}\bigl(1+\|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}\bigr).
\]
By \eqref{eq:spde-dpd-duhamel-time-gain}, \(T\) can be chosen so small that
\(\Psi\) maps the closed ball \(B_R\subset C_TH^\beta\) into itself.
For \(Y,Z\in B_R\), equations
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-nonlinearity-lipschitz} and
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-duhamel-time-gain} give
\[
  \|\Psi Y-\Psi Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}
  \leq
  |\lambda|C_{\kappa,R,m}
  T^{1-\theta}
  \bigl(1+\|\mathbb X\|_{C_TH^{-\kappa}}\bigr)
  \|Y-Z\|_{C_TH^\beta}.
\]
Reducing \(T\) once more makes the coefficient \(<1\).  Starting from
any \(Y_0^\sharp\in B_R\) and setting \(Y_{n+1}^\sharp=\Psi Y_n^\sharp\),
the differences obey
\[
  \|Y_{n+1}^\sharp-Y_n^\sharp\|_{C_TH^\beta}
  \leq q^n\|Y_1^\sharp-Y_0^\sharp\|_{C_TH^\beta},
  \qquad q<1.
\]
The resulting sequence is Cauchy in the complete space \(C_TH^\beta\);
its limit is a fixed point, and the same inequality applied to two fixed
points forces equality.  Applying the contraction estimate to two sets of
data gives local Lipschitz dependence, after taking the smaller of the two
local existence times.
\end{proof}

This theorem is deliberately weaker than the sharp Da Prato--Debussche
theorem in Holder--Besov spaces, but it proves the exact deterministic
logic: enhanced noise first makes every term in the nonlinearity meaningful,
then heat smoothing supplies a positive time power, and the local solution
is obtained by contraction.  The remaining global and invariant-measure
steps require estimates beyond this local theorem.

\begin{proposition}[Smooth enhanced-noise energy estimate]
\label{prop:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate}
Let \(X_1,X_2,X_3\) be smooth real functions on
\([0,T]\times\mathbb T^2\), let \(m^2>0\), \(\lambda>0\), and let \(Y\) be
a smooth solution of
\begin{equation}
  \partial_tY
  =
  (\Delta-m^2)Y
  -\lambda\bigl(Y^3+3Y^2X_1+3YX_2+X_3\bigr).
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-equation}
\end{equation}
Then, for a numerical constant \(C\),
\begin{align}
  \frac12\frac{d}{dt}\|Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2
  &+
  \|\nabla Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2
  +m^2\|Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2
  +\frac{\lambda}{4}\|Y(t)\|_{L^4}^4
  \notag\\
  &\leq
  C\lambda
  \left(
    \|X_1(t)\|_{L^4}^4
    +\|X_2(t)\|_{L^2}^2
    +\|X_3(t)\|_{L^{4/3}}^{4/3}
  \right).
  \label{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate}
\end{align}
Consequently no finite-time blowup can occur in a smooth Galerkin
approximation whose enhanced noise norms on the right-hand side stay
integrable on compact stochastic-time intervals.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Multiply \eqref{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-equation} by \(Y\) and integrate
over the torus.  Periodicity gives
\[
  \int_{\mathbb T^2}Y\Delta Y\,\dd^2x
  =
  -\|\nabla Y\|_{L^2}^2 .
\]
Thus
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \frac12\frac{d}{dt}\|Y\|_{L^2}^2
  &+
  \|\nabla Y\|_{L^2}^2
  +m^2\|Y\|_{L^2}^2
  +\lambda\|Y\|_{L^4}^4 \\
  &=
  -3\lambda\int Y^3X_1
  -3\lambda\int Y^2X_2
  -\lambda\int YX_3 .
\end{aligned}
\]
Young's inequality with conjugate exponents \((4/3,4)\), \((2,2)\), and
\((4,4/3)\) gives pointwise bounds
\[
\begin{aligned}
  3|Y|^3|X_1|
  &\leq \frac14 |Y|^4+C|X_1|^4,\\
  3|Y|^2|X_2|
  &\leq \frac14 |Y|^4+C|X_2|^2,\\
  |Y||X_3|
  &\leq \frac14 |Y|^4+C|X_3|^{4/3}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Substituting these three estimates into the energy identity and retaining
the remaining positive \(\lambda\|Y\|_{L^4}^4/4\) proves
\eqref{eq:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate}.  Integrating the inequality in
time bounds \(\|Y(t)\|_{L^2}^2\), the accumulated \(H^1\) norm, and the
accumulated \(L^4\) norm by the initial \(L^2\) norm and the three enhanced
noise norms.  A smooth Galerkin solution can be continued as long as its
finite-dimensional norm is finite; the displayed bound prevents blowup on
intervals where the right-hand side is integrable.
\end{proof}

The rough Da Prato--Debussche global argument replaces the smooth norms in
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-dpd-smooth-energy-estimate} by Besov or
paracontrolled norms of the enhanced noise.  The algebra is the same: the
quartic term absorbs powers of \(Y\), and all remaining terms are functions
only of the enhanced stochastic convolution on the time interval under
consideration.  The analytic work is to justify this calculation by
approximation and to prove integrability of the rough enhanced-noise norms.

\begin{proposition}[Passing invariance through a cutoff limit]
\label{prop:spde-invariant-measure-limit}
Let \(E\) be a Polish space, let \(P_t^{(n)}\) and \(P_t\) be Markov
semigroups on bounded continuous functions on \(E\), and let
\(\mu_n\) be an invariant probability measure for \(P_t^{(n)}\).  Suppose
\(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\) weakly and that, for every bounded continuous
function \(f\) and every fixed \(t\geq0\),
\[
  P_t^{(n)}f\longrightarrow P_tf
  \quad\hbox{uniformly on compact subsets of }E .
\]
Assume also that \(\{\mu_n\}\) is tight and that the convergence above is
uniform on a compact set whose \(\mu_n\)-mass is arbitrarily close to one,
uniformly in \(n\).  Then \(\mu\) is invariant for \(P_t\):
\[
  \int_E P_tf\,\dd\mu=\int_E f\,\dd\mu .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(f\in C_b(E)\), \(t\geq0\), and \(\eta>0\).  By tightness, choose a
compact set \(K\subset E\) with \(\mu_n(K)\geq1-\eta\) for all sufficiently
large \(n\).  Then
\[
  \left|
    \int P_t^{(n)}f\,\dd\mu_n
    -
    \int P_tf\,\dd\mu_n
  \right|
  \leq
  \sup_{x\in K}|P_t^{(n)}f(x)-P_tf(x)|
  +2\|f\|_\infty\eta ,
\]
which tends to at most \(2\|f\|_\infty\eta\).  Letting \(\eta\downarrow0\)
gives
\[
  \int P_t^{(n)}f\,\dd\mu_n
  -
  \int P_tf\,\dd\mu_n
  \longrightarrow0 .
\]
Since \(P_tf\) is bounded and continuous, weak convergence of \(\mu_n\)
gives \(\int P_tf\,\dd\mu_n\to\int P_tf\,\dd\mu\).  Invariance of
\(\mu_n\) gives \(\int P_t^{(n)}f\,\dd\mu_n=\int f\,\dd\mu_n\), and weak
convergence also gives \(\int f\,\dd\mu_n\to\int f\,\dd\mu\).  Combining
these limits proves the assertion.
\end{proof}

For singular SPDE, the hypotheses of
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-invariant-measure-limit} are not automatic.
They require tightness of the stationary cutoff laws in the chosen field
topology, convergence of the renormalized solution maps on high-probability
compact sets of enhanced noises and initial data, and continuity of the
limiting Markov semigroup on the same topology.  This is the exact
invariant-law step that sits between cutoff Langevin stationarity and a
Euclidean measure for QFT.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Da Prato--Debussche solution mechanism]
\label{thm:dpd-solution-mechanism}
Fix \(m^2>0\), \(\lambda>0\), and a finite spatial torus in dimension two.
For almost every realization of the enhanced noise
\[
  \mathbb X=(X,:\!X^2\!:,:\!X^3\!:),
\]
the remainder equation \eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation} has local
pathwise solutions in a positive-regularity function space, and the positive
quartic drift supplies a priori bounds that extend the Markov process
globally.  The invariant law is the Wick-ordered \(\Phi^4_2\) Euclidean
measure on the torus.
\end{quotedtheorem}

This Da Prato--Debussche theorem is quoted here as a precise SPDE theorem
boundary, not as a monograph-proved result.  The quoted source is the
Da Prato--Debussche stochastic-quantization construction for strong
solutions.  The mechanism is as follows.
The mild form of \eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation} is
\[
  Y(\tau)
  =
  e^{\tau(\Delta-m^2)}Y(0)
  -
  \lambda\int_0^\tau
  e^{(\tau-s)(\Delta-m^2)}
  \mathcal N(Y(s),\mathbb X(s))\,\dd s ,
\]
where \(\mathcal N\) is the cubic expression in
\eqref{eq:dpd-remainder-equation}.  Parabolic Schauder estimates increase
spatial regularity by two derivatives relative to the forcing; the Sobolev
version is Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-heat-kernel-smoothing}, while the
sharp Da Prato--Debussche proof uses the corresponding parabolic
Besov--Holder estimates.  Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-two-dimensional-wick-powers}
constructs the Wick powers in a Sobolev topology; the sharper statement used
in the fixed point places \(:\!X^2\!:\) and \(:\!X^3\!:\) in negative
Holder/Besov spaces of arbitrarily small negative regularity.
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-dpd-sobolev-products} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-dpd-deterministic-local-fixed-point} give a
self-contained Sobolev version of the local fixed-point argument.  The sharp
proof upgrades these Sobolev exponents to the scale-invariant parabolic
Besov--Holder spaces.  Multiplying the equation by \(Y\), integrating over
space, and using the sign of
\(\lambda Y^4\) gives an energy inequality with random lower-order forcing;
that estimate prevents blowup and gives global solutions.  The invariant law
is identified by applying the finite-cutoff Langevin identity and proving
tightness plus convergence of the enhanced noise.  In the final standard of
this monograph, these Schauder, multiplication, energy, tightness, and
invariant-law identification estimates are proof obligations, not facts
accepted merely because they are standard in the SPDE literature.

\section{\texorpdfstring{Regularity Structures and \(\Phi^4_3\)}{Regularity Structures and Phi-four-three}}

In three spatial dimensions the decomposition \(\Phi=X+Y\) is not
sufficient: \(X\) has regularity \(-\frac12-\kappa\), and the products
generated by the cubic drift fall below the classical multiplication
threshold.  Regularity structures supply the missing local expansion
calculus.

\begin{definition}[Regularity structure]
\label{def:regularity-structure}
A regularity structure is a triple \((A,T,G)\), where \(A\subset\R\) is a
locally finite set of homogeneities bounded below, \(T=\bigoplus_{\alpha\in
A}T_\alpha\) is a graded model space, and \(G\) is a structure group acting
on \(T\) such that for \(\tau\in T_\alpha\),
\[
  \Gamma\tau-\tau\in\bigoplus_{\beta<\alpha}T_\beta
  \qquad(\Gamma\in G).
\]
A model on spacetime assigns to every point \(z\) a linear map
\(\Pi_z:T\to\mathcal D'(\R^{1+d})\) and to every pair \(z,z'\) an element
\(\Gamma_{zz'}\in G\), satisfying
\[
  \Pi_z=\Pi_{z'}\Gamma_{z'z},\qquad
  \Gamma_{zz'}\Gamma_{z'z''}=\Gamma_{zz''},
\]
together with analytic bounds compatible with the homogeneities.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Parabolic model and modelled-distribution norms]
\label{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}
Let \(\mathfrak s=(2,1,\ldots,1)\) be the parabolic scaling on
\(\R^{1+d}\), let \(|\mathfrak s|=d+2\), and set
\[
  \|(\tau,x)\|_{\mathfrak s}
  =
  |\tau|^{1/2}+|x_1|+\cdots+|x_d|.
\]
For \(0<\delta\leq1\), \(z\in\R^{1+d}\), and
\(\varphi\in C_c^r(\R^{1+d})\), define
\[
  \varphi_z^\delta(w)
  =
  \delta^{-|\mathfrak s|}
  \varphi\bigl(\delta^{-2}(w_0-z_0),
               \delta^{-1}(w_1-z_1),\ldots,
               \delta^{-1}(w_d-z_d)\bigr).
\]
Let \(\mathcal B_r\) be the set of such test functions supported in the unit
\(\mathfrak s\)-ball and satisfying \(\|\varphi\|_{C^r}\leq1\).  Fix a
compact set \(K\) and \(\gamma>0\).  The model seminorm below \(\gamma\) is
finite when
\[
  \|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}
  =
  \sup_{\substack{z\in K,\ \alpha<\gamma\\
                  \tau\in T_\alpha,\ \|\tau\|_\alpha=1\\
                  \varphi\in\mathcal B_r,\ 0<\delta\leq1}}
  \delta^{-\alpha}
  \left|\langle\Pi_z\tau,\varphi_z^\delta\rangle\right|
  <\infty
\]
and
\[
  \|\Gamma\|_{K;\gamma}
  =
  \sup_{\substack{z,z'\in K,\ 0<\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq1\\
                  \beta<\alpha<\gamma,\ \tau\in T_\alpha,\ \|\tau\|_\alpha=1}}
  \frac{\|(\Gamma_{zz'}\tau)_\beta\|_\beta}
       {\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\alpha-\beta}}
  <\infty .
\]
Here \((\cdot)_\beta\) denotes projection to \(T_\beta\).  A modelled
distribution of regularity \(\gamma\) on \(K\) is a function
\[
  F:K\longrightarrow T_{<\gamma}:=\bigoplus_{\alpha<\gamma}T_\alpha
\]
with finite norm
\[
  \|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  =
  \sup_{z\in K,\ \alpha<\gamma}\|F_\alpha(z)\|_\alpha
  +
  \sup_{\substack{z,z'\in K,\ 0<\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq1\\ \alpha<\gamma}}
  \frac{\|F_\alpha(z)-(\Gamma_{zz'}F(z'))_\alpha\|_\alpha}
       {\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\gamma-\alpha}} .
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Coherence of a modelled local germ]
\label{lem:spde-modelled-germ-coherence}
Let \(F\) be a modelled distribution in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}, and set
\(\zeta_z=\Pi_zF(z)\).  If \(z,z'\in K\),
\(\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq\delta\leq1\), and
\(\varphi\in\mathcal B_r\), then
\[
  \left|
    \left\langle \zeta_z-\zeta_{z'},\varphi_z^\delta\right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  C_K\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\,\delta^\gamma .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The model identity gives
\[
  \Pi_{z'}F(z')=\Pi_z\Gamma_{zz'}F(z') ,
\]
hence
\[
  \zeta_z-\zeta_{z'}
  =
  \Pi_z\bigl(F(z)-\Gamma_{zz'}F(z')\bigr).
\]
Decompose the vector in parentheses into homogeneous components.  For each
\(\alpha<\gamma\), the modelled-distribution norm gives
\[
  \|(F(z)-\Gamma_{zz'}F(z'))_\alpha\|_\alpha
  \leq
  \|F\|_{\gamma;K}\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\gamma-\alpha}.
\]
The model bound gives
\[
  \left|
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z (F(z)-\Gamma_{zz'}F(z'))_\alpha,
    \varphi_z^\delta
  \right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  \|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^\alpha\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\gamma-\alpha}.
\]
Since \(\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq\delta\) and the set
\(\{\alpha\in A:\alpha<\gamma\}\) is finite on compact homogeneity windows,
the sum over \(\alpha\) is bounded by the displayed constant times
\(\delta^\gamma\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Compact finite-sector reconstruction theorem]
\label{thm:spde-compact-reconstruction}
Assume that the set \(A_{<\gamma}=\{\alpha\in A:\alpha<\gamma\}\) is finite,
assume \(0<\gamma<|\mathfrak s|\),
let
\[
  r>\max\{-\min A_{<\gamma},\,\gamma\},
\]
and let the model seminorms of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms} be finite on
a compact parabolic neighborhood of \(K\).  For every modelled distribution
\(F\) of regularity \(\gamma>0\) on that neighborhood, there is a unique
distribution \(\mathcal RF\) on the interior of \(K\) such that, for every
\(\varphi\in\mathcal B_r\), \(z\in K\), and \(0<\delta\leq1\) with
\(\operatorname{supp}\varphi_z^\delta\subset K\),
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    \left\langle
      \mathcal RF-\Pi_zF(z),\varphi_z^\delta
    \right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  C_K
  (1+\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma})(1+\|\Gamma\|_{K;\gamma})
  \|F\|_{\gamma;K}\,\delta^\gamma .
  \label{eq:spde-reconstruction-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose a compactly supported parabolic multiresolution system of class
\(C^r\): scaling functions \(\varphi_y^0\) at scale \(1\), wavelets
\(\psi_y^n\) at scale \(2^{-n}\), \(y\in\Lambda_n\), with vanishing moments
against all parabolic polynomials of degree \(<r\), finite overlap at each
scale, and the expansion formula
\[
  \eta
  =
  \sum_{y\in\Lambda_0}\langle\eta,\varphi_y^0\rangle\varphi_y^0
  +
  \sum_{n\geq0}\sum_{y\in\Lambda_n}
  \langle\eta,\psi_y^n\rangle\psi_y^n
\]
for every test function \(\eta\) supported in the interior of \(K\).  The
wavelets are \(L^2\)-normalized, so
\(\psi_y^n=2^{n|\mathfrak s|/2}\psi_y^{2^{-n}}\) in the notation of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}.

Define a distribution by prescribing its coefficients
\[
  \langle\mathcal RF,\varphi_y^0\rangle
  =
  \langle\Pi_yF(y),\varphi_y^0\rangle,\qquad
  \langle\mathcal RF,\psi_y^n\rangle
  =
  \langle\Pi_yF(y),\psi_y^n\rangle .
\]
The coefficient estimate for \(\psi_y^n\) is
\[
  |\langle\Pi_yF(y),\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  2^{-n(\alpha_0+|\mathfrak s|/2)},
  \qquad
  \alpha_0=\min A_{<\gamma}.
\]
For a \(C^r\) test function \(\eta\), the wavelet moment cancellation gives
\[
  |\langle\eta,\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C_\eta 2^{-n(r+|\mathfrak s|/2)} .
\]
Only \(O(2^{n|\mathfrak s|})\) wavelets at scale \(2^{-n}\) meet
\(\operatorname{supp}\eta\), hence the absolute value of the scale-\(n\)
contribution is bounded by
\[
  C_\eta\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  2^{n|\mathfrak s|}
  2^{-n(\alpha_0+|\mathfrak s|/2)}
  2^{-n(r+|\mathfrak s|/2)}
  =
  C_\eta\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}2^{-n(\alpha_0+r)} .
\]
The hypothesis \(r>-\alpha_0\) makes the series converge.  Thus
\(\mathcal RF\) is a well-defined distribution.

It remains to prove the local estimate.  Fix \(z\), \(\delta\), and
\(\varphi_z^\delta\).  Put \(\ell_n=2^{-n}\).  The coefficient of
\(\mathcal RF-\Pi_zF(z)\) against a wavelet \(\psi_y^n\) equals
\[
  \langle\Pi_yF(y)-\Pi_zF(z),\psi_y^n\rangle .
\]
If \(\psi_y^n\) meets the support of \(\varphi_z^\delta\), then
\(\|y-z\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq C(\ell_n+\delta)\).  Split the wavelet expansion
into coarse scales \(\ell_n\geq\delta\) and fine scales \(\ell_n<\delta\).

At a coarse scale, \(\|y-z\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq C\ell_n\), so
Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-modelled-germ-coherence}, tested at scale \(\ell_n\),
gives
\[
  |\langle\Pi_yF(y)-\Pi_zF(z),\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \ell_n^{\gamma+|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
The \(r\) vanishing moments of \(\psi_y^n\) and the \(C^r\) bound of
\(\varphi_z^\delta\) give
\[
  |\langle\varphi_z^\delta,\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\,\delta^r\ell_n^{-r-|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
Only a bounded number of coarse-scale wavelets meet the support of
\(\varphi_z^\delta\).  Therefore the coarse contribution is bounded by
\[
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\delta^\gamma ,
\]
because
\[
  \sum_{\ell_n\geq\delta}\delta^r\ell_n^{\gamma-r}
  \leq
  C\delta^\gamma
\]
when \(r>\gamma\).
The scale-one scaling functions are handled in the same coarse block except
that no moment cancellation is needed: their coefficients are bounded and
their pairing with \(\varphi_z^\delta\) is \(O(\delta^{|\mathfrak s|})\),
which is \(O(\delta^\gamma)\) under the hypothesis \(\gamma<|\mathfrak s|\).

At a fine scale, write the modelled-distribution difference by homogeneity:
\[
  \langle\mathcal RF-\Pi_zF(z),\psi_y^n\rangle
  =
  \sum_{\alpha<\gamma}
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z\bigl(\Gamma_{zy}F(y)-F(z)\bigr)_\alpha,\psi_y^n
  \right\rangle .
\]
For wavelets meeting the support of \(\varphi_z^\delta\),
\(\|y-z\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq C\delta\).  The \(\alpha\)-component of the
modelled-distribution difference is therefore bounded by
\[
  C\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\delta^{\gamma-\alpha}.
\]
Testing the corresponding model component at wavelet scale \(\ell_n\) gives
\[
  \left|
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z\bigl(\Gamma_{zy}F(y)-F(z)\bigr)_\alpha,\psi_y^n
  \right\rangle
  \right|
  \leq
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^{\gamma-\alpha}\ell_n^{\alpha+|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
The wavelet moment estimate in the opposite scale regime gives
\[
  |\langle\varphi_z^\delta,\psi_y^n\rangle|
  \leq
  C\,\delta^{-|\mathfrak s|-r}\ell_n^{r+|\mathfrak s|/2}.
\]
There are \(O((\delta/\ell_n)^{|\mathfrak s|})\) fine-scale wavelets meeting
the support of \(\varphi_z^\delta\).  The scale-\(n\) contribution of a fixed
homogeneity \(\alpha\) is thus bounded by
\[
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^{\gamma-\alpha-r}\ell_n^{\alpha+r}.
\]
Since \(r>-\alpha\), the sum over \(\ell_n<\delta\) is bounded by
\[
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}
  \delta^{\gamma-\alpha-r}\delta^{\alpha+r}
  =
  C\|\Pi\|_{K;\gamma}\|F\|_{\gamma;K}\delta^\gamma .
\]
There are only finitely many \(\alpha<\gamma\), so summing the homogeneities
proves \eqref{eq:spde-reconstruction-bound}.

For uniqueness, let \(U\) be the difference of two distributions satisfying
\eqref{eq:spde-reconstruction-bound}.  Choose a non-negative
\(\chi\in C_c^r\) with integral one.  For any test function \(\eta\) with
support in the interior of \(K\),
\[
  \langle U,\eta\rangle
  =
  \lim_{\delta\downarrow0}
  \int \eta(z)\langle U,\chi_z^\delta\rangle\,\dd z .
\]
The bound \(|\langle U,\chi_z^\delta\rangle|\leq C\delta^\gamma\) and
\(\gamma>0\) force the limit to vanish.  Hence \(U=0\).
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-compact-reconstruction} supplies the reconstruction
component of the finite-sector theory used by the local \(\Phi^4_3\)
regularity structure.  The still-open part for dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) is not
the reconstruction step itself; it is the construction of the
BPHZ-renormalized random model with uniform analytic bounds, the convergence
of those models as the mollifier is removed, and the nonlinear fixed-point
argument in the corresponding modelled-distribution spaces.

\begin{theorem}[Dyadic Cauchy criterion for random models]
\label{thm:spde-random-model-cauchy-criterion}
Fix a compact parabolic set \(K\), a regularity threshold \(\gamma>0\), and a
finite homogeneity set \(A_{<\gamma}\).  Choose the test regularity
\[
  r>-\min A_{<\gamma}.
\]
Let \(Z_n=(\Pi^{(n)},\Gamma^{(n)})\), \(n\geq1\), be random models on this
finite sector.  Define
\[
  N_n=
  1+\|\Pi^{(n)}\|_{K;\gamma}+\|\Gamma^{(n)}\|_{K;\gamma},
\]
where the seminorms are those of
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms}.  Let
\(d_{K;\gamma}(Z,Z')\) denote the corresponding model distance, obtained by
replacing \(\Pi\) and \(\Gamma\) in those seminorms by
\(\Pi-\Pi'\) and \(\Gamma-\Gamma'\).  Suppose that, for some
\(p\geq1\), \(\rho>0\), and constants \(C_N,C_D<\infty\),
\begin{equation}
  \sup_{n\geq1}\mathbb E N_n^p\leq C_N,
  \qquad
  \mathbb E\,d_{K;\gamma}(Z_{n+1},Z_n)^p\leq C_D2^{-p\rho n}.
  \label{eq:spde-random-model-cauchy-hypotheses}
\end{equation}
Then there exists a random model
\[
  Z_\infty=(\Pi^{(\infty)},\Gamma^{(\infty)})
\]
on the same finite sector such that
\[
  d_{K;\gamma}(Z_n,Z_\infty)\longrightarrow0
  \quad\hbox{in }L^p(\Omega)\hbox{ and almost surely}.
\]
More precisely,
\begin{equation}
  \left(
    \mathbb E\,d_{K;\gamma}(Z_n,Z_\infty)^p
  \right)^{1/p}
  \leq
  \frac{C_D^{1/p}}{1-2^{-\rho}}\,2^{-\rho n}.
  \label{eq:spde-random-model-lp-tail}
\end{equation}
For every \(0<\rho'<\rho\), there is an almost surely finite random constant
\(C_{\rho'}(\omega)\) such that
\begin{equation}
  d_{K;\gamma}(Z_n(\omega),Z_\infty(\omega))
  \leq
  C_{\rho'}(\omega)2^{-\rho'n}
  \label{eq:spde-random-model-almost-sure-tail}
\end{equation}
for all \(n\).  The limiting model satisfies
\[
  \mathbb E
  \bigl(
    1+\|\Pi^{(\infty)}\|_{K;\gamma}
     +\|\Gamma^{(\infty)}\|_{K;\gamma}
  \bigr)^p
  \leq C_N .
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Put
\[
  D_n=d_{K;\gamma}(Z_{n+1},Z_n).
\]
Minkowski's inequality and
\eqref{eq:spde-random-model-cauchy-hypotheses} give, for \(m>n\),
\[
  \left(
    \mathbb E\,d_{K;\gamma}(Z_m,Z_n)^p
  \right)^{1/p}
  \leq
  \sum_{j=n}^{m-1}(\mathbb E D_j^p)^{1/p}
  \leq
  C_D^{1/p}\sum_{j=n}^{m-1}2^{-\rho j}.
\]
The geometric series is Cauchy, so \(Z_n\) is Cauchy in \(L^p\) for the
model distance.  Letting \(m\to\infty\) after constructing the limit below
will give \eqref{eq:spde-random-model-lp-tail}.

We now construct the pointwise limiting model on the event where
\(\sum_{n\geq1}D_n<\infty\).  This event has probability one, because
\[
  \mathbb E\sum_{n\geq1}D_n
  \leq
  C_D^{1/p}\sum_{n\geq1}2^{-\rho n}<\infty .
\]
Fix \(\omega\) in this event.  For each \(z\in K\),
\(\alpha<\gamma\), and \(\tau\in T_\alpha\), the numbers
\[
  \left\langle
    \Pi_z^{(n)}\tau,\varphi_z^\delta
  \right\rangle
\]
are Cauchy uniformly over \(\varphi\in\mathcal B_r\) and
\(\delta\in(0,1]\) after multiplication by \(\delta^{-\alpha}\).  Since
\(r>-\min A_{<\gamma}\), the dyadic wavelet expansion used in the proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-compact-reconstruction} implies that this Cauchy
property determines a unique distribution, denoted
\(\Pi_z^{(\infty)}\tau\), on the interior of \(K\).  The same distance
bound gives Cauchy convergence of each finite-dimensional component
\((\Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}\tau)_\beta\); define its limit to be
\((\Gamma_{zz'}^{(\infty)}\tau)_\beta\).

The algebraic model identities pass to the limit.  For instance, at every
finite cutoff,
\[
  \Pi_{z'}^{(n)}\tau
  =
  \Pi_z^{(n)}\Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}\tau .
\]
Pair this identity with an arbitrary \(C^r\) test function supported in the
interior of \(K\), expand the right-hand side into the finitely many
homogeneous components of \(\Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}\tau\), and take
\(n\to\infty\).  The distributional convergence of the \(\Pi\)-components
and the finite-dimensional convergence of the \(\Gamma\)-components give
\[
  \Pi_{z'}^{(\infty)}\tau
  =
  \Pi_z^{(\infty)}\Gamma_{zz'}^{(\infty)}\tau .
\]
The identities
\(\Gamma_{zz}^{(n)}=\mathbf 1\) and
\(\Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}\Gamma_{z'z''}^{(n)}=\Gamma_{zz''}^{(n)}\) are
polynomial identities among finitely many matrix entries on
\(\bigoplus_{\alpha<\gamma}T_\alpha\), so their limits satisfy the same
identities.

The analytic bounds also pass to the limit.  For every fixed normalized test
configuration and every fixed reexpansion component, the limiting value is
the limit of the finite-cutoff values.  Taking the supremum gives
\[
  1+\|\Pi^{(\infty)}\|_{K;\gamma}
   +\|\Gamma^{(\infty)}\|_{K;\gamma}
  \leq
  \liminf_{n\to\infty} N_n .
\]
Fatou's lemma therefore gives the displayed \(L^p\) bound on the limiting
model.  The same telescoping estimate gives, on the probability-one event,
\[
  d_{K;\gamma}(Z_n,Z_\infty)
  \leq
  \sum_{j=n}^{\infty}D_j .
\]
Taking the \(L^p\) norm of this inequality proves
\eqref{eq:spde-random-model-lp-tail}.

It remains to prove the almost-sure rate.  Let \(0<\rho'<\rho\).  Markov's
inequality gives
\[
  \mathbb P(D_n>2^{-\rho'n})
  \leq
  C_D\,2^{-p(\rho-\rho')n}.
\]
The right-hand side is summable in \(n\).  Borel--Cantelli implies that
\(D_n\leq2^{-\rho'n}\) for all sufficiently large \(n\), almost surely.
The finitely many earlier terms are absorbed into \(C_{\rho'}(\omega)\), and
the tail bound
\[
  \sum_{j=n}^{\infty}2^{-\rho'j}
  =
  \frac{2^{-\rho'n}}{1-2^{-\rho'}}
\]
proves \eqref{eq:spde-random-model-almost-sure-tail}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-random-model-cauchy-criterion} reduces the convergence
of a renormalized random model to two explicit analytic estimates: a uniform
moment bound for the model seminorm and a dyadic Cauchy moment estimate for
successive cutoff scales.  In a BPHZ construction these estimates are proved
tree by tree, after the forest character has removed every divergent local
subtree and after the remaining kernels have been bounded in the parabolic
model norm.  The theorem above proves what those estimates imply; it does
not prove the estimates for the concrete dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) trees.

\begin{lemma}[Finite Wiener-chaos moment bound]
\label{lem:spde-finite-wiener-chaos-moment-bound}
Let \(H\) be a real Hilbert space and let
\(\xi:H\to L^2(\Omega)\) be an isonormal Gaussian process:
\[
  \mathbb E\xi(h)=0,\qquad
  \mathbb E\xi(h)\xi(k)=\langle h,k\rangle_H .
\]
For \(q\geq1\) and \(f\in H^{\odot q}\), let \(I_q(f)\) denote the
\(q\)-fold Wick multiple integral, normalized by
\[
  I_q(h^{\otimes q})
  =
  \|h\|_H^q
  H_q\!\left(\frac{\xi(h)}{\|h\|_H}\right)
  \quad(h\neq0),
\]
where \(H_q\) is the probabilists' Hermite polynomial and extension to
\(H^{\odot q}\) is by polarization and completion.  Set \(I_0(c)=c\).  Then
\[
  \mathbb E\,I_q(f)I_{q'}(g)
  =
  \delta_{q q'}q!\,\langle f,g\rangle_{H^{\otimes q}} .
  \label{eq:spde-wiener-chaos-isometry}
\]
For every integer \(m\geq1\) there is a finite constant \(C_{q,m}\),
depending only on \(q\) and \(m\), such that
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E|I_q(f)|^{2m}
  \leq
  C_{q,m}\|f\|_{H^{\otimes q}}^{2m}.
  \label{eq:spde-wiener-chaos-even-moment-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The exponential definition of Wick ordering is
\[
  :\!\exp \xi(h)\!:
  =
  \exp\left(\xi(h)-\frac12\|h\|_H^2\right).
\]
For \(s,t\in\mathbb R\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \mathbb E\bigl(:\!\exp t\xi(h)\!:\,:\!\exp s\xi(k)\!:\bigr)
  &=
  \exp\left(
    \frac12\|th+sk\|_H^2
    -\frac12t^2\|h\|_H^2
    -\frac12s^2\|k\|_H^2
  \right)  \\
  &=
  \exp(ts\langle h,k\rangle_H).
\end{aligned}
\]
Comparing the coefficient of \(t^q s^{q'}\) gives
\[
  \mathbb E\,I_q(h^{\otimes q})I_{q'}(k^{\otimes q'})
  =
  \delta_{q q'}q!\,\langle h,k\rangle_H^q .
\]
Polarization gives \eqref{eq:spde-wiener-chaos-isometry} for finite sums of
pure symmetric tensors, and completion gives the general case.

For the even moment, first take \(f\) to be a finite-rank symmetric tensor.
The needed pairing formula is derived from the same generating function.  For
vectors \(h_1,\ldots,h_N\in H\),
\[
  \mathbb E\prod_{\ell=1}^N :\!\exp t_\ell\xi(h_\ell)\!:
  =
  \exp\left(
    \sum_{1\leq i<j\leq N}
    t_it_j\langle h_i,h_j\rangle_H
  \right).
\]
Expanding the right-hand side and comparing coefficients gives pairings
only between distinct Wick blocks.  By multilinearity, the product of
\(2m\) finite-rank Wick monomials therefore obeys
\[
  \mathbb E\,I_q(f)^{2m}
  =
  \sum_{\pi\in\mathcal P_{q,2m}} C_\pi(f,\ldots,f).
\]
Here \(\mathcal P_{q,2m}\) is the finite set of pairings of the
\(\{1,\ldots,2m\}\times\{1,\ldots,q\}\) labels that pair no two labels in
the same block \(\{\ell\}\times\{1,\ldots,q\}\), and \(C_\pi\) is the
complete tensor contraction prescribed by \(\pi\).  Every \(C_\pi\) is a
composition of tensor permutations and Hilbert contractions.  Tensor
permutations are unitary and each Hilbert contraction has operator norm
\(\leq1\), hence
\[
  |C_\pi(f,\ldots,f)|
  \leq
  \|f\|_{H^{\otimes q}}^{2m}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \mathbb E|I_q(f)|^{2m}
  =
  \mathbb E I_q(f)^{2m}
  \leq
  |\mathcal P_{q,2m}|\,
  \|f\|_{H^{\otimes q}}^{2m}.
\]
Take \(C_{q,m}=|\mathcal P_{q,2m}|\) for finite-rank tensors.  Approximate
a general \(f\in H^{\odot q}\) by finite-rank tensors in \(H^{\otimes q}\).
The isometry \eqref{eq:spde-wiener-chaos-isometry} gives convergence in
\(L^2(\Omega)\), hence a subsequence converges almost surely.  Fatou's lemma
applied to that subsequence gives the same \(2m\)-moment bound for the
limit.  This proves \eqref{eq:spde-wiener-chaos-even-moment-bound}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Kernel bounds imply stochastic coordinate moments]
\label{cor:spde-chaos-kernel-coordinate-moments}
Let \(\mathcal A\) be an index set and let
\(\sigma:\mathcal A\to(0,\infty)\) be a deterministic scale weight.  Suppose
that random variables \(Y_n(a)\), \(a\in\mathcal A\), have finite chaos
expansions
\[
  Y_n(a)=\sum_{q=0}^Q I_q(f_{n,a,q}),
  \qquad f_{n,a,q}\in H^{\odot q},
\]
and that, for constants \(B_q,\widetilde B_q<\infty\),
\[
  \|f_{n,a,q}\|_{H^{\otimes q}}\leq B_q\sigma(a),
  \qquad
  \|f_{n+1,a,q}-f_{n,a,q}\|_{H^{\otimes q}}
  \leq
  \widetilde B_q2^{-\rho n}\sigma(a).
\]
Then, for every integer \(m\geq1\), there are finite constants
\(M_m,\widetilde M_m\), depending only on \(m\), \(Q\), and the displayed
kernel constants, such that
\[
  \mathbb E|Y_n(a)|^{2m}\leq M_m\sigma(a)^{2m},
  \qquad
  \mathbb E|Y_{n+1}(a)-Y_n(a)|^{2m}
  \leq
  \widetilde M_m2^{-2m\rho n}\sigma(a)^{2m}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Use the elementary convexity inequality
\[
  \left|\sum_{q=0}^Q x_q\right|^{2m}
  \leq
  (Q+1)^{2m-1}\sum_{q=0}^Q |x_q|^{2m}.
\]
Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-finite-wiener-chaos-moment-bound} to each chaos
component.  The deterministic \(q=0\) component is included with
\(C_{0,m}=1\).  This gives
\[
  M_m
  =
  (Q+1)^{2m-1}
  \sum_{q=0}^Q C_{q,m}B_q^{2m}
\]
and the same expression with \(B_q\) replaced by \(\widetilde B_q\) for
\(\widetilde M_m\).  The factor \(2^{-2m\rho n}\) comes from raising the
dyadic kernel-difference estimate to the \(2m\)-th power.
\end{proof}

Corollary~\ref{cor:spde-chaos-kernel-coordinate-moments} is the stochastic
part of a BPHZ model estimate.  A concrete tree calculation must still prove
the deterministic kernel bounds for every tested model coordinate and must
upgrade the resulting pointwise coordinate estimates to the full model
seminorm by spatial and scale regularity estimates.  Once those deterministic
steps are done, Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-random-model-cauchy-criterion} turns
the coordinate estimates into convergence of the random model.

\begin{proposition}[Dyadic parabolic convolution bound]
\label{prop:spde-dyadic-parabolic-convolution-bound}
Let \((\mathbb R^D,\mathfrak s)\) be a parabolically scaled space with
homogeneous dimension
\[
  Q=|\mathfrak s|.
\]
Assume the parabolic balls satisfy
\[
  |B_{\mathfrak s}(0,r)|\leq V r^Q,\qquad 0<r\leq1 .
\]
Let \(a,b>0\).  For \(i,j\geq0\), let \(K_i,L_j\) be measurable kernels
such that, for constants \(A,C_K,C_L\),
\[
  \operatorname{supp}K_i,\operatorname{supp}L_i
  \subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-i}),
\]
and
\[
  |K_i(x)|\leq C_K2^{(Q-a)i},
  \qquad
  |L_i(x)|\leq C_L2^{(Q-b)i}.
\]
Define the \(k\)-th output scale of the convolution by
\[
  M_k=\sum_{\min(i,j)=k}K_i\ast L_j .
\]
Then the series defining \(M_k\) converges absolutely in \(L^\infty\),
\[
  \operatorname{supp}M_k\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,2A2^{-k}),
\]
and
\begin{equation}
  \|M_k\|_{L^\infty}
  \leq
  C\,C_KC_L\,2^{(Q-a-b)k},
  \label{eq:spde-dyadic-convolution-linfty}
\end{equation}
where
\[
  C=V A^Q
  \left(
    \frac{1}{1-2^{-b}}
    +
    \frac{2^{-a}}{1-2^{-a}}
  \right).
\]
Consequently
\begin{equation}
  \|M_k\|_{L^1}
  \leq
  C'\,C_KC_L\,2^{-(a+b)k}
  \label{eq:spde-dyadic-convolution-lone}
\end{equation}
for a constant \(C'\) depending only on \(A,V,a,b,Q\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For fixed \(i,j\), the convolution is supported in
\[
  B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-i})+B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-j})
  \subset
  B_{\mathfrak s}(0,2A2^{-\min(i,j)}).
\]
The pointwise bound follows by estimating the volume of the smaller support:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  |(K_i\ast L_j)(x)|
  &\leq
  C_KC_L
  2^{(Q-a)i+(Q-b)j}
  \left|B_{\mathfrak s}\bigl(0,A2^{-\max(i,j)}\bigr)\right|        \\
  &\leq
  V A^Q C_KC_L
  2^{(Q-a)i+(Q-b)j-Q\max(i,j)} .
\end{aligned}
\]
Fix \(k\).  First take \(i=k\) and \(j=k+r\), \(r\geq0\).  The preceding
estimate gives
\[
  |(K_k\ast L_{k+r})(x)|
  \leq
  V A^Q C_KC_L
  2^{(Q-a-b)k}2^{-br}.
\]
Summing \(r\geq0\) gives the first geometric factor in \(C\).  Next take
\(j=k\) and \(i=k+r\), \(r\geq1\); the term \(r=0\) has already been counted.
Then
\[
  |(K_{k+r}\ast L_k)(x)|
  \leq
  V A^Q C_KC_L
  2^{(Q-a-b)k}2^{-ar}.
\]
Summing \(r\geq1\) gives the second geometric factor.  This proves absolute
convergence in \(L^\infty\) and
\eqref{eq:spde-dyadic-convolution-linfty}.

The support statement for \(M_k\) follows from the support statement for each
summand with \(\min(i,j)=k\).  Multiplying the
\(L^\infty\) estimate by
\[
  |B_{\mathfrak s}(0,2A2^{-k})|
  \leq
  V(2A)^Q2^{-Qk}
\]
gives \eqref{eq:spde-dyadic-convolution-lone}.
\end{proof}

The exponents in
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-dyadic-parabolic-convolution-bound} are the
deterministic scale arithmetic behind many model-coordinate estimates.  A
kernel of order \(a\) has scale-\(i\) size \(2^{(Q-a)i}\) and \(L^1\)-size
\(2^{-ai}\).  After convolution with an order-\(b\) kernel, the output scale
\(k=\min(i,j)\) has size \(2^{(Q-a-b)k}\), which is exactly the size of an
order-\((a+b)\) kernel.  In a BPHZ proof, Taylor subtraction at divergent
subgraphs supplies additional powers of the small scale; the proposition
above is the unsubtracted convolution estimate to which those extra powers
are attached.

\begin{lemma}[Parabolic Taylor-subtraction gain]
\label{lem:spde-parabolic-taylor-subtraction-gain}
Let \(K_i\) be a dyadic kernel on \((\mathbb R^D,\mathfrak s)\) such that
\[
  \operatorname{supp}K_i\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-i}),
  \qquad
  \|K_i\|_{L^1}\leq C_K2^{-ai},
\]
with \(a>0\).  Let \(r>0\), let \(F\) be a function on
\(B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-i})\), and let \(P\) be a polynomial such that
\begin{equation}
  |F(h)-P(h)|
  \leq
  H\|h\|_{\mathfrak s}^{r}
  \label{eq:spde-parabolic-taylor-remainder-hypothesis}
\end{equation}
for all \(h\) in that ball.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    \int K_i(h)\bigl(F(h)-P(h)\bigr)\,\dd h
  \right|
  \leq
  C_KH A^r2^{-(a+r)i}.
  \label{eq:spde-taylor-subtraction-gain}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
On the support of \(K_i\), the remainder hypothesis gives
\[
  |F(h)-P(h)|\leq H A^r2^{-ri}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \left|
    \int K_i(h)\bigl(F(h)-P(h)\bigr)\,\dd h
  \right|
  &\leq
  H A^r2^{-ri}\int |K_i(h)|\,\dd h        \\
  &\leq
  C_KH A^r2^{-(a+r)i}.
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{proof}

Lemma~\ref{lem:spde-parabolic-taylor-subtraction-gain} is the elementary
scale gain produced by replacing a local factor \(F\) by its Taylor
remainder after subtraction of \(P\).  The lemma deliberately separates this
gain from the harder BPHZ bookkeeping: one must still specify which
polynomial is subtracted for each divergent subgraph, prove that the forest
formula does not double count overlapping local parts, and combine the gain
with the dyadic convolution bound in
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-dyadic-parabolic-convolution-bound}.

\begin{proposition}[One-loop relative-scale gap for a local subtraction]
\label{prop:spde-one-loop-relative-scale-gap}
Let \((\mathbb R^D,\mathfrak s)\) be a parabolically scaled space with
homogeneous dimension \(Q=|\mathfrak s|\).  Let \(a,b,\theta>0\).  Suppose
that dyadic kernels \(K_i,L_j\), \(i,j\geq0\), obey
\[
  \operatorname{supp}K_i\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-i}),\qquad
  |K_i(h)|\leq C_K2^{(Q-a)i},\qquad
  \|K_i\|_{L^1}\leq C_K'2^{-ai},
\]
and
\[
  \operatorname{supp}L_j\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-j}),\qquad
  |L_j(h)|\leq C_L2^{(Q-b)j}.
\]
Assume also that, whenever \(j\leq i\) and
\(\|h\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq A2^{-i}\),
\begin{equation}
  |L_j(h)-L_j(0)|
  \leq
  C_L'2^{(Q-b)j}2^{-\theta(i-j)} .
  \label{eq:spde-one-loop-holder-scale-bound}
\end{equation}
Define
\[
  T_{i,j}^{\rm loc}
  =
  \int K_i(h)\bigl(L_j(h)-L_j(0)\bigr)\,\dd h
  \quad(j\leq i),
\]
and
\[
  T_{i,j}^{\rm off}
  =
  \int K_i(h)L_j(h)\,\dd h
  \quad(j>i).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  |T_{i,j}^{\rm loc}|
  \leq
  C_K'C_L'\,
  2^{(Q-a-b)j}2^{-(a+\theta)(i-j)}
  \quad(j\leq i),
  \label{eq:spde-one-loop-local-sector-gap}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  |T_{i,j}^{\rm off}|
  \leq
  C_KC_L V_A\,
  2^{(Q-a-b)i}2^{-b(j-i)}
  \quad(j>i),
  \label{eq:spde-one-loop-off-sector-gap}
\end{equation}
where
\[
  V_A=\sup_{0<\rho\leq A}|B_{\mathfrak s}(0,\rho)|\,\rho^{-Q}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume first that \(j\leq i\).  On the support of \(K_i\), the Holder-scale
bound \eqref{eq:spde-one-loop-holder-scale-bound} applies.  Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
  |T_{i,j}^{\rm loc}|
  &\leq
  \|K_i\|_{L^1}C_L'2^{(Q-b)j}2^{-\theta(i-j)}        \\
  &\leq
  C_K'C_L'
  2^{-ai}2^{(Q-b)j}2^{-\theta(i-j)}                  \\
  &=
  C_K'C_L'2^{(Q-a-b)j}2^{-(a+\theta)(i-j)} .
\end{aligned}
\]
This is \eqref{eq:spde-one-loop-local-sector-gap}.

Assume next that \(j>i\).  The integrand is supported where \(L_j\) is
supported.  Hence
\[
\begin{aligned}
  |T_{i,j}^{\rm off}|
  &\leq
  C_KC_L2^{(Q-a)i+(Q-b)j}
  |B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-j})|                       \\
  &\leq
  C_KC_LV_A
  2^{(Q-a)i+(Q-b)j-Qj}                               \\
  &=
  C_KC_LV_A2^{(Q-a-b)i}2^{-b(j-i)} .
\end{aligned}
\]
This proves \eqref{eq:spde-one-loop-off-sector-gap}.
\end{proof}

For the one-loop dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) contraction, \(Q=5\) and the heat and
covariance kernels both have order \(2\), so \(a=b=2\).  The base factor
\(2^{(Q-a-b)k}=2^k\), where \(k=\min(i,j)\), is the local mass-coordinate
divergence.  Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-one-loop-relative-scale-gap} proves
that the two relative-scale sectors have strictly positive gap exponents:
\(2+\theta\) in the subtracted sector \(j\leq i\) and \(2\) in the
off-diagonal sector \(j>i\).  Thus the relative scales are summable once the
local coordinate has been separated.

\begin{proposition}[Two-loop sunset gaps and nested forest cancellation]
\label{prop:spde-two-loop-sunset-scale-gaps}
Let \((\mathbb R^D,\mathfrak s)\) be a parabolically scaled space with
homogeneous dimension \(Q=|\mathfrak s|\), and assume
\[
  |B_{\mathfrak s}(0,r)|\leq V r^Q,\qquad 0<r\leq A .
\]
Let \(a,b,c\in(0,Q)\) satisfy
\[
  a+b+c=2Q .
\]
For \(i,j,\ell\geq0\), let \(K_i,L_j,M_\ell\) be measurable kernels such
that
\[
  \operatorname{supp}K_i\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-i}),\quad
  \operatorname{supp}L_j\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-j}),\quad
  \operatorname{supp}M_\ell\subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-\ell}),
\]
and
\[
  |K_i(h)|\leq C_K2^{(Q-a)i},\quad
  |L_j(h)|\leq C_L2^{(Q-b)j},\quad
  |M_\ell(h)|\leq C_M2^{(Q-c)\ell}.
\]
Define
\[
  S_{i,j,\ell}=\int K_i(h)L_j(h)M_\ell(h)\,\dd h,
  \qquad
  n_*=\max(i,j,\ell),
\]
and the non-negative relative gaps
\[
  r_K=n_*-i,\qquad r_L=n_*-j,\qquad r_M=n_*-\ell .
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  |S_{i,j,\ell}|
  \leq
  V A^Q C_KC_LC_M
  2^{-(Q-a)r_K-(Q-b)r_L-(Q-c)r_M}.
  \label{eq:spde-two-loop-sunset-gap-bound}
\end{equation}
Consequently, if
\[
  S_N=\sum_{\max(i,j,\ell)\leq N}S_{i,j,\ell},
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  |S_{N+1}-S_N|
  \leq
  V A^Q C_KC_LC_M
  \sum_{\nu\in\{K,L,M\}}
  \prod_{\mu\neq\nu}\frac{1}{1-2^{-\eta_\mu}},
  \label{eq:spde-two-loop-sunset-shell-bound}
\end{equation}
where
\[
  \eta_K=Q-a,\qquad \eta_L=Q-b,\qquad \eta_M=Q-c .
\]

For dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\), \(Q=5\).  The heat kernel in the retarded edge has
order \(a=2\).  Each stationary covariance edge has order \(4\): it is the
time convolution of two order-\(2\) heat edges, so
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-dyadic-parabolic-convolution-bound} gives the
order \(2+2=4\) dyadic bound.  Thus \(b=c=4\) and
\[
  \eta_K=3,\qquad \eta_L=\eta_M=1,
\]
and the non-nested two-loop local sunset sectors obey
\begin{equation}
  |S_{i,j,\ell}|
  \leq
  C\,2^{-3r_K-r_L-r_M},
  \qquad
  |S_{N+1}-S_N|\leq \frac{60}{7}C ,
  \label{eq:spde-phi-four-three-sunset-sector-bound}
\end{equation}
with \(C=V A^5C_KC_LC_M\).

In the same finite cutoff chart, write
\[
  G=\sum_{\ell\geq0}G_\ell,\qquad C_{1,\ell}=G_\ell(0).
\]
At fixed scales \(i,j,\ell\), the nested local coefficient in
\[
  X(z)^2\int K_i(z-w)X(w)^3\,\dd w
\]
is
\[
  6C_{1,\ell}\int K_i(h)G_j(h)\,\dd h .
\]
After the recursive one-loop forest subtraction
\[
  X(w)^3\longmapsto X(w)^3-3C_{1,\ell}X(w),
\]
the corresponding local coefficient from the subtracted term is
\[
  -6C_{1,\ell}\int K_i(h)G_j(h)\,\dd h .
\]
Therefore the renormalized nested two-loop local coefficient is zero for
each triple \((i,j,\ell)\).  The only new two-loop local coordinate left by
the forest operation is the non-nested sunset coefficient bounded above.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The product \(K_iL_jM_\ell\) is supported in the smallest of the three
supports.  Hence
\[
  \operatorname{supp}(K_iL_jM_\ell)
  \subset B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-n_*}) .
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
  |S_{i,j,\ell}|
  &\leq
  C_KC_LC_M
  2^{(Q-a)i+(Q-b)j+(Q-c)\ell}
  |B_{\mathfrak s}(0,A2^{-n_*})|                  \\
  &\leq
  V A^Q C_KC_LC_M
  2^{(Q-a)i+(Q-b)j+(Q-c)\ell-Qn_*}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Substitute
\[
  i=n_*-r_K,\qquad j=n_*-r_L,\qquad \ell=n_*-r_M .
\]
The coefficient of \(n_*\) is
\[
  (Q-a)+(Q-b)+(Q-c)-Q=2Q-(a+b+c)=0,
\]
so the remaining factor is exactly the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:spde-two-loop-sunset-gap-bound}.

The increment \(S_{N+1}-S_N\) is the sum over triples with
\(\max(i,j,\ell)=N+1\).  Equivalently, at least one of the gaps
\(r_K,r_L,r_M\) is zero.  This shell is contained in the union of the three
sets \(r_\nu=0\).  Summing the geometric majorant from
\eqref{eq:spde-two-loop-sunset-gap-bound} over that union gives
\eqref{eq:spde-two-loop-sunset-shell-bound}.  For
\((Q,a,b,c)=(5,2,4,4)\), this shell constant is
\[
  \frac{1}{(1-2^{-1})^2}
  +
  2\,\frac{1}{(1-2^{-3})(1-2^{-1})}
  =
  \frac{60}{7}.
\]

It remains to verify the nested cancellation.  In
\[
  X(z)^2X(w)^3
\]
leave one of the three fields at \(w\) uncontracted, contract the two fields
at \(z\) with the two remaining fields at \(w\), and then Taylor-expand the
uncontracted field at \(w\) to its local value at \(z\).  This is the
non-nested sunset and gives \(3\cdot2=6\) bridge pairings.  For the nested
coefficient, instead leave one of the two fields at \(z\) uncontracted,
contract the other field at \(z\) with one of the three fields at \(w\), and
contract the two remaining fields at \(w\) with each other.  This gives
\[
  2\cdot3=6
\]
pairings and the coefficient
\[
  6C_{1,\ell}\int K_i(h)G_j(h)\,\dd h .
\]
The inner one-loop forest subtraction contributes
\[
  -3C_{1,\ell}X(w).
\]
Multiplying by \(X(z)^2\), leaving one of the two fields at \(z\)
uncontracted, and contracting the other one with \(X(w)\) gives
\[
  -3\cdot2\,C_{1,\ell}\int K_i(h)G_j(h)\,\dd h .
\]
This is the negative of the nested coefficient above.  The cancellation is
scale-wise because \(C_{1,\ell}=G_\ell(0)\) was kept at fixed tadpole scale
\(\ell\) throughout the calculation.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Multiscale sector summability for coordinate kernels]
\label{thm:spde-multiscale-sector-kernel-summability}
Let \(R\geq1\) and let \(\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_R\) be positive real numbers.
Here \(\mathbb N=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}\).
Set
\[
  \eta_*=\min_{1\leq h\leq R}\eta_h,\qquad
  G=\prod_{h=1}^R\frac{1}{1-2^{-\eta_h}},
\]
and
\[
  \widetilde G
  =
  \sum_{h=1}^R
  \prod_{\substack{1\leq k\leq R\\ k\neq h}}
  \frac{1}{1-2^{-\eta_k}} .
\]
Let \(\mathcal H\) be a real Hilbert space.  Let \(\mathcal A\) be an index
set with a positive scale weight
\[
  \sigma:\mathcal A\to(0,\infty).
\]
For \(q=0,\ldots,Q\), \(a\in\mathcal A\), and
\(\mathbf r=(r_1,\ldots,r_R)\in\mathbb N^R\), let
\[
  f_{\mathbf r,a,q}\in \mathcal H^{\odot q}
\]
with \(\mathcal H^{\odot0}=\mathbb R\).  Suppose that, for constants
\(B_q<\infty\),
\begin{equation}
  \|f_{\mathbf r,a,q}\|_{\mathcal H^{\otimes q}}
  \leq
  B_q\sigma(a)\prod_{h=1}^R2^{-\eta_h r_h}
  \label{eq:spde-sector-kernel-gap-bound}
\end{equation}
for all displayed indices.  Define the cutoff kernel
\[
  f_{n,a,q}
  =
  \sum_{\substack{\mathbf r\in\mathbb N^R\\
                  \max_h r_h\leq n}}
  f_{\mathbf r,a,q}.
\]
Then the sum defining \(f_{n,a,q}\) is absolutely convergent in
\(\mathcal H^{\otimes q}\), and
\begin{equation}
  \|f_{n,a,q}\|_{\mathcal H^{\otimes q}}
  \leq
  B_qG\sigma(a).
  \label{eq:spde-sector-uniform-kernel-bound}
\end{equation}
Moreover,
\begin{equation}
  \|f_{n+1,a,q}-f_{n,a,q}\|_{\mathcal H^{\otimes q}}
  \leq
  B_q\widetilde G\,2^{-\eta_*(n+1)}\sigma(a)
  \leq
  B_q\widetilde G\,2^{-\eta_*n}\sigma(a).
  \label{eq:spde-sector-kernel-increment-bound}
\end{equation}
Consequently, for any isonormal Gaussian process
\(\xi:\mathcal H\to L^2(\Omega)\), the random variables
\[
  Y_n(a)=\sum_{q=0}^Q I_q(f_{n,a,q})
\]
satisfy the hypotheses of
Corollary~\ref{cor:spde-chaos-kernel-coordinate-moments} with the
replacement
\[
  B_q\mapsto B_qG,\qquad
  \widetilde B_q\mapsto B_q\widetilde G,\qquad
  \rho=\eta_* .
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The triangle inequality and
\eqref{eq:spde-sector-kernel-gap-bound} give
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \|f_{n,a,q}\|_{\mathcal H^{\otimes q}}
  &\leq
  B_q\sigma(a)
  \sum_{\substack{\mathbf r\in\mathbb N^R\\ \max_h r_h\leq n}}
  \prod_{h=1}^R2^{-\eta_h r_h}        \\
  &\leq
  B_q\sigma(a)
  \prod_{h=1}^R
  \sum_{r_h=0}^{\infty}2^{-\eta_h r_h}
  =
  B_qG\sigma(a).
\end{aligned}
\]
This proves absolute convergence and
\eqref{eq:spde-sector-uniform-kernel-bound}.

The increment \(f_{n+1,a,q}-f_{n,a,q}\) is the sum of those scale-gap
assignments for which at least one \(r_h\) is equal to \(n+1\) and no
\(r_h\) is larger than \(n+1\).  This set is contained in the union, over
\(h=1,\ldots,R\), of the sets with \(r_h=n+1\).  Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \|f_{n+1,a,q}-f_{n,a,q}\|_{\mathcal H^{\otimes q}}
  &\leq
  B_q\sigma(a)
  \sum_{h=1}^R
  2^{-\eta_h(n+1)}
  \prod_{\substack{1\leq k\leq R\\k\neq h}}
  \sum_{r_k=0}^{\infty}2^{-\eta_k r_k}        \\
  &\leq
  B_q\sigma(a)\,2^{-\eta_*(n+1)}
  \sum_{h=1}^R
  \prod_{\substack{1\leq k\leq R\\k\neq h}}
  \frac{1}{1-2^{-\eta_k}} .
\end{aligned}
\]
This is \eqref{eq:spde-sector-kernel-increment-bound}.  The final assertion
is obtained by comparing
\eqref{eq:spde-sector-uniform-kernel-bound} and
\eqref{eq:spde-sector-kernel-increment-bound} with the deterministic kernel
hypotheses of
Corollary~\ref{cor:spde-chaos-kernel-coordinate-moments}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-multiscale-sector-kernel-summability} is the
multiscale summation step behind a BPHZ coordinate estimate.  In an actual
tree calculation, a sector decomposition rewrites relative scale assignments
as non-negative gap variables \(r_h\).  Taylor subtraction and the forest
formula must then prove positive deficits \(\eta_h\) for every gap variable.
Once that has been done, the theorem above converts those deficits into
uniform kernel bounds and dyadic cutoff-increment bounds for the finite
chaos coordinates.  The difficult part left for dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) is
therefore the derivation of
\eqref{eq:spde-sector-kernel-gap-bound} for each negative tree and each
coordinate entering the model seminorm.

\begin{theorem}[Dyadic-net upgrade from coordinates to a supremum]
\label{thm:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-upgrade}
Let \((\mathcal X,d_{\mathcal X})\) be a compact metric space.  Assume that
there are finite sets \(\mathcal X_\ell\subset\mathcal X\),
\(\ell\geq0\), and maps
\[
  \pi_\ell:\mathcal X\to\mathcal X_\ell
\]
such that \(\pi_\ell x\to x\) for every \(x\in\mathcal X\), and such that
the edge sets
\[
  \mathcal E_\ell
  =
  \{(\pi_\ell x,\pi_{\ell+1}x):x\in\mathcal X\}
\]
satisfy
\[
  |\mathcal X_0|\leq C_0,\qquad
  |\mathcal E_\ell|\leq C_1 2^{d\ell}
  \quad(\ell\geq0)
\]
for constants \(C_0,C_1,d>0\).  Let \(Y:\mathcal X\to L^p(\Omega)\) be a
random field with almost surely continuous sample paths, where \(p\geq1\).
Assume that there are constants \(B_0,B,\varepsilon>0\) such that
\[
  \mathbb E|Y(u)|^p\leq B_0^p
  \quad(u\in\mathcal X_0),
\]
and
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb E|Y(v)-Y(u)|^p
  \leq
  B^p2^{-(d+\varepsilon)\ell}
  \quad((u,v)\in\mathcal E_\ell).
  \label{eq:spde-dyadic-net-increment-bound}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
  \left\|
    \sup_{x\in\mathcal X}|Y(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}
  \leq
  C_0^{1/p}B_0
  +
  \frac{C_1^{1/p}B}{1-2^{-\varepsilon/p}} .
  \label{eq:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-bound}
\end{equation}
If \(Y=Y_n\) depends on a cutoff index \(n\) and both \(B_0\) and \(B\) in
the two hypotheses are replaced by \(2^{-\rho n}B_0\) and
\(2^{-\rho n}B\), then the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-bound} is multiplied by \(2^{-\rho n}\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For every \(x\in\mathcal X\), continuity of the sample path and
\(\pi_\ell x\to x\) give
\[
  Y(x)=Y(\pi_0x)+
  \sum_{\ell\geq0}\bigl(Y(\pi_{\ell+1}x)-Y(\pi_\ell x)\bigr)
\]
on the event of continuity.  Taking the supremum over \(x\) gives the
pointwise bound
\[
  \sup_{x\in\mathcal X}|Y(x)|
  \leq
  \max_{u\in\mathcal X_0}|Y(u)|
  +
  \sum_{\ell\geq0}
  \max_{(u,v)\in\mathcal E_\ell}|Y(v)-Y(u)|.
\]
The \(L^p\) triangle inequality gives
\[
  \left\|
    \sup_{x\in\mathcal X}|Y(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p}
  \leq
  \left\|\max_{u\in\mathcal X_0}|Y(u)|\right\|_{L^p}
  +
  \sum_{\ell\geq0}
  \left\|\max_{(u,v)\in\mathcal E_\ell}|Y(v)-Y(u)|\right\|_{L^p}.
\]
For the base net,
\[
  \left\|\max_{u\in\mathcal X_0}|Y(u)|\right\|_{L^p}^p
  \leq
  \sum_{u\in\mathcal X_0}\mathbb E|Y(u)|^p
  \leq
  C_0B_0^p .
\]
For each edge level, \eqref{eq:spde-dyadic-net-increment-bound} gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \left\|\max_{(u,v)\in\mathcal E_\ell}|Y(v)-Y(u)|\right\|_{L^p}^p
  &\leq
  \sum_{(u,v)\in\mathcal E_\ell}\mathbb E|Y(v)-Y(u)|^p        \\
  &\leq
  C_1B^p2^{-\varepsilon\ell}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Taking \(p\)-th roots and summing the geometric series gives
\eqref{eq:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-bound}.  The cutoff-dependent statement
is the same calculation after multiplying the base and edge hypotheses by
\(2^{-\rho n p}\).
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-upgrade} is the analytic bridge
from pointwise model-coordinate estimates to compact model-seminorm
estimates.  In an application, \(\mathcal X\) is a finite product of compact
base-point, scale, test-function, and normalized-symbol parameter spaces
after a finite regularity sector has been fixed.  The integer \(d\) records
the metric entropy of these parameters.  The missing BPHZ estimates must
therefore include not only a moment bound for each fixed coordinate, but also
the increment bound \eqref{eq:spde-dyadic-net-increment-bound} in the
parameters that enter the model seminorm.

\begin{theorem}[Scale-summed dyadic-net coordinate upgrade]
\label{thm:spde-scale-summed-coordinate-upgrade}
Let \(p\geq1\).  For every \(m\in\mathbb N=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}\), let
\((\mathcal X_m,d_m)\) be a compact metric space with finite nets
\(\mathcal X_{m,\ell}\), \(\ell\geq0\), projections
\[
  \pi_{m,\ell}:\mathcal X_m\to\mathcal X_{m,\ell},
\]
and edge sets
\[
  \mathcal E_{m,\ell}
  =
  \{(\pi_{m,\ell}x,\pi_{m,\ell+1}x):x\in\mathcal X_m\}.
\]
Assume
\[
  |\mathcal X_{m,0}|\leq C_0 2^{D m},
  \qquad
  |\mathcal E_{m,\ell}|\leq C_1 2^{D m}2^{d\ell}
  \quad(m,\ell\geq0),
  \label{eq:spde-scale-net-entropy}
\]
for constants \(C_0,C_1,D,d\geq0\).  Let \(Y_m:\mathcal X_m\to
L^p(\Omega)\) have almost surely continuous sample paths.  Suppose there are
constants \(B_0,B,\sigma,\varepsilon>0\) such that
\[
  \mathbb E|Y_m(u)|^p\leq B_0^p2^{-p\sigma m}
  \quad(u\in\mathcal X_{m,0})
\]
and
\[
  \mathbb E|Y_m(v)-Y_m(u)|^p
  \leq
  B^p2^{-p\sigma m}2^{-(d+\varepsilon)\ell}
  \quad((u,v)\in\mathcal E_{m,\ell}).
\]
If
\[
  \sigma>\frac{D}{p},
  \label{eq:spde-scale-slack-condition}
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  \left\|
    \sup_{m\geq0}\sup_{x\in\mathcal X_m}|Y_m(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}
  \leq
  \frac{
    C_0^{1/p}B_0+
    C_1^{1/p}B(1-2^{-\varepsilon/p})^{-1}
  }
  {1-2^{-(\sigma-D/p)}} .
  \label{eq:spde-scale-summed-supremum-bound}
\end{equation}

If \(Y_m=Y_{n,m}\) depends on a cutoff index \(n\) and the two moment
hypotheses are multiplied by \(2^{-p\rho n}\), with \(\rho>0\), then the
right-hand side of \eqref{eq:spde-scale-summed-supremum-bound} is multiplied
by \(2^{-\rho n}\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(m\).  Apply
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-upgrade} to the compact space
\(\mathcal X_m\).  The base entropy and edge entropy in
\eqref{eq:spde-scale-net-entropy} replace \(C_0\) and \(C_1\) by
\(C_0 2^{Dm}\) and \(C_1 2^{Dm}\), while the coordinate moment constants are
multiplied by \(2^{-\sigma m}\).  Hence
\[
  \left\|
    \sup_{x\in\mathcal X_m}|Y_m(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p}
  \leq
  \left(
    C_0^{1/p}B_0+
    \frac{C_1^{1/p}B}{1-2^{-\varepsilon/p}}
  \right)
  2^{-(\sigma-D/p)m}.
\]
The \(L^p\) triangle inequality gives
\[
  \left\|
    \sup_{m\geq0}\sup_{x\in\mathcal X_m}|Y_m(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p}
  \leq
  \sum_{m\geq0}
  \left\|
    \sup_{x\in\mathcal X_m}|Y_m(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p}.
\]
The geometric series in \(m\) converges exactly under
\eqref{eq:spde-scale-slack-condition} and gives
\eqref{eq:spde-scale-summed-supremum-bound}.  The cutoff-dependent statement
follows by carrying the factor \(2^{-\rho n}\) through the same inequalities.
\end{proof}

The parameter \(m\) in
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-scale-summed-coordinate-upgrade} is the physical
testing scale, \(\delta\simeq2^{-m}\), in a model seminorm.  The exponent
\(D\) is the entropy cost of resolving base points and test functions at
that scale.  The exponent \(\sigma\) is the regularity slack between the
assigned homogeneity and the moment regularity proved for the stochastic
coordinate.  Thus the condition \(\sigma>D/p\) is the precise high-moment
requirement: by choosing \(p\) large enough, a positive regularity slack
beats the scale entropy.

\begin{theorem}[Coordinate estimates imply finite-sector model convergence]
\label{thm:spde-coordinate-estimates-model-convergence}
Fix \(K\), \(\gamma\), \(A_{<\gamma}\), and \(r\) as in
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-random-model-cauchy-criterion}.  Let
\[
  Z_n=(\Pi^{(n)},\Gamma^{(n)}),\qquad n\geq1,
\]
be random models on the finite sector, and define
\[
  N_n=
  1+\|\Pi^{(n)}\|_{K;\gamma}+\|\Gamma^{(n)}\|_{K;\gamma}.
\]
Let \(d_{K;\gamma}\) be the corresponding model distance.  Let
\[
  I=\{1,\ldots,M\}
\]
be a finite coordinate index set.  For every \(i\in I\), let
\((\mathcal X_i,d_{\mathcal X_i})\) be a compact metric space with finite nets
\(\mathcal X_{i,\ell}\), projections
\[
  \pi_{i,\ell}:\mathcal X_i\to\mathcal X_{i,\ell},
\]
with \(\pi_{i,\ell}x\to x\) for every \(x\in\mathcal X_i\), and edge sets
\[
  \mathcal E_{i,\ell}
  =
  \{(\pi_{i,\ell}x,\pi_{i,\ell+1}x):x\in\mathcal X_i\}
\]
such that
\[
  |\mathcal X_{i,0}|\leq C_{i,0},\qquad
  |\mathcal E_{i,\ell}|\leq C_{i,1}2^{D_i\ell}
\]
for constants \(C_{i,0},C_{i,1},D_i>0\).
Fix \(p\geq1\), \(\rho>0\), and \(\varepsilon_i>0\) for \(i\in I\).
Suppose there are real random fields
\[
  U_{n,i},V_{n,i}:\mathcal X_i\to L^p(\Omega),
  \qquad n\geq1,\quad i\in I,
\]
with almost surely continuous sample paths, and a deterministic constant
\(A_{\rm ctrl}<\infty\), such that, almost surely,
\begin{equation}
  N_n
  \leq
  A_{\rm ctrl}
  \left(1+\sum_{i\in I}\sup_{x\in\mathcal X_i}|U_{n,i}(x)|\right),
  \label{eq:spde-coordinate-controls-model-norm}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  d_{K;\gamma}(Z_{n+1},Z_n)
  \leq
  A_{\rm ctrl}
  \sum_{i\in I}\sup_{x\in\mathcal X_i}|V_{n,i}(x)|.
  \label{eq:spde-coordinate-controls-model-distance}
\end{equation}
For the uniform bound, assume
\[
  \mathbb E|U_{n,i}(u)|^p\leq B_{i,0}^p
  \quad(n\geq1,\ u\in\mathcal X_{i,0}),
\]
and
\[
  \mathbb E|U_{n,i}(v)-U_{n,i}(u)|^p
  \leq
  B_i^p2^{-(D_i+\varepsilon_i)\ell}
  \quad(n\geq1,\ (u,v)\in\mathcal E_{i,\ell}).
\]
For the cutoff increment, assume
\[
  \mathbb E|V_{n,i}(u)|^p
  \leq
  \widetilde B_{i,0}^{\,p}2^{-p\rho n}
  \quad(u\in\mathcal X_{i,0}),
\]
and
\[
  \mathbb E|V_{n,i}(v)-V_{n,i}(u)|^p
  \leq
  \widetilde B_i^{\,p}
  2^{-p\rho n}2^{-(D_i+\varepsilon_i)\ell}
  \quad((u,v)\in\mathcal E_{i,\ell}).
\]
Define
\[
  S_i=
  C_{i,0}^{1/p}B_{i,0}
  +
  \frac{C_{i,1}^{1/p}B_i}{1-2^{-\varepsilon_i/p}},
  \qquad
  \widetilde S_i=
  C_{i,0}^{1/p}\widetilde B_{i,0}
  +
  \frac{C_{i,1}^{1/p}\widetilde B_i}{1-2^{-\varepsilon_i/p}} .
\]
Then
\[
  \sup_{n\geq1}
  \|N_n\|_{L^p(\Omega)}
  \leq
  A_{\rm ctrl}\left(1+\sum_{i\in I}S_i\right),
\]
and
\begin{equation}
  \|d_{K;\gamma}(Z_{n+1},Z_n)\|_{L^p(\Omega)}
  \leq
  A_{\rm ctrl}
  \left(\sum_{i\in I}\widetilde S_i\right)2^{-\rho n}.
  \label{eq:spde-coordinate-model-distance-bound}
\end{equation}
Consequently, \(Z_n\) satisfies
\eqref{eq:spde-random-model-cauchy-hypotheses} with
\[
  C_N=
  A_{\rm ctrl}^p\left(1+\sum_{i\in I}S_i\right)^p,
  \qquad
  C_D=
  A_{\rm ctrl}^p\left(\sum_{i\in I}\widetilde S_i\right)^p .
\]
Thus the limiting random model and the convergence estimates of
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-random-model-cauchy-criterion} follow.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-upgrade} to each
\(U_{n,i}\).  The hypotheses for \(U_{n,i}\) give
\[
  \left\|
    \sup_{x\in\mathcal X_i}|U_{n,i}(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p}
  \leq S_i
  \quad(n\geq1).
\]
The \(L^p\) triangle inequality and
\eqref{eq:spde-coordinate-controls-model-norm} therefore give
\[
  \|N_n\|_{L^p}
  \leq
  A_{\rm ctrl}
  \left(1+\sum_{i\in I}S_i\right).
\]
Taking the supremum over \(n\) gives the uniform model-seminorm moment
bound.

Apply the cutoff-dependent part of
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-dyadic-net-supremum-upgrade} to \(V_{n,i}\).  The
base and edge bounds for \(V_{n,i}\) give
\[
  \left\|
    \sup_{x\in\mathcal X_i}|V_{n,i}(x)|
  \right\|_{L^p}
  \leq
  \widetilde S_i2^{-\rho n}.
\]
Together with \eqref{eq:spde-coordinate-controls-model-distance}, this gives
\eqref{eq:spde-coordinate-model-distance-bound}.  Raising the two displayed
\(L^p\) bounds to the \(p\)-th power gives the constants \(C_N\) and \(C_D\)
in \eqref{eq:spde-random-model-cauchy-hypotheses}.  The final assertion is
exactly Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-random-model-cauchy-criterion}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-coordinate-estimates-model-convergence} fixes the
logical boundary of the remaining BPHZ task.  A finite-dimensional
reexpansion component or a finite basis component of \(T_\alpha\) is included
by enlarging the finite index set \(I\).  The model construction for
dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) must therefore provide, for each negative tree and each
coordinate chart entering the model seminorm, the two estimates appearing in
the theorem: the uniform coordinate moment estimate and the dyadic cutoff
increment estimate with enough excess exponent \(\varepsilon_i\) to dominate
the metric entropy of the coordinate parameters.

For dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\), the symbols are generated by the noise \(\Xi\), the
abstract integration map \(\mathcal I\), multiplication, and polynomials in
the spacetime coordinates.  Negative-homogeneity trees encode precisely the
subdivergent stochastic diagrams.  Renormalization acts on these trees before
the reconstruction theorem is applied.

\begin{proposition}[Finite negative-sector coordinates for dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\)]
\label{prop:spde-phi-four-three-negative-sector-coordinate-chart}
Let \(0<\kappa<1/14\), and set
\[
  X=\mathcal I(\Xi),\qquad
  Y=\mathcal I(X^3),\qquad
  |X|=-\frac12-\kappa,\qquad |Y|=\frac12-3\kappa .
\]
Put
\[
  \gamma_-=\frac12-7\kappa>0 .
\]
Among the monomials \(X^aY^b\) with \(a+b\leq3\), the non-polynomial
symbols of homogeneity \(<\gamma_-\) are
\[
  X,\quad X^2,\quad X^3,\quad XY,\quad X^2Y .
  \label{eq:spde-negative-sector-monomial-list}
\]
Together with the noise symbol \(\Xi\) and the constant symbol \(\mathbf 1\),
these form the finite sector
\[
  \mathcal T_-=
  \{\mathbf 1,\Xi,X,X^2,X^3,XY,X^2Y\}.
\]
Their homogeneities are
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
  \tau & |\tau|\\
  \hline
  \mathbf 1 & 0\\
  \Xi & -\frac52-\kappa\\
  X & -\frac12-\kappa\\
  X^2 & -1-2\kappa\\
  X^3 & -\frac32-3\kappa\\
  XY & -4\kappa\\
  X^2Y & -\frac12-5\kappa .
\end{array}
\]
The excluded cubic monomial \(XY^2\) has homogeneity
\[
  |XY^2|=\gamma_-,
\]
and is therefore not part of the strict sector \(T_{<\gamma_-}\).

Let \(Z_n=(\Pi^{(n)},\Gamma^{(n)})\) be cutoff models whose restriction to
\(\mathcal T_-\) is multiplicative and whose reexpansion of \(Y\), modulo
terms of homogeneity \(\geq\gamma_-\), has the form
\[
  \Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}Y
  =
  Y+c_n(z,z')\mathbf 1 .
  \label{eq:spde-y-reexpansion-coefficient}
\]
Then, on \(\mathcal T_-\), the only strict lower stochastic reexpansion
coefficients are
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}(XY)
  =
  XY+c_n(z,z')X,
  \qquad
  \Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}(X^2Y)
  =
  X^2Y+c_n(z,z')X^2 .
  \label{eq:spde-negative-sector-gamma-coordinates}
\end{equation}
In particular, for a compact \(K\), the model seminorm below
\(\gamma_-\) is controlled by the following explicit coordinate fields:
\[
  P_{n,\tau}(z,\delta,\varphi)
  =
  \delta^{-|\tau|}
  \left|
    \langle\Pi_z^{(n)}\tau,\varphi_z^\delta\rangle
  \right|,
  \qquad
  \tau\in\{\Xi,X,X^2,X^3,XY,X^2Y\},
\]
and
\[
  G_n(z,z')
  =
  \frac{|c_n(z,z')|}
       {\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}^{\,|Y|}}
  \qquad
  (0<\|z-z'\|_{\mathfrak s}\leq1).
\]
More precisely, with the basis vectors above assigned norm one,
\begin{equation}
  1+\|\Pi^{(n)}\|_{K;\gamma_-}+\|\Gamma^{(n)}\|_{K;\gamma_-}
  \leq
  A_-
  \left(
    1+
    \sum_{\tau\in\mathcal T_-\setminus\{\mathbf 1\}}
    \sup_{z,\delta,\varphi} P_{n,\tau}(z,\delta,\varphi)
    +
    \sup_{z,z'}G_n(z,z')
  \right)
  \label{eq:spde-negative-sector-coordinate-domination}
\end{equation}
for a numerical constant \(A_-<\infty\) depending only on this finite basis.
The same inequality holds for the model distance
\(d_{K;\gamma_-}(Z_{n+1},Z_n)\) after replacing \(P_{n,\tau}\) and \(G_n\)
by the corresponding cutoff differences.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The homogeneity of \(X^aY^b\) is
\[
  a\left(-\frac12-\kappa\right)
  +
  b\left(\frac12-3\kappa\right).
\]
For \(a+b\leq3\), direct evaluation gives
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
  X & -\frac12-\kappa\\
  X^2 & -1-2\kappa\\
  X^3 & -\frac32-3\kappa\\
  Y & \frac12-3\kappa\\
  XY & -4\kappa\\
  X^2Y & -\frac12-5\kappa\\
  Y^2 & 1-6\kappa\\
  XY^2 & \frac12-7\kappa\\
  Y^3 & \frac32-9\kappa .
\end{array}
\]
Since \(0<\kappa<1/14\), the entries in
\eqref{eq:spde-negative-sector-monomial-list} are strictly below
\(\gamma_-\), while \(Y\), \(Y^2\), and \(Y^3\) are above
\(\gamma_-\), and \(XY^2\) is exactly at \(\gamma_-\).  The strict
truncation \(T_{<\gamma_-}\) therefore contains the displayed
non-polynomial monomials and not \(XY^2\).

The symbol \(Y\) has homogeneity \(|Y|=\frac12-3\kappa\), which lies in
\((0,1)\).  Below \(\gamma_-\), the only polynomial symbol with lower
homogeneity than \(Y\) that can appear in its reexpansion is the constant
symbol.  This is the coefficient \(c_n(z,z')\) in
\eqref{eq:spde-y-reexpansion-coefficient}.  The negative symbols
\(\Xi,X,X^2,X^3\) have no lower polynomial component in the sector
\(\mathcal T_-\).  Multiplicativity of the model then gives
\[
  \Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}(XY)
  =
  \Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}X\,\Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}Y
  =
  X(Y+c_n(z,z')\mathbf 1),
\]
and similarly
\[
  \Gamma_{zz'}^{(n)}(X^2Y)
  =
  X^2(Y+c_n(z,z')\mathbf 1).
\]
This proves \eqref{eq:spde-negative-sector-gamma-coordinates}.  The
homogeneity gaps are
\[
  |XY|-|X|=|Y|,\qquad
  |X^2Y|-|X^2|=|Y|.
\]
Thus the normalized \(\Gamma\)-seminorms of these two strict lower
components are both bounded by \(\sup G_n\); all other strict lower
components in this sector vanish.

For \(\Pi\), the seminorm in
Definition~\ref{def:spde-parabolic-modelled-distribution-norms} is the
supremum over unit vectors in a finite-dimensional graded space.  In the
chosen basis, the unit-vector supremum is bounded by a finite multiple of
the sum of the basis-vector suprema.  The constant symbol contributes the
deterministic bound
\[
  |\langle1,\varphi_z^\delta\rangle|
  \leq
  \|\varphi\|_{L^1}
  \leq C_r .
\]
Combining this deterministic constant, the six non-polynomial \(\Pi\)
coordinates, and the single \(c_n\)-coordinate proves
\eqref{eq:spde-negative-sector-coordinate-domination}.  Applying the same
finite-dimensional norm comparison to
\(\Pi^{(n+1)}-\Pi^{(n)}\) and to the coefficient
\(c_{n+1}-c_n\) gives the stated model-distance version.
\end{proof}

\section{BPHZ Renormalization of the Dynamic Equation}

For the normalization
\[
  \partial_\tau\Phi
  =
  \Delta\Phi-m^2\Phi-\lambda\Phi^3+\xi ,
\]
the parabolic dimension is \(|\mathfrak s|=5\).  A mollified white noise
\(\xi_\epsilon\) is assigned homogeneity
\[
  |\Xi|=-\frac52-\kappa ,
\]
where \(\kappa>0\) is chosen small.  The heat integration map raises
homogeneity by \(2\).  Thus the stochastic convolution symbol
\(X=\mathcal I(\Xi)\) has
\[
  |X|=-\frac12-\kappa ,
  \qquad
  |X^2|=-1-2\kappa ,
  \qquad
  |X^3|=-\frac32-3\kappa .
\]
The one-loop subtraction is already forced by \(X^3\).  The first nonlinear
response \(\mathcal I(X^3)\) has homogeneity
\[
  |\mathcal I(X^3)|=\frac12-3\kappa ,
\]
and the product \(X^2\mathcal I(X^3)\) has
\[
  |X^2\mathcal I(X^3)|=-\frac12-5\kappa .
\]
The next proposition records exactly which cubic drift monomials have
negative homogeneity in the first nonlinear expansion.  This finite
drift-level ledger determines the one-loop and two-loop local mass
coordinates in the equation displayed below; the full
regularity-structure construction then adds the model, reexpansion, and
analytic-coordinate estimates for these symbols.

\begin{proposition}[Drift-level negative homogeneity ledger]
\label{prop:spde-phi-four-three-negative-drift-ledger}
Let \(0<\kappa<1/14\).  Put
\[
  \alpha=-\frac12-\kappa,\qquad
  \beta=\frac12-3\kappa .
\]
Here \(\alpha\) is the homogeneity of \(X=\mathcal I(\Xi)\) and \(\beta\) is
the homogeneity of the first nonlinear response
\(Y=\mathcal I(X^3)\).  Among the four cubic monomials
\[
  X^3,\qquad X^2Y,\qquad XY^2,\qquad Y^3 ,
\]
the monomials of negative homogeneity are exactly \(X^3\) and \(X^2Y\).
Their homogeneities are
\[
  |X^3|=-\frac32-3\kappa,\qquad
  |X^2Y|=-\frac12-5\kappa .
\]
The remaining two have positive homogeneity:
\[
  |XY^2|=\frac12-7\kappa>0,\qquad
  |Y^3|=\frac32-9\kappa>0 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Homogeneity is additive under multiplication and is raised by \(2\) under
\(\mathcal I\).  Therefore
\[
  |Y|=|\mathcal I(X^3)|=3\alpha+2=\frac12-3\kappa .
\]
The four cubic monomials in the first nonlinear expansion have homogeneities
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
  \text{monomial} & \text{homogeneity} \\
  \hline
  X^3 & 3\alpha=-\frac32-3\kappa \\
  X^2Y & 2\alpha+\beta=-\frac12-5\kappa \\
  XY^2 & \alpha+2\beta=\frac12-7\kappa \\
  Y^3 & 3\beta=\frac32-9\kappa .
\end{array}
\]
The first two numbers are negative for every \(\kappa>0\).  The inequality
\(\kappa<1/14\) gives \(1/2-7\kappa>0\), and the weaker inequality
\(\kappa<1/6\) gives \(3/2-9\kappa>0\).  Hence the displayed list contains
all and only the negative cubic drift monomials at this stage.
\end{proof}

The coordinate attached to \(X^3\) is the one-loop Wick contraction
\(C_{1,\epsilon}\).  The coordinate attached to \(X^2\mathcal I(X^3)\) is
the two-loop local contraction \(C_{2,\epsilon}\) after the nested
one-loop subgraph has already been subtracted.

With these conventions, the renormalized cutoff equation in the BPHZ
regularity-structure convention
has the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\tau\Phi_\epsilon
  =
  \Delta\Phi_\epsilon-m^2\Phi_\epsilon
  -\lambda\Phi_\epsilon^3
  +
  \bigl(3\lambda C_{1,\epsilon}
        -9\lambda^2 C_{2,\epsilon}
        +c_{\rm fin}\bigr)\Phi_\epsilon
  +\xi_\epsilon .
  \label{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde}
\end{equation}
Here \(C_{1,\epsilon}\) is the one-loop contraction already visible in
\(:\!\Phi^3\!:\), \(C_{2,\epsilon}\) is the two-loop local contraction
arising from the negative tree with two cubic vertices, and \(c_{\rm fin}\)
is the finite mass coordinate.  Different smooth mollifiers or subtraction
schemes change \(C_{1,\epsilon}\), \(C_{2,\epsilon}\), and \(c_{\rm fin}\)
separately, but not the statement that only the local linear drift and
vacuum-energy coordinates are needed in this superrenormalizable model.

\begin{proposition}[Sharp spatial cutoff one-loop coordinate]
\label{prop:spde-phi-four-three-c1-fourier-growth}
Work on \(\mathbb T^3=(\mathbb R/2\pi\mathbb Z)^3\) with Fourier basis
\[
  e_n(x)=(2\pi)^{-3/2}e^{in\cdot x},\qquad n\in\mathbb Z^3 .
\]
Let \(m>0\), and let \(X_M\) be the stationary solution of the linear
equation
\[
  \partial_\tau X_M=(\Delta-m^2)X_M+\xi_M
\]
obtained by projecting the noise to modes \(|n|_\infty\leq M\), with
normalization
\[
  \mathbb E\,\xi(\tau,x)\xi(\tau',x')
  =
  2\delta(\tau-\tau')\delta(x-x').
\]
Then the one-loop local coordinate is
\[
  C_1(M)
  =
  \mathbb E X_M(\tau,x)^2
  =
  (2\pi)^{-3}
  \sum_{\substack{n\in\mathbb Z^3\\ |n|_\infty\leq M}}
  \frac{1}{|n|^2+m^2},
  \label{eq:spde-c1-sharp-fourier-cutoff}
\]
independent of \((\tau,x)\).  There are constants \(0<c_m<C_m<\infty\)
such that, for all \(M\geq1\),
\[
  C_1(M)\leq C_m(1+M),
\]
and, for all \(M\geq \max(1,\lceil m\rceil)\),
\[
  C_1(M)\geq c_m M .
\]
Consequently, for dyadic cutoffs \(M_N=2^N\),
\[
  0\leq C_1(M_{N+1})-C_1(M_N)\leq C_m'2^N
\]
with \(C_m'<\infty\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a fixed Fourier mode \(n\), the linear equation is the
Ornstein--Uhlenbeck equation
\[
  dX_n=-(|n|^2+m^2)X_n\,d\tau+\sqrt2\,dB_n(\tau),
\]
with the real-field constraint \(X_{-n}=\overline{X_n}\).  The stationary
variance is the ratio between the noise variance \(2\) and twice the damping
rate:
\[
  \mathbb E|X_n|^2=\frac{1}{|n|^2+m^2}.
\]
Since \(|e_n(x)|^2=(2\pi)^{-3}\), summing the retained modes gives
\eqref{eq:spde-c1-sharp-fourier-cutoff}.

Let
\[
  S_r=\{n\in\mathbb Z^3:|n|_\infty=r\}.
\]
For \(r\geq1\),
\[
  |S_r|=(2r+1)^3-(2r-1)^3=24r^2+2 .
\]
If \(n\in S_r\), then \(r^2\leq |n|^2\leq3r^2\).  Hence
\[
  \sum_{n\in S_r}\frac{1}{|n|^2+m^2}
  \leq
  \frac{24r^2+2}{r^2}
  \leq 26 .
\]
Adding the zero mode gives the upper bound \(C_1(M)\leq C_m(1+M)\).

For \(r\geq\max(1,\lceil m\rceil)\), the same shell satisfies
\[
  |n|^2+m^2\leq 3r^2+r^2=4r^2 .
\]
Therefore
\[
  \sum_{n\in S_r}\frac{1}{|n|^2+m^2}
  \geq
  \frac{24r^2}{4r^2}
  =
  6 .
\]
Summing these shell lower bounds from
\(\max(1,\lceil m\rceil)\) to \(M\) gives \(C_1(M)\geq c_mM\) after
adjusting \(c_m\) to cover the finite initial range.  The dyadic increment
bound follows from the upper shell estimate applied to
\(M_N<|n|_\infty\leq M_{N+1}\), whose shell radii are
\(M_N+1,\ldots,M_{N+1}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Spatial Fourier cutoff two-loop coordinate]
\label{prop:spde-phi-four-three-c2-fourier-log-growth}
Use the notation of
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-phi-four-three-c1-fourier-growth}, and put
\[
  a_n=|n|^2+m^2,\qquad n\in\mathbb Z^3 .
\]
Let
\[
  K_M(t,x)
  =
  \mathbf 1_{t>0}(2\pi)^{-3}
  \sum_{\substack{k\in\mathbb Z^3\\ |k|_\infty\leq M}}
  e^{-a_kt}e^{ik\cdot x}
\]
be the retarded heat kernel with the same spatial cutoff, and let
\[
  G_M(t,x)
  =
  (2\pi)^{-3}
  \sum_{\substack{p\in\mathbb Z^3\\ |p|_\infty\leq M}}
  \frac{e^{-a_p|t|}e^{ip\cdot x}}{a_p}
\]
be the stationary covariance.  Define
\begin{equation}
  C_2(M)
  =
  2\int_0^\infty\int_{\mathbb T^3}
  K_M(t,x)G_M(t,x)^2\,\dd x\,\dd t .
  \label{eq:spde-c2-fourier-cutoff-integral}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
  C_2(M)
  =
  2(2\pi)^{-6}
  \sum_{\substack{p,q\in\mathbb Z^3\\
                  |p|_\infty,|q|_\infty,|p+q|_\infty\leq M}}
  \frac{1}{a_pa_q(a_p+a_q+a_{p+q})}.
  \label{eq:spde-c2-fourier-cutoff-sum}
\end{equation}
There are constants \(0<c_m<C_m<\infty\), depending on \(m\) but not on
\(M\), such that
\[
  C_2(M)\leq C_m\bigl(1+\log(1+M)\bigr)
  \qquad(M\geq1),
\]
and
\[
  C_2(M)\geq c_m\log M
  \qquad(M\geq16).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Substituting the Fourier series for \(K_M\) and \(G_M\) into
\eqref{eq:spde-c2-fourier-cutoff-integral} gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  C_2(M)
  &=
  2(2\pi)^{-9}
  \sum_{\substack{k,p,q\in\mathbb Z^3\\
                  |k|_\infty,|p|_\infty,|q|_\infty\leq M}}
  \frac{1}{a_pa_q}
  \int_0^\infty e^{-(a_k+a_p+a_q)t}\,\dd t
  \int_{\mathbb T^3}e^{i(k+p+q)\cdot x}\,\dd x .
\end{aligned}
\]
The spatial integral is \((2\pi)^3\) when \(k+p+q=0\) and is zero otherwise.
Eliminating \(k\) gives \eqref{eq:spde-c2-fourier-cutoff-sum}.

For the upper bound, discard the restriction \(|p+q|_\infty\leq M\).  Let
\[
  B_0=\{n\in\mathbb Z^3:|n|_\infty<2\},
  \qquad
  B_\ell=\{n\in\mathbb Z^3:2^\ell\leq |n|_\infty<2^{\ell+1}\}
  \quad(\ell\geq1).
\]
There is a constant \(A<\infty\) such that
\[
  |B_\ell|\leq A2^{3\ell}\qquad(\ell\geq0).
\]
For \(\ell\geq1\) this follows from
\[
  |B_\ell|\leq |\{n:|n|_\infty<2^{\ell+1}\}|
  =(2^{\ell+2}-1)^3,
\]
and \(B_0\) is finite.  There is also \(b_m>0\), for instance
\[
  b_m=\min(m^2,1),
\]
such that, for \(n\in B_\ell\),
\[
  a_n\geq b_m2^{2\ell}\qquad(\ell\geq0).
\]
If \(p\in B_\ell\) and \(q\in B_s\), then
\[
  a_p+a_q+a_{p+q}\geq a_p+a_q
  \geq b_m2^{2\max(\ell,s)} .
\]
Thus the contribution of the block \(B_\ell\times B_s\) is bounded by
\[
  C_m
  \frac{2^{3\ell}2^{3s}}
       {2^{2\ell}2^{2s}2^{2\max(\ell,s)}}
  =
  C_m2^{-|\ell-s|}.
\]
If \(|p|_\infty,|q|_\infty\leq M\), then only
\(\ell,s\leq L_M=\lceil\log_2(1+M)\rceil\) occur.  Hence
\[
  C_2(M)
  \leq
  C_m
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{L_M}\sum_{s=0}^{L_M}2^{-|\ell-s|}
  \leq
  3C_m(L_M+1),
\]
which is the stated upper bound after increasing \(C_m\).

For the lower bound, write \(N=\lfloor\log_2M\rfloor\).  For
\(2\leq \ell\leq N-2\), let
\[
  P_\ell
  =
  \{n\in\mathbb Z^3:
    2^\ell\leq n_i<2^\ell+2^{\ell-2},\ i=1,2,3\}.
\]
Then \(|P_\ell|\geq 2^{3\ell-6}\).  If \(p,q\in P_\ell\), then
\[
  |p|_\infty,\ |q|_\infty,\ |p+q|_\infty\leq 2^{\ell+2}\leq M,
\]
and
\[
  a_p,\ a_q,\ a_{p+q}\leq A_m2^{2\ell}
\]
with \(A_m<\infty\) independent of \(\ell\).  The summands in
\eqref{eq:spde-c2-fourier-cutoff-sum} are therefore bounded below by
\(A_m^{-3}2^{-6\ell}\) on \(P_\ell\times P_\ell\).  The contribution of
this block is at least
\[
  2(2\pi)^{-6}|P_\ell|^2A_m^{-3}2^{-6\ell}
  \geq c_m .
\]
Summing the disjoint lower-bound blocks for
\(\ell=2,\ldots,N-2\) gives \(C_2(M)\geq c_m(N-3)\).  Since
there is an absolute constant \(c_0>0\) with
\[
  N-3\geq c_0\log M\qquad(M\geq16),
\]
the lower bound follows after decreasing \(c_m\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Dyadic shell bound for the two-loop coordinate]
\label{prop:spde-phi-four-three-c2-dyadic-shell-bound}
Let \(C_2(M)\) be the sharp spatial Fourier-cutoff two-loop coordinate in
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-phi-four-three-c2-fourier-log-growth}, and set
\[
  M_N=2^N,\qquad N\in\mathbb N .
\]
There is a constant \(C_m^{\rm shell}<\infty\), depending on \(m\) but not
on \(N\), such that, for all \(N\geq2\),
\[
  0\leq C_2(M_{N+1})-C_2(M_N)\leq C_m^{\rm shell}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write
\[
  D_N=\{(p,q)\in\mathbb Z^3\times\mathbb Z^3:
       |p|_\infty,|q|_\infty,|p+q|_\infty\leq2^N\}.
\]
The summand in \eqref{eq:spde-c2-fourier-cutoff-sum} is non-negative, and
\(D_N\subset D_{N+1}\), so the increment is non-negative.

Use the dyadic annuli \(B_\ell\) from the proof of
Proposition~\ref{prop:spde-phi-four-three-c2-fourier-log-growth}.  The same
block estimate gives a constant \(A_m<\infty\) such that, for every
\(\ell,s\geq0\), the contribution of
\[
  (B_\ell\times B_s)\cap D_{N+1}
\]
to \(C_2(M_{N+1})\) is bounded by
\[
  A_m2^{-|\ell-s|}.
\]
If \((p,q)\in D_{N+1}\setminus D_N\) and
\[
  p\in B_\ell,\qquad q\in B_s,
\]
then \(\ell,s\leq N+1\).  Moreover, \(\max(\ell,s)\geq N-1\).  Indeed, if
\(\ell,s\leq N-2\), then
\[
  |p|_\infty<2^{N-1},\qquad |q|_\infty<2^{N-1},
\]
hence \(|p+q|_\infty<2^N\), so \((p,q)\in D_N\), a contradiction.
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
  C_2(M_{N+1})-C_2(M_N)
  &\leq
  A_m
  \sum_{\substack{0\leq\ell,s\leq N+1\\
                  \max(\ell,s)\geq N-1}}
  2^{-|\ell-s|}                                      \\
  &\leq
  A_m\sum_{t=N-1}^{N+1}
  \left(1+2\sum_{r=0}^{t-1}2^{-(t-r)}\right)          \\
  &\leq
  9A_m .
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus \(C_m^{\rm shell}=9A_m\) is admissible.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Finite-cutoff local counterterm calculation]
\label{prop:spde-phi-four-three-local-counterterm-calculation}
Let \(X_\epsilon\) be the smooth stationary Gaussian solution of the
linearized cutoff equation and write
\[
  G_\epsilon(z,w)
  =
  \mathbb E\bigl[X_\epsilon(z)X_\epsilon(w)\bigr].
\]
Let \(K_\epsilon\) denote the retarded heat kernel with the same ultraviolet
mollification and define the local constants
\[
  C_{1,\epsilon}=G_\epsilon(z,z),
  \qquad
  C_{2,\epsilon}
  =
  2\int K_\epsilon(z,w)G_\epsilon(z,w)^2\,\dd w ,
  \label{eq:spde-c1-c2-local-definitions}
\]
where translation invariance on the spacetime torus makes the right-hand
sides independent of \(z\).  Consider the perturbative fixed-point expansion
\[
  \Phi_\epsilon
  =
  X_\epsilon
  -
  \lambda K_\epsilon(X_\epsilon^3)
  +O(\lambda^2),
\]
and let \(\mathcal L\) denote the projection that retains only the local
linear part produced by Wick contractions and the zeroth Taylor coefficient
of the remaining field at \(w=z\).  Then
\[
  \mathcal L\!\left[-\lambda X_\epsilon^3
  +3\lambda^2X_\epsilon^2K_\epsilon(X_\epsilon^3)\right]
  =
  \bigl(-3\lambda C_{1,\epsilon}
        +9\lambda^2C_{2,\epsilon}\bigr)X_\epsilon .
\]
Consequently the counterterm inserted in the cutoff drift is the negative of
this local singular part, namely
\[
  \bigl(3\lambda C_{1,\epsilon}
        -9\lambda^2C_{2,\epsilon}\bigr)\Phi_\epsilon ,
\]
up to the independent finite mass coordinate \(c_{\rm fin}\Phi_\epsilon\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The one-loop term is the Wick contraction of two of the three fields in
\(X_\epsilon^3\):
\[
  X_\epsilon(z)^3
  =
  :\!X_\epsilon(z)^3\!:
  +3G_\epsilon(z,z)X_\epsilon(z).
\]
Thus
\[
  \mathcal L[-\lambda X_\epsilon^3]
  =
  -3\lambda C_{1,\epsilon}X_\epsilon .
\]

For the two-loop term, expand the nonlinearity to first order in the
nonlinear response:
\[
  -\lambda\Phi_\epsilon^3
  =
  -\lambda X_\epsilon^3
  +3\lambda^2X_\epsilon^2K_\epsilon(X_\epsilon^3)
  +O(\lambda^3).
\]
The coefficient \(3\) is the number of choices for the factor of
\(\Phi_\epsilon\) in \(\Phi_\epsilon^3\) that is replaced by
\(-\lambda K_\epsilon(X_\epsilon^3)\); the two minus signs give the positive
sign in the displayed term.  At a spacetime point \(z\),
\[
  X_\epsilon(z)^2K_\epsilon(X_\epsilon^3)(z)
  =
  \int K_\epsilon(z,w)
  X_\epsilon(z)^2X_\epsilon(w)^3\,\dd w .
\]
Its local linear part is obtained by contracting four of the five Gaussian
fields and replacing the remaining field at \(w\) by its zeroth Taylor term
at \(z\).  There are two possible forms of such contractions.

First leave one of the three \(X_\epsilon(w)\) factors uncontracted and
contract the two \(X_\epsilon(z)\) factors with the two remaining
\(X_\epsilon(w)\) factors.  This gives \(3\cdot2=6\) pairings and contributes
\[
  6
  \int K_\epsilon(z,w)G_\epsilon(z,w)^2\,\dd w\;X_\epsilon(z)
  =
  3C_{2,\epsilon}X_\epsilon(z).
\]
Second leave one of the two \(X_\epsilon(z)\) factors uncontracted, contract
the other \(X_\epsilon(z)\) with one of the three \(X_\epsilon(w)\) factors,
and contract the remaining two \(X_\epsilon(w)\) factors with each other.
This produces
\[
  6
  \int K_\epsilon(z,w)G_\epsilon(z,w)G_\epsilon(w,w)\,\dd w\;X_\epsilon(z).
\]
This is the one-loop tadpole inserted into the inner cubic vertex.  The
finite-cutoff forest subtraction used here is the following recursive local
operation: before extracting the new local part of a graph, replace every
proper divergent subgraph by its already computed local counterterm.  For the
present tree this means replacing the inner \(X_\epsilon(w)^3\) by its
one-loop Wick-ordered cubic, so the displayed nested tadpole is removed
before the two-loop coordinate is defined.  Therefore the new two-loop local
coordinate is the first contribution only, with the normalization in
\eqref{eq:spde-c1-c2-local-definitions}.  Multiplying by the outer factor
\(3\lambda^2\) gives
\[
  \mathcal L[3\lambda^2X_\epsilon^2K_\epsilon(X_\epsilon^3)]
  =
  9\lambda^2C_{2,\epsilon}X_\epsilon .
\]
Combining the one-loop and two-loop local parts proves the displayed
formula.  The finite coordinate \(c_{\rm fin}\) is not fixed by singular
subtraction; it labels the chosen renormalized mass coordinate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Abstract local fixed point in modelled distributions]
\label{thm:spde-abstract-modelled-fixed-point}
Let \(0<T\leq1\).  Let \(E_T\) be a Banach space of modelled distributions
on the parabolic time slab \([0,T]\times\mathbb T^3\), let \(F_T\) be a
Banach space of modelled distributional forcing terms on the same slab, and
assume the following data and estimates.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item A zero-initial-time abstract heat integration operator
  \(\mathcal K_T:F_T\to E_T\) satisfies, for constants \(A>0\) and
  \(\theta>0\),
  \begin{equation}
    \|\mathcal K_T H\|_{E_T}
    \leq
    A T^\theta \|H\|_{F_T}.
    \label{eq:spde-abstract-schauder-small-time}
  \end{equation}
  The power \(T^\theta\) encodes the positive time gain obtained after the
  singular local model has been fixed.
  \item A reconstructed stochastic convolution \(X_T\in E_T\) and an
  initial-data lift \(P_Tu_0\in E_T\) are given.  Put
  \[
    R_0=\|P_Tu_0+X_T\|_{E_T}.
  \]
  \item The cubic product is a locally Lipschitz map \(E_T\to F_T\):
  for a constant \(B>0\),
  \[
    \|U^3\|_{F_T}\leq B\|U\|_{E_T}^3,
  \]
  and
  \begin{equation}
    \|U^3-V^3\|_{F_T}
    \leq
    B(\|U\|_{E_T}^2+\|V\|_{E_T}^2)\|U-V\|_{E_T}.
    \label{eq:spde-cubic-modelled-lipschitz}
  \end{equation}
  \item The local linear term is bounded as a map \(E_T\to F_T\):
  \[
    \|U\|_{F_T}\leq D\|U\|_{E_T}
  \]
  for a constant \(D>0\).
\end{enumerate}
Fix real parameters \(\lambda\) and \(M\).  Define
\begin{equation}
  \Psi_T(U)
  =
  P_Tu_0+X_T
  +
  \mathcal K_T\bigl(-\lambda U^3+MU\bigr).
  \label{eq:spde-modelled-fixed-point-map}
\end{equation}
Let \(R=2R_0\).  If
\begin{align}
  A T^\theta\bigl(B|\lambda|R^3+D|M|R\bigr)
  &\leq \frac{R}{2},
  \label{eq:spde-fixed-point-ball-condition}\\
  A T^\theta\bigl(2B|\lambda|R^2+D|M|\bigr)
  &<1,
  \label{eq:spde-fixed-point-contraction-condition}
\end{align}
then \(\Psi_T\) has a unique fixed point in the closed ball
\[
  \mathbb B_R=\{U\in E_T:\|U\|_{E_T}\leq R\}.
\]
The fixed point depends Lipschitz-continuously on \(P_Tu_0+X_T\) as long as
the same constants and the same ball are used.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For \(U\in\mathbb B_R\), the Schauder estimate
\eqref{eq:spde-abstract-schauder-small-time}, the cubic bound, and the
linear bound give
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \|\Psi_T(U)\|_{E_T}
  &\leq
  R_0
  +
  A T^\theta
  \bigl(|\lambda|\|U^3\|_{F_T}+|M|\|U\|_{F_T}\bigr)\\
  &\leq
  \frac{R}{2}
  +
  A T^\theta
  \bigl(B|\lambda|R^3+D|M|R\bigr)
  \leq R .
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus \(\Psi_T\) maps \(\mathbb B_R\) into itself.  For
\(U,V\in\mathbb B_R\), the Lipschitz estimate
\eqref{eq:spde-cubic-modelled-lipschitz} gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \|\Psi_T(U)-\Psi_T(V)\|_{E_T}
  &\leq
  A T^\theta
  \left(
    |\lambda|\|U^3-V^3\|_{F_T}
    +|M|\|U-V\|_{F_T}
  \right)\\
  &\leq
  A T^\theta
  \bigl(2B|\lambda|R^2+D|M|\bigr)\|U-V\|_{E_T}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Condition~\eqref{eq:spde-fixed-point-contraction-condition} makes this
Lipschitz constant strictly smaller than one; call it \(q\).  Choose
\(U_0\in\mathbb B_R\) and set \(U_{n+1}=\Psi_T(U_n)\).  Since
\(\Psi_T(\mathbb B_R)\subset\mathbb B_R\),
\[
  \|U_{n+1}-U_n\|_{E_T}
  \leq
  q^n\|U_1-U_0\|_{E_T}.
\]
For \(m>n\),
\[
  \|U_m-U_n\|_{E_T}
  \leq
  \sum_{j=n}^{m-1}q^j\|U_1-U_0\|_{E_T}
  \leq
  \frac{q^n}{1-q}\|U_1-U_0\|_{E_T}.
\]
The space \(E_T\) is complete, so \(U_n\) converges to some
\(U_\ast\in\mathbb B_R\).  Continuity of \(\Psi_T\) gives
\(\Psi_T(U_\ast)=U_\ast\).  If \(V_\ast\) is another fixed point in
\(\mathbb B_R\), then
\[
  \|U_\ast-V_\ast\|_{E_T}
  \leq
  q\|U_\ast-V_\ast\|_{E_T},
\]
and \(q<1\) forces \(U_\ast=V_\ast\).

For dependence on the inhomogeneous term, let \(Y=P_Tu_0+X_T\) and
\(\widetilde Y=\widetilde P_T\widetilde u_0+\widetilde X_T\), and suppose
the two corresponding fixed points \(U,\widetilde U\) lie in the same ball
\(\mathbb B_R\).  The same estimate gives
\[
  \|U-\widetilde U\|_{E_T}
  \leq
  \|Y-\widetilde Y\|_{E_T}
  +
  q\|U-\widetilde U\|_{E_T},
  \qquad
  q=A T^\theta(2B|\lambda|R^2+D|M|)<1.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \|U-\widetilde U\|_{E_T}
  \leq
  \frac{1}{1-q}\|Y-\widetilde Y\|_{E_T}.
\]
\end{proof}

In the dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) application, \(E_T\) is a weighted
modelled-distribution space whose lowest components encode the stochastic
convolution and whose positive components encode the unknown remainder.
The space \(F_T\) is the corresponding forcing space after subtracting the
singular local parts of the cubic.  The operator \(\mathcal K_T\) is the
abstract heat integration map, and
\[
  M=3\lambda C_{1,\epsilon}-9\lambda^2C_{2,\epsilon}+c_{\rm fin}
\]
in the finite-cutoff equation before passing to the renormalized model.  The
theorem proves the fixed-point step once the model bounds, product bounds,
and Schauder bound have been established.  It does not prove convergence of
the BPHZ-renormalized models; that remains a separate stochastic estimate.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Renormalized dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) theorem package]
\label{thm:renormalized-phi-four-three-spde-datum}
Let the spatial manifold be a finite three-dimensional torus, let
\(\xi_\epsilon=\rho_\epsilon\ast\xi\) be a smooth space-time mollification
of white noise, and fix the normalization of
\eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde}.  In the BPHZ
regularity-structure chart there are local constants
\(C_{1,\epsilon}\), \(C_{2,\epsilon}\), and a finite mass coordinate
\(c_{\rm fin}\), such that the following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item For each fixed \(\epsilon>0\) and smooth initial field, the cutoff
  equation \eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde} has a unique smooth
  local solution.  The solution map is Markovian and depends continuously on
  the initial field and on the smooth noise.
  \item After enhancing the mollified noise by its BPHZ-renormalized negative
  trees, the cutoff solutions converge in probability, on compact
  stochastic-time intervals before explosion and in negative spatial Holder
  or Besov topologies, to a limit independent of the mollifier up to finite
  local coordinate changes.
  \item The positive quartic drift, together with the renormalized mass
  coordinate, gives global solutions and invariant probability measures for
  the limiting Markov process in the massive phase.  Starting the cutoff
  equation in its finite-cutoff Gibbs law gives stationary solutions, and
  every limiting stationary law is invariant for the limiting Markov
  semigroup.
  \item In a common finite-volume regulator and renormalization chart, this
  invariant law has the same Schwinger moments as the constructive
  \(\Phi^4_3\) Euclidean measure with the same renormalized mass coordinate.
  Equivalently, after the field and mass normalizations are matched, the
  SPDE stationary construction and the constructive Euclidean construction
  determine the same OS input hierarchy.
\end{enumerate}
\end{quotedtheorem}

This renormalized \(\Phi^4_3\) SPDE theorem is a quoted theorem-boundary for
the regularity-structures construction, not a proof completed here.  The
quoted source lineage is Hairer's regularity-structures construction and the
Hairer--Mourrat--Weber treatments of dynamic \(\Phi^4_3\) renormalization.
The four clauses are logically distinct.  Local cutoff well-posedness is an
ordinary smooth parabolic theorem.  Renormalized convergence is the singular
SPDE theorem: one first builds the canonical model from the mollified noise;
its negative tree components diverge as \(\epsilon\downarrow0\); the BPHZ
character subtracts the expectations of all divergent negative subtrees,
producing a renormalized model satisfying uniform analytic bounds; compactness
and Kolmogorov estimates then give convergence of the model.

After the renormalized model has been constructed, the local fixed-point
step has the form proved in
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-abstract-modelled-fixed-point}.  The missing analytic
input is the verification, for the actual BPHZ model, of the Schauder,
product, and small-time estimates used in that theorem.  Reconstructing the
fixed point then gives the distribution-valued solution by
Theorem~\ref{thm:spde-compact-reconstruction}.  The explicit local term in
\eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde} is the image, in the concrete
equation, of the BPHZ action on the negative trees.  Invariant measures are
identified by finite-cutoff stationarity, tightness, and convergence of
stationary solutions.  Identification with the constructive Euclidean
\(\Phi^4_3\) measure is still another step: both constructions must be placed
in the same finite-volume cutoff chart, the mass and field coordinates must be
matched, and equality of the limiting Schwinger moments must be proved.  Each
sentence in this paragraph names a theorem-level estimate; none is to be
treated as proved merely by being quoted.

The theorem-boundary statements still left in this chapter concern the
global Da Prato--Debussche construction of two-dimensional stochastic
quantization and the BPHZ-renormalized construction of the dynamic
\(\Phi^4_3\) model.\footnote{See G.~Da Prato and A.~Debussche, ``Strong
solutions to the stochastic quantization equations,'' \emph{Ann. Probab.}
31 (2003); M.~Hairer, ``A theory of regularity structures,''
\emph{Invent. Math.} 198 (2014); and the Hairer--Mourrat--Weber
regularity-structures treatment of \(\Phi^4_3\) renormalization.  For recent
singular-SPDE work in gauge contexts, see A.~Chandra, I.~Chevyrev,
M.~Hairer, and H.~Shen, ``Stochastic quantisation of Yang--Mills,''
Invent. Math. \textbf{230} (2022) 1025--1096.}  The compact finite-sector
reconstruction theorem has been proved above because its proof is a local
analytic argument once the model bounds and modelled-distribution bounds are
available.  The cited works identify deeper analytic machinery that remains
to be reconstructed in this monograph; they do not by themselves settle the
monograph's proof obligations.

\end{openproblem}

\section{Paracontrolled and RG Routes}

Regularity structures are not the only analytic language for singular SPDE.
Paracontrolled distributions decompose products using Bony's paraproduct
\[
  fg=f\prec g+f\circ g+f\succ g,
\]
and control the resonant product \(f\circ g\) by enhancing the noise with the
few ill-defined resonant objects needed by the equation.  For \(\Phi^4_3\),
this route uses the same local counterterm content as
\eqref{eq:phi-four-three-renormalized-spde}, expressed in Fourier/Besov
language rather than in a tree Hopf algebra.

Kupiainen-style RG treats the stochastic convolution and nonlinear response
by scale integration.  It is closer in form to the constructive RG of
Chapter~\ref{ch:constructive-rigorous-renormalization-group}.  The common
structure is that no finite truncation of diagrams is being trusted by
itself: the theorem estimates the infinite irrelevant tail in a norm and
shows convergence of the renormalized limit.

\section{Invariant Measure and OS Data}

If the renormalized Markov process exists and is ergodic with invariant
measure \(\mu\), the Euclidean Schwinger functions are
\begin{equation}
  S_n(f_1,\ldots,f_n)
  =
  \int \Phi(f_1)\cdots\Phi(f_n)\,\dd\mu(\Phi).
  \label{eq:spde-invariant-measure-schwinger}
\end{equation}
This is not yet a complete QFT construction.  One must verify:
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{Euclidean covariance},\qquad
  \text{reflection positivity},\\
  \text{regularity and OS growth bounds},\qquad
  \text{clustering or phase decomposition}.
\end{gathered}
\]
Stochastic quantization is especially powerful because it gives a dynamic
construction of the measure and a precise algebraic account of stochastic
counterterms.  Reflection positivity, however, is a property of the
Euclidean field measure under physical time reflection; it is not automatic
from Markov positivity in the auxiliary stochastic time.

\begin{remark}[Gauge theories and BV compatibility]
\label{rem:spde-gauge-bv-compatibility}
For gauge theories, stochastic quantization must be reconciled with gauge
symmetry, gauge fixing, and the BV master equation.  A stochastic evolution
for gauge potentials before quotienting is not by itself a construction of a
gauge-invariant local QFT.  The constructive target is a measure or
observable theory for gauge-invariant fields, or a gauge-fixed/BV
construction whose quantum master equation and positivity replacement are
controlled.  Two- and three-dimensional Yang--Mills SPDE programs provide
important laboratories; four-dimensional Yang--Mills remains a separate open
problem.
\end{remark}

\end{openproblem}
