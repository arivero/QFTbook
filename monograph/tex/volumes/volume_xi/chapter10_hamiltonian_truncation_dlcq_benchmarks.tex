\chapter{Hamiltonian Truncation, DLCQ, And Benchmark Protocols}
\label{ch:constructive-hamiltonian-truncation-dlcq-benchmarks}

\ConventionsLorentzian

Hamiltonian numerical methods are regulator frameworks for QFT.  This
chapter formulates Hamiltonian truncation, light-front discretization, and
benchmark scripts as controlled approximations with explicitly declared
Hilbert spaces, cutoffs, counterterms, and convergence tests.

\begin{definition}[Hamiltonian truncation regulator datum]
\label{def:hamiltonian-truncation-regulator-datum}
A Hamiltonian truncation regulator datum is a tuple
\[
  \mathfrak H_{\rm trunc}
  =
  \bigl(
    \Hilb,\mathcal Q_H,
    \{\Hilb_\Lambda,P_\Lambda\}_{\Lambda\in I},
    \mathcal B_\Lambda,
    \{\mathcal O_a,c_a(\Lambda)\}_{a\in\mathcal A_{\rm ct}},
    \mathcal O_{\rm obs},
    \mathcal E_{\rm sys}
  \bigr)
\]
with the following entries.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item \(\Hilb\) is the target Hilbert space and \(\mathcal Q_H\) is a
  lower-bounded closed quadratic form on a dense form domain
  \(\operatorname{Dom}\mathcal Q_H\).  When the limiting Hamiltonian is known
  as a self-adjoint operator \(H\), \(\mathcal Q_H\) is its associated form.
\item \(I\) is a directed cutoff set.  For each \(\Lambda\in I\),
  \(\Hilb_\Lambda\subset\operatorname{Dom}\mathcal Q_H\) is a
  finite-dimensional trial space and \(P_\Lambda\) is the Hilbert-space
  projection onto \(\Hilb_\Lambda\).  The datum specifies whether the intended
  density statement is strong, form-norm, graph-norm, or another declared
  topology.
\item \(\mathcal B_\Lambda\) is the ordered computational basis used to build
  finite matrices.  The basis choice includes inner-product conventions,
  quantum-number sectors, symmetry projections, and normalization of finite
  volume states.
\item \(\mathcal O_a\) are local counterterm or improvement operators admitted
  by the regulator, and \(c_a(\Lambda)\) are their cutoff-dependent
  coordinates in a declared scheme.  The finite Hamiltonian matrix is
  \[
    H_\Lambda^{\rm ren}
    =
    P_\Lambda H P_\Lambda
    +
    \sum_{a\in\mathcal A_{\rm ct}}
    c_a(\Lambda)P_\Lambda\mathcal O_aP_\Lambda
  \]
  when the formal operator expression is meaningful; otherwise the same
  formula denotes the finite quadratic form specified in the basis
  \(\mathcal B_\Lambda\).
\item \(\mathcal O_{\rm obs}\) is the list of finite-regulator observables to
  be extracted from \(H_\Lambda^{\rm ren}\), together with their normalization
  and symmetry-sector labels.
\item \(\mathcal E_{\rm sys}\) is the systematic-error model: cutoff
  extrapolation ansatz, finite-volume correction model, counterterm-fitting
  prescription, residual-certificate tolerance, and benchmark scripts used to
  check finite-matrix algebra.
\end{enumerate}
The datum records a regulator family.  A continuum or infinite-volume claim
requires an additional convergence theorem or controlled approximation that
states the topology, the limiting quadratic form or observable, and an error
bound.
\end{definition}

\section{Projected Hamiltonian Datum}

The elementary version of Definition~\ref{def:hamiltonian-truncation-regulator-datum}
starts with a known self-adjoint Hamiltonian \(H\) on \(\Hilb\), bounded
below.  Choose finite-dimensional subspaces
\[
  \Hilb_\Lambda\subset\Hilb,\qquad P_\Lambda:\Hilb\to\Hilb_\Lambda,
\]
with \(P_\Lambda\to I\) strongly.  The projected Hamiltonian is
\[
  H_\Lambda=P_\Lambda H P_\Lambda
  \]
on \(\Hilb_\Lambda\).  If \(H\) is defined by a QFT regulator, \(H_\Lambda\)
must be supplemented by local counterterms:
\[
  H_\Lambda^{\mathrm{ren}}
  =
  P_\Lambda H P_\Lambda
  +
  \sum_a c_a(\Lambda)P_\Lambda\mathcal O_aP_\Lambda.
\]
The pair \((\Hilb_\Lambda,H_\Lambda^{\mathrm{ren}})\) is the numerical
regulator datum.

\section{Rayleigh--Ritz Bound}

\begin{lemma}[Rayleigh--Ritz bound at fixed Hamiltonian]
\label{lem:rayleigh-ritz-fixed-hamiltonian}
Let \(H\) be a self-adjoint operator on \(\Hilb\), bounded below, and suppose
that
\[
  E_0\le E_1\le\cdots\le E_n
\]
are isolated eigenvalues below the bottom of the essential spectrum, counted
with multiplicity.  Let \(\mathcal Q_H\) be the closed quadratic form of
\(H\).  For a finite-dimensional subspace
\(\Hilb_\Lambda\subset\operatorname{Dom}\mathcal Q_H\), define
\[
  E_n(\Lambda)
  =
  \min_{\substack{W\subset\Hilb_\Lambda\\ \dim W=n+1}}
  \max_{\substack{\psi\in W\\ \|\psi\|=1}}\mathcal Q_H(\psi,\psi).
\]
Then \(E_n(\Lambda)\ge E_n\).  If \(\{\Hilb_\Lambda\}\) is an increasing net
and, for some orthonormal eigenbasis
\(\varphi_0,\ldots,\varphi_n\) of the first \(n+1\) eigenspaces, there are
\(\eta_j^\Lambda\in\Hilb_\Lambda\) with
\[
  \eta_j^\Lambda\to\varphi_j
  \quad\text{in the form norm}\quad
  \|\psi\|_{\mathcal Q}^2
  =
  \|\psi\|^2+\mathcal Q_H(\psi,\psi)-c\|\psi\|^2
\]
for a constant \(c<\inf\operatorname{spec}H\), then
\(E_n(\Lambda)\downarrow E_n\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The min--max characterization of the \(n\)-th discrete eigenvalue gives
\[
  E_n
  =
  \inf_{\substack{W\subset\operatorname{Dom}\mathcal Q_H\\ \dim W=n+1}}
  \sup_{\substack{\psi\in W\\ \|\psi\|=1}}\mathcal Q_H(\psi,\psi).
\]
The variational problem defining \(E_n(\Lambda)\) restricts the same infimum
to the smaller family of subspaces contained in \(\Hilb_\Lambda\), hence
\(E_n(\Lambda)\ge E_n\).  By the approximation hypothesis, Gram--Schmidt in
the ordinary Hilbert norm produces an \((n+1)\)-dimensional subspace
\(W_\Lambda\subset\Hilb_\Lambda\) whose unit sphere converges in form norm to
the unit sphere of
\(\operatorname{span}\{\varphi_0,\ldots,\varphi_n\}\).  The Gram--Schmidt
coefficients are rational functions of inner products whose limiting
denominators are nonzero, so the form-norm convergence is preserved.  The
supremum of \(\mathcal Q_H\) on that unit sphere therefore converges to
\(E_n\).
The upper bound \(E_n(\Lambda)\le \sup_{\psi\in W_\Lambda,\|\psi\|=1}
\mathcal Q_H(\psi,\psi)\) gives \(\limsup_\Lambda E_n(\Lambda)\le E_n\).
Together with the lower bound this proves convergence.  Monotonic decrease
follows because increasing \(\Hilb_\Lambda\) enlarges the family of trial
subspaces.
\end{proof}

This proposition concerns a fixed self-adjoint operator \(H\).  It does not
assert monotonicity for a family \(H_\Lambda^{\mathrm{ren}}\) whose
counterterms change with \(\Lambda\), nor for a sequence of finite matrices
obtained from different regulators.  Those comparisons require their own
cutoff-error estimates.

\begin{proposition}[Gap-stable Ritz counting for a fixed Hamiltonian]
\label{prop:gap-stable-ritz-counting-fixed-hamiltonian}
Let \(H\) and \(\mathcal Q_H\) be as in
Lemma~\ref{lem:rayleigh-ritz-fixed-hamiltonian}.  Let
\(\{\Hilb_\Lambda\}\) be an increasing net of finite-dimensional subspaces of
\(\operatorname{Dom}\mathcal Q_H\) which is dense in the form norm.  Let
\[
  E_0\leq E_1\leq\cdots
\]
be the discrete eigenvalues of \(H\) below the bottom of the essential
spectrum, counted with multiplicity, and let
\[
  E_0(\Lambda)\leq E_1(\Lambda)\leq\cdots
       \leq E_{\dim\Hilb_\Lambda-1}(\Lambda)
\]
be the Ritz eigenvalues obtained by restricting \(\mathcal Q_H\) to
\(\Hilb_\Lambda\).  Suppose \(a<b<\inf\operatorname{spec}_{\rm ess}H\) and
that \(a,b\notin\operatorname{spec}H\).  Define
\[
  N_H(c)=\#\{j:E_j<c\},
  \qquad c\in\{a,b\},
\]
and \(m=N_H(b)-N_H(a)\).  Then, for all sufficiently large \(\Lambda\),
exactly \(m\) Ritz eigenvalues lie in the interval \((a,b)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(b\) lies below the essential spectrum and is not an eigenvalue, there
are only finitely many eigenvalues below \(b\).  Put
\[
  M=N_H(b),\qquad r=N_H(a).
\]
For each \(j<M\), the form-norm density of \(\bigcup_\Lambda\Hilb_\Lambda\)
implies the approximation hypothesis in
Lemma~\ref{lem:rayleigh-ritz-fixed-hamiltonian} for the first
\(j+1\) eigenspaces; hence
\begin{equation}
  E_j(\Lambda)\downarrow E_j .
  \label{eq:fixed-H-ritz-eigenvalue-convergence}
\end{equation}
If \(j<r\), then \(E_j<a\), and
\eqref{eq:fixed-H-ritz-eigenvalue-convergence} gives
\[
  E_j(\Lambda)<a
\]
for all sufficiently large \(\Lambda\).  If \(r\leq j<M\), then
\[
  a<E_j<b .
\]
The lower inequality \(E_j(\Lambda)\geq E_j>a\) follows from
Lemma~\ref{lem:rayleigh-ritz-fixed-hamiltonian}; convergence gives
\[
  E_j(\Lambda)<b
\]
for all sufficiently large \(\Lambda\).  Thus, eventually, at least
\(M-r=m\) Ritz values lie in \((a,b)\), namely those with labels
\[
  r,\ldots,M-1 .
\]
It remains to exclude extra Ritz values below \(b\).  If
\(E_M(\Lambda)<b\) for some \(\Lambda\), then the min--max definition of
\(E_M(\Lambda)\) supplies an \((M+1)\)-dimensional subspace of
\(\operatorname{Dom}\mathcal Q_H\) on whose unit sphere the quadratic form is
strictly less than \(b\).  Applying the min--max characterization in the full
form domain would give \(E_M<b\), contradicting \(M=N_H(b)\).  Therefore no
Ritz value with label \(j\geq M\) can lie below \(b\).  Combining the two
statements gives exactly \(m\) Ritz values in \((a,b)\) for all sufficiently
large \(\Lambda\).
\end{proof}

The proposition is a theorem about Galerkin approximation to one fixed
self-adjoint Hamiltonian and one fixed quadratic form.  Its hypotheses are
visible: a lower-bounded self-adjoint operator, an isolated finite spectral
window, and form-norm density of the trial spaces.  It does not justify a
counterterm-dependent truncation unless one has first proved that the
renormalized finite matrices converge to a fixed limiting quadratic form in a
sense strong enough to replace the form-norm density assumption.

\section{Truncated Conformal Space}

For a two-dimensional QFT built as a relevant deformation of a CFT, choose the
CFT Hilbert space on a circle and keep states satisfying
\[
  L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c}{12}\le E_{\mathrm{cut}}\,\frac{L}{2\pi}.
\]
The Hamiltonian matrix elements are
\[
  \langle i|H|j\rangle
  =
  \frac{2\pi}{L}
  \left(\Delta_i-\frac{c}{12}\right)\delta_{ij}
  +
  \sum_a\lambda_a
  \left(\frac{L}{2\pi}\right)^{1-\Delta_a}
  \langle i|\mathcal O_a(1)|j\rangle.
\]
High-energy states generate cutoff dependence.  The local Hamiltonian
counterterms are determined by matching low-energy observables as
\(E_{\mathrm{cut}}\) is varied.

\section{Exactly Solvable TCSA Benchmark: Ising Energy Deformation}

A useful benchmark must distinguish the finite Hamiltonian matrix from the
continuum theory it approximates.  The thermal deformation of the two
dimensional Ising CFT is special because the deformed theory is a free massive
Majorana fermion.  On a circle of circumference \(L\), in the
Neveu--Schwarz sector, positive modes are labelled by
\[
  r\in \mathbb Z_{\geq0}+\frac12,
  \qquad
  p_r=\frac{2\pi r}{L}.
\]
For each \(r\), the finite-mode Hamiltonian reduces to the real symmetric
Bogoliubov block
\[
  H_r=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    -p_r&m\\
    m&p_r
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
where \(m\) is the Majorana mass induced by the energy-operator coupling in a
fixed normalization.  The corresponding finite truncation with \(N\) modes is
the direct sum
\[
  H_N=\bigoplus_{r=1/2}^{N-1/2} H_r .
\]

\begin{remark}[Finite-mode Majorana benchmark]
\label{rem:ising-energy-tcsa-benchmark}
The eigenvalues of \(H_N\) are
\[
  \left\{
    \pm\sqrt{p_r^2+m^2}\ :\
    r=\frac12,\frac32,\ldots,N-\frac12
  \right\}.
\]
For a fixed mode \(r\),
\[
  \det(H_r-\lambda I)
  =
  (-p_r-\lambda)(p_r-\lambda)-m^2
  =
  \lambda^2-(p_r^2+m^2).
\]
Thus the two eigenvalues of \(H_r\) are
\(\lambda=\pm\sqrt{p_r^2+m^2}\).  The matrix \(H_N\) is a direct sum of the
blocks \(H_r\), so its characteristic polynomial is the product of the block
characteristic polynomials.  The benchmark is therefore exact at every finite
cutoff \(N\); any numerical deviation from these values measures an
implementation or diagonalization error rather than a truncation effect.
\end{remark}

The script
\[
  \texttt{qft\_scripts/tcsa\_ising\_energy\_benchmark.py}
\]
diagonalizes \(H_N\) and checks Remark
\ref{rem:ising-energy-tcsa-benchmark}.  Its role is narrow and rigorous: it
is a public finite-matrix benchmark for Hamiltonian-truncation conventions
before nonintegrable perturbations, counterterm fitting, and cutoff
extrapolation are introduced.  The magnetic-field Ising TCSA problem and the
\(E_8\) mass-spectrum computation require additional sectors and perturbing
operators.

\section{Ising TCSA And TFFSA With Spin Deformation}

The two-dimensional Ising field theory with both thermal and magnetic
deformations is the basic test case in which Hamiltonian truncation is useful
and conceptually delicate.  Start from the critical Ising CFT on a spatial
circle of circumference \(L\).  Let \(H_{\rm CFT}(L)\) be the self-adjoint
Hamiltonian generated by cylinder time translations,
\[
  H_{\rm CFT}(L)
  =
  \frac{2\pi}{L}
  \left(L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c}{12}\right),
  \qquad c=\frac12 .
\]
Let \(\varepsilon\) be the energy operator of scaling dimension
\(\Delta_\varepsilon=1\), and let \(\sigma\) be the spin operator of scaling
dimension \(\Delta_\sigma=1/8\).  A TCSA regulator for the two-coupling Ising
family is the finite matrix
\begin{equation}
  H_{\rm TCSA}(L;E_{\rm cut})
  =
  P_{E_{\rm cut}}
  \left[
    H_{\rm CFT}(L)
    +
    \tau\int_0^L\varepsilon_{\rm cyl}(0,x)\,\dd x
    +
    h\int_0^L\sigma_{\rm cyl}(0,x)\,\dd x
  \right]
  P_{E_{\rm cut}}
  +
  H_{\rm ct}(E_{\rm cut}).
  \label{eq:ising-two-coupling-tcsa-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
Here \(P_{E_{\rm cut}}\) projects onto CFT states satisfying
\((2\pi/L)(L_0+\bar L_0-c/12)\le E_{\rm cut}\).  The symbol
\(\mathcal O_{\rm cyl}\) means the cylinder field obtained from the plane
field by the conformal map.  After fixing a plane normalization, the
finite-volume matrix elements contain the factors \((2\pi/L)^{\Delta_a}\)
dictated by the state-operator correspondence.  The couplings have
engineering dimensions
\[
  [\tau]=1,\qquad [h]=\frac{15}{8}.
\]
The counterterm matrix \(H_{\rm ct}(E_{\rm cut})\) is part of the regulator.
It is determined by a declared matching prescription; omitting it is a choice
of truncation scheme, not a theorem that no high-energy effects exist.

The same physical family admits a second truncation.  Treat the thermal
deformation exactly.  For \(\tau\ne0\), the unperturbed theory is the free
massive Majorana theory with mass \(m\) proportional to \(\tau\) in the chosen
normalization.  The magnetic perturbation is then
\begin{equation}
  H_{\rm IFT}(L)
  =
  H_{\rm Maj}(m,L)
  +
  h\int_0^L\sigma(0,x)\,\dd x .
  \label{eq:ising-tffsa-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
Truncating \eqref{eq:ising-tffsa-hamiltonian} in the finite-volume fermion
Fock space is the truncated free-fermion-space approach.  Its advantage is
not a new definition of the continuum theory; rather, it moves the relevant
thermal deformation from the perturbation into the basis.  This can reduce
cutoff sensitivity when the low-energy states are better approximated by
massive fermion states than by a small number of conformal states.

We now spell out the finite matrix.  Let \(\mathfrak s\) denote a fermion
spin-structure sector on the circle, with allowed momenta
\[
  p_n=\frac{2\pi n}{L},\qquad n\in Q_{\mathfrak s},
\]
where \(Q_{\mathfrak s}\) is either \(\mathbb Z+\frac12\) or
\(\mathbb Z\), depending on the sector.  A finite-volume rapidity
\(\theta_n\) is defined by
\[
  p_n=m\sinh\theta_n,\qquad
  \omega_n=m\cosh\theta_n .
\]
For a finite set \(I=\{n_1,\ldots,n_k\}\subset Q_{\mathfrak s}\), write
\[
  \ket{I}_{\mathfrak s}
  =
  a_{n_1}^\dagger\cdots a_{n_k}^\dagger\ket{0}_{\mathfrak s},
  \qquad
  E_I^{(0)}(L)=E_{\mathfrak s}^{\rm vac}(L)+\sum_{n\in I}\omega_n,
  \qquad
  P_I=\sum_{n\in I}p_n .
\]
The TFFSA cutoff is an energy cutoff in this massive basis:
\[
  \Hilb_{\rm FF}(L,E_{\rm cut})
  =
  \operatorname{span}\{\ket{I}_{\mathfrak s}:E_I^{(0)}(L)\le E_{\rm cut}\}.
\]
The diagonal part of \(H_{\rm Maj}(m,L)\) is exact in this basis.  The
nontrivial matrix is the spin perturbation.

\begin{definition}[TFFSA spin matrix element]
\label{def:tffsa-spin-matrix-element}
For two finite-volume fermion states \(I,J\), the connected infinite-volume
asymptotic spin matrix element is
\begin{equation}
  V_{IJ}^{\rm conn}
  =
  hL\,\delta_{P_I,P_J}\,
  \frac{
    F_{\rm conn}^{\sigma}(\{\theta_i:i\in I\}\mid \{\theta_j:j\in J\})
  }{
    \sqrt{\rho_I\rho_J}
  },
  \qquad
  \rho_I=\prod_{i\in I}mL\cosh\theta_i .
  \label{eq:tffsa-connected-spin-matrix-element}
\end{equation}
Here \(F_{\rm conn}^{\sigma}\) is the crossed spin-field form factor derived
in Chapter~\ref{ch:integrable-form-factor-bootstrap-local-operators}, and
\(\rho_I\) is the free Bethe-state density of states.  The full finite-volume
matrix element also contains disconnected contraction terms when \(I\) and
\(J\) share rapidities, and exponentially small finite-size corrections of
order \(e^{-mL}\) in a massive theory.  A numerical TFFSA scheme must specify
whether it uses exact finite-volume Ising form factors, the asymptotic formula
\eqref{eq:tffsa-connected-spin-matrix-element}, or a hybrid prescription.
\end{definition}

This definition makes the status of the computation explicit.  At fixed
\((L,E_{\rm cut})\), after all matrix elements have been declared, TFFSA is
ordinary finite-dimensional spectral theory.  The physical assertions concern
the removal or extrapolation of the cutoff and, if
\eqref{eq:tffsa-connected-spin-matrix-element} is used, the large-\(mL\)
control of the finite-volume form-factor approximation.

\begin{definition}[Zero-momentum connected TFFSA block]
\label{def:zero-momentum-connected-tffsa-block}
Fix \(m>0\), \(L>0\), an integer \(N_{\rm pair}\geq0\), a spin-field
normalization \(\bar\sigma\), and a magnetic coupling \(h\).  For
\[
  r_n=n+\frac12,\qquad
  p_n=\frac{2\pi r_n}{L},\qquad
  p_n=m\sinh\theta_n,\qquad
  \omega_n=m\cosh\theta_n ,
  \quad n=0,\ldots,N_{\rm pair}-1,
\]
let
\[
  \ket{0},\qquad
  \ket{n}:=\ket{\theta_n,-\theta_n},\quad
  n=0,\ldots,N_{\rm pair}-1,
\]
be the finite zero-total-momentum basis consisting of the vacuum and
two-particle pairs.  Set
\[
  \rho_n=(mL\cosh\theta_n)^2 .
\]
The connected-block TFFSA matrix is the Hermitian
\((N_{\rm pair}+1)\times(N_{\rm pair}+1)\) matrix
\[
  H_{ij}=E_i^{(0)}\delta_{ij}+hL\,V_{ij},
\]
where \(E_0^{(0)}=0\), \(E_n^{(0)}=2\omega_n\) for \(n\geq1\), and
\[
  V_{ii}=\bar\sigma .
\]
For \(i<j\), \(V_{ij}\) is defined by the crossed connected form factor
\begin{equation}
  V_{ij}
  =
  \frac{
    F_{\rm conn}^{\sigma}(
      \{\theta_a+\ii\pi:a\in I_i\}\mid \{\theta_b:b\in I_j\})
  }{
    \sqrt{\rho_i\rho_j}
  },
  \qquad
  V_{ji}=\overline{V_{ij}},
  \label{eq:zero-momentum-tffsa-block}
\end{equation}
with \(\rho_0=1\) and \(I_0=\varnothing\).  The diagonal convention
\(V_{ii}=\bar\sigma\) keeps only the disconnected vacuum-expectation
contribution.  The omitted diagonal connected finite parts are singular
crossed limits and require a separate diagonal finite-volume prescription.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Connected TFFSA block]
\label{rem:zero-momentum-connected-tffsa-hermitian}
The matrix in Definition~\ref{def:zero-momentum-connected-tffsa-block} is
Hermitian.  If \(h=0\), its eigenvalues are
\[
  0,\quad 2\omega_0,\quad 2\omega_1,\quad\ldots,\quad
  2\omega_{N_{\rm pair}-1}.
\]
The diagonal entries \(E_i^{(0)}+hL\bar\sigma\) are real.  For \(i<j\) the
lower-triangular entry is defined to be \(\overline{V_{ij}}\), hence
\[
  H_{ji}=hL\,\overline{V_{ij}}=\overline{hL\,V_{ij}}=\overline{H_{ij}}
\]
for real \(h\) and \(L\).  Thus \(H^\dagger=H\).  At \(h=0\), all
off-diagonal entries vanish and the matrix is diagonal with the displayed
free finite-volume energies.
\end{remark}

The script
\[
  \texttt{qft\_scripts/tffsa\_ising\_spin\_connected.py}
\]
implements Definition~\ref{def:zero-momentum-connected-tffsa-block}.  It
checks Hermiticity in smoke mode and prints the finite eigenvalues together
with the corresponding free energies.  The purpose is to make the
form-factor normalization, density factors, and diagonal convention
executable.  It is not a claim that the small connected block is the full
magnetic-Ising spectrum.

\begin{lemma}[Finite TFFSA spectral-flow derivative]
\label{lem:finite-tffsa-hellmann-feynman}
Let
\begin{equation}
  H(h)=H_0+hW,\qquad H_0=H_0^\dagger,\quad W=W^\dagger ,
  \label{eq:finite-affine-hermitian-flow}
\end{equation}
be a finite Hermitian matrix family, with \(h\in\mathbb R\).  Suppose
\(\lambda_*(h_*)\) is a simple eigenvalue of \(H(h_*)\), and let \(u_*\) be a
normalized eigenvector.  If
\begin{equation}
  g=\operatorname{dist}\bigl(\lambda_*(h_*),
      \operatorname{spec}H(h_*)\setminus\{\lambda_*(h_*)\}\bigr)>0 ,
  \label{eq:finite-spectral-flow-gap}
\end{equation}
then for \(|h-h_*|\|W\|<g/2\) there is a unique eigenvalue branch
\(\lambda_*(h)\) near \(\lambda_*(h_*)\), counted with its normalized
eigenvector \(u(h)\) after fixing a differentiable phase.  Its derivative at
\(h=h_*\) is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd\lambda_*}{\dd h}(h_*)
  =
  u_*^\dagger W u_* .
  \label{eq:finite-hellmann-feynman-derivative}
\end{equation}
If all eigenvalues of \(H(h_*)\) are simple and
\(\lambda_a'(h_*)=u_a^\dagger W u_a\), then
\begin{equation}
  \sum_a\lambda_a'(h_*)=\operatorname{tr} W .
  \label{eq:finite-spectral-flow-trace-identity}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a positively oriented circle \(C\) centered at \(\lambda_*(h_*)\) with
radius smaller than \(g/2\).  For \(z\in C\),
\[
  \|(h-h_*)W(H(h_*)-z)^{-1}\|
  \leq
  |h-h_*|\|W\|\,\|(H(h_*)-z)^{-1}\|
  <
  1 ,
\]
when \(|h-h_*|\|W\|<g/2\).  Thus the resolvent identity and the Neumann series
give a well-defined resolvent \((H(h)-z)^{-1}\) on \(C\), analytic in \(h\).
The Riesz projection
\[
  P(h)=\frac{1}{2\pi\ii}\oint_C (z-H(h))^{-1}\,\dd z
\]
therefore has rank one for \(h\) in this neighborhood and depends
differentiably on \(h\).  Taking \(u(h)=P(h)u_*/\|P(h)u_*\|\), with the phase
chosen so that \(u_*^\dagger u(h)\) is positive real near \(h_*\), gives a
differentiable normalized eigenvector for the unique eigenvalue branch inside
\(C\).

Differentiate
\[
  H(h)u(h)=\lambda_*(h)u(h)
\]
at \(h=h_*\).  Since \(H'(h)=W\),
\[
  Wu_*+H(h_*)u'(h_*)
  =
  \lambda_*'(h_*)u_*+\lambda_*(h_*)u'(h_*) .
\]
Taking the inner product with \(u_*\), and using
\(u_*^\dagger H(h_*)=\lambda_*(h_*)u_*^\dagger\), cancels the terms
containing \(u'(h_*)\) and gives
\eqref{eq:finite-hellmann-feynman-derivative}.  If all eigenvalues are simple,
their normalized eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis, so
\[
  \sum_a\lambda_a'(h_*)
  =
  \sum_a u_a^\dagger W u_a
  =
  \operatorname{tr}W .
\]
\end{proof}

For the connected Ising TFFSA block,
\eqref{eq:finite-affine-hermitian-flow} is exactly the finite matrix
\[
  H(h)=H_0+hL\,V .
\]
The companion script
\[
  \texttt{qft\_scripts/tffsa\_ising\_spectral\_flow.py}
\]
diagonalizes this finite block on a small grid of \(h\)-values and checks
\eqref{eq:finite-hellmann-feynman-derivative} against centered finite
differences at a point where the finite spectrum is separated.  This is a
useful nonintegrable benchmark because it checks the actual finite spectral
flow generated by spin-field form factors.  It remains a finite-matrix
certificate: comparison with the continuum magnetic-Ising spectrum requires
the cutoff, finite-volume, diagonal-form-factor, and counterterm estimates
described around \eqref{eq:tffsa-connected-spin-matrix-element}.

\begin{lemma}[Feshbach--Schur reduction with resolvent control]
\label{lem:hamiltonian-truncation-schur-complement}
In a finite regulated Hilbert space, let \(H=H^\dagger\) be a Hermitian
matrix and let \(\Hilb=P\Hilb\oplus Q\Hilb\), where \(P\) is an orthogonal
projection and \(Q=1-P\).  Suppose \(E\) is not an eigenvalue of the
finite matrix \(QHQ:Q\Hilb\to Q\Hilb\).  Then \(E\) is an eigenvalue of
\(H\) with \(P\psi\ne0\) only if
\begin{equation}
  \left[
    PHP
    -
    PHQ(QHQ-E)^{-1}QHP
  \right]P\psi
  =
  E\,P\psi .
  \label{eq:feshbach-schur-truncation-effective-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
Conversely, if \(u\in P\Hilb\) satisfies
\eqref{eq:feshbach-schur-truncation-effective-hamiltonian}, then
\[
  \psi
  =
  u
  -
  (QHQ-E)^{-1}QHPu
\]
is an eigenvector of \(H\) with eigenvalue \(E\).
Moreover, if
\[
  d(E)=\operatorname{dist}(E,\operatorname{spec}(QHQ))>0,
\]
then the eliminated component obeys the finite-cutoff estimate
\begin{equation}
  \|Q\psi\|
  \leq
  d(E)^{-1}\,\|QHP\,P\psi\|.
  \label{eq:feshbach-q-component-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write the eigenvalue equation \(H\psi=E\psi\) as two block equations:
\[
  (PHP-E)P\psi+PHQ\,Q\psi=0,
  \qquad
  QHP\,P\psi+(QHQ-E)Q\psi=0 .
\]
Since \(E\) is in the resolvent set of \(QHQ\), the second equation gives
\[
  Q\psi=-(QHQ-E)^{-1}QHP\,P\psi .
\]
Substituting this into the first equation gives
\eqref{eq:feshbach-schur-truncation-effective-hamiltonian}.  This proves that
any eigenvector with nonzero \(P\)-projection gives a vector in the kernel of
the energy-dependent effective operator on \(P\Hilb\).  Conversely, suppose
\(u\in P\Hilb\) obeys the Schur-complement equation and define
\[
  \psi=u-(QHQ-E)^{-1}QHPu .
\]
Then \(P\psi=u\) and the second block equation is satisfied by construction,
because \((QHQ-E)(QHQ-E)^{-1}=Q\) on \(Q\Hilb\).  Substituting the
Schur-complement equation into the first block equation gives zero there as
well.  Hence \(H\psi=E\psi\).

Since \(QHQ\) is a finite Hermitian matrix, the spectral theorem gives
\[
  \|(QHQ-E)^{-1}\|=d(E)^{-1}.
\]
Applying this to the explicit formula for \(Q\psi\) gives
\eqref{eq:feshbach-q-component-bound}.  This estimate is the part of the
finite-dimensional identity that survives as useful information in truncation
theory: a high-energy block can be eliminated only together with control of
the resolvent distance and the coupling \(QHP\).
\end{proof}

Equation~\eqref{eq:feshbach-schur-truncation-effective-hamiltonian} is the
precise operator identity behind cutoff counterterms in TCSA and TFFSA.  In
practice one expands the \(Q\)-space resolvent at high energy and replaces its
low-energy action by local or quasilocal operators allowed by the symmetries.
For the Ising spin perturbation this expansion is governed by the short-
distance operator product of \(\sigma\) with itself; for the free-fermion
basis it is equivalently encoded in the high-rapidity behavior of the spin
form factors.  A claimed numerical spectrum is therefore meaningful only
after the truncation scheme states the cutoff, the finite-volume form-factor
prescription, the symmetry sector, the counterterm coordinates, and the
extrapolation model.

\section{Finite-Matrix Spectral Certificates}
\label{sec:finite-matrix-spectral-certificates}

Every Hamiltonian-truncation or DLCQ computation ultimately diagonalizes a
finite Hermitian matrix.  This elementary fact is useful only if the finite
linear-algebra output is certified separately from the continuum claim.  We
therefore record the minimal certificate used by the companion scripts.

\begin{definition}[Residual certificate for a finite truncation]
\label{def:finite-truncation-residual-certificate}
Let \(A=A^\dagger\) be a finite Hermitian matrix on a finite-dimensional
Hilbert space \(V\).  A residual certificate for a proposed eigenpair is a
triple
\begin{equation}
  (\lambda,u,\rho),\qquad \|u\|=1,\qquad
  \rho=\|(A-\lambda)u\|.
  \label{eq:finite-matrix-residual-certificate}
\end{equation}
It is a certificate for the finite matrix \(A\) only.  The number
\(\lambda\) becomes a QFT energy or invariant mass only after the regulator
label, counterterm prescription, symmetry sector, finite-volume convention,
and continuum-extrapolation hypothesis have also been supplied.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Spectral meaning of a finite residual.}
Let \(A=A^\dagger\) be a finite Hermitian matrix, let
\(\|u\|=1\), and set \(\rho=\|(A-\lambda)u\|\).
Then
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{dist}(\lambda,\operatorname{spec}A)\leq \rho .
  \label{eq:residual-distance-to-spectrum}
\end{equation}
More generally, let \(S\subset\operatorname{spec}A\) and let \(P_S\) be the
orthogonal spectral projection onto the span of eigenvectors whose
eigenvalues lie in \(S\).  If
\begin{equation}
  g=\operatorname{dist}\bigl(\lambda,\operatorname{spec}A\setminus S\bigr)>0,
  \label{eq:finite-spectral-gap-from-complement}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
  \|(1-P_S)u\|\leq \frac{\rho}{g}.
  \label{eq:finite-projector-leakage-bound}
\end{equation}

Choose an orthonormal eigenbasis \(e_a\) of \(A\),
\[
  Ae_a=E_a e_a,\qquad u=\sum_a c_a e_a,\qquad \sum_a |c_a|^2=1.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \rho^2
  =
  \|(A-\lambda)u\|^2
  =
  \sum_a |c_a|^2 |E_a-\lambda|^2 .
  \label{eq:residual-spectral-expansion}
\end{equation}
If every eigenvalue obeyed \(|E_a-\lambda|>\rho\), the right hand side of
\eqref{eq:residual-spectral-expansion} would be strictly larger than
\(\rho^2\), a contradiction.  This proves
\eqref{eq:residual-distance-to-spectrum}.  For the projection bound, split
the sum into \(E_a\in S\) and \(E_a\notin S\).  On the complement,
\(|E_a-\lambda|\geq g\), hence
\[
  \rho^2
  \geq
  g^2\sum_{E_a\notin S}|c_a|^2
  =
  g^2\|(1-P_S)u\|^2 .
\]
Taking square roots gives
\eqref{eq:finite-projector-leakage-bound}.

The first inequality says that a small residual forces the existence of some
finite-matrix eigenvalue near \(\lambda\).  The second says that, when a
target spectral cluster is separated from the rest of the finite spectrum,
the approximate eigenvector has quantitatively small leakage out of that
cluster.  These statements do not estimate the distance between different
cutoffs.  Cutoff comparison belongs to the Feshbach identity above, to
counterterm matching, and to the extrapolation bounds below.

For a Hermitian block decomposition \(V=PV\oplus QV\), the same finite
linear algebra gives a practical diagnostic for a truncation counterterm.
At a chosen low-energy value \(E\), the omitted-space correction is exactly
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma_P(E)=PHQ(QHQ-E)^{-1}QHP .
  \label{eq:finite-schur-self-energy}
\end{equation}
Approximating \(\Sigma_P(E)\) by a low-dimensional local counterterm is a
separate approximation; the identity itself is exact only before this
replacement is made.  A controlled truncation must therefore state a norm,
an energy window, and a bound on
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma_P(E)-\Sigma_P^{\rm ct}(E)
  \label{eq:schur-counterterm-remainder}
\end{equation}
in that window.

The companion check
\[
  \texttt{calculation-checks/hamiltonian\_truncation\_dlcq\_checks.py}
\]
verifies the finite residual bound
\eqref{eq:residual-distance-to-spectrum}, the projector leakage estimate
\eqref{eq:finite-projector-leakage-bound}, the Feshbach determinant identity
behind \eqref{eq:feshbach-schur-truncation-effective-hamiltonian}, the
Ising-energy Bogoliubov benchmark, the connected Ising TFFSA block
normalization, the finite Ising TFFSA spectral-flow derivative identity, the
finite large-\(N\) QCD DLCQ quadratic-form identity, and the exact-intercept
cases of the finite large-\(K\) extrapolation diagnostic.

\section{Krylov Data as Finite Spectral Evidence}
\label{sec:krylov-finite-spectral-evidence}

Large truncation matrices are rarely diagonalized by forming a complete
eigenbasis.  The usual numerical output is a Krylov subspace calculation:
Lanczos vectors, a tridiagonal compression, residuals, and sometimes a
spectral density with respect to a chosen seed vector.  These objects have
exact finite-matrix meanings.  They become evidence for a QFT spectrum only
after the finite regulator, symmetry sector, seed overlap, counterterms, and
continuum extrapolation are separately controlled.

\begin{definition}[Krylov spectral certificate]
\label{def:krylov-spectral-certificate}
Let \(A=A^\dagger\) be a finite Hermitian matrix on a finite-dimensional
Hilbert space \(V\), and let \(v\in V\) be a normalized seed vector.  For
\(m\geq1\), the \(m\)-th Krylov subspace is
\[
  \mathcal K_m(A,v)
  =
  \operatorname{span}\{v,Av,\ldots,A^{m-1}v\}.
\]
A Krylov spectral certificate consists of an orthonormal basis
\[
  V_m=(q_0,\ldots,q_{m-1})
  \quad\text{of}\quad
  \mathcal K_m(A,v),
  \qquad q_0=v,
\]
the compression
\[
  T_m=V_m^\dagger A V_m ,
\]
and the residual matrix
\[
  R_m=A V_m-V_mT_m .
\]
When the basis is the exact Lanczos basis before breakdown, \(T_m\) is
tridiagonal and
\begin{equation}
  A V_m=V_mT_m+\beta_m q_m e_m^\dagger ,
  \label{eq:lanczos-residual-relation}
\end{equation}
where \(q_m\perp\mathcal K_m(A,v)\), \(\|q_m\|=1\), \(e_m\) is the last
coordinate vector in \(\mathbb C^m\), and \(\beta_m\geq0\).  Breakdown
means \(\beta_m=0\), in which case \(\mathcal K_m(A,v)\) is already an
invariant subspace for \(A\).
\end{definition}

Suppose \(T_m y=\theta y\), \(\|y\|=1\), and set \(u=V_m y\).  Multiplying
\eqref{eq:lanczos-residual-relation} by \(y\) gives the exact finite
residual identity
\begin{equation}
  (A-\theta)u=\beta_m\, y_m\, q_m,
  \qquad
  \|(A-\theta)u\|=\beta_m |y_m| .
  \label{eq:lanczos-ritz-residual-certificate}
\end{equation}
Thus a reported Lanczos Ritz value is accompanied by a finite-matrix
certificate of the kind defined in
Definition~\ref{def:finite-truncation-residual-certificate}.  Its content is
finite and sectorwise: the regulated matrix in the declared sector has
spectrum within \(\beta_m|y_m|\) of \(\theta\), and the approximate
eigenvector leakage into a separated finite spectral cluster is bounded by
\eqref{eq:finite-projector-leakage-bound}.  A continuum QFT spectral claim
then requires the regulator and extrapolation data specified in
Definition~\ref{def:hamiltonian-truncation-regulator-datum}.

The same Krylov data carry a spectral-measure certificate.  Let
\[
  \mu_v=\sum_a |\langle e_a,v\rangle|^2\delta_{E_a}
\]
be the finite spectral measure of \(A\) in the seed state, where
\(\{e_a\}\) is an orthonormal eigenbasis of \(A\), \(Ae_a=E_a e_a\).  If
\(\theta_r\) are the eigenvalues of \(T_m\) and \(s^{(r)}\) are normalized
eigenvectors of \(T_m\), define
\[
  w_r=|\langle e_1,s^{(r)}\rangle|^2 ,
  \qquad
  \mu_{v,m}^{\rm Krylov}=\sum_{r=1}^{m}w_r\delta_{\theta_r}.
\]
For every polynomial \(p\) of degree at most \(2m-1\),
\begin{equation}
  \int p(\lambda)\,\dd\mu_v(\lambda)
  =
  \sum_{r=1}^{m} w_r p(\theta_r).
  \label{eq:krylov-gauss-moment-exactness}
\end{equation}
To see the mechanism, first note that if \(\deg b\leq m-1\), then
\[
  b(A)v=V_m b(T_m)e_1 .
\]
Let \(\pi_m(\lambda)=\det(\lambda I-T_m)\).  The Lanczos recurrence implies
that \(\pi_m(A)v\) is orthogonal to \(\mathcal K_m(A,v)\), while
\(\pi_m(T_m)=0\).  Any polynomial \(p\) with \(\deg p\leq2m-1\) decomposes
as \(p=a\pi_m+b\) with \(\deg a,\deg b\leq m-1\).  Since
\(a(A)v\in\mathcal K_m(A,v)\),
\[
  \langle v,p(A)v\rangle
  =
  \langle v,b(A)v\rangle
  =
  e_1^\dagger b(T_m)e_1
  =
  e_1^\dagger p(T_m)e_1,
\]
and diagonalizing \(T_m\) gives
\eqref{eq:krylov-gauss-moment-exactness}.  The finite Krylov measure is
therefore a certified quadrature rule for the seed spectral measure, not a
complete list of all finite eigenvalues unless the chosen seeds have overlap
with the relevant spectral subspaces.

Symmetry sectors must be part of the same certificate.  If a finite group or
compact group acts by unitary matrices commuting with \(A\), and \(P_\alpha\)
is the exact projector onto a declared irreducible sector, then the Krylov
calculation for that sector is the calculation for the finite matrix
\[
  A_\alpha=P_\alpha A P_\alpha
  \quad\text{on}\quad
  P_\alpha V .
\]
A seed \(v\) with \(P_\alpha v=0\) contains no spectral information about
that sector, and a single nonzero seed can miss degenerate eigenvectors
orthogonal to its cyclic subspace.  A numerical claim about absence of states
in a finite window must therefore use either full diagonalization in the
sector, a block-Krylov seed family with a rank certificate for the target
subspace, or an independent variational lower bound.  Without such sector
coverage, Lanczos data are reliable spectral evidence for the cyclic
subspace generated by the stated seed, not for the whole Hilbert space.

\section{Variational Ansatz Certificates}
\label{sec:variational-ansatz-certificates}

Tensor-network, DMRG, MERA, and neural-state computations enter QFT as
finite-regulator variational methods.  The ansatz may be sophisticated, but
the first mathematical object is still a finite Hilbert space, a finite
Hermitian operator, and a family of trial vectors.  The following finite
certificates are the common core behind those methods.

\begin{definition}[Finite variational ansatz datum]
\label{def:finite-variational-ansatz-datum}
A finite variational ansatz datum for a regulated Hamiltonian consists of
\[
  (V,A,\Theta,\Psi,\mathcal S,\mathcal E_{\rm samp}) .
\]
Here \(V\) is a finite-dimensional Hilbert space, \(A=A^\dagger\) is the
finite Hamiltonian or invariant-mass matrix in a declared sector,
\(\Theta\) is the parameter space, and
\[
  \Psi:\Theta\to V\setminus\{0\}
\]
is the trial-vector map.  The entry \(\mathcal S\) records exact constraints
imposed on the ansatz, such as gauge invariance, global charge, momentum,
parity, tensor-network bond dimension, locality of tensor contractions, or a
neural architecture.  The entry \(\mathcal E_{\rm samp}\) records the
sampling law and estimator if the Rayleigh quotient is estimated
stochastically rather than contracted exactly.
\end{definition}

For \(\psi\neq0\), write
\[
  E_\psi=\frac{\langle\psi,A\psi\rangle}{\langle\psi,\psi\rangle},
  \qquad
  r_\psi=(A-E_\psi)\psi,
  \qquad
  \Delta_\psi^2=\frac{\|r_\psi\|^2}{\|\psi\|^2}.
\]
The number \(\Delta_\psi^2\) is the energy variance in the trial state:
\[
  \Delta_\psi^2
  =
  \frac{\langle\psi,A^2\psi\rangle}{\langle\psi,\psi\rangle}
  -
  E_\psi^2 .
\]
Consequently
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{dist}(E_\psi,\operatorname{spec}A)\leq \Delta_\psi .
  \label{eq:variational-variance-spectrum-distance}
\end{equation}
This is exactly the residual certificate
\eqref{eq:residual-distance-to-spectrum} applied to
\(\psi/\|\psi\|\).  If \(S\subset\operatorname{spec}A\) is a separated
finite spectral cluster and
\[
  g=\operatorname{dist}(E_\psi,\operatorname{spec}A\setminus S)>0 ,
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  \|(1-P_S)\psi\|
  \leq
  \frac{\Delta_\psi}{g}\,\|\psi\| .
  \label{eq:variational-variance-projector-leakage}
\end{equation}
Thus variance is a finite-matrix eigenstate certificate.  It measures
spectral concentration of the vector already produced by the ansatz; it does
not by itself measure the expressive power of the ansatz family.

The Rayleigh quotient supplies a different certificate when an exact
lower spectral edge is available.  Let
\[
  E_0<E_1
\]
be the lowest two distinct eigenvalues of \(A\), and let \(P_0\) be the
ground-space projection.  Expanding a normalized vector in an eigenbasis gives
\[
  E_\psi-E_0
  =
  \sum_a |\langle e_a,\psi\rangle|^2(E_a-E_0)
  \geq
  (E_1-E_0)\|(1-P_0)\psi\|^2 .
\]
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \|(1-P_0)\psi\|^2
  \leq
  \frac{E_\psi-E_0}{E_1-E_0}.
  \label{eq:variational-ground-projector-bound}
\end{equation}
This bound is useful only after \(E_0\) and a finite gap are known by another
method, or after a certified lower bound replaces the exact ground energy.
The variational upper bound \(E_\psi\geq E_0\) alone is insufficient to
certify distance to the ground-state subspace.

For a smooth normalized ansatz \(\psi(\theta)\), the differential of the
Rayleigh quotient exposes the precise meaning of a variational optimum.  If
\(\|\psi(\theta)\|=1\) and \(\xi\in T_\theta\Theta\), put
\(\dot\psi_\xi=d\psi_\theta(\xi)\).  Differentiating
\(\langle\psi,A\psi\rangle\) and using
\(\operatorname{Re}\langle\dot\psi_\xi,\psi\rangle=0\) gives
\begin{equation}
  dE_\theta(\xi)
  =
  2\,\operatorname{Re}
  \langle \dot\psi_\xi,(A-E_\theta)\psi(\theta)\rangle .
  \label{eq:variational-tangent-gradient}
\end{equation}
A stationary variational parameter therefore makes the residual orthogonal
to the tangent space of the ansatz manifold.  An eigenvector certificate is
stronger: it requires the residual norm \(\|(A-E_\theta)\psi(\theta)\|\) to
be small, or a separate argument that the tangent directions cover the
missing finite directions.

For neural-state and Monte Carlo variational calculations one also needs the
finite estimator identity.  Choose an orthonormal computational basis
\(\{|x\rangle\}_{x\in X}\) and assume \(\psi_x=\langle x|\psi\rangle\neq0\)
on the sampled support.  Define
\[
  p_\psi(x)=\frac{|\psi_x|^2}{\sum_y|\psi_y|^2},
  \qquad
  E_{\rm loc}(x)=\frac{(A\psi)_x}{\psi_x}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  E_\psi=\sum_x p_\psi(x)E_{\rm loc}(x),
  \label{eq:variational-local-energy-mean}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_\psi^2
  =
  \sum_x p_\psi(x)|E_{\rm loc}(x)-E_\psi|^2 .
  \label{eq:variational-local-energy-variance}
\end{equation}
The second identity uses Hermiticity of \(A\):
\[
  \sum_x p_\psi(x)|E_{\rm loc}(x)-E_\psi|^2
  =
  \frac{\|(A-E_\psi)\psi\|^2}{\|\psi\|^2}.
\]
Sampling estimates of \eqref{eq:variational-local-energy-mean} and
\eqref{eq:variational-local-energy-variance} require the Markov-chain or
independent-sample error analysis of
Section~\ref{sec:blocking-jackknife-bootstrap-error-coordinates}; optimization
bias, autocorrelation, and architecture bias are additional entries of
\(\mathcal E_{\rm samp}\), not properties of the finite identities.

Tensor-network terminology fits this datum by specifying \(\Psi\).  A
finite matrix-product state on a spin chain of length \(L\), physical local
dimension \(d\), and bond dimension \(\chi\) is a vector whose amplitudes
are finite contractions of matrices
\[
  \psi_{i_1\ldots i_L}
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}\!\left(
    A^{i_1}_1A^{i_2}_2\cdots A^{i_L}_L
  \right),
  \qquad
  A^i_\ell\in\operatorname{Mat}_{\chi_{\ell-1}\times\chi_\ell}(\mathbb C).
\]
For an infinite translation-invariant MPS, a transfer-operator spectrum can
estimate a correlation length, but a QFT continuum claim still requires a
sequence of finite regulators, a scaling map for operators, and convergence
of the corresponding Schwinger or Wightman data.  Bond dimension, discarded
weight, transfer-operator gap, local-energy variance, and Krylov residuals
are finite evidence coordinates.  The continuum claim is the additional
statement that those coordinates control the regulator limit in a declared
topology.

\section{Light-Front Kinematic And Constraint Datum}
\label{sec:light-front-kinematic-constraint-datum}

Light-front Hamiltonian methods use a null hyperplane as the time slice.  In
the mostly-plus convention, choose one spatial direction \(x^1\) and set
\begin{equation}
  x^\pm=\frac{x^0\pm x^1}{\sqrt2},
  \qquad
  p^\pm=\frac{p^0\pm p^1}{\sqrt2}.
  \label{eq:general-light-front-coordinates}
\end{equation}
For \(D\)-dimensional Minkowski space,
\begin{equation}
  \dd s^2=-2\,\dd x^+\dd x^-+\dd x_\perp^2,
  \qquad
  p\cdot x=-p^-x^+-p^+x^-+p_\perp\cdot x_\perp,
  \label{eq:general-light-front-metric}
\end{equation}
and \(x^+\) is the evolution parameter.  The generator conjugate to \(x^+\)
is \(P^-\); the longitudinal momentum \(P^+\) and transverse momenta
\(P_\perp\) label superselection sectors for the finite light-front
Hamiltonians used below.  These identities are kinematic; they do not define
an interacting QFT until the constraint and zero-mode sectors have also been
specified.

\begin{definition}[Light-front Hamiltonian regulator datum]
\label{def:light-front-hamiltonian-regulator-datum}
A light-front Hamiltonian regulator datum is a Hamiltonian truncation
regulator datum
\(\mathfrak H_{\rm trunc}\) together with the following additional
structure.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item A null-coordinate chart \eqref{eq:general-light-front-coordinates},
  the sign convention \eqref{eq:general-light-front-metric}, and a declared
  evolution parameter \(x^+\).
\item A positive-longitudinal nonzero-mode sector, with one-particle labels
  carrying \(p^+>0\) and whatever transverse, spin, flavor, color, and
  statistics labels are kept by the regulator.
\item A zero-mode and constraint sector
  \[
    \mathcal Z_\Lambda,\qquad
    \mathcal C_{\Lambda,\alpha}=0,\qquad
    \mathcal G_{\Lambda,\rm res},
  \]
  recording the \(p^+=0\) modes, finite global Gauss-law constraints,
  residual gauge transformations, and any additional compact or topological
  degrees of freedom.  Eliminating, quantizing, or imposing these variables
  are different regulator choices and must be part of the datum.
\item A finite physical Hilbert space
  \[
    \Hilb^{\rm phys}_\Lambda
    \subset
    \Hilb^{p^+>0}_\Lambda\otimes\Hilb_{\mathcal Z,\Lambda}
  \]
  obtained after the stated constraint prescription, together with commuting
  finite operators \(P^+_\Lambda\) and \(P_{\perp,\Lambda}\) on the physical
  sector.
\item A finite self-adjoint light-front Hamiltonian \(P^-_\Lambda\) on
  \(\Hilb^{\rm phys}_\Lambda\), commuting with the retained translation
  generators on the finite physical sector.  In a simultaneous
  \(P^+=p^+>0\), \(P_\perp=p_\perp\) sector, the finite invariant-mass
  operator is
  \begin{equation}
    M_\Lambda^2
    =
    2p^+P^-_\Lambda-p_\perp^2.
    \label{eq:light-front-mass-operator-fixed-sector}
  \end{equation}
  Outside a fixed momentum sector the same formula is read as a joint
  functional calculus expression on the common spectral representation of the
  commuting momenta and \(P^-_\Lambda\).
\item A continuum diagnostic specifying how \(p^+\)-cutoffs, harmonic
  resolution, transverse cutoffs, counterterms, principal-value
  prescriptions, and zero-mode constraints are taken to their limiting
  values.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\paragraph{One-particle measure.}
The free massive representation fixes the basic measure behind the
positive-\(p^+\) Fock construction.  For a test function \(F\) on momentum
space and \(m>0\),
\begin{equation}
  \int_{\mathbb R^D}\dd^Dp\,
  \theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+m^2)F(p)
  =
  \int_{p^+>0}
  \frac{\dd p^+\,\dd^{D-2}p_\perp}{2p^+}\,
  F\!\left(p^+,\,\frac{m^2+p_\perp^2}{2p^+},\,p_\perp\right).
  \label{eq:light-front-one-particle-measure}
\end{equation}
Indeed,
\[
  p^2+m^2=-2p^+p^-+p_\perp^2+m^2,
\]
so the \(p^-\)-integration localizes at
\begin{equation}
  p^-=\frac{m^2+p_\perp^2}{2p^+}
  \label{eq:light-front-mass-shell-relation}
\end{equation}
with Jacobian \(|\partial(p^2+m^2)/\partial p^-|=2p^+\).  The
positive-energy condition selects \(p^+>0\) on the massive shell.  The
massless boundary \(p^+=0\), and the constrained zero modes of gauge
theories, are not included in this one-particle measure; they are entries of
\(\mathcal Z_\Lambda\) and \(\mathcal C_{\Lambda,\alpha}\) in
Definition~\ref{def:light-front-hamiltonian-regulator-datum}.

\paragraph{Spectral interpretation.}
At fixed \(p^+\) and \(p_\perp\), \(M_\Lambda^2\) in
\eqref{eq:light-front-mass-operator-fixed-sector} and \(P^-_\Lambda\) have
the same finite eigenvectors, and their eigenvalues are related by
\[
  M^2=2p^+P^- -p_\perp^2.
\]
This is the finite-sector reason light-front calculations often diagonalize
\(P^-\) while reporting invariant masses.  A gauge-theory light-front
calculation is therefore specified only after the nonzero-mode Hamiltonian,
zero-mode/constraint prescription, and residual gauge data have all been
declared in the same regulator scheme.

\section{DLCQ Datum}

DLCQ is the compact positive-longitudinal-momentum specialization of
Definition~\ref{def:light-front-hamiltonian-regulator-datum}.  It
compactifies
\[
  x^-\sim x^-+2\pi R
\]
so that
\[
  p^+=\frac{n}{R},\qquad n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0}.
\]
The total longitudinal momentum is
\[
  P^+=\frac{K}{R},
\]
where \(K\) is the harmonic resolution.  The finite DLCQ Hilbert space at
fixed \(K\) consists of partitions of \(K\) among partons, with transverse and
internal cutoffs added as needed.  Zero modes with \(n=0\) are constrained
variables or additional degrees of freedom depending on the theory; their
treatment is part of the regulator.

\begin{definition}[DLCQ regulator datum]
\label{def:dlcq-regulator-datum}
A DLCQ regulator datum is a Hamiltonian truncation regulator datum together
with the following light-front entries.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item The spatial light-front coordinate is compactified as
  \(x^-\sim x^-+2\pi R\), and the total longitudinal momentum sector is fixed
  by \(P^+=K/R\) with harmonic resolution \(K\in\mathbb Z_{>0}\).
\item The finite basis consists of all declared parton partitions
  \(K=n_1+\cdots+n_N\), with \(n_i>0\), together with the transverse,
  internal, gauge, and statistics labels kept by the regulator.
\item The zero-mode sector is specified as part of the datum: constrained
  variables, eliminated modes, residual global constraints, and additional
  zero-mode degrees of freedom are all recorded before diagonalization.
\item The finite operator is the light-front Hamiltonian \(P^-_K\), or
  equivalently the invariant mass operator
  \(M_K^2=2P^+P^-_K-P_\perp^2\), with counterterms and principal-value
  prescriptions stated in the same regulator scheme.
\item The continuum diagnostic specifies the ordered finite labels followed as
  \(K\to\infty\), the extrapolation variable, and the remainder model used to
  interpret finite-\(K\) data.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\section{\texorpdfstring{Large-\(N\) Two-Dimensional QCD: A Finite DLCQ Matrix}{Large-N Two-Dimensional QCD: A Finite DLCQ Matrix}}

The standard large-\(N\) two-dimensional QCD meson equation involves the
principal-value operator
\[
  (\mathcal K\phi)(x)
  =
  \operatorname{PV}\int_0^1
  \frac{\phi(x)-\phi(y)}{(x-y)^2}\,\dd y ,
  \qquad 0<x<1,
\]
together with endpoint mass terms
\[
  \frac{m_1^2}{x}\phi(x)+\frac{m_2^2}{1-x}\phi(x).
\]
This section records a finite regulator, not a continuum theorem.  Fix a
harmonic resolution \(K\geq3\) and grid points \(x_n=n/K\),
\(n=1,\ldots,K-1\).  For \(\gamma>0\), define the real symmetric matrix
\[
  (M_K^2)_{nm}
  =
  \delta_{nm}
  \left[
    \frac{m_1^2}{x_n}
    +
    \frac{m_2^2}{1-x_n}
    +
    \gamma K\sum_{\substack{j=1\\ j\neq n}}^{K-1}
      \frac1{(n-j)^2}
  \right]
  -
  (1-\delta_{nm})\frac{\gamma K}{(n-m)^2}.
\]
The normalization of \(\gamma\) depends on the convention for
\(g^2N/\pi\).  The finite object above is sufficient to illustrate harmonic
resolution, positivity, and extrapolation obligations.

\paragraph{Finite principal-value quadratic form.}
For \(m_1^2,m_2^2,\gamma>0\) and any
\(v=(v_1,\ldots,v_{K-1})\in\mathbb R^{K-1}\),
\begin{equation}
  v^T M_K^2 v
  =
  \sum_{n=1}^{K-1}
  \left(
    \frac{m_1^2}{x_n}
    +
    \frac{m_2^2}{1-x_n}
  \right)v_n^2
  +
  \gamma K
  \sum_{1\leq n<m\leq K-1}
  \frac{(v_n-v_m)^2}{(n-m)^2}.
  \label{eq:thooft-dlcq-finite-quadratic-form}
\end{equation}
In particular \(M_K^2\) is positive definite.

Substitute the definition of \(M_K^2\) into \(v^TM_K^2v\).  The mass terms
give the first displayed sum.  The off-diagonal kernel gives
\[
  \gamma K
  \sum_{n=1}^{K-1}
  \sum_{\substack{m=1\\m\neq n}}^{K-1}
  \frac{v_n^2-v_nv_m}{(n-m)^2}.
\]
Pair the term \((n,m)\) with \((m,n)\).  For each unordered pair
\(\{n,m\}\),
\[
  \frac{v_n^2-v_nv_m}{(n-m)^2}
  +
  \frac{v_m^2-v_mv_n}{(m-n)^2}
  =
  \frac{(v_n-v_m)^2}{(n-m)^2}.
\]
This gives the stated quadratic form.  Since \(0<x_n<1\), the mass
coefficients are positive; hence \(v^TM_K^2v>0\) for \(v\neq0\).
The calculation is a finite-regulator algebra check: it proves positivity of
the displayed matrix.  Positivity and self-adjointness of the continuum
principal-value operator require a separate continuum-domain construction.

The script
\[
  \texttt{qft\_scripts/thooft\_dlcq.py}
\]
constructs this matrix and diagonalizes it at finite \(K\).  A continuum
claim would require an analysis of the \(K\to\infty\) limit, endpoint
behavior, zero modes, normalization of \(\gamma\), and comparison with a
Lorentz-covariant reconstruction.  The script therefore prints finite
eigenvalues and a quadratic-form positivity check rather than presenting the
numbers as physical meson masses.

\section{Finite-Regulator Extrapolation}
\label{sec:finite-regulator-extrapolation}

The output of a lattice, Hamiltonian-truncation, or DLCQ script is a number
attached to a regulator.  A continuum claim is a separate mathematical
statement about a limiting procedure.  This section records the minimal
finite-dimensional facts used in the companion scripts and explains what an
extrapolation hypothesis must contain.

\begin{definition}[Finite-regulator observable datum]
\label{def:finite-regulator-observable-datum}
A finite-regulator observable datum consists of:
\[
  \bigl(I,\preceq;\ X_i,\ A_i,\ \sigma_i,\ \mathcal E_i\bigr) .
\]
Here \((I,\preceq)\) is a directed set of regulator labels, \(X_i\) is the
finite object being computed at label \(i\), \(A_i\in\mathbb R\) is the
finite observable extracted from \(X_i\), \(\sigma_i\ge0\) is the statistical
standard error if \(A_i\) is estimated by a random algorithm, and
\(\mathcal E_i\) is the declared systematic error model.  For deterministic
TCSA or DLCQ diagonalization one has \(\sigma_i=0\), but \(\mathcal E_i\) is
not zero: it contains cutoff, finite-volume, counterterm, endpoint, or
zero-mode errors.  For a Markov-chain computation, \(A_i\) itself is an
estimator and \(\sigma_i\) must include autocorrelation.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Interpolation ambiguity of finite cutoff data]
\label{rem:finite-data-do-not-determine-continuum-limit}
Let \(K_1,\ldots,K_N\) be distinct positive regulator values and let
\(A_1,\ldots,A_N\in\mathbb R\) be finite outputs.  For every proposed
continuum value \(A_\infty\in\mathbb R\), there is a polynomial \(p(t)\) of
degree at most \(N\) such that
\[
  p(0)=A_\infty,
  \qquad
  p(K_j^{-1})=A_j,\quad j=1,\ldots,N .
\]
Consequently finitely many cutoff values, by themselves, cannot prove a
continuum limit or its value.
The \(N+1\) points
\[
  0,\quad K_1^{-1},\ldots,K_N^{-1}
\]
are distinct.  Lagrange interpolation gives a unique polynomial of degree at
most \(N\) taking the prescribed values \(A_\infty,A_1,\ldots,A_N\) at those
points.  Since \(A_\infty\) was arbitrary, the same finite data are
compatible with arbitrarily many candidate limits unless additional
information is supplied.  The obstruction is the missing analytic ansatz,
monotonicity estimate, remainder bound, or convergence theorem that would
restrict the allowed interpolating functions near \(K^{-1}=0\).
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Power-law cutoff expansion with a remainder bound]
\label{def:power-law-cutoff-expansion}
Let \(K\) be a positive regulator parameter tending to infinity.  A
deterministic sequence \(A_K\) has an \(m\)-term power-law cutoff expansion
with base exponent \(\omega>0\) and controlled remainder if there are real
numbers \(A_\infty,c_1,\ldots,c_m\), a constant \(C\), and \(K_0\) such that
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    A_K
    -
    A_\infty
    -
    \sum_{a=1}^m c_a K^{-a\omega}
  \right|
  \le
  C K^{-(m+1)\omega},
  \qquad K\ge K_0 .
  \label{eq:power-law-cutoff-remainder-bound}
\end{equation}
The same definition applies to a subsequence, for example
\(K,Ks_1,\ldots,Ks_m\), provided these are admissible regulator labels and
the displayed bound holds on that subsequence.  In a TCSA computation the
exponent \(\omega\) and the counterterm coordinates should be derived from
the high-energy tail or OPE; in a DLCQ computation they should be derived
from endpoint and zero-mode analysis.  A fit without
\eqref{eq:power-law-cutoff-remainder-bound} is a diagnostic, not a theorem.
\end{definition}

For a two-cutoff Richardson extrapolant, the leading correction cancels only
under a stated asymptotic expansion with a controlled remainder.  Assume
\[
  A_K=A_\infty+cK^{-\omega}+r_K,
  \qquad
  |r_K|\le C K^{-\eta},
  \qquad
  \eta>\omega>0 ,
\]
for \(K\ge K_0\).  Define
\begin{equation}
  R_K
  =
  \frac{2^\omega A_{2K}-A_K}{2^\omega-1}.
  \label{eq:two-cutoff-richardson-estimator}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
  |R_K-A_\infty|
  \le
  \frac{2^\omega 2^{-\eta}+1}{2^\omega-1}\,
  C K^{-\eta}.
  \label{eq:two-cutoff-richardson-bound}
\end{equation}
Substitute the assumed expansion into
\eqref{eq:two-cutoff-richardson-estimator}:
\[
  2^\omega A_{2K}-A_K
  =
  (2^\omega-1)A_\infty
  +
  2^\omega c(2K)^{-\omega}
  -
  cK^{-\omega}
  +
  2^\omega r_{2K}-r_K .
\]
The two terms proportional to \(c\) cancel because
\(2^\omega(2K)^{-\omega}=K^{-\omega}\).  Dividing by \(2^\omega-1\) gives
\[
  R_K-A_\infty
  =
  \frac{2^\omega r_{2K}-r_K}{2^\omega-1}.
\]
Using \(|r_{2K}|\le C(2K)^{-\eta}\) gives
\eqref{eq:two-cutoff-richardson-bound}.

\paragraph{Integer-power extrapolation with explicit weights.}
Let \(s_0,\ldots,s_m\) be distinct positive real numbers and suppose
\(A_K\) satisfies \eqref{eq:power-law-cutoff-remainder-bound} on the
subsequence \(s_jK\), \(j=0,\ldots,m\).  Let \(w_0,\ldots,w_m\) be the unique
solution of
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{j=0}^m w_j=1,
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=0}^m w_j s_j^{-a\omega}=0,
  \quad a=1,\ldots,m .
  \label{eq:extrapolation-weight-moments}
\end{equation}
Then the extrapolated value
\[
  R_K^{(m)}
  =
  \sum_{j=0}^m w_j A_{s_jK}
\]
satisfies
\begin{equation}
  |R_K^{(m)}-A_\infty|
  \le
  C K^{-(m+1)\omega}
  \sum_{j=0}^m |w_j|\,s_j^{-(m+1)\omega}.
  \label{eq:integer-power-extrapolation-bound}
\end{equation}

Set \(q_j=s_j^{-\omega}\).  The numbers \(q_0,\ldots,q_m\) are distinct,
so the Vandermonde matrix \((q_j^a)_{0\le a,j\le m}\) is invertible.  Hence
\eqref{eq:extrapolation-weight-moments} has a unique solution.  Insert
\eqref{eq:power-law-cutoff-remainder-bound} into \(R_K^{(m)}\).  The
coefficient of \(A_\infty\) is \(\sum_jw_j=1\).  For
\(a=1,\ldots,m\), the coefficient of \(c_aK^{-a\omega}\) is
\(\sum_jw_js_j^{-a\omega}=0\).  The remaining error is bounded by the
triangle inequality:
\[
  \left|\sum_{j=0}^m w_j r_{s_jK}\right|
  \le
  \sum_{j=0}^m |w_j|\,C(s_jK)^{-(m+1)\omega}.
\]
This is \eqref{eq:integer-power-extrapolation-bound}.
The finite algebra is exact, but its use in a continuum claim is only as good
as the expansion and remainder estimate in
Definition~\ref{def:power-law-cutoff-expansion}.  The weights cancel declared
integer powers; the QFT problem separately supplies the evidence that these
powers are the correct cutoff expansion.

\paragraph{\texorpdfstring{Large-\(K\) diagnostic for the finite 't Hooft matrices}{Large-K diagnostic for the finite 't Hooft matrices}.}
For the finite matrices \(M_K^2\) whose quadratic form is displayed in
\eqref{eq:thooft-dlcq-finite-quadratic-form}, write their eigenvalues in
nondecreasing order as
\[
  \lambda_0(K)\leq\lambda_1(K)\leq\cdots\leq\lambda_{K-2}(K).
\]
For a fixed label \(\ell\), the numerical sequence
\[
  A_K=\lambda_\ell(K)
\]
is a finite-regulator sequence in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:finite-regulator-observable-datum}.  Keeping the label
\(\ell\) fixed is only a diagnostic convention until one proves that the
corresponding continuum state is isolated and that nearby finite-\(K\) levels
do not interchange in a way that changes the limiting object.  Choose distinct
resolutions \(K_1<\cdots<K_N\), a base exponent \(\omega>0\), and an integer
\(p<N\).  Set
\[
  q_r=K_r^{-\omega},\qquad
  X_{ra}=q_r^a,\qquad
  r=1,\ldots,N,\quad a=0,\ldots,p .
\]
The least-squares polynomial fit is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat c=(X^T X)^{-1}X^T A,\qquad
  A=(A_{K_1},\ldots,A_{K_N})^T ,
  \label{eq:dlcq-large-K-least-squares-fit}
\end{equation}
provided \(X\) has full column rank.  The reported continuum diagnostic is
\(\widehat c_0\), the fitted intercept at \(q=0\).

\paragraph{\texorpdfstring{Finite large-\(K\) fit with an explicit remainder amplifier}{Finite large-K fit with an explicit remainder amplifier}.}
Assume that the finite data used in
\eqref{eq:dlcq-large-K-least-squares-fit} have the form
\begin{equation}
  A_{K_r}
  =
  A_\infty+\sum_{a=1}^p c_a K_r^{-a\omega}+\rho_r ,
  \qquad r=1,\ldots,N ,
  \label{eq:large-K-fit-model-with-remainder}
\end{equation}
with \(X\) of full column rank.  Let
\[
  B=(X^T X)^{-1}X^T .
\]
Then the fitted intercept satisfies
\begin{equation}
  |\widehat c_0-A_\infty|
  \leq
  \left(\sum_{r=1}^N |B_{0r}|\right)\max_{1\leq r\leq N}|\rho_r| .
  \label{eq:large-K-intercept-remainder-amplification}
\end{equation}
In particular, if all \(\rho_r\) vanish, the fit recovers \(A_\infty\)
exactly, independently of the overdetermination \(N>p+1\).

Let
\[
  c=(A_\infty,c_1,\ldots,c_p)^T,
  \qquad
  \rho=(\rho_1,\ldots,\rho_N)^T .
\]
Equation~\eqref{eq:large-K-fit-model-with-remainder} is the vector identity
\[
  A=Xc+\rho .
\]
Since \(X\) has full column rank, \(BX=I_{p+1}\).  Hence
\[
  \widehat c
  =
  BA
  =
  B(Xc+\rho)
  =
  c+B\rho .
\]
Taking the zeroth component gives
\[
  \widehat c_0-A_\infty
  =
  \sum_{r=1}^N B_{0r}\rho_r .
\]
The triangle inequality gives
\eqref{eq:large-K-intercept-remainder-amplification}.  If \(\rho=0\), then
\(\widehat c=c\) and the intercept is exact.

The companion script
\[
  \texttt{qft\_scripts/thooft\_dlcq\_extrapolation.py}
\]
implements this finite procedure for the matrices \(M_K^2\): it prints the
finite eigenvalues, the fitted intercepts, the residuals, the condition number
of \(X\), and the factor
\(\sum_r |B_{0r}|\) appearing in
\eqref{eq:large-K-intercept-remainder-amplification}.  These quantities
measure how a chosen finite data set responds to a chosen ansatz.  They do not
prove that the large-\(K\) limit of the large-\(N\) QCD meson equation exists
or that the selected exponent \(\omega\) is correct.  Those conclusions require
separate endpoint and zero-mode estimates producing a bound of the form
\eqref{eq:large-K-fit-model-with-remainder}.

The companion calculation
\[
  \texttt{calculation-checks/numerical\_extrapolation\_checks.py}
\]
verifies the algebra in
\eqref{eq:two-cutoff-richardson-estimator}--\eqref{eq:integer-power-extrapolation-bound}
with exact rational arithmetic.  It does not verify that a particular QFT
observable obeys \eqref{eq:power-law-cutoff-remainder-bound}; that remains a
problem-specific analytic estimate.

The companion calculation
\[
  \texttt{calculation-checks/hamiltonian\_truncation\_dlcq\_checks.py}
\]
also verifies the exact-intercept and constant-sequence cases of
the finite large-\(K\) fit calculation above.

\paragraph{Correlated fits and window stability.}
Production lattice, tensor-network, and Hamiltonian-truncation data are rarely
independent scalar data.  A continuum extrapolation datum at a chosen fit
window \(W=\{i_1,\ldots,i_N\}\) therefore contains
\[
  A=(A_{i_1},\ldots,A_{i_N})^T,\qquad
  \Sigma_W,\qquad
  f_0,\ldots,f_p,\qquad
  R_1,\ldots,R_N .
\]
Here \(A_i\) are the finite-regulator outputs, \(\Sigma_W\) is the covariance
matrix of their statistical errors on the window, \(f_a(i)\) are the declared
fit functions with \(f_0(i)=1\), and \(R_r\) is a deterministic bound or
declared systematic envelope for the part of \(A_{i_r}\) not represented by
the chosen fit functions.  Put
\[
  X_{ra}=f_a(i_r),\qquad
  X\in\operatorname{Mat}_{N\times(p+1)}(\mathbb R).
\]
Assume in this paragraph that \(\Sigma_W\) is positive definite and that
\(X\) has full column rank.  If an estimated covariance matrix is singular,
the shrinkage, projection, or diagonal approximation used to replace it is a
new member of the datum, not an invisible numerical detail.

The correlated least-squares coordinate is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat c
  =
  (X^T\Sigma_W^{-1}X)^{-1}X^T\Sigma_W^{-1}A .
  \label{eq:correlated-fit-coordinate}
\end{equation}
Writing
\[
  G=X^T\Sigma_W^{-1}X,\qquad
  B=G^{-1}X^T\Sigma_W^{-1},
\]
one has \(BX=I_{p+1}\).  If the data on the window decompose as
\begin{equation}
  A=Xc+\rho+\epsilon,
  \qquad
  \mathbb E\epsilon=0,\qquad
  \mathbb E(\epsilon\epsilon^T)=\Sigma_W ,
  \label{eq:correlated-fit-data-decomposition}
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
  \widehat c-c=B\rho+B\epsilon .
  \label{eq:correlated-fit-error-decomposition}
\end{equation}
Thus the intercept \(\widehat A_\infty=\widehat c_0\) has the exact finite
error decomposition
\[
  \widehat A_\infty-A_\infty
  =
  \sum_{r=1}^N B_{0r}\rho_r
  +
  \sum_{r=1}^N B_{0r}\epsilon_r .
\]
If \(|\rho_r|\leq R_r\), the deterministic fit-window systematic coordinate is
\begin{equation}
  S_W=\sum_{r=1}^N |B_{0r}|R_r .
  \label{eq:correlated-fit-systematic-coordinate}
\end{equation}
The stochastic variance of the intercept is
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\infty,W}^2
  =
  e_0^T B\Sigma_WB^Te_0
  =
  e_0^TG^{-1}e_0 ,
  \label{eq:correlated-fit-intercept-variance}
\end{equation}
where \(e_0=(1,0,\ldots,0)^T\).  The second equality follows from
\[
  B\Sigma_WB^T
  =
  G^{-1}X^T\Sigma_W^{-1}\Sigma_W\Sigma_W^{-1}XG^{-1}
  =
  G^{-1}.
\]
The correlated residual
\begin{equation}
  \chi_W^2
  =
  (A-X\widehat c)^T\Sigma_W^{-1}(A-X\widehat c)
  \label{eq:correlated-fit-chi-square-coordinate}
\end{equation}
is a finite diagnostic for the chosen window and model.  Its probabilistic
interpretation requires the sampling law of the estimator, the covariance
estimator, and the treatment of autocorrelation described in
Section~\ref{sec:blocking-jackknife-bootstrap-error-coordinates}.

A window-stability analysis is the finite collection
\[
  \mathcal W=\{(W_\alpha,X_\alpha,\Sigma_\alpha,R_\alpha)\}_{\alpha\in A}
\]
together with the resulting coordinates
\[
  \widehat A_{\infty,\alpha},\qquad
  \sigma_{\infty,\alpha},\qquad
  S_\alpha,\qquad
  \chi_\alpha^2 .
\]
Agreement of these coordinates across windows is evidence about the chosen
finite-regulator family only after the windows use the same physical
observable, the same renormalization convention, and compatible operator
normalizations.  The analytic estimate producing the envelopes
\(R_\alpha\) remains the source of continuum control.  For tensor-network and
neural-state calculations the window label must also record bond dimension or
architecture, optimizer termination criterion, local-energy variance or
discarded weight, and any symmetry projection used to select the finite
sector.  Otherwise a stable sequence of numbers may be comparing different
finite problems rather than a single regulated QFT observable.

A benchmark for any truncation method should specify:
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{regulated Hilbert space},\qquad
  \text{operator basis},\qquad
  \text{counterterm coordinates},\\
  \text{observables used for matching},\qquad
  \text{extrapolation model},\qquad
  \text{independent checks}.
\end{gathered}
\]
Independent checks include Ward identities, known finite-volume limits,
reflection positivity where applicable, spectral sum rules, and comparison
with Euclidean lattice or exact integrable data in overlapping regimes.
