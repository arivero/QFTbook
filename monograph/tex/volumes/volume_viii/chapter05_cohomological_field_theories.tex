\chapter{Cohomological Field Theories}
\label{ch:tqft-cohomological-field-theories}

\ConventionsEuclidean

A cohomological field theory is a QFT equipped with an odd symmetry whose
cohomology controls the observables of interest.  Metric independence is
then a theorem when the metric variation of the action and of the observables
is exact for that odd symmetry and boundary terms in field space vanish.

\section{Cohomological Datum}

\begin{definition}[Cohomological QFT datum]
\label{def:cohomological-qft-datum}
A cohomological QFT datum consists of a regulated field space or BV complex
\(\mathcal F\), an odd derivation \(Q\) on functionals, an action \(S\), a
class of integration cycles, and a class of observables \(\mathcal O\) such
that
\[
  Q^2=\mathcal L_\xi+\delta_{\rm gauge}
\]
on the declared variables, \(QS=0\), and physical insertions are \(Q\)-closed
modulo \(Q\)-exact insertions.
\end{definition}

The vector field \(\xi\) may be zero or may generate a compact bosonic
symmetry.  If \(Q^2\neq0\), the cohomology is taken on observables invariant
under the bosonic symmetry generated by \(Q^2\).

\section{Metric Independence}

Let \(g\) be a background metric and suppose
\[
  \frac{\delta S}{\delta g^{\mu\nu}(x)}
  =
  Q\,G_{\mu\nu}(x)
\]
for a local functional \(G_{\mu\nu}\).  If \(\mathcal O\) is \(Q\)-closed and
the regulated measure has no \(Q\)-anomaly, then
\[
  \frac{\delta}{\delta g^{\mu\nu}(x)}
  \langle\mathcal O\rangle
  =
  -\langle (QG_{\mu\nu}(x))\mathcal O\rangle
  =
  -\langle Q(G_{\mu\nu}(x)\mathcal O)\rangle
  =
  0 .
\]
The last equality is Stokes' theorem on the regulated integration cycle.
Thus metric independence requires both \(Q\)-exact stress tensor and absence
of boundary or anomaly terms.

\section{Exact Deformations}

For a \(Q\)-invariant observable and a \(Q\)-exact deformation
\[
  S_t=S+t\,QV,
\]
the derivative of the expectation value is
\[
  \frac{d}{dt}\langle\mathcal O\rangle_t
  =
  -\langle (QV)\mathcal O\rangle_t
  +\langle\mathcal O\rangle_t\langle QV\rangle_t
  =
  0
\]
when \(Q\)-Stokes holds for the normalized measure.  This is the algebraic
core of localization.  It does not identify the fixed locus or the one-loop
determinant; those require analysis of the bosonic part of \(QV\), zero
modes, orientations, and noncompact directions.

\section{Finite-Dimensional Localization Lemma}

\begin{proposition}[Stationary-phase localization for a nondegenerate model]
\label{prop:finite-dimensional-cohomological-localization}
Let \(X\) be an oriented finite-dimensional supermanifold with compact
integration cycle, odd vector field \(Q\), \(Q^2\) generating a compact
bosonic symmetry, and \(Q\)-invariant measure.  Let \(V\) be chosen so that
the bosonic part of \(QV\) is nonnegative and vanishes on a compact
nondegenerate fixed locus \(F\).  Then the integral of a \(Q\)-closed
observable is independent of the coefficient \(t\) of \(QV\) and is computed
by the Gaussian normal integral around \(F\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Independence of \(t\) is the \(Q\)-Stokes argument above.  Taking
\(t\to+\infty\), the nonnegative bosonic part makes the contribution from
the complement of an arbitrarily small neighborhood of \(F\) vanish by
dominated convergence in this finite-dimensional compact setting.  In normal
coordinates to \(F\), rescale the normal variables by \(t^{-1/2}\).  The
Taylor expansion of \(QV\) has a nondegenerate quadratic part by hypothesis,
and terms of polynomial degree \(r>2\) carry the explicit factor
\(t^{1-r/2}\).  The limiting Gaussian integral over normal bosonic and
fermionic coordinates gives the ratio of equivariant Euler determinants
multiplying the restriction of the observable to \(F\).
\end{proof}

\section{BV Form}

For gauge theories, \(Q\) is combined with the BRST/BV differential.  The
cohomological action must solve the classical or quantum master equation, and
observables are BV cohomology classes.  Gauge fixing is a choice of Lagrangian
subspace in the BV phase space.  A \(Q\)-exact deformation is legitimate only
when it is compatible with this BV structure and does not move the integration
cycle across singularities or boundaries.

\section{Examples and Boundaries}

Donaldson--Witten theory, A-model and B-model topological sigma models, and
finite-dimensional equivariant integrals all fit this template after their
field spaces, \(Q\)-transformations, observables, and compactness data are
specified.  The difficult part in infinite-dimensional QFT is not the
algebraic identity \(d\langle\mathcal O\rangle/dt=0\); it is defining the
regulated integral and proving the boundary terms vanish.

\begin{openproblem}[Cohomological QFT beyond formal localization]
\label{op:cohomological-qft-beyond-formal-localization}
Develop regulator-level cohomological QFT constructions in which \(Q\)-Stokes
theorem, compactness of fixed loci, orientation lines, one-loop determinants,
and boundary strata are all controlled.  This is the prerequisite for using
cohomological field theory as a theorem-level bridge between QFT and
topological invariants.
\end{openproblem}
