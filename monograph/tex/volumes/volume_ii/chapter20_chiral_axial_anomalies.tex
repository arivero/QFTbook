\section{Classical Chiral Currents}

Let \(\psi\) be a Dirac fermion in four dimensions.  Throughout this chapter
the Lorentzian metric is mostly plus and the Dirac adjoint is
\[
  \bar\psi:=\ii\psi^\dagger\gamma^0 .
\]
These are the gamma-matrix conventions developed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:spinor-fields-fermionic-path-integrals} and centralized in
Section~\ref{sec:spinor-conventions}.  The anomaly signs below use the same
orientation, chirality matrix, and dimensional-continuation prescription.
The flat oriented coordinate systems used in the perturbative calculations are
fixed by
\[
  \epsilon^{01}=+1
  \quad\hbox{in two dimensions},
  \qquad
  \epsilon^{0123}=+1
  \quad\hbox{in four dimensions}.
\]
The symbols \(\epsilon^{\mu\nu}\) and \(\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\) are
totally antisymmetric with these normalizations.  In four dimensions the
chirality convention is
\[
  \gamma_5=-\ii\gamma^0\gamma^1\gamma^2\gamma^3,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}
  \bigl(\gamma_5\gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu\gamma^\rho\gamma^\sigma\bigr)
  =
  4\ii\,\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}.
\]
Gauge generators in this chapter use the same Hermitian convention as
Chapter~\ref{ch:vol2-classical-yang-mills-matter}: if
\(A_\mu=A_\mu^a t^a\), then
\[
  [t^a,t^b]=\ii f^c{}_{ab}t^c,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}(t^a t^b)=\delta^{ab}
\]
unless a different trace is explicitly declared.  The trace-form anomaly
expressions are written in terms of the matrix-valued curvature
\[
  F_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu-\ii[A_\mu,A_\nu],
\]
so a change of generator normalization rescales the component fields and
coupling coordinates but not the matrix trace formula itself.
With this adjoint the Hermitian bilinear current associated with a real
phase rotation contains an explicit factor of \(\ii\).  Couple \(\psi\) to a
background or dynamical gauge field through
\[
  D_\mu\psi=\partial_\mu\psi-\ii A_\mu\psi .
\]

\begin{definition}[Anomaly representative conventions]
\label{def:anomaly-representative-conventions}
An anomaly formula in this chapter means a renormalized local Ward identity
for specified composite currents after four pieces of data have been fixed.
First, the gauge and vector Ward identities that define the background
coupling are preserved by the regulator and by the counterterm convention.
Second, the remaining local term is represented in the Hermitian trace
normalization stated above, namely
\(\operatorname{tr}(t^at^b)=\delta^{ab}\) unless another trace is declared.
Third, the orientation and chirality matrix are the displayed ones.  Fourth,
local counterterms have been chosen; changing them changes the representative
of the anomaly but not its BRST cohomology class.

The perturbative triangle calculations below use dimensional reduction in
the following restricted sense: the loop momentum is analytically continued
while \(\gamma_5\) and the antisymmetric tensor are kept in the physical
two- or four-dimensional subspace.  The heat-kernel calculation in the
measure section is instead a spectral regulator for a Berezinian trace.  No
Pauli--Villars auxiliary field is used, and no claim is made that
dimensional continuation by itself defines a nonperturbative fermionic path
integral.
\end{definition}

\begin{warning}[Anomaly signs are tied to the full convention datum]
The anomaly coefficients in this chapter are not portable by changing one
symbol at a time.  The signs and factors use simultaneously the mostly-plus
Dirac adjoint, \(\gamma_5=-\ii\gamma^0\gamma^1\gamma^2\gamma^3\),
\(\epsilon^{0123}=+1\), the Hermitian covariant derivative
\(D_\mu=\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu\), the curvature
\(F_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu-\ii[A_\mu,A_\nu]\), and
the trace convention fixed in Chapter~\ref{ch:vol2-classical-yang-mills-matter}.
Changing any one of these choices requires translating the whole convention
datum, including the topological charge normalization and the local
counterterm representative.  Dimensional reduction below is only the
perturbative regulator used to compute the displayed Ward-identity
representative; it is not being used as a nonperturbative definition of the
fermionic path integral.
\end{warning}

\begin{proposition}[Anomaly-coordinate comparison]
\label{prop:anomaly-coordinate-comparison}
Fix a regulated background-field groupoid
\(\mathcal B_\Lambda//\mathcal G_\Lambda\), with right action
\(A\mapsto A^g\), and a local regulator chart in which the chiral fermion
functional is a scalar \(W_\Lambda[A]\).  Then the finite cochain
\[
  C_\Lambda(g;A)=W_\Lambda[A^g]-W_\Lambda[A]
\]
is the additive coordinate of the anomaly line.  More precisely:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item functoriality of the groupoid action gives
  \(C_\Lambda(gh;A)=C_\Lambda(h;A^g)+C_\Lambda(g;A)\);
  \item a local counterterm \(B_\Lambda[A]\) changes this coordinate by the
  coboundary \(B_\Lambda[A^g]-B_\Lambda[A]\);
  \item after choosing a local frame \(s_A\) for the anomaly line, the
  exponentiated transport cocycle is
  \(\alpha_\Lambda(A,g)=\exp C_\Lambda(g;A)\), and the same counterterm is
  the inverse frame change \(s_A\mapsto \exp(-B_\Lambda[A])s_A\);
  \item the inflow bulk carries the inverse line coordinate
  \(\alpha_\Lambda(A,g)^{-1}\);
  \item differentiating \(C_\Lambda(\exp(t\zeta);A)\) at \(t=0\), then taking
  the local continuum representative, gives the consistent local anomaly
  density \(a_4^{(1)}(\zeta,A)\), whose relative BRST class is the
  perturbative obstruction.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently local BRST cancellation removes the obstruction on the identity
component of the background groupoid, while flat determinant- or Pfaffian-line
holonomies on based loops remain separate global anomaly data.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first identity is just the scalar form of functoriality:
\[
  W_\Lambda[A^{gh}]-W_\Lambda[A]
  =
  \bigl(W_\Lambda[(A^g)^h]-W_\Lambda[A^g]\bigr)
  +
  \bigl(W_\Lambda[A^g]-W_\Lambda[A]\bigr).
\]
Replacing \(W_\Lambda\) by \(W_\Lambda+B_\Lambda\) gives the displayed
coboundary.  If the line is locally framed by \(s_A\) and
\[
  T_gs_A=\alpha_\Lambda(A,g)s_{A^g},
\]
then the frame \(s'_A=\exp(-B_\Lambda[A])s_A\) gives
\[
  T_gs'_A
  =
  \exp(-B_\Lambda[A])\alpha_\Lambda(A,g)
  \exp(B_\Lambda[A^g])s'_{A^g},
\]
which is the exponential of the same additive coboundary.  The bulk inflow
line is the inverse anomaly line, so its transport cocycle is
\(\alpha_\Lambda^{-1}\), and tensoring boundary and bulk transports gives the
identity transport.

For a one-parameter gauge transformation \(g(t)=\exp(t\zeta)\), the derivative
of the finite cocycle is
\[
  \frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0}C_\Lambda(g(t);A)
  =
  \delta_\zeta W_\Lambda[A].
\]
After subtracting local counterterms and passing to the continuum local
functional, this derivative is represented by
\(\int_Ma_4^{(1)}(\zeta,A)\).  Differentiating the finite cocycle identity in
two infinitesimal directions gives the Wess--Zumino consistency condition,
and replacing the even gauge parameters by the odd ghost gives the relative
BRST cocycle in \(H^{1,4}(s\mid d)\), developed later in
Proposition~\ref{prop:local-anomalies-relative-brst-classes}.  A local
counterterm changes the representative by the corresponding relative
coboundary.  This infinitesimal argument only sees the identity component of
the background groupoid.  A flat but nontrivial line holonomy along a based
loop has zero local curvature and zero local descent class, but still
prevents a gauge-invariant global scalar section; that global obstruction is
the determinant/Pfaffian-line story developed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:curved-eta-invariants-global-anomalies}.
\end{proof}

For a mass \(m\), the Dirac Lagrangian is
\[
  \mathcal L
  =
  -\bar\psi\gamma^\mu D_\mu\psi
  -m\bar\psi\psi .
\]
When \(m=0\), the classical equations of motion imply conservation of the
vector and axial currents
\[
  j^\mu=\ii\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi,
  \qquad
  j_5^\mu=\ii\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\gamma_5\psi,
  \qquad
  \partial_\mu j^\mu=0,
  \qquad
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu=0 .
\]
For \(m\neq0\), the axial divergence becomes
\[
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu=2\ii m\,\bar\psi\gamma_5\psi
\]
with the \(\gamma_5\) convention stated above.
Here \(D_\mu\bar\psi=\partial_\mu\bar\psi+\ii\bar\psi A_\mu\), and the
classical equations of motion are
\((\gamma^\mu D_\mu+m)\psi=0\) and
\((D_\mu\bar\psi)\gamma^\mu-m\bar\psi=0\).  Since
\(\{\gamma_5,\gamma^\mu\}=0\), these equations give
\(\partial_\mu(\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\gamma_5\psi)=2m\bar\psi\gamma_5\psi\);
the explicit factor of \(\ii\) in \(j_5^\mu\) gives the displayed
normalization.

The corresponding infinitesimal transformations are
\[
  \delta_V\psi=\ii\alpha\psi,\qquad
  \delta_A\psi=\ii\beta\gamma_5\psi,
\]
where \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are constant real parameters.  Classically, for
massless fermions, both transformations are symmetries of the local
Lagrangian.  Quantum mechanically, the vector transformation is preserved
when it is gauged consistently, while the axial current acquires a local
divergence determined by the gauge background.

\section{A Two-Dimensional Contact-Term Calculation}

The local origin of an axial anomaly is already visible in a free
two-dimensional Dirac fermion.  Use the Lorentzian action
\[
  S_2=-\int\dd^2x\,\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi,
  \qquad
  \{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\}=2\eta^{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  \bar\psi=\ii\psi^\dagger\gamma^0,
\]
and define
\[
  \gamma:=\gamma^0\gamma^1,
  \qquad
  \gamma^2=1,
  \qquad
  j^\mu=\ii\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi,
  \qquad
  j_A^\mu=\ii\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\gamma\psi .
\]
The classical massless action is invariant under
\(\psi\mapsto \ee^{\ii\alpha}\psi\) and under
\(\psi\mapsto\ee^{\ii\beta\gamma}\psi\).  The quantum question is the
renormalized Ward identity obeyed by time-ordered current products.  Consider
\[
  G_A^{\mu\nu}(x)
  :=
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j_A^\mu(x)j^\nu(0)
  \ket{\vac} .
\]
At one loop the relevant momentum-space graph is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:two-dimensional-axial-vector-loop}; the figure fixes the loop
momentum routing and distinguishes the axial-current insertion from the vector
current insertion used in the Ward identity below.
\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \begin{tikzpicture}[>=Stealth,scale=1.0,baseline={(0,-0.2)},
    fermion/.style={line width=0.75pt},
    current/.style={circle,fill=black,inner sep=1.7pt}]
    \coordinate (L) at (-1.45,0);
    \coordinate (R) at (1.45,0);
    \draw[fermion,->] (L) to[bend left=55] node[above] {\(p+k\)} (R);
    \draw[fermion,->] (R) to[bend left=55] node[below] {\(k\)} (L);
    \draw[fermion,->] (-2.6,0) -- (L);
    \draw[fermion,->] (R) -- (2.6,0);
    \node[current,label=below:\(\gamma^\mu\gamma\)] at (L) {};
    \node[current,label=below:\(\gamma^\nu\)] at (R) {};
    \node[left] at (-2.6,0) {\(p\)};
  \end{tikzpicture}
  \caption{The two-dimensional current two-point function.  The left insertion
  is the axial current and the right insertion is the vector current.}
  \label{fig:two-dimensional-axial-vector-loop}
\end{figure}
\[
  -\int\frac{\dd^2k}{(2\pi)^2}\,
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
      \ii\gamma^\nu(\not p+\not k)
      \ii\gamma^\mu\gamma\,\not k
    \right]
  }{(k^2-\ii\epsilon)((p+k)^2-\ii\epsilon)} .
\]
Contracting with \(p_\mu\), Feynman parametrizing, and shifting
\(k\mapsto k-xp\) gives
\begin{align}
  \int\dd^2x\,\ee^{-\ii p\cdot x}
  \partial_\mu G_A^{\mu\nu}(x)
  &=
  \ii\int_0^1\dd x
  \int\frac{\dd^2k}{(2\pi)^2}\,
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}(\gamma^\nu\not k\,\not p\,\gamma\,\not k)
    -x(1-x)p^2
    \operatorname{tr}(\gamma^\nu\gamma\not p)
  }{(k^2+p^2x(1-x)-\ii\epsilon)^2}.
  \label{eq:two-dimensional-anomaly-shifted-integral}
\end{align}
The expression is a contact term, so its value depends on the regulator used
to define the composite current.  Continue the loop momentum to
\(D=2-\epsilon\) dimensions while keeping the chirality matrix
\(\gamma=\gamma^0\gamma^1\) two-dimensional.  Rotational averaging gives
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(\gamma^\nu\not k\,\not p\,\gamma\,\not k)
  \longmapsto
  \frac{k^2}{D}
  \operatorname{tr}(\gamma^\nu\gamma_\rho\not p\,\gamma\,\gamma^\rho)
  =
  \frac{2-D}{D}k^2
  \operatorname{tr}(\gamma^\nu\not p\,\gamma).
\]
The factor \(2-D=\epsilon\) multiplies the logarithmic divergence of the loop
integral and leaves a finite local term.  With
\(\operatorname{tr}(\gamma^\nu\gamma\not p)=2\epsilon^{\mu\nu}p_\mu\), one
finds
\[
  \int\dd^2x\,\ee^{-\ii p\cdot x}
  \partial_\mu G_A^{\mu\nu}(x)
  =
  -\frac1{\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}p_\mu .
\]
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j_A^\mu(x)j^\nu(y)
  \ket{\vac}
  =
  \frac{\ii}{\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}
  \partial_\mu\delta^{(2)}(x-y),
  \label{eq:two-dimensional-axial-vector-contact-term}
\end{equation}
whereas the vector Ward identity is preserved:
\[
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j^\mu(x)j^\nu(y)
  \ket{\vac}
  =0 .
\]

Now couple the vector current to a background \(U(1)\) gauge field,
\[
  D_\mu=\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu,
  \qquad
  \Delta S=\int\dd^2x\,j^\mu A_\mu .
\]
Expanding time-ordered correlators in powers of \(A\), the first-order term in
\eqref{eq:two-dimensional-axial-vector-contact-term} gives the local operator
identity
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu j_A^\mu(x)
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}(x),
  \qquad
  F_{\mu\nu}:=\partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu .
  \label{eq:two-dimensional-axial-anomaly}
\end{equation}
Thus the anomaly is already present before any interaction among the fermions:
it is the finite remnant of a regulated contact term in a composite-current
Ward identity.

\begin{proposition}[Two-dimensional axial contact term]
\label{prop:two-dimensional-axial-contact-term}
For the massless two-dimensional Dirac fermion with current conventions of
this section, choose the renormalized current representative whose vector
Ward identity is preserved.  Then the axial-vector current product obeys
\[
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j_A^\mu(x)j^\nu(y)
  \ket{\vac}
  =
  \frac{\ii}{\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}
  \partial_\mu\delta^{(2)}(x-y),
  \qquad
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j^\mu(x)j^\nu(y)
  \ket{\vac}
  =
  0 .
\]
Coupling \(j^\mu\) to a background Abelian gauge field with
\(D_\mu=\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu\) gives the local operator identity
\[
  \partial_\mu j_A^\mu
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The one-loop contraction of
\(p_\mu\langle j_A^\mu j^\nu\rangle\) reduces, after Feynman
parametrization, to
\eqref{eq:two-dimensional-anomaly-shifted-integral}.  In dimensional
reduction the term with two loop momenta contains the factor
\((2-D)/D\), because only the physical two-dimensional gamma matrices
anticommute with \(\gamma\).  This evanescent factor multiplies the logarithmic
ultraviolet divergence of the integral and leaves the finite polynomial
\(-\pi^{-1}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}p_\mu\).  Fourier inversion gives the displayed
contact term.  The vector-current representative is fixed by subtracting the
local contact term that would otherwise violate the vector Ward identity.

Expanding the background-field insertion
\(\exp(\ii\int j^\mu A_\mu)\) to first order and using the contact term gives
\[
  \partial_\mu j_A^\mu(x)
  =
  \frac1{\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu A_\nu(x)
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}\epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}(x),
\]
where the last equality uses antisymmetry of \(\epsilon^{\mu\nu}\).
\end{proof}

\section{Contour Charges and Anomalous Charge Transport}

The contact term has a geometric interpretation.  In Euclidean two-space, a
conserved current \(j^\mu\) defines a charge on a closed contour \(C\),
\[
  Q_C=\int_C j^\mu n_\mu\,\dd s ,
\]
where \(n_\mu\) is the outward unit normal to the contour.  If
\(\partial_\mu j^\mu=0\) away from operator insertions, the contour can be
deformed through regions with no insertions.
Figure~\ref{fig:conserved-contour-charge-deformation} shows the allowed
contour deformation and the point at which an attempted deformation would cross
an operator insertion, where the contact term enters the Ward identity.

Using \eqref{eq:two-dimensional-axial-anomaly},
\begin{equation}
  \Delta Q_A
  =
  Q_{A,C_\infty}-\sum_iQ_{A,C_i}
  =
  \int_D\dd^2x\,\partial_\mu j_A^\mu
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}\int_D\dd^2x\,
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}.
  \label{eq:two-dimensional-anomalous-charge-shift}
\end{equation}
Therefore an axial rotation by \(\alpha\) multiplies the correlation function
by the background-dependent phase
\[
  \exp(\ii\alpha\Delta Q_A).
\]
Equivalently, in the background-field effective action the transformation
shifts the action by
\[
  \Delta S
  =
  \frac{\alpha}{2\pi}\int\dd^2x\,
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu}F_{\mu\nu}.
\]

The divergence of the axial insertion is the Fourier transform of a local
contact term.  In the dimensional-reduction implementation of the perturbative
regulator, set \(D=4-\epsilon\) and split the loop momentum as
\[
  \ell=\ell_\parallel+\ell_\perp ,
\]
where \(\ell_\parallel\) lies in the physical four-dimensional subspace.
Keep \(\gamma_5=-\ii\gamma^{0123}\) four-dimensional, so that
\(\not\ell_\parallel\) anticommutes with \(\gamma_5\) and
\(\not\ell_\perp\) commutes with \(\gamma_5\).  In the first triangle one uses
\[
  \not p\,\gamma_5
  =
  \bigl[(\not\ell+\not p)\gamma_5+\gamma_5\not\ell\bigr]
  -2\not\ell_\perp\gamma_5 .
\]
The two bracketed terms are adjacent inverse-propagator numerators.  In the two
oriented triangle graphs they reduce one fermion propagator and become a pair of
vector-current contact terms with opposite routing.  Here is the cancellation
explicitly for the leading routing in Figure
\ref{fig:four-dimensional-axial-vector-vector-triangle}.  Set
\[
  D_0=\ell^2,\qquad
  D_k=(\ell-k)^2,\qquad
  D_p=(\ell+p)^2 .
\]
The bracketed part of the first orientation gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal C^{\nu\rho}_1
  =
  -\ii\int\frac{\dd^D\ell}{(2\pi)^D}
  \bigg[
  &
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
      \gamma_5\not\ell\gamma^\nu(\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho
    \right]
  }{D_0D_k}
\\
  &+
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
      \gamma_5\gamma^\nu(\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho(\not\ell+\not p)
    \right]
  }{D_kD_p}
  \bigg].
\end{aligned}
\]
The first term came from the adjacent factor
\((\not\ell+\not p)^2=D_p\), after cyclically moving the first
\((\not\ell+\not p)\) to the end of the trace; the second came from
\(\not\ell^2=D_0\).  The bracketed part of the opposite orientation is
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal C^{\nu\rho}_2
  =
  -\ii\int\frac{\dd^D\ell}{(2\pi)^D}
  \bigg[
  &
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
      \gamma_5\not\ell\gamma^\rho
      (\not\ell+\not p+\not k)\gamma^\nu
    \right]
  }{\ell^2(\ell+p+k)^2}
\\
  &+
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
      \gamma_5\gamma^\rho
      (\not\ell+\not p+\not k)\gamma^\nu(\not\ell+\not p)
    \right]
  }{(\ell+p+k)^2D_p}
  \bigg].
\end{aligned}
\]
The dimensional-reduction regulator is defined so that the reduced
two-propagator integrals are translation invariant; this is the perturbative
implementation of the vector Ward identities.  In the second term of
\(\mathcal C_2\), set
\(\ell'=\ell+p+k\) and then rename \(\ell'\) as \(\ell\).  It has the same
denominator \(D_0D_k\) as the first term of \(\mathcal C_1\), while
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\operatorname{tr}\!\left[
    \gamma_5\not\ell\gamma^\nu(\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho
  \right]
  =
  4\ii\epsilon^{\alpha\nu\beta\rho}\ell_\alpha(\ell-k)_\beta,
\\
  &\operatorname{tr}\!\left[
    \gamma_5\gamma^\rho\not\ell\gamma^\nu(\not\ell-\not k)
  \right]
  =
  4\ii\epsilon^{\rho\alpha\nu\beta}\ell_\alpha(\ell-k)_\beta
  =
  -4\ii\epsilon^{\alpha\nu\beta\rho}\ell_\alpha(\ell-k)_\beta .
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus these two terms cancel.  In the first term of \(\mathcal C_2\), set
\(\ell'=\ell+k\).  It has the same denominator \(D_kD_p\) as the second term
of \(\mathcal C_1\), and
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\operatorname{tr}\!\left[
    \gamma_5\gamma^\nu(\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho(\not\ell+\not p)
  \right]
  =
  4\ii\epsilon^{\nu\alpha\rho\beta}
  (\ell-k)_\alpha(\ell+p)_\beta,
\\
  &\operatorname{tr}\!\left[
    \gamma_5(\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho(\not\ell+\not p)\gamma^\nu
  \right]
  =
  4\ii\epsilon^{\alpha\rho\beta\nu}
  (\ell-k)_\alpha(\ell+p)_\beta
\\
  &\hspace{3.6cm}
  =
  -4\ii\epsilon^{\nu\alpha\rho\beta}
  (\ell-k)_\alpha(\ell+p)_\beta .
\end{aligned}
\]
The bracketed contact terms therefore cancel pairwise once the vector Ward
identities are imposed by the regulator.  The entire axial contact term is
carried by the evanescent \(\ell_\perp\) contribution.  For the first
orientation this contribution is
\[
  2\ii
  \int\frac{\dd^D\ell}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{
    \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
      \gamma_5\not\ell_\perp\not\ell\gamma^\nu
      (\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho(\not\ell+\not p)
    \right]
  }{D_0D_kD_p}.
\]
The coefficient of the local two-vector contact term is obtained by keeping
the part with two external momenta and no physical loop momentum in the
\(\gamma_5\)-trace.  Terms with a symmetric pair of physical loop momenta
contracted into the antisymmetric tensor vanish after rotational averaging, and
terms with an unpaired perpendicular gamma matrix or the wrong number of
physical gamma matrices have zero \(\gamma_5\)-trace.  Using
\[
  \not\ell_\perp\not\ell_\perp=\ell_\perp^2,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}\!
  \left(\gamma_5\gamma^\nu\gamma^\alpha\gamma^\rho\gamma^\beta\right)
  =
  4\ii\epsilon^{\nu\alpha\rho\beta},
\]
one obtains
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \operatorname{tr}\!\left[
    \gamma_5\not\ell_\perp\not\ell\gamma^\nu
    (\not\ell-\not k)\gamma^\rho(\not\ell+\not p)
  \right]_{\rm local}
  &=
  -\ell_\perp^2 k_\alpha p_\beta
  \operatorname{tr}\!
  \left(\gamma_5\gamma^\nu\gamma^\alpha\gamma^\rho\gamma^\beta\right)
\\
  &=
  4\ii\,\ell_\perp^2
  \epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}k_\alpha p_\beta .
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus the first orientation contributes
\[
  -8\,\epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}k_\alpha p_\beta\,
  I(k,p),
  \qquad
  I(k,p):=
  \int\frac{\dd^D\ell}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{\ell_\perp^2}{D_0D_kD_p}.
\]
The opposite orientation gives the same local tensor after
\((\nu,k)\leftrightarrow(\rho,-p-k)\).

It remains to evaluate \(I(k,p)\).  Take the external momenta nonexceptional
during the ultraviolet computation; the contact coefficient extracted below is
independent of this infrared choice.  Feynman parametrization gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  I(k,p)
  &=
  2\int_0^1\dd x\int_0^1\dd y\int_0^1\dd z\,
  \delta(1-x-y-z)
  \int\frac{\dd^D r}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{r_\perp^2}{(r^2+\Delta)^{3}},
\\
  r&=\ell-y k+z p,
\\
  \Delta
  &=
  y(1-y)k^2+z(1-z)p^2+2yz\,k\cdot p-\ii0 .
\end{aligned}
\]
The shift does not change the perpendicular component because \(p\) and \(k\)
lie in the physical four-dimensional subspace.  In the continued
\(D=4-\epsilon\) dimensional rotation-invariant integral,
\[
  \int\dd^D r\,r_\perp^2 f(r^2)
  =
  \frac{D-4}{D}
  \int\dd^D r\,r^2 f(r^2)
  =
  -\frac{\epsilon}{4-\epsilon}
  \int\dd^D r\,r^2 f(r^2).
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \int\frac{\dd^D r}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{r_\perp^2}{(r^2+\Delta)^3}
  &=
  \frac{D-4}{D}
  \int\frac{\dd^D r}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{r^2}{(r^2+\Delta)^3}
\\
  &=
  \frac{D-4}{4}
  \frac{\Gamma(2-D/2)}{(4\pi)^{D/2}}
  \Delta^{D/2-2}
\\
  &=
  -\frac1{32\pi^2}+O(\epsilon).
\end{aligned}
\]
Since
\[
  2\int_0^1\dd x\int_0^1\dd y\int_0^1\dd z\,
  \delta(1-x-y-z)=1
\]
and
\(\Delta^{-\epsilon/2}=1+O(\epsilon)\), the required scalar integral is
\[
  I(k,p)
  =
  -\frac1{32\pi^2}.
\]
With the sign convention of \eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly}, this gives
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j_5^\mu(x)j^\nu(y)j^\rho(0)
  \ket{\vac}
  =
  \frac1{2\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}
  \partial_\alpha\delta^{(4)}(x)
  \partial_\beta\delta^{(4)}(y).
  \label{eq:four-dimensional-triangle-contact-term}
\end{equation}
The local coefficient is independent of the long-distance state; it is fixed
by the regulated short-distance singularity of the current product.

Coupling the vector current to a background field,
\[
  \Delta S=\int\dd^4x\,A_\mu j^\mu,
\]
and expanding the time-ordered exponential to second order, the contact term
\eqref{eq:four-dimensional-triangle-contact-term} yields
\[
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu
  =
  \frac1{16\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}
\]
for a unit-charge Dirac fermion in the convention of
\eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly}.  A fermion of electric charge \(q\) coupled
with electromagnetic coupling \(e\) replaces \(F\) by \(eqF\), giving the
factor \(e^2q^2\) in the next section.

\begin{proposition}[Four-dimensional axial contact term]
\label{prop:four-dimensional-axial-contact-term}
For a massless four-dimensional Dirac fermion, in the anomaly representative
of Definition~\ref{def:anomaly-representative-conventions}, the regulated
one-axial two-vector current product has local divergence
\[
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j_5^\mu(x)j^\nu(y)j^\rho(0)
  \ket{\vac}
  =
  \frac1{2\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}
  \partial_\alpha\delta^{(4)}(x)
  \partial_\beta\delta^{(4)}(y).
\]
Consequently, after coupling the vector current to a background Abelian field
and preserving the vector Ward identity, a unit-charge Dirac fermion obeys
\[
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu
  =
  \frac1{16\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}
\]
in the massless theory.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Contracting the axial vertex in the two oriented triangles writes the
numerator as vector-current contact terms plus the evanescent
\(\ell_\perp\) term.  Translation invariance of the dimensionally reduced
two-propagator integrals is precisely the regulator statement that the vector
Ward identities are preserved; with this prescription the vector-contact
terms cancel pairwise between the two orientations.  The remaining local
piece is
\[
  -8\,\epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}k_\alpha p_\beta I(k,p)
\]
from one orientation, with the opposite orientation contributing the
corresponding exchanged tensor.  The Feynman-parameter integral has the
regulator-independent local limit
\[
  I(k,p)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^{4-\epsilon}\ell}{(2\pi)^{4-\epsilon}}
  \frac{\ell_\perp^2}{\ell^2(\ell-k)^2(\ell+p)^2}
  =
  -\frac1{32\pi^2},
\]
because the factor \(D-4\) from \(\ell_\perp^2\) cancels the logarithmic
ultraviolet pole.  Substituting this value and Fourier transforming gives
\eqref{eq:four-dimensional-triangle-contact-term}.

The background-field identity follows by expanding the time-ordered
exponential to second order.  The factor \(1/2\) in the exponential is
cancelled by the two identical vector insertions in the contact term, and
antisymmetrizing the two derivatives of \(A\) rewrites the result as
\(\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}/(16\pi^2)\) in the
normalization of \eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly}.
\end{proof}

\section{The Abelian Axial Anomaly}

For a Dirac fermion of electric charge \(q\) coupled to an electromagnetic
field with coupling \(e\), the renormalized axial current may be chosen so
that
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu
  =
  2\ii m\,\bar\psi\gamma_5\psi
  +
  \frac{e^2 q^2}{16\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}.
  \label{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly}
\end{equation}
The coefficient is fixed by the short-distance singularity of the triangle
diagram with one axial-current insertion and two vector-current insertions.
The vector Ward identity is imposed because the vector current is the current
coupled to the gauge field.  The remaining local term appears in the axial
Ward identity.

Equation \eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly} is a statement about a
renormalized composite operator identity.  Each symbol in it has a prescribed
meaning: \(F_{\mu\nu}\) is the electromagnetic field strength,
\(\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\) is the four-dimensional antisymmetric symbol
with the orientation fixed at the start of the chapter,
\(\ii m\bar\psi\gamma_5\psi\) is the pseudoscalar mass-breaking operator in the
mostly-plus adjoint convention, and \(j_5^\mu\) is the renormalized current
whose vector-current Ward identities have been preserved.

The factor relating this component formula to the heat-kernel trace below is
as follows.  Define
\[
  \widetilde F^{\mu\nu}
  =
  \frac12\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\rho\sigma},
  \qquad
  F=\frac12F_{\mu\nu}\dd x^\mu\wedge\dd x^\nu .
\]
Then
\[
  F_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^{\mu\nu}
  =
  \frac12\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma},
  \qquad
  F\wedge F
  =
  \frac14\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}\,\dd^4x .
\]
The local heat-kernel trace
\eqref{eq:heat-kernel-anomaly-density} gives the index density
\((32\pi^2)^{-1}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
\operatorname{tr}_{R}(F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma})\).  The Dirac axial Ward
identity has twice this density because the chiral change of variables rotates
\(\psi\) and \(\bar\psi\) with the same sign, giving the prefactor \(2\ii\) in
\eqref{eq:fujikawa-regulated-trace}.  For a single charge-one Abelian Dirac
fermion, the representation trace is trivial and
\[
  2\cdot
  \frac{1}{32\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}
  =
  \frac{1}{16\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma},
\]
which is the coefficient in \eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly}; a field of
charge \(eq\) contributes the additional factor \(e^2q^2\).

\section{Measure Derivation}

The same coefficient is obtained from the Berezinian change of variables in a
regulated fermion path integral.  The construction uses the finite-dimensional
Berezin calculus of Chapter~\ref{ch:spinor-fields-fermionic-path-integrals};
the object that transforms is an ordered Berezinian density on a finite set of
odd coefficients, not a Borel measure on a set of fermion fields.

Work first on a compact Euclidean spin four-manifold, or in a finite box with
elliptic boundary conditions, so that the Euclidean Dirac operator
\[
  \mathcal D_A=\gamma^\mu(\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu)
\]
has a discrete \(L^2\)-orthonormal eigenbasis after the infrared regulator has
been fixed.  The local formula obtained below is independent of this
infrared auxiliary choice.  Let \(P_\Lambda\) be the spectral projection onto
the span
\[
  V_\Lambda
  =
  \operatorname{span}\{\varphi_n:\ |\lambda_n|\le \Lambda\},
  \qquad
  \mathcal D_A\varphi_n=\lambda_n\varphi_n .
\]
The regulated fermion variables are odd coordinates
\[
  \psi_\Lambda(x)=\sum_{n\in I_\Lambda} c_n\varphi_n(x),
  \qquad
  \bar\psi_\Lambda(x)=\sum_{n\in I_\Lambda}\bar c_n\varphi_n^\dagger(x),
\]
with ordered Berezinian density
\[
  \Omega_\Lambda
  =
  \prod_{n\in I_\Lambda} d\bar c_n\,dc_n .
\]
An infinitesimal local chiral rotation
\[
  \psi(x)\mapsto \ee^{\ii\beta(x)\gamma_5}\psi(x),
  \qquad
  \bar\psi(x)\mapsto \bar\psi(x)\ee^{\ii\beta(x)\gamma_5},
\]
acts on \(V_\Lambda\) by the finite matrix
\[
  U_{\Lambda,\beta}
  =
  1+\ii P_\Lambda\beta\gamma_5P_\Lambda+O(\beta^2),
\]
where \(\beta\) denotes the multiplication operator by the smooth function
\(\beta(x)\).  The inverse determinant in the odd Berezinian transformation
law gives, with the convention that \(J_\Lambda\) is the factor multiplying
the transformed finite-dimensional Berezin integral,
\[
  \log J_\Lambda
  =
  2\ii\,
  \operatorname{tr}_{V_\Lambda}
  (P_\Lambda\beta\gamma_5P_\Lambda)
  +O(\beta^2).
\]
The factor of \(2\) records that \(\psi\) and \(\bar\psi\) are rotated with
the same chiral sign.

The sharp projection \(P_\Lambda\) may be replaced, for the local asymptotic
calculation, by the smooth gauge-covariant spectral regulator
\[
  \exp(-\mathcal D_A^2/M^2).
\]
This is the heat-kernel regulator of the Berezinian trace.  It introduces no
regulator field; \(M\) is the ultraviolet scale in the heat semigroup of the
elliptic operator \(\mathcal D_A^2\).  In the terminology of
Table~\ref{tab:regulator-integration-status-catalog}, this is a spectral
regulator for an operator trace, not a Pauli--Villars auxiliary-field or
determinant-ratio regulator.  The regulated logarithm used below is
\begin{equation}
  \log J_M
  =
  2\ii\lim_{M\to\infty}
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \left[
  \beta\,\gamma_5
  \exp\left(-\mathcal D_A^2/M^2\right)
  \right],
  \label{eq:fujikawa-regulated-trace}
\end{equation}
where \(\operatorname{Tr}\) is the trace over spacetime, spin, and internal
indices.  Equation \eqref{eq:fujikawa-regulated-trace} is therefore a
regulated finite-mode Berezinian statement followed by a heat-kernel
asymptotic limit.  It is the measure-language counterpart of the
dimensionally regulated triangle computation above, in the scheme where the
vector gauge Ward identity is preserved and the axial Ward identity carries
the local term.

The heat-kernel expansion gives the local limit
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{M\to\infty}
  \operatorname{tr}_{\mathrm{spin}}
  \left[
  \gamma_5
  \exp\left(-\mathcal D_A^2/M^2\right)
  \right](x,x)
  =
  \frac{1}{32\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}(F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}),
  \label{eq:heat-kernel-anomaly-density}
\end{equation}
with \(\operatorname{tr}_{R}\) the trace in the fermion representation.  The
anomaly density is therefore the heat-kernel limit of the regulated
Berezinian traces of \(\gamma_5\) over cutoff eigenspaces.  Finite-dimensional
cyclic trace manipulations at fixed cutoff do not remove
\eqref{eq:heat-kernel-anomaly-density}, because the regulator is part of the
definition of the composite current.

The local heat-kernel density becomes a Fredholm index after the following
global data are specified.  The analytic input is the spin Dirac case of the
Atiyah--Singer index theorem.\footnote{M. F. Atiyah and
I. M. Singer, ``The index of elliptic operators. I,'' Ann. of Math. (2) 87
(1968), 484--530.  The boundary theorem used below is M. F. Atiyah,
V. K. Patodi, and I. M. Singer, ``Spectral asymmetry and Riemannian
geometry. I,'' Math. Proc. Cambridge Philos. Soc. 77 (1975), 43--69, with
Parts II and III in Math. Proc. Cambridge Philos. Soc. 78 (1975), 405--432
and 79 (1976), 71--99.}

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Spin Dirac index theorem, four-dimensional form]
\label{qthm:spin-dirac-index-four-dimensional}
Let \(M\) be a smooth compact oriented Riemannian spin four-manifold without
boundary.  Let \(S=S^+\oplus S^-\) be its complex spinor bundle, let
\(E_R\to M\) be a smooth Hermitian vector bundle associated to the fermion
representation \(R\), and let \(\nabla^E\) be a smooth unitary connection with
curvature \(F_R\) in the same gauge convention as the local anomaly
calculation.  The chiral Dirac operator
\[
  \mathcal D_{A,+}:
  \Gamma(S^+\otimes E_R)\longrightarrow
  \Gamma(S^-\otimes E_R)
\]
is elliptic and hence has finite-dimensional kernel and cokernel.  Its
Fredholm index is
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{index}\mathcal D_{A,+}
  =
  \dim\ker\mathcal D_{A,+}
  -
  \dim\ker\mathcal D_{A,-}
  =
  \int_M
  \bigl[\widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}(E_R,\nabla^E)\bigr]_4 ,
  \label{eq:dirac-index-as-form}
\end{equation}
where \([\cdots]_4\) denotes the four-form part of the Chern--Weil form,
\(\widehat A(TM)\) is the \(\widehat A\)-class of the tangent bundle, and
\(\operatorname{ch}(E_R,\nabla^E)\) is the Chern character form of the gauge
bundle.  The operator \(\mathcal D_{A,-}\) is the opposite-chirality Dirac
operator
\(\Gamma(S^-\otimes E_R)\to\Gamma(S^+\otimes E_R)\).
\end{quotedtheorem}

\paragraph{Index mechanism and four-form normalization.}
The quoted theorem imports the global equality between a Fredholm index and a
characteristic number.  The part used by the anomaly calculation can be made
more explicit.  Let
\[
  \mathcal D_A
  =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0&\mathcal D_{A,-}\\
    \mathcal D_{A,+}&0
  \end{pmatrix}
\]
be the self-adjoint Dirac operator on \(S\otimes E_R\), and let
\(\Gamma_5\) denote the Euclidean chirality operator, acting by \(+1\) on
\(S^+\) and by \(-1\) on \(S^-\).  For every \(t>0\), elliptic heat-kernel
theory makes \(\Gamma_5\ee^{-t\mathcal D_A^2}\) trace class, and the
McKean--Singer cancellation gives
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\Gamma_5\ee^{-t\mathcal D_A^2}\right)
  =
  \dim\ker\mathcal D_{A,+}
  -
  \dim\ker\mathcal D_{A,-}.
  \label{eq:dirac-index-mckean-singer-anomaly}
\end{equation}
Indeed, every positive eigenvector of
\(\mathcal D_{A,-}\mathcal D_{A,+}\) is paired by \(\mathcal D_{A,+}\) with a
positive eigenvector of \(\mathcal D_{A,+}\mathcal D_{A,-}\), with the same
eigenvalue and opposite chirality contribution.  The heat supertrace is
therefore independent of \(t\), and the index is the small-\(t\) integral of
the local supertrace coefficient.

The local coefficient is the analytic part of the index theorem.  In the
heat-kernel proof it is obtained from the Lichnerowicz formula, Chern--Weil
invariance, and Getzler rescaling of the Clifford variables.  The pure gauge
coefficient can be checked locally, and this check fixes the convention used
in the anomaly density.  Choose normal coordinates at a point \(x\), trivialize
\(E_R\) so that the connection one-form vanishes at \(x\), and write
\(\Gamma^{\mu\nu}=\frac12[\Gamma^\mu,\Gamma^\nu]\).  The gauge-curvature
endomorphism in the square of the Dirac operator is
\[
  \mathcal E_F(x)
  =
  \frac12\Gamma^{\mu\nu}F_{R,\mu\nu}(x).
\]
Terms with fewer than four Clifford matrices have zero
\(\Gamma_5\)-supertrace.  The first pure gauge term that survives in the
diagonal heat-kernel parametrix is therefore
\[
  (4\pi t)^{-2}
  \frac{t^2}{2}
  \operatorname{tr}_{\mathrm{spin},R}
  \left(\Gamma_5\mathcal E_F^2\right).
\]
Using
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_{\mathrm{spin}}
  \left(\Gamma_5\Gamma^{\mu\nu}\Gamma^{\rho\sigma}\right)
  =
  4\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma},
  \qquad \epsilon^{0123}=+1,
\]
one obtains
\[
  (4\pi)^{-2}\frac12\frac14\,
  4\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_R(F_{R,\mu\nu}F_{R,\rho\sigma})
  =
  \frac{1}{32\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_R(F_{R,\mu\nu}F_{R,\rho\sigma}).
\]
The spin-curvature terms in the same Getzler expansion assemble with the
pure gauge term into the Chern--Weil form
\(\widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}(E_R,\nabla^E)\).  With the chapter's
anti-Hermitian curvature convention
\(\mathsf F_R=-\ii F_R\), the Chern character appearing in
Theorem~\ref{qthm:spin-dirac-index-four-dimensional} is
\[
  \operatorname{ch}(E_R,\nabla^E)
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \exp\!\left(\frac{\ii\mathsf F_R}{2\pi}\right)
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \exp\!\left(\frac{F_R}{2\pi}\right).
\]
Consequently the gauge part of the four-form is
\begin{equation}
  \bigl[\operatorname{ch}(E_R,\nabla^E)\bigr]_4
  =
  \frac1{2(2\pi)^2}\operatorname{tr}_R(F_R\wedge F_R)
  =
  \frac1{32\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_R(F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma})\,\dd^4x .
  \label{eq:dirac-index-four-form-normalization}
\end{equation}
The first equality is Chern--Weil algebra; the second uses
\(F_R=\frac12F_{\mu\nu}\dd x^\mu\wedge\dd x^\nu\) and the orientation
\(\epsilon^{0123}=+1\).  This explains why the local Berezinian density
\eqref{eq:heat-kernel-anomaly-density} and the global Fredholm index have the
same coefficient once the closed spin hypotheses of the theorem are imposed.

On a flat four-torus or on \(\mathbb R^4\) after compactification by a smooth
finite-action connection for which no boundary contribution is present, the
tangent-bundle factor contributes only \(1\), and the gauge part of
\eqref{eq:dirac-index-as-form} becomes the density computed in
\eqref{eq:heat-kernel-anomaly-density}:
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{index}\mathcal D_{A,+}
  =
  n_+-n_-
  =
  \frac1{32\pi^2}
  \int_M
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}(F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma})\,\dd^4x .
  \label{eq:dirac-index-anomaly}
\end{equation}
Here \(n_+=\dim\ker\mathcal D_{A,+}\) and
\(n_-=\dim\ker\mathcal D_{A,-}\), equivalently the numbers of zero modes of
chirality \(+1\) and \(-1\) in the convention of this chapter.  The equality
between the integrated heat-kernel density and this integer uses the global
hypotheses in the theorem.  Without those hypotheses the local Ward identity
still has the density \eqref{eq:heat-kernel-anomaly-density}, but its integral
is not by itself a Fredholm index.

If \(M\) has a boundary, assume first that the Riemannian metric and the gauge
connection have product form on a collar \(u\in[0,\varepsilon)\) of
\(\partial M\).  In this collar the chiral Dirac operator can be written
\[
  \mathcal D_A
  =
  \gamma(n)\bigl(\partial_u+B_A\bigr),
\]
where \(n\) is the outward unit normal and \(B_A\) is the self-adjoint
tangential Dirac operator on \(\partial M\).  Let
\[
  \eta_{B_A}(s)
  =
  \sum_{\lambda\in\operatorname{Spec}(B_A)\setminus\{0\}}
  \operatorname{sign}(\lambda)|\lambda|^{-s}
\]
for \(\operatorname{Re}s\) large, continued meromorphically to \(s=0\), and
let \(h_{B_A}=\dim\ker B_A\).  With the APS boundary condition
\(P_{\geq0}\psi|_{\partial M}=0\), where \(P_{\geq0}\) is the spectral
projection of \(B_A\) onto nonnegative eigenvalues, the Atiyah--Patodi--Singer
theorem gives
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{index}\mathcal D^{\mathrm{APS}}_{A,+}
  =
  \int_M
  \bigl[\widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}(E_R,\nabla^E)\bigr]_4
  -
  \frac{\eta_{B_A}(0)+h_{B_A}}{2}.
  \label{eq:aps-index-boundary}
\end{equation}
For non-product data one either deforms to product form near the boundary, or
includes the corresponding local transgression term; the sum of the bulk,
local boundary, and spectral terms is the invariant integer.  The appearance
of \(\eta_{B_A}\) shows that boundary or interface anomaly data include
spectral terms in addition to the bulk density.  Boundary extension,
global-anomaly, and inflow applications use \eqref{eq:aps-index-boundary};
the local Ward identity in the present chapter uses only the bulk density
before this spectral correction is imposed.

\section{Nonabelian Flavor and Gauge Anomalies}

\begin{definition}[Trace anomaly coefficient]
\label{def:trace-anomaly-coefficient}
For several Weyl fermions, let \(T^A\) be a generator of a global symmetry
current and let \(t^a,t^b\) be generators of gauge or background gauge fields.
The Hermitian generator and curvature conventions are those stated in
\eqref{eq:ym-lie-algebra-conventions}, \eqref{eq:ym-trace-kappa}, and
\eqref{eq:ym-lagrangian-density}; in particular the default basis has
\(\operatorname{tr}(t^a t^b)=\delta^{ab}\).  The mixed anomaly coefficient is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A^{Aab}
  =
  \frac12
  \left[
  \operatorname{tr}_{\mathrm{left}}
  \bigl(T^A\{t^a,t^b\}\bigr)
  -
  \operatorname{tr}_{\mathrm{right}}
  \bigl(T^A\{t^a,t^b\}\bigr)
  \right].
  \label{eq:flavor-anomaly-coefficient}
\end{equation}
With background field strengths \(F^a_{\mu\nu}\), the corresponding current
identity has the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu J_A^\mu
  =
  \frac1{32\pi^2}
  \mathcal A^{Aab}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  F^a_{\mu\nu}F^b_{\rho\sigma}
  +
  \text{explicit breaking terms}.
  \label{eq:nonabelian-flavor-anomaly}
\end{equation}
The factor \(1/2\) in \eqref{eq:flavor-anomaly-coefficient} is part of the
definition of \(\mathcal A^{Aab}\); it compensates for the two identical vector
insertions in the anticommutator.  For a single Dirac fermion with an axial
generator acting by \(+1\) on the left-handed component and \(-1\) on the
right-handed component, and with a unit Abelian vector charge, this definition
gives \(\mathcal A=2\), so \eqref{eq:nonabelian-flavor-anomaly} reduces to the
coefficient \(1/(16\pi^2)\) in \eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Cubic gauge obstruction]
\label{prop:cubic-gauge-anomaly-obstruction}
For left-handed Weyl fermions in representations \(R_i\) of a gauge group,
the cubic part of the one-loop local consistent gauge anomaly vanishes if and
only if
\begin{equation}
  \sum_i
  \operatorname{tr}_{R_i}
  \bigl(t^a\{t^b,t^c\}\bigr)
  =
  0
  \qquad
  \text{for all }a,b,c.
  \label{eq:gauge-anomaly-cancellation}
\end{equation}
If this tensor is nonzero for a dynamical gauge symmetry, no local
counterterm can make the corresponding one-loop effective action satisfy the
Slavnov--Taylor identity in that background-field chart.  If the tensor
vanishes, this cubic local obstruction is absent; maintaining the full
Slavnov--Taylor identities then reduces to the absence of the remaining
BRST-anomaly classes in the chosen theory and regulator scheme.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof from local descent]
The obstruction is computed in the perturbative local BRST bicomplex on a
contractible coordinate chart of the bundle of background connections.  The
anti-Hermitian curvature \(\mathsf F=\mathsf F^a t^a\) is an even two-form,
so the exterior products \(\mathsf F^a\mathsf F^b\mathsf F^c\) are symmetric
under interchange of \(a,b,c\).  Therefore the degree-three invariant
polynomial of one left-handed Weyl fermion depends on the representation only
through the symmetric cubic tensor:
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}(\mathsf F^3)
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}(t^{(a}t^bt^{c)})
  \mathsf F^a\mathsf F^b\mathsf F^c .
\]
Equivalently, after one gauge variation has been converted into a ghost and
two curvatures are read as external gauge-field insertions, the same tensor is
written as
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}\bigl(t^a\{t^b,t^c\}\bigr)
\]
up to the conventional normalization already fixed in
\eqref{eq:flavor-anomaly-coefficient}.  Right-handed Weyl fermions contribute
with the opposite sign because the chirality projector entering the regulated
fermion determinant is reversed.

The descent map sends the invariant polynomial to a local representative
\(I_4^{(1)}\in\Omega_{\rm loc}^{4,1}\):
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}(\mathsf F^3)=\dd I_5^{(0)},
  \qquad
  s I_5^{(0)}=\dd I_4^{(1)} .
\]
Changing the subtraction convention for the one-loop effective action adds a
local counterterm \(B_4^{(0)}\), and the anomaly representative shifts by
\[
  I_4^{(1)}
  \longmapsto
  I_4^{(1)}+sB_4^{(0)}+\dd B_3^{(1)} .
\]
Thus counterterms change representatives inside the same class of
\(H^{1,4}(s\mid\dd)\).  The cohomological input used here is the local
BRST-classification theorem: for four-dimensional chiral Yang--Mills
theories in this polynomial local setting, the semisimple cubic gauge part of
\(H^{1,4}(s\mid\dd)\) is generated by the descent classes of the degree-three
invariant polynomials \(\operatorname{tr}_{R}(\mathsf F^3)\).  The vanishing
of the total symmetric cubic tensor therefore removes precisely this local
class, while a nonzero tensor gives a class outside the image of local
counterterm variations.

The non-removability of a nonzero cubic tensor can also be seen without using
the full local BRST classification.  Restrict the background to a maximal
torus \(T\) with Lie algebra \(\mathfrak t\).  Since an invariant polynomial
on a complex semisimple Lie algebra is determined by its restriction to a
Cartan subalgebra, a nonzero cubic invariant has a nonzero restriction to
\(\mathfrak t\).  Indeed, regular semisimple elements are conjugate to
\(\mathfrak t\) and form a Zariski-dense subset; an invariant polynomial that
vanishes on \(\mathfrak t\) therefore vanishes on all regular semisimple
elements and hence identically.  Thus a nonzero semisimple cubic gauge
anomaly can be detected after Abelianizing to commuting gauge fields
\[
  A=A^i H_i,\qquad F^i=\dd A^i .
\]
On this Abelianized chart the cubic anomaly representative has the form
\[
  \int_M C_{i;jk}\lambda^i F^jF^k,
  \qquad C_{i;jk}=C_{i;kj},
\]
and its invariant cubic coordinate is the completely symmetric part of
\(C_{i;jk}\).  The most general cubic local counterterm that can change this
\(\lambda F F\) coefficient has, modulo gauge-invariant terms and total
derivatives, the form
\[
  B_4
  =
  \frac12 H_{ij,k}A^iA^jF^k,
  \qquad H_{ij,k}=-H_{ji,k}.
\]
Using \(\delta_\lambda A^i=\dd\lambda^i\), \(\delta_\lambda F^i=0\), and
\(\dd F^i=0\), its variation on a closed spacetime is
\[
  \delta_\lambda\!\int_M B_4
  =
  -\int_M
  H_{ij,k}\lambda^i F^jF^k .
\]
Since \(F^jF^k=F^kF^j\), the effective shift of the coefficient symmetric in
the two curvature labels is
\[
  \Delta C_{i;jk}
  =
  -\frac12\bigl(H_{ij,k}+H_{ik,j}\bigr).
\]
The complete symmetrization over \(i,j,k\) vanishes identically because
\(H\) is antisymmetric in its first two labels.  Therefore local
counterterms cannot change the fully symmetric Cartan coefficient of the
Abelianized anomaly.  By the Cartan-restriction argument above, a nonzero
semisimple cubic invariant tensor cannot be removed by a local counterterm.
This elementary Abelianized calculation is the part of the obstruction that
the anomaly-matching and WZW sections use repeatedly.

The cubic gauge part of the anomaly polynomial for a left-handed Weyl fermion
in \(R_i\) is proportional to
\(\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}(\mathsf F^3)\).  Expanding
\(\mathsf F=\mathsf F^a t^a\), the coefficient of
\(\mathsf F^a\mathsf F^b\mathsf F^c\) is the completely symmetric tensor
\(\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}(t^{(a}t^b t^{c)})\), equivalently
\(\frac16\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}\) summed over all permutations.  In component
Ward identities the same tensor is written as
\(\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}(t^a\{t^b,t^c\})\), with the conventional factor
absorbed into the anomaly coefficient.  Summing over fermions gives
\eqref{eq:gauge-anomaly-cancellation}.

A local counterterm changes the anomaly representative by a BRST coboundary.
The Abelianized counterterm calculation above already proves that a nonzero
semisimple cubic invariant tensor cannot be removed by changing local
counterterms.  The remaining use of local BRST cohomology is the converse
classification statement: in the polynomial local perturbative Yang--Mills
setting, once this invariant-polynomial coefficient vanishes, the semisimple
cubic gauge-anomaly class itself is absent.  Other anomaly classes, global
anomaly holonomies, and regulator-specific Slavnov--Taylor restoration
conditions are separate data.  The last sentence is therefore not an
all-purpose nonperturbative existence theorem; it is the perturbative BRST
consistency reduction in a specified gauge-fixed regularized framework.
\end{proof}

\section{Consistency Condition and Descent}

The anomaly of a gauge symmetry is constrained before any coefficient is
computed.  Let \(W[A]\) be the renormalized effective action for a background
gauge field \(A\).  Under an infinitesimal gauge transformation with parameter
\(\zeta=\zeta^a t^a\), write
\[
  \delta_\zeta W[A]=\mathcal A(\zeta,A).
\]
Here the infinitesimal action on a functional \(F[A]\) is the derivation
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\zeta F[A]
  =
  \int_M \dd^4x\,
  \frac{\delta F}{\delta A_\mu^a(x)}
  \,(D_\mu\zeta)^a(x),
  \qquad
  D_\mu\zeta=\partial_\mu\zeta+[A_\mu,\zeta]_{\mathrm H},
  \label{eq:gauge-variation-functional-derivation}
\end{equation}
understood either after introducing a regulator that makes the functional
derivatives ordinary finite-dimensional derivatives, or in the formal local
functional calculus.  The gauge parameters are external test functions and
are not varied by \(\delta_{\zeta_i}\).  With these conventions the
variations close on the connection:
\begin{equation}
  [\delta_{\zeta_1},\delta_{\zeta_2}]A_\mu
  =
  D_\mu[\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}.
  \label{eq:gauge-variation-closure-on-connection}
\end{equation}
Indeed,
\[
  \delta_{\zeta_1}\delta_{\zeta_2}A_\mu
  -
  \delta_{\zeta_2}\delta_{\zeta_1}A_\mu
  =
  [D_\mu\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}
  -
  [D_\mu\zeta_2,\zeta_1]_{\mathrm H},
\]
and expanding \(D_\mu\) gives
\[
  [\partial_\mu\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}
  +
  [\zeta_1,\partial_\mu\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}
  +
  [[A_\mu,\zeta_1]_{\mathrm H},\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}
  +
  [\zeta_1,[A_\mu,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}]_{\mathrm H}
  =
  \partial_\mu[\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}
  +[A_\mu,[\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}]_{\mathrm H},
\]
where the last equality is the Jacobi identity.  Equation
\eqref{eq:gauge-variation-closure-on-connection} implies, by the chain rule,
\[
  [\delta_{\zeta_1},\delta_{\zeta_2}]F[A]
  =
  \delta_{[\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H}}F[A]
\]
for every functional \(F\) in the same differentiability class.
Applying this identity to \(W[A]\), and using
\(\mathcal A(\zeta,A)=\delta_\zeta W[A]\), gives
\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\zeta_1}\mathcal A(\zeta_2,A)
  -
  \delta_{\zeta_2}\mathcal A(\zeta_1,A)
  =
  \mathcal A([\zeta_1,\zeta_2]_{\mathrm H},A).
  \label{eq:wess-zumino-consistency}
\end{equation}
This is the Wess--Zumino consistency condition.  It is an integrability
condition: a proposed local expression for \(\mathcal A\) is the gauge
variation of a single effective action only if its first variations satisfy
the gauge-algebra commutator above.  In BRST language, replace \(\zeta\) by
the ghost \(c\).  The anomaly is a local ghost-number-one functional
\(\mathcal A(c,A)\) obeying
\begin{equation}
  s\mathcal A=0,
  \label{eq:brst-anomaly-cocycle}
\end{equation}
and adding a local counterterm \(X[A]\) shifts it by
\[
  \mathcal A\longmapsto \mathcal A+sX .
\]
Thus the possible local gauge anomalies are cohomology classes of the BRST
differential at ghost number one.

The local form of this statement is the relative cohomology described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-18}.  Let
\[
  \Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{p,q}
\]
be the vector space of local \(p\)-forms of ghost number \(q\), built from the
background connection, the ghost, and finitely many derivatives, with
coefficients that are polynomial or formal power series in the fields in the
chosen local coordinate chart.  In the abstract cohomological notation
\(H(s\mid d)\), write \(d\) for the spacetime exterior derivative; in the
connection formulas below the same exterior derivative is denoted \(\dd\).
The derivative \(d\) has bidegree \((1,0)\),
\[
  d:\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{p,q}\longrightarrow
  \Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{p+1,q},
\]
and the BRST differential has bidegree \((0,1)\),
\[
  s:\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{p,q}\longrightarrow
  \Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{p,q+1}.
\]
Both are odd with respect to total degree, and with the sign convention used
here
\begin{equation}
  d^2=0,\qquad s^2=0,\qquad sd+ds=0 .
  \label{eq:descent-bicomplex-differentials}
\end{equation}
Thus \(\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{\bullet,\bullet}\) is a bicomplex.  For a
four-dimensional integrated anomaly, choose a representative
\[
  a_4^{(1)}\in\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{4,1}.
\]
It is a relative cocycle when there exists
\(a_3^{(2)}\in\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{3,2}\) such that
\begin{equation}
  s a_4^{(1)}-d a_3^{(2)}=0 .
  \label{eq:relative-anomaly-cocycle}
\end{equation}
Two representatives define the same relative class when there exist
\(b_4^{(0)}\in\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{4,0}\) and
\(b_3^{(1)}\in\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{3,1}\) such that
\begin{equation}
  a_4^{(1)}
  \sim
  a_4^{(1)}+s b_4^{(0)}+d b_3^{(1)} .
  \label{eq:relative-anomaly-coboundary}
\end{equation}
The quotient of cocycles by this equivalence relation is
\[
  H^{1,4}(s\mid d).
\]

\begin{proposition}[Local anomalies as relative BRST classes]
\label{prop:local-anomalies-relative-brst-classes}
At the level of local functionals, a four-dimensional consistent gauge anomaly
with local density \(a_4^{(1)}\) determines a class
\([a_4^{(1)}]\in H^{1,4}(s\mid d)\).  Changing the renormalized effective
action by a local counterterm changes \(a_4^{(1)}\) by the coboundary
\eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-coboundary}.  Conversely, a representative of
\(H^{1,4}(s\mid d)\) defines a local functional whose BRST variation obeys
the infinitesimal Wess--Zumino consistency condition.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write the anomalous variation as
\[
  \delta_\zeta W[A]=\int_M a_4^{(1)}(\zeta,A).
\]
Replacing the even parameter \(\zeta\) by the odd ghost gives
\(\int_M a_4^{(1)}(c,A)\).  The BRST differential \(s\) is the
Chevalley--Eilenberg differential for infinitesimal gauge transformations,
with the field-dependent term in \(s c=-\frac12[c,c]\) included.  Equation
\eqref{eq:wess-zumino-consistency} is precisely the statement that this
ghost-number-one integrated functional is a Chevalley--Eilenberg cocycle,
hence
\[
  s\int_M a_4^{(1)}=0 .
\]
For a local density this means that \(s a_4^{(1)}\) integrates to zero for all
compactly supported field and ghost configurations.  In the local variational
bicomplex, the algebraic Poincare lemma identifies such local top forms with
total derivatives modulo globally topological terms fixed separately by the
chosen bundle sector.  Thus there exists
\(a_3^{(2)}\in\Omega_{\rm loc}^{3,2}\) with
\[
  s a_4^{(1)}=d a_3^{(2)},
\]
which is \eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-cocycle}.  If \(W[A]\) is changed by a
local counterterm \(\int_M b_4^{(0)}\), its anomalous variation changes by
\[
  s\int_M b_4^{(0)}
  =
  \int_M s b_4^{(0)} ,
\]
and adding an identically integrated total derivative changes the local
representative by \(d b_3^{(1)}\).  This is exactly the equivalence
\eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-coboundary}; it is the statement that a finite
local counterterm changes the representative but not the relative cohomology
class.

Conversely, if \eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-cocycle} holds, then on a closed
spacetime, or with boundary conditions for which the boundary integral is part
of the specified inflow problem, the integrated BRST variation of
\(\int_M a_4^{(1)}\) vanishes.  Expanding \(s\) in ghosts recovers the
antisymmetrized commutator of two ordinary gauge variations, while
\(s c=-\frac12[c,c]\) supplies the term with gauge parameter
\([\zeta_1,\zeta_2]\).  This is exactly the infinitesimal
Wess--Zumino consistency condition \eqref{eq:wess-zumino-consistency}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Finite-regulator scheme meaning of the class.}
The relative class above is the infinitesimal local shadow of a finite
regulator statement.  Let \(\mathcal B_\Lambda\) be a regulated space of
background fields, let \(\mathcal G_\Lambda\) be the regulated background
gauge group acting on the right, \(A\mapsto A^g\), and suppose a local chart
of the regulated determinant line has been chosen so that the fermion
functional is written as a scalar \(W_\Lambda[A]\).  Its finite anomaly
cochain is
\begin{equation}
  C_\Lambda(g;A)
  =
  W_\Lambda[A^g]-W_\Lambda[A].
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-anomaly-cochain}
\end{equation}
At this stage the statement is an identity in the regulated chart; continuum
content enters after locality, convergence, and counterterm estimates have
been supplied.
Functoriality of the gauge action gives the exact groupoid cocycle equation
\begin{equation}
  C_\Lambda(gh;A)
  =
  C_\Lambda(h;A^g)+C_\Lambda(g;A),
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-anomaly-cocycle}
\end{equation}
with the order fixed by \((A^g)^h=A^{gh}\).  Differentiating
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-anomaly-cocycle} at
\(g=\exp(t\zeta_1)\), \(h=\exp(t\zeta_2)\), and then taking the local
continuum representative gives the Wess--Zumino condition
\eqref{eq:wess-zumino-consistency}; replacing the even parameters by the
ghost gives the relative BRST cocycle
\eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-cocycle}.

Changing the regulated Lagrangian by a finite local counterterm
\(B_\Lambda[A]\), or choosing a determinant-line frame whose scalar logarithm
differs by \(B_\Lambda[A]\), replaces \(W_\Lambda\) by
\(W_\Lambda+B_\Lambda\) and hence
\begin{equation}
  C_\Lambda(g;A)
  \longmapsto
  C_\Lambda(g;A)+B_\Lambda[A^g]-B_\Lambda[A].
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-anomaly-coboundary}
\end{equation}
The infinitesimal local density changes by
\(a_4^{(1)}\mapsto a_4^{(1)}+s b_4^{(0)}+d b_3^{(1)}\), exactly as in
\eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-coboundary}.  Thus regulator and subtraction
choices that remain within the same local background-field scheme change the
representative, not the relative cohomology class.  A nonzero local class
means that no local counterterm in that perturbative gauge-fixed scheme can
turn the regulated determinant chart into a gauge-invariant scalar effective
action.  The statement is local and perturbative: vanishing of this class is
the condition that removes this obstruction, while an order-by-order
construction of a gauge theory must still build the counterterms and verify
the Slavnov--Taylor or BV quantum master identities in the chosen regulator.
Flat global holonomies of the determinant or Pfaffian line are separate
finite-background data and are treated in
Chapter~\ref{ch:curved-eta-invariants-global-anomalies}.

For four-dimensional chiral fermions, this relative cohomology class is
represented by the descent of a six-form anomaly polynomial.  To state the
normalization without hiding convention-dependent factors, introduce the
anti-Hermitian connection
\[
  \mathsf A=-\ii A,
  \qquad
  \mathsf F=\dd\mathsf A+\mathsf A^2=-\ii F ,
\]
where \(A\) and \(F\) are the Hermitian gauge potential and curvature used in
the Yang--Mills chapters.  Let \(\mathsf c\) be the corresponding odd
anti-Hermitian ghost.  On the descent variables,
\begin{equation}
  s\mathsf A=\dd\mathsf c+[\mathsf A,\mathsf c],
  \qquad
  s\mathsf c=-\mathsf c^2,
  \qquad
  s\mathsf F=[\mathsf F,\mathsf c].
  \label{eq:descent-brst-connection}
\end{equation}
Products of differential forms below include both matrix multiplication and
wedge product with total-degree Koszul signs.  For a left-handed Weyl fermion
in a representation \(R\), define the six-form
\[
  I_6^R
  =
  \kappa\,\operatorname{tr}_R(\mathsf F^3),
  \qquad
  \kappa=\frac{\ii}{24\pi^2}
\]
in the Euclidean effective-action convention.  Removing the overall Euclidean
phase gives the corresponding Lorentzian Ward-identity normalization.  A
right-handed Weyl fermion contributes with the opposite sign.  For a set of
left- and right-handed Weyl fermions,
\[
  I_6
  =
  \kappa
  \left[
    \sum_{i\in L}\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}(\mathsf F^3)
    -
    \sum_{j\in R}\operatorname{tr}_{R_j}(\mathsf F^3)
  \right].
\]

The Chern--Simons five-form \(I_5^{(0)}\) is any local form satisfying
\[
  I_6=\dd I_5^{(0)}.
\]
For one left-handed representation \(R\), a standard representative is
\[
  I_{5,R}^{(0)}
  =
  \kappa\,\operatorname{tr}_R
  \left(
    \mathsf A(\dd\mathsf A)^2
    +
    \frac32\mathsf A^3\dd\mathsf A
    +
    \frac35\mathsf A^5
  \right),
  \qquad
  \dd I_{5,R}^{(0)}=I_6^R .
\]
The numerical coefficients in this representative are fixed by the
Chern--Weil homotopy, not by convention.  Set
\[
  \mathsf A_t=t\mathsf A,\qquad
  \mathsf F_t=\dd\mathsf A_t+\mathsf A_t^2
  =t\,\dd\mathsf A+t^2\mathsf A^2 .
\]
For the invariant polynomial
\(P(X)=\kappa\,\operatorname{tr}_R(X^3)\), the Bianchi identity for
\(\mathsf F_t\) and cyclicity of the trace give
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd t}P(\mathsf F_t)
  =
  3\kappa\,\dd\,\operatorname{tr}_R(\mathsf A\,\mathsf F_t^2).
\]
Since \(\mathsf F_0=0\), integration in \(t\) gives
\[
  I_{5,R}^{(0)}
  =
  3\kappa\int_0^1
  \operatorname{tr}_R\!\left[
    \mathsf A
    \bigl(t\,\dd\mathsf A+t^2\mathsf A^2\bigr)^2
  \right]\dd t .
\]
Expanding the square and using cyclicity with the form degrees
\(\deg\mathsf A=1\), \(\deg\dd\mathsf A=2\), one obtains
\[
\begin{aligned}
  I_{5,R}^{(0)}
  =
  \kappa\,\operatorname{tr}_R
  \biggl[
    &3\!\int_0^1 t^2\dd t\,
      \mathsf A(\dd\mathsf A)^2
\\
    &+3\!\int_0^1 t^3\dd t\,
      \mathsf A\bigl((\dd\mathsf A)\mathsf A^2
      +\mathsf A^2\dd\mathsf A\bigr)
    +3\!\int_0^1 t^4\dd t\,\mathsf A^5
  \biggr]
\\
  =
  \kappa\,\operatorname{tr}_R
  \left(
    \mathsf A(\dd\mathsf A)^2
    +\frac32\mathsf A^3\dd\mathsf A
    +\frac35\mathsf A^5
  \right).
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus the factors \(1,\frac32,\frac35\) are the explicit transgression
integrals \(3\int_0^1t^2\dd t\),
\(3\int_0^1 2t^3\dd t\), and
\(3\int_0^1t^4\dd t\).  They use only the fixed trace convention and the
matrix-valued differential-form product.  A different path of connections or
a different Chern--Simons representative changes \(I_5^{(0)}\) by an exact
form and hence changes \(I_4^{(1)}\) only by a relative coboundary of the
kind already displayed in \eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-coboundary}.
Because \(sI_6=0\) and \(I_6=\dd I_5^{(0)}\),
\[
  \dd(sI_5^{(0)})=-s(\dd I_5^{(0)})=-sI_6=0 .
\]
The algebraic Poincare lemma on local forms then gives the first descent
equation
\begin{equation}
  s I_5^{(0)}
  =
  \dd I_4^{(1)}(\mathsf c,\mathsf A).
  \label{eq:first-brst-descent}
\end{equation}
Applying \(s\) again and using \eqref{eq:descent-bicomplex-differentials}
gives a second descent equation
\begin{equation}
  s I_4^{(1)}
  =
  \dd I_3^{(2)}
  \label{eq:second-brst-descent}
\end{equation}
for some \(I_3^{(2)}\in\Omega_{\mathrm{loc}}^{3,2}\).  Hence
\(I_4^{(1)}\) is a representative of \(H^{1,4}(s\mid d)\) in the convention
of \eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-cocycle}.  Replacing the ghost \(\mathsf c\) by
an even infinitesimal parameter \(\lambda\) gives the ordinary gauge variation
\[
  \delta_\lambda \mathsf A=\dd\lambda+[\mathsf A,\lambda],
  \qquad
  \delta_\lambda I_5^{(0)}
  =
  \dd I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A).
\]
In the same left-handed convention,
\[
  I_{4,R}^{(1)}(\mathsf c,\mathsf A)
  =
  \kappa\,\operatorname{tr}_R
  \left[
    \mathsf c\,\dd
    \left(
      \mathsf A\,\dd\mathsf A+\frac12\mathsf A^3
    \right)
  \right],
\]
up to adding \(\dd\)-exact terms and BRST-exact local counterterm variations.
The relative coefficient \(\frac12\) in this four-form is the corresponding
homotopy coefficient \(6\int_0^1(1-t)t^2\dd t=\frac12\); the coefficient of
\(\mathsf A\dd\mathsf A\) is \(6\int_0^1(1-t)t\dd t=1\).  This is the same
descent calculation as above, now evaluated on the vertical gauge direction
\(\delta_\lambda\mathsf A=\dd\lambda+[\mathsf A,\lambda]\).  The displayed
\(\mathsf c\,\dd(\cdots)\) representative is chosen because its integrated
form pairs directly with the effective-action variation
\eqref{eq:consistent-descent-anomaly-representative}; the equivalent
\(\dd\mathsf c\)-representatives differ from it by exact local forms.
The consistent anomaly is
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\lambda W[\mathsf A]
  =
  \int_M I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A).
  \label{eq:consistent-descent-anomaly-representative}
\end{equation}
The cancellation condition \eqref{eq:gauge-anomaly-cancellation} is precisely
the vanishing of the six-form polynomial \(I_6\) for the gauged symmetry, or
equivalently the vanishing of the symmetric cubic tensor
\[
  d^{abc}
  =
  \sum_{i\in L}\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}
  \bigl(t^{(a}t^b t^{c)}\bigr)
  -
  \sum_{j\in R}\operatorname{tr}_{R_j}
  \bigl(t^{(a}t^b t^{c)}\bigr).
\]
If this polynomial is nonzero only for a background field coupled to a global
symmetry, it is an anomaly to be matched rather than an inconsistency.

The representative \(I_4^{(1)}\) is not the invariant datum by itself.  A
local four-dimensional counterterm can move the anomaly among the currents,
or equivalently change the Chern--Simons representative \(I_5^{(0)}\) by a
local transgression.  In an Abelianized chart with several background
one-forms \(A^a\), the elementary Bardeen counterterm
\[
  B_4
  =
  h_{ab,c}\,A^aA^bF^c,
  \qquad h_{ab,c}=-h_{ba,c},
\]
changes a representative written as
\[
  I_4^{(1)}
  =
  d_{abc}\,\lambda^aF^bF^c
\]
to one with
\[
  d'_{abc}
  =
  d_{abc}
  +
  \frac12\bigl(h_{ab,c}+h_{ac,b}\bigr).
\]
The completely symmetric part is unchanged:
\[
  d'_{(abc)}=d_{(abc)}.
\]
This finite algebra is the local form of the statement that counterterms
alter representatives but not the six-form polynomial \(I_6\).  Consequently,
when the next chapter matches the pion Wess--Zumino--Witten functional to the
microscopic flavor anomaly, the coefficient being compared is the symmetric
cohomology coordinate carried by \(I_6\), not an arbitrary distribution of
the anomaly among particular background currents.

\paragraph{Abelianized descent coordinate used in WZW matching.}
\label{par:abelianized-descent-coordinate-wzw}
The coefficient comparison in the Wess--Zumino--Witten application uses a
particularly simple projection of the same relative class.  Restrict the
background connection to a commuting finite-dimensional subspace of the flavor
Lie algebra, so that
\[
  A=A^aT_a,\qquad F^a=\dd A^a,\qquad [T_a,T_b]=0
\]
inside the chosen test subspace.  The degree-three invariant polynomial then
reduces to
\[
  I_6
  =
  C_{abc}\,F^aF^bF^c,
\]
where \(C_{abc}=C_{(abc)}\) is the symmetric coefficient obtained from the
trace in the representation under discussion, multiplied by the normalization
factor fixed above.  On this Abelianized slice a Chern--Simons representative
and its infinitesimal descent are not hidden behind non-Abelian notation:
\begin{equation}
  I_5^{(0)}
  =
  C_{abc}\,A^aF^bF^c,
  \qquad
  \delta_\lambda I_5^{(0)}
  =
  C_{abc}\,\dd\lambda^a F^bF^c
  =
  \dd\!\left(C_{abc}\lambda^aF^bF^c\right),
  \label{eq:abelianized-cubic-descent-coordinate}
\end{equation}
using \(\dd F^a=0\).  Thus the displayed
\(\lambda\,\dd A\,\dd A\) term is not a low-order approximation to a
different anomaly.  It is the Abelianized coordinate of the same descent class
whose full non-Abelian representatives differ by the coboundaries described in
\eqref{eq:relative-anomaly-coboundary}.

For a compact connected semisimple flavor group, the invariant cubic
polynomial is determined by its restriction to a Cartan subalgebra; equivalently,
its symmetric trilinear coefficient is recovered from the homogeneous cubic
function \(P(x)=C_{abc}x^ax^bx^c\) by the polarization identity
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  C(x,y,z)
  =
  \frac16\bigl[
    &P(x+y+z)-P(x+y)-P(x+z)-P(y+z)
\\
    &\hspace{2.8em}+P(x)+P(y)+P(z)
  \bigr].
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:cubic-polarization-identity-for-anomaly-coordinate}
\end{equation}
Consequently matching the coefficient of
\(\operatorname{Tr}(\alpha\,\dd A\,\dd A)\) for arbitrary commuting flavor
backgrounds fixes the symmetric cubic anomaly coordinate.  It does not fix a
preferred current representative, and it does not choose a Bardeen convention;
those choices affect \(I_4^{(1)}\) by local counterterm coboundaries while
leaving \(C_{abc}\) unchanged.

\section{Index-Normalized Anomaly Polynomials}
\label{sec:index-normalized-anomaly-polynomials}

The descent representative above is written in the effective-action
normalization convenient for Ward identities.  For global comparisons,
especially when gravitational backgrounds are included, it is useful to keep a
second normalization whose periods are the ones supplied by the Dirac index.
Let \(\nabla^{TM}\) be a Riemannian connection on the tangent bundle with
anti-Hermitian curvature \(\mathsf R\), and let
\(\nabla^E\) be a unitary connection on the fermion bundle \(E_R\) with
anti-Hermitian curvature \(\mathsf F_R\).  Define the Chern--Weil forms
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{ch}_R(\mathsf F_R)
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \exp\!\left(\frac{\ii\mathsf F_R}{2\pi}\right),
  \qquad
  p_1(TM)
  =
  -\frac1{8\pi^2}
  \operatorname{tr}_{TM}(\mathsf R^2),
  \label{eq:index-normalized-chern-pontryagin}
\end{equation}
where \(\operatorname{tr}_{TM}\) is the trace in the defining real tangent
representation after complexification.  The six-form anomaly polynomial for a
single left-handed Weyl fermion in \(R\), in the index normalization, is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal I_6(R)
  =
  \bigl[
    \widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}_R(\mathsf F_R)
  \bigr]_6 .
  \label{eq:index-normalized-anomaly-polynomial-general}
\end{equation}
A right-handed Weyl fermion contributes the negative of
\eqref{eq:index-normalized-anomaly-polynomial-general}; equivalently it may be
rewritten as a left-handed fermion in the conjugate representation and then
the same formula may be applied to the left-handed list.

Expanding
\[
  \widehat A(TM)=1-\frac1{24}p_1(TM)+O(\deg 8)
\]
and keeping six-forms gives
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal I_6(R)
  =
  \frac16
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \left(\frac{\ii\mathsf F_R}{2\pi}\right)^3
  -
  \frac1{24}\,
  p_1(TM)\,
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \left(\frac{\ii\mathsf F_R}{2\pi}\right).
  \label{eq:four-dimensional-index-polynomial-expanded}
\end{equation}
The first term is the cubic gauge or flavor anomaly.  The second term is the
mixed gauge-gravitational anomaly.  It is present only for symmetries whose
generator has a nonzero trace over the chiral fermion representation.  Thus it
vanishes for a semisimple gauge group in any representation with traceless Lie
algebra generators, but it is a genuine constraint for a gauged \(U(1)\).

The relation between \(\mathcal I_6\) and the effective-action normalization
used in \eqref{eq:consistent-descent-anomaly-representative} is as follows.
Choose a Chern--Simons form \(\mathcal I_5^{(0)}\) with
\[
  d\mathcal I_5^{(0)}=\mathcal I_6,
  \qquad
  \delta_\lambda\mathcal I_5^{(0)}
  =
  d\mathcal I_4^{(1)}(\lambda).
\]
Then the Euclidean anomalous variation of a left-handed determinant is
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\lambda W_E
  =
  2\pi\ii\int_M
  \mathcal I_4^{(1)}(\lambda).
  \label{eq:index-polynomial-effective-action-normalization}
\end{equation}
The anti-Hermitian form \(I_6\) of the previous section is this same descent
data after multiplying by \(2\pi\ii\) and rewriting the connection as
\(\mathsf A=-\ii A\).  Keeping both notations visible prevents a common
coefficient error: the index-normalized polynomial is a characteristic class,
whereas the Ward identity carries the additional factor \(2\pi\ii\) and the
chosen Lorentzian continuation.

\begin{example}[Four-dimensional \(U(1)\) anomaly polynomial]
\label{ex:u1-index-anomaly-polynomial}
Let a left-handed Weyl fermion have integer charge \(q\) under a background
\(U(1)\) connection \(A\) with curvature \(F=dA\).  In the Hermitian curvature
notation \( \ii\mathsf F_R=qF \), and
\eqref{eq:four-dimensional-index-polynomial-expanded} becomes
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal I_6(q)
  =
  \frac{q^3}{6(2\pi)^3}F^3
  -
  \frac{q}{24(2\pi)}p_1(TM)F .
  \label{eq:u1-index-anomaly-polynomial}
\end{equation}
The cubic \(U(1)^3\) coefficient is \(\sum_i q_i^3\) over left-handed Weyl
fields.  The mixed gravitational-\(U(1)\) coefficient is
\(\sum_i q_i\).  If the \(U(1)\) is gauged, both sums must vanish, in addition
to the mixed nonabelian-\(U(1)\) coefficients
\[
  \sum_i q_i\,T_{G_a}(R_i)=0
\]
for each simple nonabelian factor \(G_a\), where
\(\operatorname{tr}_{R_i}(t^a t^b)=T_{G_a}(R_i)\delta^{ab}\) in the chosen
generator normalization.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[\(SU(N)\) cubic anomaly in fundamental coordinates]
\label{ex:su-n-fundamental-adjoint-anomaly}
Let \(G=SU(N)\), \(N\geq3\), and choose a reference symmetric tensor
\[
  d_\square^{abc}
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_{\square}
  \bigl(t^{(a}t^b t^{c)}\bigr)
\]
in the monograph trace convention.  For an irreducible representation \(R\)
define \(A(R)\) by
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}
  \bigl(t^{(a}t^b t^{c)}\bigr)
  =
  A(R)d_\square^{abc}.
\]
This equation defines the cubic-anomaly coordinate; rescaling all generators
rescales both sides and leaves \(A(R)\) unchanged.  The conjugate
representation obeys \(A(\overline R)=-A(R)\), and every real representation
has \(A(R)=0\).  In particular,
\[
  A(\square)=1,
  \qquad
  A(\overline\square)=-1,
  \qquad
  A(\operatorname{adj})=0 .
\]
Therefore a chiral \(SU(N)\) gauge theory containing
\(n_\square\) left-handed fundamentals,
\(n_{\overline\square}\) left-handed antifundamentals, and any number of
left-handed adjoints has cubic gauge anomaly
\begin{equation}
  d_{\rm total}^{abc}
  =
  (n_\square-n_{\overline\square})d_\square^{abc}.
  \label{eq:su-n-fund-anti-adjoint-anomaly}
\end{equation}
A vectorlike Dirac fundamental consists, in a left-handed description, of one
\(\square\) and one \(\overline\square\); its contribution to
\eqref{eq:su-n-fund-anti-adjoint-anomaly} vanishes.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[One Standard Model generation]
\label{ex:standard-model-anomaly-sums}
Use left-handed Weyl fields
\[
  Q_L:(3,2)_{1/6},\qquad
  u^c:(\overline3,1)_{-2/3},\qquad
  d^c:(\overline3,1)_{1/3},
\]
\[
  L_L:(1,2)_{-1/2},\qquad
  e^c:(1,1)_1 .
\]
The mixed nonabelian-\(U(1)_Y\) sums, with the common quadratic index of the
fundamental factored out, are
\[
  SU(3)^2U(1)_Y:
  \quad
  2\!\left(\frac16\right)
  -\frac23
  +\frac13
  =
  0,
\]
and
\[
  SU(2)^2U(1)_Y:
  \quad
  3\!\left(\frac16\right)
  -\frac12
  =
  0.
\]
The cubic and mixed gravitational hypercharge sums are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \sum_{\rm Weyl}Y^3
  &=
  6\left(\frac16\right)^3
  +3\left(-\frac23\right)^3
  +3\left(\frac13\right)^3
  +2\left(-\frac12\right)^3
  +1
  =
  0,
\\
  \sum_{\rm Weyl}Y
  &=
  6\left(\frac16\right)
  +3\left(-\frac23\right)
  +3\left(\frac13\right)
  +2\left(-\frac12\right)
  +1
  =
  0 .
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:standard-model-hypercharge-anomaly-sums}
\end{equation}
The same field list contains \(3+1=4\) weak doublets, so the finite
\(SU(2)\) anomaly discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-21} is absent for

charge assignments; they do not follow from perturbative renormalizability
alone.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Anomaly matching as equality of anomaly lines]
\label{rem:anomaly-matching-as-line-equality}
Consider an RG flow from a ultraviolet theory \(\mathcal Q_{\rm UV}\) to an
infrared description \(\mathcal Q_{\rm IR}\), and suppose a background-field
groupoid \(\mathcal B(M)\) for a global symmetry is defined along the flow.
Assume that the regulator and the integrating-out procedure preserve this
background coupling up to local counterterms.  Then the UV and IR partition
functions are sections of anomaly lines whose ratio is a local counterterm
line.  Consequently the anomaly classes of \(\mathcal Q_{\rm UV}\) and
\(\mathcal Q_{\rm IR}\) agree in the quotient by local counterterms.
At a finite cutoff, splitting the fields into high- and low-energy variables
is a change of variables in the regulated path integral, together with an
ordinary integration over the high-energy variables.  Under a background gauge
transformation, the regulated Berezinian or bosonic density changes by a
local phase representing the UV anomaly.  The integral over high-energy modes
produces an effective functional of the low-energy variables and backgrounds.
Any dependence on the arbitrary separating scale is a local functional of the
backgrounds and the retained sources, because the modes integrated out have
no singularities at distances larger than the cutoff scale used in the split.
Thus changing the scale changes the anomaly representative by a gauge
variation of a local counterterm.

Taking the continuum and infrared limits within the assumed background-field
chart gives two sections over the same background groupoid.  Their
transformation laws differ only by the local counterterm accumulated during
the flow.  Passing to the anomaly class, where local counterterm variations
are quotiented, gives equality.  If the symmetry is spontaneously broken, the
Goldstone fields and Wess--Zumino--Witten functional provide one possible
infrared section of the same line; if the infrared theory is topological, its
invertible response supplies the section.  The statement does not assert a
unique infrared realization, only equality of the background anomaly class.
\end{remark}

\section{Anomaly Inflow for Local Anomalies}

The descent equations have a second interpretation when the four-dimensional
theory is placed on the boundary of a five-dimensional invertible response.
Let \(X\) be an oriented five-manifold with boundary
\(\partial X=M\), and let \(\mathsf A_X\) be a smooth background connection on
\(X\) whose restriction to \(M\) is the four-dimensional connection
\(\mathsf A\).  The local Chern--Simons functional
\begin{equation}
  W_X^{\mathrm{bulk}}[\mathsf A_X]
  =
  -\int_X I_5^{(0)}(\mathsf A_X)
  \label{eq:local-inflow-bulk-functional}
\end{equation}
has gauge variation
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\lambda W_X^{\mathrm{bulk}}
  =
  -\int_X \dd I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A_X)
  =
  -\int_M I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A).
  \label{eq:local-inflow-bulk-variation}
\end{equation}
Writing the four-dimensional effective action in
\eqref{eq:consistent-descent-anomaly-representative} as \(W_M[\mathsf A]\),
one obtains the bulk-boundary identity
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\lambda
  \left(
    W_M[\mathsf A]+W_X^{\mathrm{bulk}}[\mathsf A_X]
  \right)
  =
  \int_M I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A)
  -
  \int_{\partial X} I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A_X)
  =
  0,
  \label{eq:local-anomaly-inflow-cancellation}
\end{equation}
where the boundary orientation on \(\partial X\) is the orientation of \(M\).
This is anomaly inflow.  The boundary theory by itself transforms by the
consistent anomaly; the bulk Chern--Simons response transforms by the opposite
boundary term.  The gauge-invariant object is the combined bulk-boundary
system.

In the Callan--Harvey realization, \(X\) is a physical gapped bulk or a
domain-wall geometry, and \(M\) supports chiral modes localized at the wall.
Integrating out the massive bulk modes produces a Chern--Simons response whose
coefficient jumps across the wall.  The jump supplies precisely the term
\(-\int_M I_4^{(1)}\) in \eqref{eq:local-inflow-bulk-variation}, while the
localized chiral modes supply \(+\int_M I_4^{(1)}\).  The boundary chiral
modes retain their consistent anomaly, and gauge invariance is a property of
the combined bulk-boundary system.

The formula \eqref{eq:local-inflow-bulk-functional} is local in a chosen
trivialization.  On topologically nontrivial bundles the Chern--Simons form is
defined patchwise, and the exponentiated bulk functional must be defined by
differential-geometric data with integral periods, equivalently by an
invertible five-dimensional field theory whose curvature is the anomaly
polynomial \(I_6\).  The local descent computation fixes the infinitesimal
variation.  Global questions require the finite version: a closed
five-manifold carrying a background field is assigned a phase, and cutting it
open gives the anomalous transformation law of a boundary theory.  That finite
form is the starting point of the global-anomaly discussion in
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-21}.

\section{Consistent and Covariant Currents}

The current obtained by differentiating the effective action,
\[
  J_{\mathrm{cons}}^{a\mu}(x)
  =
  \frac{\delta W[A]}{\delta A^a_\mu(x)},
\]
is the consistent current.  Its anomaly is the variation of a functional and
therefore obeys the Wess--Zumino consistency condition.  This integrability is
the reason that the consistent anomaly is the object classified by
\(H^{1,4}(s\mid d)\).  More precisely, the anomaly class modulo local
counterterms is the consistent descent class represented by
\(\int_M I_4^{(1)}\); covariant Ward identities are obtained after taking a
local differential of this class and adding a Bardeen--Zumino polynomial.

The consistent current need not transform covariantly under background gauge
transformations.  There is a local polynomial current
\[
  J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^{a\mu}(A)
\]
called the Bardeen--Zumino current, whose addition gives a covariant current
\[
  J_{\mathrm{cov}}^{a\mu}
  =
  J_{\mathrm{cons}}^{a\mu}+J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^{a\mu}.
\]
In the anti-Hermitian form convention used in the descent formula above, the
dual three-form of the Bardeen--Zumino current for one left-handed fermion in
the representation \(R\) may be chosen as
\begin{equation}
  \star J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^a
  =
  \kappa\,
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \left[
    T_R^a
    \left(
      \mathsf A\mathsf F+\mathsf F\mathsf A
      -\frac12\mathsf A^3
    \right)
  \right],
  \qquad
  \kappa=\frac{\ii}{24\pi^2},
  \label{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-polynomial}
\end{equation}
where \(T_R^a\) is the anti-Hermitian generator used to vary
\(\mathsf A\), products include wedge product and matrix multiplication, and
\(\star J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^a\) denotes
\[
  \star J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^a
  =
  \frac1{3!}
  \epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^{a\mu}
  \dd x^\nu\wedge\dd x^\rho\wedge\dd x^\sigma .
\]
Equivalently, with
\[
  \mathsf A=\mathsf A_\mu\dd x^\mu,\qquad
  \mathsf F=\frac12\mathsf F_{\rho\sigma}\dd x^\rho\wedge\dd x^\sigma,
\]
the component current is the local polynomial
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^{a\mu}
  =
  \frac{\kappa}{2}\,
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \left[
    T_R^a
    \left(
      \mathsf A_\nu\mathsf F_{\rho\sigma}
      +
      \mathsf F_{\rho\sigma}\mathsf A_\nu
      -
      \mathsf A_\nu\mathsf A_\rho\mathsf A_\sigma
    \right)
  \right].
  \label{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-component-curvature}
\end{equation}
Expanding
\[
  \mathsf F_{\rho\sigma}
  =
  \partial_\rho\mathsf A_\sigma
  -
  \partial_\sigma\mathsf A_\rho
  +
  [\mathsf A_\rho,\mathsf A_\sigma]
\]
and using antisymmetry of
\(\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\) gives the derivative form
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{BZ}}^{a\mu}
  =
  \kappa\,\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \left[
    T_R^a
    \left(
      \mathsf A_\nu\partial_\rho\mathsf A_\sigma
      +
      \partial_\rho\mathsf A_\sigma\,\mathsf A_\nu
      +
      \frac12\mathsf A_\nu\mathsf A_\rho\mathsf A_\sigma
      +
      \mathsf A_\rho\mathsf A_\sigma\mathsf A_\nu
    \right)
  \right].
  \label{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-component-derivative}
\end{equation}
The two displayed component expressions are the same polynomial written once
with \(\mathsf F\) and once with \(\partial\mathsf A\).  The ordering of
matrices in \eqref{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-component-derivative} is part of
the convention; replacing it by a commutative shorthand would lose the
distinction between consistent and covariant currents for a nonabelian
background.

\paragraph{Abelian calibration of the Bardeen--Zumino factor.}
The simplest check of the normalization is the one-generator commuting
subspace.  Let
\[
  I_6=C\,\mathsf F^3,\qquad \mathsf F=\dd\mathsf A ,
\]
where \(C\) includes the trace and the factor \(\kappa\).  The corresponding
Chern--Simons and consistent descent forms are
\begin{equation}
  I_5^{(0)}=C\,\mathsf A\mathsf F^2,\qquad
  I_4^{(1)}(\lambda,\mathsf A)=C\,\lambda\,\mathsf F^2 .
  \label{eq:abelian-consistent-descent-calibration}
\end{equation}
Thus the consistent Ward identity carries the coefficient \(C\).  In the
same Abelianized coordinate the Bardeen--Zumino current
\eqref{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-polynomial} reduces to
\begin{equation}
  \star J_{\mathrm{BZ}}
  =
  2C\,\mathsf A\mathsf F ,
  \qquad
  \dd\star J_{\mathrm{BZ}}
  =
  2C\,\mathsf F^2 ,
  \label{eq:abelian-bz-divergence-calibration}
\end{equation}
because \(\mathsf A^3=0\) for a single one-form and \(\dd\mathsf F=0\).
The covariant Ward representative obtained by improving the current is
therefore
\begin{equation}
  C\,\lambda\,\mathsf F^2
  +
  2C\,\lambda\,\mathsf F^2
  =
  3C\,\lambda\,\mathsf F^2 .
  \label{eq:abelian-covariant-anomaly-factor-three}
\end{equation}
This factor of three is a normalization check, not a new anomaly class.  The
first term is the descent representative of the effective-action variation;
the additional two units are the local divergence of the Bardeen--Zumino
polynomial.  In the nonabelian case the same statement is encoded by the
ordered polynomial in
\eqref{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-component-derivative}; the covariant current
transforms covariantly, but it is not in general the differential of a local
effective action.
For a set of Weyl fermions, sum \eqref{eq:bardeen-zumino-current-polynomial}
over left-handed representations and subtract the corresponding right-handed
representations, in parallel with the anomaly polynomial \(I_6\).
The divergence of \(J_{\mathrm{cov}}\) is gauge covariant and is often the most
compact local expression of the anomaly.  However,
\(J_{\mathrm{cov}}\) is generally not the functional derivative of a local
effective action.  Thus it is not the current that appears directly in the
integrability condition \eqref{eq:wess-zumino-consistency}.  The consistent
anomaly is the cohomology class.  The covariant anomaly is obtained from a
particular representative by applying the local differential of that class and
adding the Bardeen--Zumino polynomial; it is a covariant representative of the
Ward identity, not a representative of a second obstruction class.
Gauge-anomaly cancellation is therefore a statement about the consistent
class; after that class vanishes, covariant currents may be used as convenient
representatives of gauge-covariant Ward identities.

\section{Local Counterterms and Preserved Currents}

An anomaly representative depends on the choice of local counterterms, while
its cohomology class does not.  Let \(B\) denote background gauge fields for a
set of currents, and let \(W[B]\) be the renormalized generating functional.
Adding a local functional \(I_{\rm ct}[B]\) changes
\[
  W[B]\longmapsto W[B]+I_{\rm ct}[B].
\]
If a background gauge transformation with parameter \(\zeta\) has anomaly
\[
  \delta_\zeta W[B]=\mathcal A(\zeta,B),
\]
then after the counterterm shift
\[
  \mathcal A(\zeta,B)
  \longmapsto
  \mathcal A(\zeta,B)+\delta_\zeta I_{\rm ct}[B].
\]
The second term is BRST-exact in the local functional complex.  It can move
the displayed divergence among currents that are coupled to backgrounds, but
it cannot remove a nonzero anomaly class.

This distinction is essential when one of the currents is gauged dynamically.
The gauge current must satisfy the Slavnov--Taylor identity; otherwise the
gauge-fixed state space cannot be quotiented to a positive physical Hilbert
space.  For a vector and an axial current coupled to background fields, a
Bardeen counterterm can shift the local expression of the anomaly between
vector and axial Ward identities.  The convention used in
\eqref{eq:abelian-axial-anomaly} is the one in which the vector gauge Ward
identity is preserved and the axial current carries the anomaly.  For a
purely global symmetry, by contrast, the anomaly is allowed and becomes an
RG-invariant background response to be matched by the infrared theory.

\section{The Strong CP Parameter}

In QCD with \(N_f\) quark flavors, the Euclidean gauge action may include the
topological term
\[
  \ii\theta\,
  \frac1{32\pi^2}
  \int
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma})\,\dd^4x .
\]
The complex quark mass matrix \(M\) was introduced in
\eqref{eq:qcd-chiral-mass-lagrangian}.  A flavor-singlet axial rotation
changes the phase of \(\det M\) and, by the anomaly, shifts \(\theta\).  With
the convention used in this chapter, the invariant CP-odd parameter is
\begin{equation}
  \bar\theta
  =
  \theta+\arg\det M .
  \label{eq:qcd-physical-theta}
\end{equation}
If the sign convention for \(\gamma_5\) or for the topological density is
reversed, both transformation laws change together and the invariant
combination is correspondingly rewritten.  The invariant statement is that
only the anomaly-invariant combination of the topological angle and the quark
mass phase is physically meaningful.

\section{Topological Charge Sectors and Periodicity}

For gauge group \(SU(N_c)\) on a compact Euclidean four-manifold, or on
\(\mathbb R^4\) with finite-action boundary conditions so that the connection
extends to a bundle over \(S^4\), the topological charge is
\[
  Q[A]
  =
  \frac1{32\pi^2}
  \int
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma})\,\dd^4x .
\]
With \(F\) regarded as the matrix-valued curvature of the fundamental
connection and with the matrix trace convention fixed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:vol2-classical-yang-mills-matter}, \(Q[A]\in\mathbb Z\).
This integer statement is a Chern--Weil statement about the curvature form;
rewriting it in components must use the same generator normalization as the
one used to define \(F=A^a t^a\).  The Euclidean functional integral
decomposes formally into connected components of fixed topological charge:
\[
  Z(\theta)
  =
  \sum_{Q\in\mathbb Z}
  \ee^{\ii\theta Q}Z_Q .
\]
This expression makes the \(2\pi\)-periodicity in \(\theta\) manifest when no
additional global identifications modify the allowed bundles or background
fields.

The axial anomaly explains why \(\theta\) cannot be assigned independently of
the fermion mass phases.  A flavor-singlet chiral rotation changes the
fermion measure by a phase proportional to \(Q[A]\) and changes
\(\arg\det M\) by the opposite amount in the mass term.  The sector
decomposition therefore gives the same invariant parameter
\(\bar\theta=\theta+\arg\det M\) as the local anomalous Ward identity.  The
local identity and the topological decomposition are two presentations of the
same anomalous Jacobian in a gauge background with nonzero topological charge.

\section{Topological Susceptibility as a Regulated Cumulant}
\label{sec:topological-susceptibility-regulated-cumulant}

The phrase ``topological susceptibility'' is often used as if it were a
number attached directly to a formal continuum expression
\(\langle Q^2\rangle/V_4\).  The object needed in QCD is more precise: it is a
thermodynamic and continuum limit of a cumulant in a specified regulator.

\begin{definition}[Finite-regulator theta data]
\label{def:finite-regulator-theta-data}
Fix a gauge-invariant Euclidean regulator on an oriented four-volume \(V_4\).
Finite-regulator theta data consist of nonnegative sector weights
\(Z_{\Lambda,V_4,Q}\), \(Q\in\mathbb Z\), for the vectorlike theory at
\(\theta=0\), such that
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\Lambda,V_4}(\theta)
  =
  \sum_{Q\in\mathbb Z}
  \ee^{\ii\theta Q}Z_{\Lambda,V_4,Q}
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-theta-partition-function}
\end{equation}
is finite for \(\theta\) in a neighborhood of zero.  The finite-regulator
vacuum-energy density near \(\theta=0\) is
\begin{equation}
  E_{\Lambda,V_4}(\theta)
  =
  -\frac1{V_4}\log Z_{\Lambda,V_4}(\theta),
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-theta-energy}
\end{equation}
where the logarithm is the branch continuous from \(\theta=0\).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Finite-volume susceptibility identity]
\label{rem:finite-volume-topological-susceptibility}
For every finite-regulator theta data set,
\begin{equation}
  E_{\Lambda,V_4}''(0)
  =
  \frac1{V_4}
  \left(
    \langle Q^2\rangle_{\Lambda,V_4,0}
    -
    \langle Q\rangle_{\Lambda,V_4,0}^2
  \right),
  \label{eq:finite-volume-topological-susceptibility}
\end{equation}
where the expectation is with respect to the positive weights
\(Z_{\Lambda,V_4,Q}/Z_{\Lambda,V_4}(0)\).  If CP is preserved at
\(\theta=0\), then \(\langle Q\rangle_{\Lambda,V_4,0}=0\), and the right hand
side is \(V_4^{-1}\langle Q^2\rangle_{\Lambda,V_4,0}\).
Differentiate \eqref{eq:finite-regulator-theta-partition-function}:
\[
  Z'(0)=\ii\sum_Q Q Z_Q,
  \qquad
  Z''(0)=-\sum_Q Q^2Z_Q ,
\]
where the regulator and volume labels are suppressed.  Hence
\[
  \frac{\dd^2}{\dd\theta^2}\log Z(\theta)\bigg|_{\theta=0}
  =
  \frac{Z''(0)}{Z(0)}
  -
  \left(\frac{Z'(0)}{Z(0)}\right)^2
  =
  -\langle Q^2\rangle+\langle Q\rangle^2 .
  \]
Multiplying by \(-1/V_4\) gives
\eqref{eq:finite-volume-topological-susceptibility}.  CP sends
\(Q\mapsto -Q\); if the \(\theta=0\) weights are CP invariant, the first
moment vanishes.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Local density form and contact convention]
\label{prop:finite-regulator-susceptibility-density-cumulant}
Assume that the finite regulator is placed on a closed four-volume, such as a
periodic box, or on a volume with boundary data for which the topological
charge is fixed, and that it supplies a regulated local density
\(q_{\Lambda,V_4}(x)\) satisfying
\begin{equation}
  Q
  =
  \int_{V_4} q_{\Lambda,V_4}(x)\,\dd^4x
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-local-topological-charge}
\end{equation}
configuration by configuration.  Then the finite-volume susceptibility is
equivalently, with the integrals interpreted as the regulator's finite
sum or integral,
\begin{equation}
  E_{\Lambda,V_4}''(0)
  =
  \frac1{V_4}
  \int_{V_4}\dd^4x
  \int_{V_4}\dd^4y\,
  \bigl\langle
    q_{\Lambda,V_4}(x)q_{\Lambda,V_4}(y)
  \bigr\rangle_{\Lambda,V_4,0}^{\rm c}.
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-density-susceptibility}
\end{equation}
If the regulator and state are translation invariant on a periodic volume,
this reduces to the zero-momentum two-point function
\begin{equation}
  E_{\Lambda,V_4}''(0)
  =
  \int_{V_4}\dd^4x\,
  \bigl\langle
    q_{\Lambda,V_4}(x)q_{\Lambda,V_4}(0)
  \bigr\rangle_{\Lambda,V_4,0}^{\rm c}.
  \label{eq:periodic-density-susceptibility}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Insert \eqref{eq:finite-regulator-local-topological-charge} into the finite
cumulant:
\[
  \langle Q^2\rangle-\langle Q\rangle^2
  =
  \int_{V_4}\dd^4x
  \int_{V_4}\dd^4y\,
  \left(
    \langle q_{\Lambda,V_4}(x)q_{\Lambda,V_4}(y)\rangle
    -
    \langle q_{\Lambda,V_4}(x)\rangle
    \langle q_{\Lambda,V_4}(y)\rangle
  \right).
\]
Dividing by \(V_4\) and using
\eqref{eq:finite-volume-topological-susceptibility} gives
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-density-susceptibility}.  Translation invariance
sets the connected correlator equal to a function of \(x-y\); the double
integral is then \(V_4\) times the integral over one relative coordinate.
\end{proof}

The continuum formula
\(\chi_{\rm top}=\int\dd^4x\,\langle q(x)q(0)\rangle^{\rm c}\) is therefore
not a bare multiplication rule for two formal \(F\widetilde F\) densities at
coincident points.  It is the limit of the regulated zero-momentum curvature
above, with the contact term selected by the same local \(\theta\)-counterterm
convention used in
Definition~\ref{def:topological-susceptibility}.  Changing the convention by
an additive local vacuum-energy term \(\frac12 c_\theta\theta^2\) shifts both
\(E''(0)\) and the integrated contact part of the density two-point function
by \(c_\theta\).  Thus Witten--Veneziano matching uses the renormalized
zero-momentum curvature of pure Yang--Mills, not an unrenormalized
point-split product with its contact term silently discarded.

\begin{definition}[Topological susceptibility]
\label{def:topological-susceptibility}
The topological susceptibility of a continuum theory in a specified phase and
regulator class is the limit
\begin{equation}
  \chi_{\rm top}
  =
  \lim_{\Lambda\to\infty}\lim_{V_4\to\infty}
  E_{\Lambda,V_4}''(0),
  \label{eq:topological-susceptibility-limit}
\end{equation}
when this limit exists and is independent of the allowed regulator choices
after the local counterterm convention for the theta term has been fixed.
For pure Yang--Mills we write the corresponding quantity as
\(\chi_{\rm YM}\).
\end{definition}

This definition intentionally separates three facts which are often conflated.
The finite identity
\eqref{eq:finite-volume-topological-susceptibility} is elementary.  The
existence and regulator independence of
\eqref{eq:topological-susceptibility-limit} is a nonperturbative statement
about the theory.  The use of \(\chi_{\rm YM}\) inside a chiral effective
description with dynamical quarks is a further large-\(N_c\) matching
assumption, developed in the next chapter.

\section{Theta Branches and Cluster Decomposition}
\label{sec:theta-branches-cluster-decomposition}

The sector sum
\(\sum_Q\ee^{\ii\theta Q}Z_Q\) explains \(2\pi\)-periodicity, but it does not
by itself identify a pure infinite-volume vacuum.  In a theory with several
large-volume branches the thermodynamic limit selects the branch of smallest
energy density, except at degeneracy loci where a phase-selection prescription
is needed.  Cluster decomposition is the local diagnostic that one has chosen
a pure phase rather than a convex mixture.

\begin{definition}[Theta-branch data]
\label{def:theta-branch-data}
Theta-branch data for a regulated Euclidean gauge theory in a volume \(V_4\)
consists of a finite or countable branch set \(\mathcal B\), branch partition
functions \(Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)\), and local normalized states
\(\omega_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta,\theta}\) on gauge-invariant observables, such
that
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\Lambda,V_4}(\theta)
  =
  \sum_{\beta\in\mathcal B}Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta),
  \qquad
  p_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)
  =
  \frac{Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)}
       {Z_{\Lambda,V_4}(\theta)} .
  \label{eq:theta-branch-partition-decomposition}
\end{equation}
When the limits exist, write
\begin{equation}
  E_\beta(\theta)
  =
  -\lim_{\Lambda\to\infty}\lim_{V_4\to\infty}
  \frac1{V_4}\log Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)
  \label{eq:theta-branch-energy-density}
\end{equation}
for the branch energy density.  The data are \(2\pi\)-periodic by branch
relabeling when there is a bijection \(T:\mathcal B\to\mathcal B\) with
\begin{equation}
  E_{T\beta}(\theta+2\pi)=E_\beta(\theta).
  \label{eq:theta-branch-periodic-relabeling}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Thermodynamic branch selection]
\label{rem:theta-thermodynamic-branch-selection}
Assume a finite branch set and a fixed \(\theta\) at which the limiting branch
energies \(E_\beta(\theta)\) exist.  If a unique branch \(\beta_*\) satisfies
\begin{equation}
  E_{\beta_*}(\theta)
  <
  E_\beta(\theta)
  \qquad
  \hbox{for every }\beta\neq\beta_*,
  \label{eq:unique-theta-branch-minimum}
\end{equation}
and if each branch partition function has the form
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)
  =
  \exp\{-V_4E_\beta(\theta)+o(V_4)\}
  \label{eq:branch-free-energy-asymptotics}
\end{equation}
uniformly over the finite branch set, then
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{\Lambda\to\infty}\lim_{V_4\to\infty}
  p_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta_*}(\theta)=1,
  \qquad
  \lim_{\Lambda\to\infty}\lim_{V_4\to\infty}
  p_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)=0
  \quad(\beta\neq\beta_*).
  \label{eq:theta-branch-selection}
\end{equation}
For \(\beta\neq\beta_*\),
\[
  \frac{Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta}(\theta)}
       {Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta_*}(\theta)}
  =
  \exp\{-V_4(E_\beta(\theta)-E_{\beta_*}(\theta))+o(V_4)\}.
\]
The exponent tends to \(-\infty\) because the energy-density gap is positive.
Since the branch set is finite, the sum of all nonminimal ratios tends to
zero.  Dividing the branch weights by
\(Z_{\Lambda,V_4,\beta_*}\) gives
\eqref{eq:theta-branch-selection}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Cluster test for mixtures of theta branches]
\label{rem:theta-branch-mixture-cluster-test}
Let \(\{\omega_\beta\}_{\beta\in\mathcal B}\) be translation-invariant pure
states for gauge-invariant local observables, and assume each branch clusters:
for local observables \(\mathcal O,\mathcal P\),
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{|a|\to\infty}
  \omega_\beta(\mathcal O(a)\mathcal P(0))
  =
  \omega_\beta(\mathcal O)\omega_\beta(\mathcal P).
  \label{eq:branch-clustering-assumption}
\end{equation}
For a convex combination
\begin{equation}
  \omega=\sum_{\beta\in\mathcal B}p_\beta\omega_\beta,
  \qquad
  p_\beta\ge0,
  \qquad
  \sum_\beta p_\beta=1,
  \label{eq:theta-branch-mixture-state}
\end{equation}
one has
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{|a|\to\infty}
  \left[
    \omega(\mathcal O(a)\mathcal P(0))
    -
    \omega(\mathcal O)\omega(\mathcal P)
  \right]
  =
  \operatorname{Cov}_{p}
  \bigl(\omega_\beta(\mathcal O),\omega_\beta(\mathcal P)\bigr).
  \label{eq:theta-branch-cluster-covariance}
\end{equation}
Thus the mixture clusters for all local observables only if the branch labels
cannot be distinguished by local one-point functions on the support of
\(p_\beta\), or if the mixture is supported on a single branch.
Using the branch clustering assumption,
\[
  \lim_{|a|\to\infty}
  \omega(\mathcal O(a)\mathcal P(0))
  =
  \sum_\beta p_\beta
  \omega_\beta(\mathcal O)\omega_\beta(\mathcal P).
  \]
The product of the one-point functions in the mixed state is
\[
  \omega(\mathcal O)\omega(\mathcal P)
  =
  \left(\sum_\beta p_\beta\omega_\beta(\mathcal O)\right)
  \left(\sum_\gamma p_\gamma\omega_\gamma(\mathcal P)\right).
  \]
Their difference is precisely the covariance of the two branch-dependent
one-point functions with respect to the probability distribution \(p\).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Theta vacua as theorem, hypothesis, and convention]
\label{rem:theta-vacua-status}
At finite regulator the sector decomposition, cumulant identity, and branch
mixture algebra above are exact.  A continuum statement that theta labels
superselection sectors requires additional input: the existence of the
continuum gauge theory, the infinite-volume limits of the local states, the
cluster property inside a selected branch, and the action of large gauge
transformations or theta-shift automorphisms on the physical representation.
The monograph therefore uses ``theta vacuum'' only after these data have been
specified.  Otherwise it speaks of a theta parameter in the regulated action
or of a theta-branch hypothesis.
\end{remark}

\section{The BPST Instanton and the Semiclassical Vertex}
\label{sec:bpst-instanton-thooft-vertex}

We now construct the basic finite-action representative of the sector
\(Q=1\).  This section is Euclidean: coordinates are
\(x^\mu\), \(\mu=1,\ldots,4\), the metric is \(\delta_{\mu\nu}\), and
\(\epsilon_{1234}=+1\).  The chapter's gauge-field convention remains
Hermitian, so
\[
  F_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu-\ii[A_\mu,A_\nu].
\]
For explicit \(SU(2)\) formulas it is economical to use the auxiliary basis
\[
  T_a=\frac{\sigma_a}{2},
  \qquad
  [T_a,T_b]=\ii\epsilon^c{}_{ab}T_c,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}(T_aT_b)=\frac12\delta_{ab}.
\]
This is only a coordinate choice for the displayed solution.  The monograph's
default trace-delta basis is \(t_a=\sqrt2\,T_a\).  If
\[
  \mathcal L_{\rm here}
  =
  -\frac1{4g_{\rm YM}^2}
  \operatorname{tr}_{\delta}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})
\]
is rewritten in the \(T_a\) basis, then the corresponding half-trace coupling
is \(g_{\rm ht}=\sqrt2\,g_{\rm YM}\).  Thus the familiar instanton action
\(8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2\) is the same number as
\(4\pi^2/g_{\rm YM}^2\) in the active trace-delta coupling coordinate.

Define the self-dual 't Hooft symbols by
\[
  \eta^a_{ij}=\epsilon_{aij},
  \qquad
  \eta^a_{i4}=\delta_{ai},
  \qquad
  \eta^a_{4i}=-\delta_{ai},
  \qquad i,j,a=1,2,3,
\]
so that
\[
  \eta^a_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \frac12\epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\eta^a_{\rho\sigma},
  \qquad
  \sum_{a,\mu,\nu}\eta^a_{\mu\nu}\eta^a_{\mu\nu}=12 .
\]
The contraction identity used below is
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon^a{}_{bc}\eta^b_{\mu\rho}\eta^c_{\nu\sigma}
  y^\rho y^\sigma
  =
  r^2\eta^a_{\mu\nu}
  +y_\mu\eta^a_{\nu\lambda}y^\lambda
  -y_\nu\eta^a_{\mu\lambda}y^\lambda .
  \label{eq:eta-symbol-quadratic-identity}
\end{equation}
It follows by checking the three possible positions of the index \(4\) in the
defining equations for \(\eta\), and then using antisymmetry in
\(\mu,\nu\).  This identity is the finite-dimensional algebraic input that
turns the apparently inhomogeneous derivative and commutator terms in the
curvature into a multiple of \(\eta^a_{\mu\nu}\).
Let \(a^\mu\in\mathbb R^4\) and \(\rho>0\).  Put \(y=x-a\) and
\(r^2=y_\mu y_\mu\).  The BPST one-instanton in regular gauge is
\begin{equation}
  A_\mu(x)
  =
  \frac{2\,\eta^a_{\mu\nu}y^\nu}{r^2+\rho^2}\,T_a .
  \label{eq:bpst-regular-gauge-potential}
\end{equation}
Write \(f=r^2+\rho^2\) and \(A_\mu=A_\mu^aT_a\).  Since
\[
  \partial_\mu A_\nu^a-\partial_\nu A_\mu^a
  =
  -\frac4f\,\eta^a_{\mu\nu}
  -
  \frac4{f^2}
  \left(
    y_\mu\eta^a_{\nu\lambda}y^\lambda
    -
    y_\nu\eta^a_{\mu\lambda}y^\lambda
  \right)
\]
and
\[
  \epsilon^a{}_{bc}A_\mu^bA_\nu^c
  =
  \frac4{f^2}
  \left(
    r^2\eta^a_{\mu\nu}
    +
    y_\mu\eta^a_{\nu\lambda}y^\lambda
    -
    y_\nu\eta^a_{\mu\lambda}y^\lambda
  \right),
\]
the curvature convention
\(F^a_{\mu\nu}
=\partial_\mu A_\nu^a-\partial_\nu A_\mu^a+\epsilon^a{}_{bc}A_\mu^bA_\nu^c\)
gives
\begin{equation}
  F_{\mu\nu}(x)
  =
  -\frac{4\rho^2}{(r^2+\rho^2)^2}\,
  \eta^a_{\mu\nu}T_a .
  \label{eq:bpst-regular-gauge-curvature}
\end{equation}
Hence \(F_{\mu\nu}=\widetilde F_{\mu\nu}\), where
\(\widetilde F_{\mu\nu}=\frac12\epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\rho\sigma}\).
The connection is smooth at \(r=0\).  At infinity it is gauge-equivalent to a
pure gauge of winding one.  Equivalently, applying the gauge transformation
\[
  g(y)=\frac{y_4\mathbf 1+\ii y_i\sigma_i}{r},
  \qquad g:S^3_\infty\to SU(2),
\]
produces the singular-gauge representative
\[
  A_\mu^{\rm sing}(x)
  =
  \frac{2\rho^2\,\bar\eta^a_{\mu\nu}y^\nu}
       {r^2(r^2+\rho^2)}\,T_a ,
\]
where \(\bar\eta\) is the anti-self-dual 't Hooft symbol obtained from
\(\eta\) by reversing the signs of the components with one index equal to
\(4\).  This representative decays as \(r^{-3}\) at infinity and has a
gauge-coordinate
singularity at \(y=0\).  The two representatives describe the same smooth
connection on the one-instanton bundle over \(S^4\).

The action saturation is an algebraic consequence of self-duality.  In the
half-trace component convention,
\[
  S_E^{\rm ht}
  =
  \frac1{4g_{\rm ht}^2}
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}\dd^4x\,
  F^a_{\mu\nu}F^a_{\mu\nu}.
\]
Completing the square gives, for any finite-action field in the sector of
charge \(k\),
\begin{equation}
  S_E^{\rm ht}
  =
  \frac1{8g_{\rm ht}^2}
  \int (F^a_{\mu\nu}\mp\widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu})^2\,\dd^4x
  \pm
  \frac1{4g_{\rm ht}^2}
  \int F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\,\dd^4x
  \ge
  \frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2}|k|.
  \label{eq:instanton-action-bound-half-trace}
\end{equation}
Equality holds precisely for self-dual fields when \(k>0\) and anti-self-dual
fields when \(k<0\).  For \eqref{eq:bpst-regular-gauge-curvature},
\[
  F^a_{\mu\nu}F^a_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \frac{192\rho^4}{(r^2+\rho^2)^4},
  \qquad
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}
  \frac{\rho^4\,\dd^4x}{(r^2+\rho^2)^4}
  =
  \frac{\pi^2}{6},
\]
where the last integral is
\[
  2\pi^2\int_0^\infty
  \frac{\rho^4r^3\,\dd r}{(r^2+\rho^2)^4}
  =
  \pi^2\int_0^\infty
  \frac{\rho^4u\,\dd u}{(u+\rho^2)^4}
  =
  \frac{\pi^2}{6}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}F^a_{\mu\nu}F^a_{\mu\nu}\,\dd^4x=32\pi^2,
  \qquad
  S_E^{\rm ht}=\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2}.
\]
Because the field is self-dual,
\[
  Q[A]
  =
  \frac1{32\pi^2}
  \int F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\,\dd^4x
  =
  1 .
\]
In the trace-delta coupling coordinate used elsewhere in this monograph this
same saddle contributes the exponential
\begin{equation}
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{4\pi^2}{g_{\rm YM}^2}+\ii\theta\right)
  =
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2}+\ii\theta\right).
  \label{eq:bpst-instanton-exponential-conventions}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[BPST one-instanton data]
\label{lem:bpst-one-instanton-data}
With the self-dual symbols and \(SU(2)\) half-trace generators
\(T_a=\sigma_a/2\) used in this section, the connection
\[
  A_\mu(x)
  =
  \frac{2\,\eta^a_{\mu\nu}(x-a)^\nu}{(x-a)^2+\rho^2}\,T_a,
  \qquad
  \rho>0,
\]
has curvature
\[
  F_{\mu\nu}(x)
  =
  -\frac{4\rho^2}{((x-a)^2+\rho^2)^2}\,
  \eta^a_{\mu\nu}T_a .
\]
It is self-dual, has topological charge \(Q=1\), and has Euclidean action
\[
  S_E^{\rm ht}=\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2}
  =
  \frac{4\pi^2}{g_{\rm YM}^2}
\]
when rewritten in the trace-delta coupling coordinate of the Yang--Mills
chapters.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Differentiating the displayed connection gives the derivative term written
above, and the commutator term is
\(\epsilon^a{}_{bc}A_\mu^bA_\nu^c\).  The contraction identity
\eqref{eq:eta-symbol-quadratic-identity} cancels the inhomogeneous
\(y_\mu\eta_{\nu\lambda}^a y^\lambda
-y_\nu\eta_{\mu\lambda}^a y^\lambda\) terms and leaves
\eqref{eq:bpst-regular-gauge-curvature}.  Since
\(\eta^a_{\mu\nu}
=\frac12\epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\eta^a_{\rho\sigma}\), this curvature is
self-dual.

The square-completion identity
\eqref{eq:instanton-action-bound-half-trace} gives the lower bound
\(8\pi^2|Q|/g_{\rm ht}^2\), saturated by a self-dual field with \(Q>0\).
For the displayed curvature,
\[
  F^a_{\mu\nu}F^a_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \frac{192\rho^4}{(r^2+\rho^2)^4},
  \qquad
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}
  \frac{\rho^4\,\dd^4x}{(r^2+\rho^2)^4}
  =
  \frac{\pi^2}{6},
\]
so \(\int F^a_{\mu\nu}F^a_{\mu\nu}=32\pi^2\).  Self-duality then gives
\[
  Q=\frac1{32\pi^2}\int F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\,\dd^4x=1
\]
and \(S_E^{\rm ht}=8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2\).  Finally,
\(\operatorname{tr}_\delta(t_at_b)=\delta_{ab}\) with
\(t_a=\sqrt2\,T_a\) implies \(g_{\rm ht}=\sqrt2\,g_{\rm YM}\), hence the
trace-delta action is \(4\pi^2/g_{\rm YM}^2\).
\end{proof}

The bosonic zero modes are tangent vectors to the moduli of solutions modulo
gauge transformations.  For \(SU(2)\), \(k=1\), the smooth moduli are
\[
  a^\mu\in\mathbb R^4,\qquad
  \rho\in\mathbb R_{>0},\qquad
  U\in SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2 .
\]
Thus there are
\[
  4+1+3=8
\]
bosonic zero modes: four translations, one dilation, and three global gauge
orientations.  For an \(SU(N_c)\) instanton obtained by embedding the above
\(SU(2)\) solution, the orientation moduli are enlarged, and the
one-instanton bosonic moduli space has dimension \(4N_c\) before quotienting
possible global identifications.  The reason for the number \(4N_c\) is
local and elementary.  The center and size contribute \(4+1\) parameters.  The
embedded \(SU(2)\) orientation is the orbit of the embedding under
\(SU(N_c)\), whose stabilizer is \(SU(N_c-2)\times U(1)\).  Hence the
orientation dimension is
\[
  (N_c^2-1)-\bigl((N_c-2)^2-1\bigr)-1=4N_c-5 ,
\]
and adding center and size gives \(4N_c\).  The precise global quotient by
the center and by stabilizers affects the coordinate range, not this local
dimension count.

\begin{definition}[Framed ADHM quotient]
\label{def:framed-adhm-quotient-datum}
Let \(W\simeq\mathbb C^{N_c}\) be the framing vector space and
\(K\simeq\mathbb C^k\) the instanton vector space.  A framed \(U(N_c)\)
ADHM quadruple of charge \(k\) is
\[
  (B_1,B_2,I,J)
  \in
  \operatorname{End}(K)^2
  \oplus\operatorname{Hom}(W,K)
  \oplus\operatorname{Hom}(K,W),
\]
with moment maps
\begin{align}
  \mu_{\mathbb C}
  &:=
  [B_1,B_2]+IJ
  \in\operatorname{End}(K),
  \nonumber\\
  \mu_{\mathbb R}
  &:=
  [B_1,B_1^\dagger]+[B_2,B_2^\dagger]
  +II^\dagger-J^\dagger J
  \in\operatorname{Herm}(K).
  \label{eq:general-adhm-moment-maps}
\end{align}
The unitary group \(U(K)\) acts by
\begin{equation}
  (B_1,B_2,I,J)
  \longmapsto
  (gB_1g^{-1},gB_2g^{-1},gI,Jg^{-1}).
  \label{eq:general-adhm-unitary-action}
\end{equation}
The smooth framed ADHM quotient is the stable part of
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal M_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}
  :=
  \{\,\mu_{\mathbb C}=0,\ \mu_{\mathbb R}=0\,\}/U(K).
  \label{eq:general-adhm-quotient}
\end{equation}
Here stability means that there is no proper subspace
\(S\subset K\) which contains \(I(W)\) and is preserved by both \(B_1\) and
\(B_2\).  On this stable locus the framed gauge data have no residual
endomorphism of \(K\) invisible to the framing.
\end{definition}

The ADHM construction identifies the smooth quotient
\eqref{eq:general-adhm-quotient} with the framed smooth instanton moduli
space on \(\mathbb R^4\), after matching the chosen orientation convention
for self-duality versus anti-self-duality.  The full transform also builds
the connection from the finite-dimensional linear algebra.  The calculation
needed for the semiclassical measure at this stage is the local quotient
dimension and the boundary behavior of the quotient, which follow directly
from Definition~\ref{def:framed-adhm-quotient-datum}.

\begin{proposition}[ADHM quotient dimension at smooth stable points]
\label{prop:adhm-quotient-dimension-count}
At a point of \(\mu_{\mathbb C}^{-1}(0)\cap\mu_{\mathbb R}^{-1}(0)\) where
the stable \(U(K)\) action is free and the moment map is transverse to zero,
the framed ADHM quotient
\(\mathcal M_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}\) has local real dimension
\[
  \dim_\mathbb R\mathcal M_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}=4kN_c .
\]
The trace parts of \(B_1\) and \(B_2\) give the four center coordinates of
the instanton configuration.  The centered quotient therefore has local real
dimension \(4kN_c-4\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The real dimension of the unconstrained variables is
\[
  2\dim_\mathbb C\bigl(\operatorname{End}(K)^2\bigr)
  +
  2\dim_\mathbb C\operatorname{Hom}(W,K)
  +
  2\dim_\mathbb C\operatorname{Hom}(K,W)
  =
  4k^2+4kN_c .
\]
The complex equation \(\mu_{\mathbb C}=0\) imposes \(k^2\) complex
conditions, hence \(2k^2\) real conditions.  The real moment-map equation
\(\mu_{\mathbb R}=0\) imposes \(k^2\) real conditions because
\(\mathfrak u(K)\) has real dimension \(k^2\).  Transversality makes these
conditions independent at the point under consideration.

Finally one divides by \(U(K)\), whose real dimension is \(k^2\).  Freeness
on the stable locus means that this quotient subtracts exactly \(k^2\)
dimensions locally.  Thus
\[
  (4k^2+4kN_c)-2k^2-k^2-k^2=4kN_c .
\]
The equations contain only commutators of \(B_1,B_2\) and the products
\(IJ,II^\dagger,J^\dagger J\).  Shifting
\[
  B_\alpha\longmapsto B_\alpha+c_\alpha\mathbf 1_K,
  \qquad \alpha=1,2,
\]
leaves the moment maps unchanged.  The two complex numbers
\(\operatorname{tr}B_1/k\) and \(\operatorname{tr}B_2/k\) are therefore the
four real translation coordinates in the complex structure used to write
\(\mathbb R^4\simeq\mathbb C^2\).  Removing them gives centered dimension
\(4kN_c-4\).
\end{proof}

The stability condition is also where the small-instanton boundary first
appears algebraically.  If a stable ADHM tuple degenerates so that a nonzero
subspace of \(K\) decouples from the framing, the quotient leaves the smooth
instanton stratum and records instanton charge concentrated at points of
\(\mathbb R^4\).  The Uhlenbeck boundary has strata of the form
\[
  \mathcal M_{k-\ell,N_c}^{\rm smooth}
  \times
  \operatorname{Sym}^{\ell}(\mathbb R^4),
  \qquad 1\le \ell\le k,
\]
where \(\ell\) units of charge have collapsed to pointlike ideal instantons.
For a path integral this compactification carries analytic content: whether the
semiclassical integral is finite depends on the determinant, operator
insertions, and regulator behavior near these boundary strata.

\begin{definition}[ADHM quotient density]
\label{def:adhm-quotient-density}
Equip the affine ADHM space
\[
  \mathbb A_{k,N_c}
  =
  \operatorname{End}(K)^2
  \oplus\operatorname{Hom}(W,K)
  \oplus\operatorname{Hom}(K,W)
\]
with the flat Hermitian metric
\[
  \|(\delta B_1,\delta B_2,\delta I,\delta J)\|^2
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_K(\delta B_1\delta B_1^\dagger)
  +\operatorname{tr}_K(\delta B_2\delta B_2^\dagger)
  +\operatorname{tr}_K(\delta I\delta I^\dagger)
  +\operatorname{tr}_W(\delta J^\dagger\delta J).
\]
On the free, stable, regular locus
\[
  Z_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}
  :=
  \{\mu_{\mathbb C}=0,\ \mu_{\mathbb R}=0\}_{\rm stable,reg}
  \subset \mathbb A_{k,N_c},
\]
let \(V_X\subset T_X Z_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}\) be the tangent space to the
\(U(K)\)-orbit and let
\[
  H_X:=V_X^\perp\cap T_XZ_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}.
\]
The differential of the quotient map identifies \(H_X\) with the tangent
space of the smooth quotient at \([X]\).  The \emph{ADHM quotient density}
\(\dd\nu_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}\) is the Riemannian density induced by this
quotient metric.
\end{definition}

This density is a finite-dimensional object.  It is not yet the complete
semiclassical Yang--Mills weight: the latter also contains the nonzero-mode
determinants, coupling-coordinate convention, operator renormalization, and
the treatment of the boundary strata.  The quotient density is the part of
the measure fixed by the classical zero-mode geometry.

\begin{proposition}[Coarea form of the ADHM quotient density]
\label{prop:adhm-quotient-density-coarea}
Fix an \(\operatorname{Ad}\)-invariant inner product on
\(\mathfrak u(K)\) and let \(\dd\operatorname{vol}_{U(K)}\) be the
associated left-invariant Riemannian density on \(U(K)\).  Choose an
orthonormal basis \(\{\xi_A\}_{A=1}^{k^2}\) of \(\mathfrak u(K)\), and let
\(\xi_A^\#(X)\) denote the corresponding infinitesimal orbit vector at
\(X\in Z_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}\).  Define the orbit Gram matrix
\[
  M_{AB}(X):=\langle \xi_A^\#(X),\xi_B^\#(X)\rangle_{\mathbb A}.
\]
If \(F\) is a compactly supported smooth \(U(K)\)-invariant function on
\(Z_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}\), then
\begin{equation}
  \int_{\mathcal M_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}}
  F([X])\,\dd\nu_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}([X])
  =
  \frac1{\operatorname{Vol}U(K)}
  \int_{Z_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}}
  \frac{F(X)}{\sqrt{\det M(X)}}\,\dd\operatorname{vol}_{Z}(X).
  \label{eq:adhm-quotient-density-levelset-coarea}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, if \(J_\mu(X)\) is the coarea Jacobian
\[
  J_\mu(X)
  :=
  \det\!\bigl(D\mu_XD\mu_X^\ast\bigr)^{1/2},
  \qquad
  \mu=(\operatorname{Re}\mu_{\mathbb C},
       \operatorname{Im}\mu_{\mathbb C},\mu_{\mathbb R}),
\]
then the same density may be represented distributionally on the ambient
affine space by
\begin{equation}
  \int_{\mathcal M_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}}
  F\,\dd\nu_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}
  =
  \frac1{\operatorname{Vol}U(K)}
  \int_{\mathbb A_{k,N_c}}
  F(X)\,
  \delta(\mu(X))\,
  \frac{J_\mu(X)}{\sqrt{\det M(X)}}\,
  \dd X ,
  \label{eq:adhm-quotient-density-ambient-coarea}
\end{equation}
on any compact set where the regularity and freeness hypotheses hold.  The
delta function in \eqref{eq:adhm-quotient-density-ambient-coarea} is
normalized with respect to the Euclidean density on the real target of
\(\mu\) induced by the same trace pairings.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The restricted flat metric on \(Z_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}\) gives the orthogonal
splitting
\[
  T_XZ_{k,N_c}^{\rm reg}=H_X\oplus V_X.
\]
In local product coordinates \((y,\theta)\), where \(y\) is a quotient
coordinate and \(\theta^A\) are group coordinates near the identity, the
metric is block diagonal at the chosen slice if \(H_X\) is used as the
horizontal distribution.  The horizontal block is the quotient metric, while
the vertical block is precisely \(M_{AB}(X)\).  Therefore the Riemannian
density on the level set factorizes as
\[
  \dd\operatorname{vol}_{Z}
  =
  \sqrt{\det M(X)}\,
  \dd\operatorname{vol}_{U(K)}\,
  \dd\nu_{k,N_c}^{\rm ADHM}.
\]
Because the flat metric and the moment-map equations are \(U(K)\)-invariant,
\(\det M(X)\) is constant along each orbit.  Integrating an invariant
function over the group coordinate gives
\eqref{eq:adhm-quotient-density-levelset-coarea}.

For the ambient-space expression, apply the ordinary finite-dimensional
coarea formula to the submersion \(\mu\):
\[
  \int_{\mathbb A_{k,N_c}}
  h(X)\,\delta(\mu(X))\,J_\mu(X)\,\dd X
  =
  \int_{\mu^{-1}(0)} h(X)\,\dd\operatorname{vol}_{Z}(X)
\]
for compactly supported \(h\) in the regular locus.  Taking
\(h=F/\sqrt{\det M}\) gives
\eqref{eq:adhm-quotient-density-ambient-coarea}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Classical zero-mode density versus determinant weight]
\label{rem:adhm-density-versus-determinants}
Equations
\eqref{eq:adhm-quotient-density-levelset-coarea} and
\eqref{eq:adhm-quotient-density-ambient-coarea} define the classical
zero-mode density on the smooth ADHM quotient.  They do not decide whether
the instanton contribution to a continuum QFT observable is finite near a
small-instanton stratum.  That question depends on the nonzero-mode
determinant, the operator insertions, and the renormalization scheme.  This
is why the Uhlenbeck boundary is part of the measure problem rather than an
afterthought about topology.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Charge-one ADHM chart and the instanton orientation orbit]
\label{prop:k-one-adhm-chart-orientation-orbit}
Let \(W\simeq\mathbb C^{N_c}\) with its Hermitian inner product and let
\(K\simeq\mathbb C\).  The centered charge-one framed \(U(N_c)\) ADHM data
are a pair
\[
  I\in\operatorname{Hom}(W,K),
  \qquad
  J\in\operatorname{Hom}(K,W),
\]
obeying
\begin{equation}
  IJ=0,
  \qquad
  II^\dagger-J^\dagger J=0,
  \label{eq:k-one-adhm-moment-equations}
\end{equation}
with \((I,J)\ne(0,0)\), modulo the \(U(1)\) action
\[
  (I,J)\longmapsto(e^{\ii\alpha}I,e^{-\ii\alpha}J).
\]
After identifying \(I\) with a vector \(u\in W\) by the Hermitian metric and
writing \(J\) as a vector \(v\in W\), the equations are
\begin{equation}
  \langle u,v\rangle=0,
  \qquad
  \|u\|=\|v\|=\rho>0 .
  \label{eq:k-one-adhm-orthonormal-pair}
\end{equation}
For fixed \(\rho\), the quotient is the homogeneous space
\begin{equation}
  V_2(\mathbb C^{N_c})/U(1)
  \simeq
  U(N_c)/(U(N_c-2)\times U(1)),
  \label{eq:k-one-adhm-orientation-orbit}
\end{equation}
where \(V_2(\mathbb C^{N_c})\) is the Stiefel manifold of ordered
orthonormal two-frames, \(U(0)\) is interpreted as the trivial group when
\(N_c=2\), and the \(U(1)\) in the quotient acts by the common phase of the
two frame vectors.  Equivalently, for \(N_c\ge3\), after
restricting to the special unitary framing, the connected orbit has the same
local form
\[
  SU(N_c)/(SU(N_c-2)\times U(1))
\]
up to a finite central quotient.  For \(N_c=2\) this specialization is the
usual \(SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2\) orientation orbit.  In every case the real
dimension is \(4N_c-5\).  Adding the size \(\rho\) and the four center
coordinates gives the local real dimension \(4N_c\) of the charge-one
instanton moduli space.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(K\simeq\mathbb C\), the ADHM endomorphisms \(B_1,B_2\in
\operatorname{End}(K)\) are complex numbers.  They commute and therefore
contribute no centered ADHM equation; their real and imaginary parts are the
four center coordinates of the instanton.  The remaining ADHM equations are
exactly \eqref{eq:k-one-adhm-moment-equations}: \(IJ=0\) is the complex
moment-map equation and \(II^\dagger-J^\dagger J=0\) is the real moment-map
equation for the \(U(1)\) acting on \(K\).

Use the Hermitian metric to identify the row \(I\) with a vector \(u\in W\)
by \(I(w)=\langle u,w\rangle\), and identify \(J\) with \(v=J(1)\).  Then
\(IJ=I(v)=\langle u,v\rangle\), while
\[
  II^\dagger=\|u\|^2\operatorname{id}_{K},
  \qquad
  J^\dagger J=\|v\|^2\operatorname{id}_{K}.
\]
Thus \eqref{eq:k-one-adhm-moment-equations} is equivalent to
\eqref{eq:k-one-adhm-orthonormal-pair}.  Since \((I,J)\ne(0,0)\), the common
norm is a positive number \(\rho\).  Dividing by \(\rho\) gives an ordered
orthonormal two-frame \((u/\rho,v/\rho)\).

The \(U(1)\) ADHM quotient sends
\((u,v)\mapsto(e^{-\ii\alpha}u,e^{-\ii\alpha}v)\), namely the common phase of
the ordered frame.  The unitary group \(U(N_c)\) acts transitively on ordered
orthonormal two-frames.  The stabilizer of the common-phase equivalence class
of the reference frame \((e_1,e_2)\) consists of an arbitrary unitary
transformation on the orthogonal complement together with a common phase on
the two displayed vectors, hence is \(U(N_c-2)\times U(1)\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:k-one-adhm-orientation-orbit}.  Its dimension is
\[
  \dim_\mathbb R U(N_c)
  -
  \dim_\mathbb R U(N_c-2)
  -
  \dim_\mathbb R U(1)
  =
  N_c^2-(N_c-2)^2-1
  =
  4N_c-5 .
\]
The size contributes one more dimension and the center contributes four.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Cone structure and the small-instanton boundary.}
On the positive-size charge-one stratum, the centered ADHM quotient is the
metric cone over the orientation orbit
\[
  \mathcal O_{N_c}
  =
  U(N_c)/(U(N_c-2)\times U(1)).
\]
With the quotient metric induced from the flat metric
\(\|dI\|^2+\|dJ\|^2\), its volume density has the local radial form
\begin{equation}
  \rho^{4N_c-5}\,\dd\rho\,\dd\Omega_{N_c},
  \label{eq:k-one-adhm-classical-cone-density}
\end{equation}
for an invariant density \(\dd\Omega_{N_c}\) on \(\mathcal O_{N_c}\).  The
endpoint \(\rho=0\) is the small-instanton boundary: the two-frame collapses
to \((I,J)=(0,0)\), the stabilizer jumps, and the positive-size quotient no
longer supplies a smooth manifold chart.
The equations \eqref{eq:k-one-adhm-orthonormal-pair} are homogeneous in
\((u,v)\).  Hence every positive-size solution is uniquely written as
\(\rho(\hat u,\hat v)\), with \((\hat u,\hat v)\in V_2(\mathbb C^{N_c})\)
and \(\rho>0\), modulo the same common phase.  The flat metric on
\((I,J)\) scales as
\[
  \|dI\|^2+\|dJ\|^2
  =
  c\,\dd\rho^2+\rho^2\,\dd s^2_{\mathcal O_{N_c}}
\]
for a positive constant \(c\) depending only on the normalization of
\(\rho\).  A metric cone over a \(d\)-dimensional base has radial volume
density proportional to \(\rho^d\dd\rho\) times the base density.  Here
\(d=\dim_\mathbb R\mathcal O_{N_c}=4N_c-5\), giving
\eqref{eq:k-one-adhm-classical-cone-density}.  At \(\rho=0\) the frame
vectors vanish and no two-frame remains; this is precisely the
finite-dimensional ADHM model of the small-instanton degeneration.

The regulated collective-coordinate measure is the one constructed in
Theorem~\ref{thm:collective-coordinate-morse-bott-measure}.  In a finite
gauge-fixed regulator its local bosonic factor has the form
\begin{equation}
  \dd\mu_{k=1,\Lambda}^{\rm bos}
  =
  \frac{\Delta_{\rm FP,\Lambda}(A_z)}
       {\operatorname{Vol}(\Gamma_z)}
  \frac{\sqrt{\det G^{(\Lambda)}_{ab}(z)}\,
        \dd^4a\,\dd\rho\,
        \dd\Omega_{SU(N_c)/(SU(N_c-2)\times U(1))}}
       {\det{}'_\Lambda(L_{A_z})^{1/2}},
  \label{eq:one-instanton-bosonic-collective-measure}
\end{equation}
multiplied by the exponential
\eqref{eq:bpst-instanton-exponential-conventions}.  Here \(z\) denotes the
center, size, and orientation coordinates; \(G^{(\Lambda)}_{ab}\) is the
zero-mode Gram matrix induced by the regulated gauge-field inner product;
\(L_{A_z}\) is the gauge-fixed quadratic operator on nonzero bosonic
fluctuations; and \(\Gamma_z\) is the residual stabilizer left by the chosen
slice.  Equation
\eqref{eq:one-instanton-bosonic-collective-measure} is not yet a continuum
formula.  Taking \(\Lambda\to\infty\) requires combining the determinant, the
coupling coordinate, and the normalization of the local operator insertions
in a specified renormalization scheme.  What is scheme independent at this
stage is the topological sector, the zero-mode count, the finite-regulator
construction of the measure, and the exponential action.

\paragraph{Finite-regulator nonzero-mode determinants.}
The symbol ``determinant constant'' hides several different objects unless
the regulator is kept explicit.  At fixed \(\Lambda\), in a gauge slice near
an instanton \(A_z\), let
\[
  L_{\Lambda,z}^{\rm bos}:N_z\mathcal M_\Lambda\longrightarrow
  N_z\mathcal M_\Lambda
\]
be the bosonic Hessian on the normal modes to the collective-coordinate
manifold, and let
\[
  M_{\Lambda,z}^{\rm gh}:C_{\Lambda,z}^{\rm gh}\longrightarrow
  C_{\Lambda,z}^{\rm gh}
\]
be the finite Faddeev--Popov ghost operator with residual stabilizer zero
modes removed.  For a fermion multiplet \(R\), write
\[
  \mathcal D_{\Lambda,z}^{R}:\mathcal S_{\Lambda,z}^{R,+}
  \longrightarrow \mathcal S_{\Lambda,z}^{R,-}
\]
for the regulated chiral Dirac operator.  Its zero modes are not included in
the nonzero-mode determinant; they are the Grassmann collective coordinates
whose saturation is described below.  The nonzero-mode determinant factor is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)
  :=
  \frac{\det{}' M_{\Lambda,z}^{\rm gh}}
       {\det{}' M_{\Lambda,0}^{\rm gh}}
  \left(
    \frac{\det L_{\Lambda,0}^{\rm bos}}
         {\det{}' L_{\Lambda,z}^{\rm bos}}
  \right)^{1/2}
  \bigotimes_R
  \frac{\operatorname{Pf}{}' \mathcal D_{\Lambda,z}^{R}}
       {\operatorname{Pf} \mathcal D_{\Lambda,0}^{R}} .
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-nonzero-determinant-factor}
\end{equation}
The reference subscript \(0\) denotes the corresponding finite-regulator
operator in the chosen topologically trivial background and in the same
normalization of fields and gauge-group density.  The primes mean that
collective-coordinate zero modes, residual gauge zero modes, and fermion zero
modes have been removed before taking the finite determinant or Pfaffian.

For a vectorlike Dirac pair, after choosing the standard conjugate pairing,
the fermion factor in
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-nonzero-determinant-factor} is an
ordinary scalar.  For a genuinely chiral fermion it is a vector in the
appropriate determinant or Pfaffian line over the background \(A_z\); a
scalar appears only after pairing this vector with the fermion zero-mode
insertions and with any anomaly-inflow or determinant-line trivialization
specified by the theory.  This is the same determinant-line convention used
in Chapter~\ref{ch:curved-eta-invariants-global-anomalies}.  Thus an instanton
amplitude is not a number until the anomaly-line pairing and zero-mode
saturation have also been specified.

With this notation the finite-regulator one-instanton contribution in a
single smooth chart, for insertions whose fermion zero-mode and anomaly-line
pairings have been specified, has the finite chart expression
\begin{equation}
  \dd\mu_{\Lambda,Q=1}(z)
  =
  \exp[-S_\Lambda(A_z)]\,
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)\,
  \dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}(z),
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-measure-density}
\end{equation}
where \(\dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}\) is the zero-mode density from
\eqref{eq:collective-coordinate-bosonic-measure}, or equivalently the ADHM
quotient density of Definition~\ref{def:adhm-quotient-density} after matching
the ADHM coordinates to the regulated gauge-field slice.  Formula
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-measure-density} is a finite-dimensional
statement.  The continuum expression is the assertion that the product of
\(\mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}\), the coupling coordinate in
\(S_\Lambda\), and the renormalized operator insertions has a limit in the
chosen scheme.

The finite Gaussian origin of
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-nonzero-determinant-factor} is as
follows.  A real bosonic normal integral with positive quadratic form \(L\)
gives \((\det L)^{-1/2}\) up to the universal power of \(2\pi\) already
accounted for in the stationary-phase convention.  A complex ghost Berezin
integral gives \(\det M^{\rm gh}\).  A real fermionic quadratic form gives a
Pfaffian; a complex chiral determinant is the corresponding determinant-line
version of the same finite exterior-algebra construction.  Removing zero
modes before taking these determinants is not optional: the removed
directions are precisely the collective coordinates or the fermion variables
which must be integrated or saturated separately.

Finally, changing the finite-regulator Lagrangian by a local counterterm
\(C_\Lambda[A]\) multiplies
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-measure-density} by
\(\exp[-C_\Lambda(A_z)+C_\Lambda(0)]\).  If the one-instanton chart reduces
this difference to
\[
  C_\Lambda(A_z)-C_\Lambda(0)
  =
  c_0(\Lambda)+c_1(\Lambda)\log(\mu\rho)
  +R_\Lambda(\rho,U),
\]
then \(c_0\) changes the scheme-dependent overall constant, \(c_1\) changes
the displayed power of \(\mu\rho\), and the remainder \(R_\Lambda\) is part
of the unresolved boundary and continuum-limit analysis.  Counterterms are
therefore not an after-the-fact correction to a pre-existing determinant
constant; they are part of the regulated path-integral Lagrangian whose
finite determinant factor is being defined.

\paragraph{Small-instanton boundary exponent.}
For the charge-one stratum the Uhlenbeck boundary is reached by
\(\rho\to0\) at fixed center \(a\) and orientation \(U\).  The local boundary
question is therefore a statement about the \(\rho\)-asymptotics of the
finite-regulator density after the nonzero-mode determinant and the
renormalized insertions have been paired.  Let \(\mathcal X\) denote the
insertions under consideration, including the fermion zero-mode factors
needed to obtain a scalar from the determinant line.  A \emph{one-instanton
boundary exponent estimate} for \(\mathcal X\) consists of numbers
\(\beta_{\mathcal X}\in\mathbb R\) and \(\delta>0\), and a locally bounded
function \(A_{\mathcal X}(a,U)\), such that in a fixed compact set of centers
and in a fixed orientation chart the renormalized finite-regulator chart
density has a limit of the form
\begin{equation}
  \dd\mu_{Q=1,\mathcal X}
  =
  C_{\mathcal X}(\mu)\,
  \rho^{\,b_0+\beta_{\mathcal X}-5}
  \bigl(A_{\mathcal X}(a,U)+O(\rho^\delta)\bigr)\,
  \dd^4a\,\dd\rho\,\dd\Omega_{N_c}.
  \label{eq:one-instanton-boundary-exponent-estimate}
\end{equation}
The \(b_0-5\) part is the universal scale and one-loop running content in
\eqref{eq:one-instanton-universal-density}.  The exponent
\(\beta_{\mathcal X}\) records the net small-size contribution of the
renormalized operator insertions, fermion zero-mode wavefunctions, masses or
sources used to saturate zero modes, and any residual determinant factor
which has a finite power-law boundary behavior.  Logarithms may be included
by allowing \(A_{\mathcal X}\) to be a polynomial in \(\log(\mu\rho)\); they
leave the power threshold below unchanged.

On this boundary chart the local integral over \(0<\rho<\epsilon\) is
controlled by
\[
  \int_0^\epsilon
  \rho^{\,b_0+\beta_{\mathcal X}-5}\,
  (\log(\mu\rho))^q\,\dd\rho,
  \qquad q\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0}.
\]
It is locally finite at \(\rho=0\) precisely when
\begin{equation}
  b_0+\beta_{\mathcal X}>4 .
  \label{eq:one-instanton-small-rho-integrability-threshold}
\end{equation}
At equality the logarithmic integral diverges, and below equality the power
integral diverges.  Thus asymptotic freedom, \(b_0>0\), is a statement about
the running coupling at short distance, while small-instanton local
integrability of a chosen observable requires the stronger
observable-dependent condition
\eqref{eq:one-instanton-small-rho-integrability-threshold}.  For the
vacuum-density power with no extra small-\(\rho\) insertion factor
\(\beta_{\mathcal X}=0\), the local boundary test is \(b_0>4\).  Different
correlators have different \(\beta_{\mathcal X}\), and center collisions
with local operator insertions introduce additional boundary faces which
must be analyzed separately from the pure \(\rho\to0\) face.

This boundary exponent estimate is weaker than a complete multi-instanton
measure theorem.  It isolates the analytic estimate needed near a single
collapsed charge.  Higher charge involves simultaneous collisions of several
instantons, partial Uhlenbeck strata
\(\mathcal M_{k-\ell,N_c}\times\operatorname{Sym}^{\ell}(\mathbb R^4)\),
and determinant behavior transverse to each stratum.

\begin{definition}[Uhlenbeck boundary-face integrability datum]
\label{def:uhlenbeck-boundary-face-integrability-datum}
Fix an instanton charge \(k\), a gauge group \(SU(N_c)\), and a scalar
insertion datum \(\mathcal X\) including fermion-zero-mode and anomaly-line
pairings.  A Uhlenbeck boundary-face integrability datum consists of:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item a bubbling charge \(1\le \ell\le k\), a residual smooth charge
  \(k-\ell\), and a chart on the open Uhlenbeck stratum
  \[
    \mathcal M_{k-\ell,N_c}^{\rm smooth}
    \times
    \operatorname{Conf}_{\ell}(\mathbb R^4)/S_\ell ,
  \]
  with the pointlike instanton centers kept away from each other and from
  the local insertions;
  \item transverse gluing scales \(0<\rho_\alpha<\epsilon\), angular variables
  \(\omega_\alpha\), and boundary exponents \(A_\alpha(\mathcal X)\) for
  the \(\ell\) collapsed charge units;
  \item if some pointlike centers approach each other or an insertion support,
  collision distances \(0<d_\beta<\epsilon\) and collision exponents
  \(G_\beta(\mathcal X)\);
  \item a finite-regulator density bound, uniform on compact subsets of the
  residual smooth stratum and angular variables, of the form
  \begin{equation}
    |\dd\mu_{k,\mathcal X}|
    \le
    C\,\dd\nu_{k-\ell}^{\rm sm}
    \prod_{\alpha=1}^{\ell}
      \rho_\alpha^{A_\alpha(\mathcal X)-1}
      (1+|\log\rho_\alpha|)^{q_\alpha}\,
      \dd\rho_\alpha\,\dd\omega_\alpha
    \prod_{\beta}
      d_\beta^{G_\beta(\mathcal X)-1}
      (1+|\log d_\beta|)^{s_\beta}\,
      \dd d_\beta\,\dd\zeta_\beta ,
    \label{eq:uhlenbeck-boundary-face-density-bound}
  \end{equation}
  with nonnegative integers \(q_\alpha,s_\beta\).
\end{enumerate}
The exponents \(A_\alpha\) and \(G_\beta\) are part of the analytic
instanton-measure problem.  Classical ADHM dimension counts identify the
boundary coordinates; determinant, insertion, and counterterm estimates
supply the powers in
\eqref{eq:uhlenbeck-boundary-face-density-bound}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Uhlenbeck face dimension and local integrability budget]
\label{prop:uhlenbeck-face-integrability-budget}
At a smooth residual instanton and \(\ell\) distinct pointlike ideal
instantons, the open Uhlenbeck stratum
\[
  \mathcal M_{k-\ell,N_c}^{\rm smooth}
  \times
  \operatorname{Conf}_{\ell}(\mathbb R^4)/S_\ell
\]
has local real codimension
\begin{equation}
  4\ell(N_c-1)
  \label{eq:uhlenbeck-open-stratum-codimension}
\end{equation}
inside the smooth charge-\(k\) ADHM dimension count.  If the \(\ell\)
pointlike charges are grouped into \(r\) collision clusters, the diagonal
collision stratum contributes the additional codimension \(4(\ell-r)\).

For a boundary-face datum satisfying
\eqref{eq:uhlenbeck-boundary-face-density-bound}, the local absolute
integral over the displayed boundary variables is finite whenever
\begin{equation}
  A_\alpha(\mathcal X)>0
  \quad\text{for every }\alpha,
  \qquad
  G_\beta(\mathcal X)>0
  \quad\text{for every }\beta .
  \label{eq:uhlenbeck-face-integrability-conditions}
\end{equation}
If one displayed exponent is zero and the leading coefficient on that face
has nonzero integral over the remaining bounded variables, the corresponding
local integral has a logarithmic divergence.  If one displayed exponent is
negative under the same nonvanishing condition, the divergence is a power
divergence.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The smooth framed charge-\(k\) dimension from
Proposition~\ref{prop:adhm-quotient-dimension-count} is \(4kN_c\).  On the
open Uhlenbeck stratum with \(\ell\) distinct ideal points, the residual
smooth instanton part has dimension \(4(k-\ell)N_c\), and the unordered
configuration space of \(\ell\) distinct points in \(\mathbb R^4\) has local
dimension \(4\ell\).  The codimension is therefore
\[
  4kN_c-\bigl(4(k-\ell)N_c+4\ell\bigr)
  =
  4\ell(N_c-1),
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:uhlenbeck-open-stratum-codimension}.  If the
\(\ell\) ideal points lie on a diagonal with \(r\) distinct collision
clusters, the point-configuration dimension drops from \(4\ell\) to \(4r\),
so the extra diagonal codimension is \(4(\ell-r)\).

For the integrability statement, the compact residual and angular variables
contribute only a finite multiplicative constant in the stated chart.  Each
scale integral is reduced to
\[
  \int_0^\epsilon
  t^{A-1}(1+|\log t|)^q\,\dd t .
\]
With \(u=-\log t\), this is bounded at \(t=0\) exactly when \(A>0\).  At
\(A=0\) it is the integral of a nonzero polynomial in \(u\) over an infinite
ray, hence logarithmic in the cutoff scale.  For \(A<0\), the antiderivative
has a negative power of the cutoff scale.  Applying this one-dimensional
criterion to every \(\rho_\alpha\) and \(d_\beta\) proves the product
criterion and the two divergence alternatives.
\end{proof}

For the charge-one boundary chart, there are no point-collision variables
and
\[
  A_1(\mathcal X)=b_0+\beta_{\mathcal X}-4 .
\]
Thus Proposition~\ref{prop:uhlenbeck-face-integrability-budget} recovers
\eqref{eq:one-instanton-small-rho-integrability-threshold}.  For \(k>1\) it
also records the extra data which a multi-instanton measure calculation must
provide: every bubbling scale, every diagonal collision of ideal instantons,
and every collision of an ideal instanton center with an inserted operator
has its own exponent.  A formula for the smooth ADHM quotient density and a
one-loop determinant on the principal stratum become a continuum
multi-instanton contribution only after these face estimates are supplied in
one compatible regulator and renormalization scheme.

\subsection{Scale and Running-Coupling Content of the One-Instanton Density}
\label{subsec:one-instanton-density-rg-content}

The preceding finite-regulator formula separates the zero-mode Jacobian from
the determinant on nonzero fluctuations.  The continuum one-instanton density
contains three universal pieces which can be fixed before computing the
scheme-dependent determinant constant.  First, the moduli
\[
  a^\mu\in\mathbb R^4,\qquad \rho\in\mathbb R_{>0}
\]
are the center and size of the instanton, while the remaining
\(4N_c-5\) coordinates are dimensionless orientation coordinates on the
embedded \(SU(2)\) orbit.  The translation-size part of a scale-covariant
local density must therefore be
\[
  \frac{\dd^4a\,\dd\rho}{\rho^5}.
\]
Indeed \(a\) and \(\rho\) both have length dimension one, so
\(\dd^4a\,\dd\rho/\rho^5\) is dimensionless and is invariant under the
simultaneous dilation \(a\mapsto\lambda a\), \(\rho\mapsto\lambda\rho\).
The power \(\rho^{-5}\) is the four-dimensional center plus one-dimensional
scale volume element.  It is the product of the zero-mode Jacobian and the
nonzero-mode determinant after their regulator-dependent powers of \(\rho\)
have been combined.

Second, the number of bosonic zero modes is \(m=4N_c\).  In the
semiclassical expansion of an action \(S[A]/g_{\rm ht}^2\), replacing the
zero-mode amplitudes by collective coordinates contributes one factor
\(g_{\rm ht}^{-1}\) for each bosonic zero mode.  Equivalently, after absorbing
normalization constants independent of \(g_{\rm ht}\) into the orientation
measure and determinant convention, the zero-mode normalization supplies
\[
  g_{\rm ht}^{-4N_c}
  =
  \left(\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2}\right)^{2N_c}
  \times
  \hbox{constant}.
\]
This is the origin of the power multiplying the one-instanton density.  The
constant depends on the precise normalization of the path-integral density
and of the invariant volume on the orientation orbit.

\paragraph{One-loop running factor in the one-instanton density.}
Use the common half-trace coupling \(g_{\rm ht}(\mu)\) in the BPST
subsection, and suppose the one-instanton density is renormalized in a
mass-independent scheme through one loop.  Let
\begin{equation}
  \mu\frac{\dd g_{\rm ht}}{\dd\mu}
  =
  -\frac{b_0}{16\pi^2}g_{\rm ht}^3+O(g_{\rm ht}^5),
  \qquad
  b_0=\frac{11}{3}N_c-\frac23N_f .
  \label{eq:instanton-density-beta0-definition}
\end{equation}
Then the part of the one-instanton measure fixed by scale covariance,
zero-mode normalization, and one-loop RG invariance is
\begin{equation}
  \dd\mu_{1}^{\rm univ}
  =
  C_{\mathcal S,N_c,N_f}\,
  \frac{\dd^4a\,\dd\rho}{\rho^5}\,
  \dd\Omega_{N_c}\,
  \left(\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right)^{2N_c}
  (\mu\rho)^{b_0}
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}+\ii\theta\right]
  \left(1+O(g_{\rm ht}^2)\right).
  \label{eq:one-instanton-universal-density}
\end{equation}
Here \(\dd\Omega_{N_c}\) is a chosen invariant density on the local
orientation orbit
\[
  SU(N_c)/(SU(N_c-2)\times U(1)),
\]
with the evident \(SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2\) specialization when \(N_c=2\).
The coefficient \(C_{\mathcal S,N_c,N_f}\) is a finite constant depending on
the regulator, subtraction convention, and normalization of
\(\dd\Omega_{N_c}\); obtaining it requires the full renormalized determinant
calculation in the chosen scheme.
The factors
\(\dd^4a\,\dd\rho/\rho^5\), \(\dd\Omega_{N_c}\), and
\((8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2)^{2N_c}\) have just been fixed by dimension,
orientation invariance, and the \(4N_c\) bosonic zero modes.  It remains to
determine the power of \(\mu\rho\) at one loop.  Write the unknown power as
\(\alpha\) and apply \(\mu\dd/\dd\mu\) to the logarithm of the density factor
\[
  g_{\rm ht}^{-4N_c}
  (\mu\rho)^\alpha
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right].
\]
The derivative is
\[
  \alpha
  -4N_c\,\frac{\beta(g_{\rm ht})}{g_{\rm ht}}
  +
  \frac{16\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^3}\,\beta(g_{\rm ht}) .
\]
Substituting \eqref{eq:instanton-density-beta0-definition} gives
\[
  \alpha-b_0+O(g_{\rm ht}^2).
\]
RG invariance of a physical one-instanton contribution through one loop
therefore fixes \(\alpha=b_0\).  The derivative of the zero-mode power
starts at order \(g_{\rm ht}^2\), so it belongs to the next perturbative order
and is absorbed into the higher-loop correction multiplying
\eqref{eq:one-instanton-universal-density}.  The finite constant
\(C_{\mathcal S,N_c,N_f}\) is outside this argument because RG invariance
fixes scale dependence, while the constant term is set by the actual
regularized determinant and measure normalization.

\begin{proposition}[Heat-kernel source of the instanton logarithm]
\label{prop:instanton-heat-kernel-beta0-logarithm}
In the same background-field gauge and half-trace convention, the logarithmic
part of the charge-\(k\) nonzero-mode fluctuation determinant has
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{\log\mu}\log\mathcal W_{\rm log}^{\rm nz}
  =
  k\left[
    \frac{11}{3}C_2(G)
    -\frac{4}{3}\sum_{\text{\rm Dirac }R}T(R)
  \right].
  \label{eq:instanton-heat-kernel-beta0-logarithm}
\end{equation}
For vectorlike \(SU(N_c)\) QCD with \(N_f\) Dirac fundamentals this coefficient
is \(k b_0\), with \(b_0\) as in
\eqref{eq:instanton-density-beta0-definition}.  Hence, in a charge-one BPST
chart,
\begin{equation}
  \log\mathcal W_{\rm log}^{\rm nz}(a,\rho,U;\mu)
  =
  b_0\log(\mu\rho)+\log\mathcal W_{\rm fin}^{\rm nz}(U;\mathcal S),
  \label{eq:instanton-nonzero-mode-log-mu-rho}
\end{equation}
up to higher-loop and local finite-subtraction terms in the chosen scheme.
Equivalently, if \(X_\kappa=8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2(\kappa)\) and
\(X_\Lambda=X_\mu+b_0\log(\Lambda/\mu)\) at one loop, then
\begin{equation}
  \exp[-X_\Lambda+\ii\theta]\,
  (\Lambda\rho)^{b_0}
  =
  \exp[-X_\mu+\ii\theta]\,
  (\mu\rho)^{b_0}.
  \label{eq:instanton-cutoff-renormalized-scale-trade}
\end{equation}
The factor \((\mu\rho)^{b_0}\) in
\eqref{eq:one-instanton-universal-density} is therefore the logarithmic
shadow of the nonzero-mode fluctuation spectrum plus coupling
renormalization, while the finite constant still requires the full spectral
determinant calculation.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The small-proper-time expansion of
\eqref{eq:proper-time-instanton-determinant-ratio} is local in the gauge
background.  With background-field gauge fixing, the vector fluctuation and
ghost complex contribute the coefficient \((11/3)C_2(G)\) multiplying the
topological charge in the logarithmic counterterm, and a Dirac fermion in
representation \(R\) contributes \(-(4/3)T(R)\).  Summing the fields gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-heat-kernel-beta0-logarithm}.  For a fundamental Dirac
fermion of \(SU(N_c)\), \(T(\square)=1/2\), so the matter term is
\(-2N_f/3\), and the total coefficient is
\((11/3)N_c-(2/3)N_f=b_0\).

A BPST connection with size \(\rho\) is obtained from the unit-size connection
by translation, global orientation, and dilation.  After the zero modes are
removed, the logarithmic scale dependence of the determinant can therefore
depend on the size only through the dimensionless combination \(\mu\rho\),
apart from finite scheme-dependent terms.  This gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-nonzero-mode-log-mu-rho}.  Finally, the one-loop running of
\(X_\kappa\) gives \(X_\Lambda=X_\mu+b_0\log(\Lambda/\mu)\); substituting this
identity into the cutoff expression proves
\eqref{eq:instanton-cutoff-renormalized-scale-trade}.
\end{proof}

Fermion zero modes are controlled by the index theorem already stated in
\eqref{eq:dirac-index-anomaly}.  In the conventional half-trace notation, a
left-handed Weyl fermion in representation \(R\) has
\begin{equation}
  n_+-n_-=2T_R\,k,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}_R(T_R^aT_R^b)=T_R\delta^{ab}.
  \label{eq:instanton-index-dynkin}
\end{equation}
For a fundamental Weyl fermion of \(SU(N_c)\), \(T_\square=1/2\), so a
\(k=1\) instanton has one normalizable zero mode of the chirality selected by
the self-dual background.  The anti-instanton has the opposite chirality.  In
the trace-delta convention the numerical value called \(T_R\) is doubled, but
the topological density and the coupling coordinate are correspondingly
rewritten; the integer kernel dimension in
\eqref{eq:instanton-index-dynkin} is the invariant statement.

The zero modes determine the Grassmann selection rule.  Expand a massless
fermion in an instanton background as
\[
  \psi(x)=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_0}\xi_\alpha\psi_{0,\alpha}(x)
  +\sum_{\lambda\neq0}\xi_\lambda\psi_\lambda(x),
\]
where \(\psi_{0,\alpha}\) are the zero modes and \(\xi_\alpha\) are odd
coefficients.  The quadratic fermion action contains no term involving
\(\xi_\alpha\).  Therefore the Berezin integral over zero-mode variables
vanishes unless operator insertions supply every missing \(\xi_\alpha\).
This is the finite-dimensional Berezin rule \(\int\dd\xi\,1=0\),
\(\int\dd\xi\,\xi=1\) applied after the spectral decomposition of the
regulated fermion field.

\begin{proposition}[Instanton sectors as physical correlator contributions]
\label{prop:instanton-sector-physical-correlator-contribution}
Fix a finite regulator, a \(Q=1\) instanton chart with collective coordinate
\(z=(a,\rho,U)\), smooth chart domain \(\mathcal U_{Q=1}\), and
renormalized insertions \(\mathcal O_1,\ldots,\mathcal O_r\).  After gauge
fixing, collective coordinate separation, and integration of nonzero
fluctuations, the instanton sector contribution has the form
\begin{equation}
  \bigl\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_r\bigr\rangle_{Q=1,\Lambda}
  =
  \int_{\mathcal U_{Q=1}}
  \ee^{-S_\Lambda(A_z)+\ii\theta}\,
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)\,
  \mathcal Z_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}^{0}(z)\,
  \dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}(z),
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-correlator-assembly}
\end{equation}
provided the determinant/Pfaffian line has been paired with the fermion
zero-mode and anomaly-line data to produce a scalar density.  Here
\(\mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}\) is the nonzero-mode determinant datum from
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-nonzero-determinant-factor}, while
\(\mathcal Z_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}^{0}(z)\) is the finite Berezin coefficient
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal Z_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}^{0}(z)
  =
  \int\dd\xi_{n_0}\cdots\dd\xi_1\,
  \mathcal O_{1,\Lambda}^{0}(z,\xi)\cdots
  \mathcal O_{r,\Lambda}^{0}(z,\xi)\,
  \exp[-S_{\rm lift,\Lambda}^{0}(z,\xi)] .
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-saturation-functional}
\end{equation}
The superscript \(0\) means that each insertion and each mass, Yukawa, or
source term has been restricted to the zero-mode subspace.  If the integrand
in \eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-saturation-functional} does not contain the
monomial \(\xi_1\cdots\xi_{n_0}\), then
\(\mathcal Z_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}^{0}(z)=0\).  If it does contain that
monomial, the coefficient of that monomial is the only part of the insertions
which is seen by the instanton sector.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At finite cutoff the gauge-fixed fields split into collective coordinates,
normal bosonic modes, ghosts, fermion zero modes, and fermion nonzero modes.
Gaussian integration over the normal bosonic and ghost variables gives the
determinant factors already isolated in
\(\mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}\); Gaussian or Pfaffian integration over
fermion nonzero modes gives the remaining nonzero-mode determinant-line
factor.  None of these operations integrates over the zero-mode variables
\(\xi_\alpha\), because the quadratic fermion operator vanishes on them.
The only finite-dimensional integral left in the fermionic zero-mode sector is
\eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-saturation-functional}.  Berezin integration
extracts the coefficient of the ordered top monomial
\(\xi_1\cdots\xi_{n_0}\).  All terms missing at least one zero-mode variable
therefore vanish, while terms containing the top monomial contribute exactly
their coefficient.  Multiplying this coefficient by the bosonic
collective-coordinate density and the nonzero-mode determinant gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-correlator-assembly}.
\end{proof}

Thus the moduli space is not the physical answer; it is the domain of a
semiclassical amplitude.  The physical question is which insertions, masses,
or sources saturate the fermion zero modes, what nonzero-mode determinant and
counterterm scheme define \(\mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}\), and whether the
resulting \(a,\rho,U\) integral is finite at its boundary faces.

For vectorlike QCD with \(N_f\) massless Dirac fundamentals, a \(Q=1\)
instanton supplies one zero mode for each flavor in one chirality sector.  The
semiclassical functional integral therefore generates the following leading
local term in the derivative expansion around external fields whose variation
scale is much larger than \(\rho\):
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_{\rm inst}
  =
  \int_0^\infty\frac{\dd\rho}{\rho^5}\,
  C_{N_c,N_f}(\rho,\mu)
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}+\ii\theta\right)
  \det_{1\le f,f'\le N_f}
  \left[
    \rho^3\bar\psi_{R f}\psi_{L f'}
  \right]
  +\hbox{anti-instanton conjugate}
  \label{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local}
\end{equation}
after integrating over the instanton center and color orientation and after
contracting spin and color indices as dictated by the zero-mode wavefunctions
and gauge invariance.  The determinant is over flavor indices; it is the local
tensor compatible with the nonabelian chiral flavor symmetry.  For flavor
diagonal external sources it reduces to the corresponding alternating sum of
products of \(N_f\) bilinears.  At one loop
\[
  C_{N_c,N_f}(\rho,\mu)
  =
  C^{(0)}_{N_c,N_f}
  \left(\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right)^{2N_c}
  (\rho\mu)^{b_0}
  \left(1+O(g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu))\right),
  \qquad
  b_0=\frac{11}{3}N_c-\frac23N_f,
\]
where \(b_0\) is defined by
\(\mu\,\dd g_{\rm ht}/\dd\mu=-b_0g_{\rm ht}^3/(16\pi^2)+O(g_{\rm ht}^5)\).
The scale and coupling dependence is the universal content of the running-factor
calculation above; the constant
\(C^{(0)}_{N_c,N_f}\) is fixed only after choosing the determinant and
orientation-volume normalization.  The dimensionless factors
\(\rho^3\bar\psi_R\psi_L\) in
\eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local} record the normalization of the
external zero-mode wavefunctions in the local limit; changing the
regularization scheme changes \(C^{(0)}_{N_c,N_f}\) and higher-loop factors,
not the zero-mode tensor or the selection rule.  When the external fields vary
on the scale \(\rho\), the same zero-mode integration gives a nonlocal kernel
rather than the local determinant in
\eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local}.  Its regulator-independent
selection rule is
\[
  \Delta Q_A=2N_f
\]
for the anomalous axial charge, in agreement with the integrated anomaly.
Small quark masses may replace missing fermion insertions by mass factors,
because the mass term pairs the corresponding zero-mode variables.

\begin{proposition}[Flavor determinant from the instanton zero modes]
\label{prop:qcd-thooft-determinant-zero-mode-amplitude}
In the \(Q=1\) sector of vectorlike \(SU(N_c)\) QCD, let
\(\lambda_f,\widetilde\lambda_f\), \(f=1,\ldots,N_f\), denote the two
Grassmann zero-mode coefficients paired by the local source matrix
\(B=(B_{ff'})\).  Choose the zero-mode orientation so that
\begin{equation}
  \int
  \prod_{f=N_f}^{1}\dd\widetilde\lambda_f\,\dd\lambda_f\,
  \exp\!\left(
    \sum_{f,f'=1}^{N_f}
    \widetilde\lambda_f B_{ff'}\lambda_{f'}
  \right)
  =
  \det B .
  \label{eq:thooft-vertex-berezin-determinant}
\end{equation}
For slowly varying external quark fields,
\(B_{ff'}=\rho^3\bar\psi_{Rf}\psi_{Lf'}\), and
\eqref{eq:thooft-vertex-berezin-determinant} is precisely the flavor
determinant in \eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local}.  If instead all
zero modes are saturated by the quark mass term, the same finite-dimensional
calculation replaces \(B\) by the mass-overlap matrix
\(B^{(m)}_{ff'}(z)\).  Its phase is the phase of \(\det M\), so the
one-instanton vacuum term has phase
\(\exp[\ii(\theta+\arg\det M)]\), up to the real positive overlap and
determinant normalizations.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Expand the exponential in
\eqref{eq:thooft-vertex-berezin-determinant}.  Every contributing top-degree
term must contain each \(\widetilde\lambda_f\) once and each \(\lambda_f\)
once.  Such a term is specified by a permutation \(\sigma\in S_{N_f}\):
\[
  \prod_{f=1}^{N_f}
  \widetilde\lambda_f B_{f,\sigma(f)}\lambda_{\sigma(f)} .
\]
Reordering the \(\lambda_{\sigma(f)}\) variables into the chosen Berezin
orientation gives the sign \(\operatorname{sgn}\sigma\).  Berezin integration
therefore extracts
\[
  \sum_{\sigma\in S_{N_f}}
  \operatorname{sgn}\sigma
  \prod_{f=1}^{N_f}B_{f,\sigma(f)}
  =
  \det B .
\]
For \(B_{ff'}=\rho^3\bar\psi_{Rf}\psi_{Lf'}\), this gives the local
\(2N_f\)-fermion operator.  If \(B\) is supplied by the mass term, each
zero-mode overlap is real after the same regulator and zero-mode orientation
have been fixed, so the only flavor phase is \(\arg\det M\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Mixed mass-source expansion of the instanton determinant]
\label{prop:instanton-mass-source-minor-expansion}
In the same \(Q=1\) QCD zero-mode sector, let \(M\) be the mass-overlap
matrix and let \(B\) be the matrix supplied by external bilinear sources or
by zero-mode-projected fermion wave packets.  With
\([N_f]=\{1,\ldots,N_f\}\), write \(M_{I^c,J^c}\) for the submatrix obtained
by deleting the rows \(I\subset [N_f]\) and columns \(J\subset [N_f]\), and
write \(B_{I,J}\) for the corresponding \(I\times J\) source submatrix, both
with increasing orders; empty determinants are \(1\).  Then
\begin{equation}
  \det(M+B)
  =
  \sum_{r=0}^{N_f}
  \sum_{\substack{I,J\subset [N_f]\\ |I|=|J|=r}}
  (-1)^{\sum_{i\in I}i+\sum_{j\in J}j}\,
  \det M_{I^c,J^c}\,
  \det B_{I,J}.
  \label{eq:instanton-mass-source-minor-expansion}
\end{equation}
Thus a correlator with \(r\) zero-mode-paired external insertions is not
obtained by appending those insertions to an already-formed vacuum
determinant.  It is the degree-\(r\) part of the same finite Berezin
determinant; the unobserved \(N_f-r\) zero-mode pairs must be saturated by the
complementary mass minor.  Equivalently, for increasing
\(I=(i_1<\cdots<i_r)\), \(J=(j_1<\cdots<j_r)\), and
\(\pi\in S_r\), the coefficient of
\(\prod_{\ell=1}^r B_{i_\ell,j_{\pi(\ell)}}\) in \(\det(M+B)\) is
\begin{equation}
  (-1)^{\sum I+\sum J}\,
  \operatorname{sgn}(\pi)\,
  \det M_{I^c,J^c}.
  \label{eq:instanton-source-differentiation-minor}
\end{equation}
For \(N_f=2\),
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \det(M+B)
  &=
  \det M
  +M_{22}B_{11}-M_{21}B_{12}
  -M_{12}B_{21}+M_{11}B_{22}  \\
  &\hspace{2.5em}
  +B_{11}B_{22}-B_{12}B_{21}.
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:instanton-two-flavor-mass-source-expansion}
\end{equation}
The last line is the massless four-fermion 't Hooft vertex.  The middle line
is the mixed amplitude in which one flavor is supplied by an external
zero-mode pair and the other by a mass insertion.  If a flavor is neither
externally inserted nor present in the complementary mass minor, the
instanton contribution to that correlator vanishes.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply the determinant expansion to \(M+B\).  Each contributing permutation
\(\sigma\in S_{N_f}\) chooses, for every row, either an \(M\)-entry or a
\(B\)-entry.  Let \(I\) be the set of rows where a \(B\)-entry was chosen and
let \(J=\sigma(I)\).  Reordering the rows and columns so that the \(B\)-block
appears in the \(I,J\) positions gives the cofactor sign
\((-1)^{\sum I+\sum J}\).  Summing over the restriction of \(\sigma\) to
\(I\) gives \(\det B_{I,J}\), while summing over the complementary
restriction gives \(\det M_{I^c,J^c}\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:instanton-mass-source-minor-expansion}.  Extracting a particular
source monomial from \(\det B_{I,J}\) gives the \(\operatorname{sgn}(\pi)\)
factor in \eqref{eq:instanton-source-differentiation-minor}.  The displayed
two-flavor formula is the specialization to \(N_f=2\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Proper-time determinant and four-fermion amplitude]
\label{prop:instanton-proper-time-four-fermion-amplitude}
Fix a finite-regulator \(Q=1\) chart \(z=(a,\rho,U)\), a background gauge
slice, and a proper-time determinant prescription with renormalization scale
\(\mu\).  For each positive nonzero-mode operator \(P_{\Lambda,z}\) in the
instanton background, compared with the corresponding operator
\(P_{\Lambda,0}\) in the trivial sector, define the regulated logarithmic
determinant ratio by
\begin{equation}
  K_\Lambda(P_z,P_0;\mu)
  =
  -\int_{\epsilon_\Lambda}^{\infty}
    \frac{\dd t}{t}\,r(t\mu^2)
    \left(
      \operatorname{Tr}' e^{-tP_{\Lambda,z}}
      -
      \operatorname{Tr} e^{-tP_{\Lambda,0}}
    \right)
  +C_{\Lambda,P}^{\rm loc}(z;\mu),
  \label{eq:proper-time-instanton-determinant-ratio}
\end{equation}
where \(r\) is a smooth cutoff profile with \(r(0)=1\), the prime removes
collective-coordinate, residual-gauge, and fermion zero modes as appropriate,
and \(C_{\Lambda,P}^{\rm loc}\) is the chosen local subtraction in the
instanton background relative to the trivial background.  The determinant
density in \eqref{eq:finite-regulator-instanton-measure-density} is then
\begin{equation}
  \log \mathcal W_{\Lambda,{\rm pt}}^{\rm nz}(z;\mu)
  =
  K_\Lambda(M_z^{\rm gh},M_0^{\rm gh};\mu)
  -\frac12 K_\Lambda(L_z^{\rm bos},L_0^{\rm bos};\mu)
  +\sum_R K_\Lambda^{\rm pf,R}(z,0;\mu),
  \label{eq:proper-time-instanton-Wnz}
\end{equation}
with \(K_\Lambda^{\rm pf,R}\) the Pfaffian or determinant-line version of the
same nonzero-mode construction for the fermion multiplet \(R\).

For two massless Dirac flavors, let \(\eta_L\) and \(\bar\eta_R\) be four
external wave packets in the channel saturated by the \(Q=1\) fermion zero
modes, and let \(\psi_{0,z}\) be the normalized fundamental zero-mode
wavefunction in the instanton background.  Define the zero-mode source matrix
\begin{equation}
  B_{\eta,ff'}(z)
  =
  \left(
    \int \dd^4x\,
    \bar\eta_{R f}(x)\psi_{0,z}(x)
  \right)
  \left(
    \int \dd^4y\,
    \psi_{0,z}^{\dagger}(y)\eta_{L f'}(y)
  \right),
  \qquad f,f'=1,2,
  \label{eq:zero-mode-projected-four-fermion-source}
\end{equation}
with spin and color indices contracted in the zero-mode line.  The
one-instanton contribution to the smeared four-fermion amplitude is
\begin{equation}
  G^{(4)}_{\eta,\Lambda,Q=1}
  =
  \int_{\mathcal U_{Q=1}}
  \exp[-S_\Lambda(A_z)+\ii\theta]\,
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda,{\rm pt}}^{\rm nz}(z;\mu)\,
  \det B_\eta(z)\,
  \dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}(z).
  \label{eq:proper-time-four-fermion-instanton-amplitude}
\end{equation}
In the slow-field limit this reduces to the local \(N_f=2\) term in
\eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local}.  The small-\(t\) expansion of
\eqref{eq:proper-time-instanton-determinant-ratio} supplies the local
counterterms and the universal \((\mu\rho)^{b_0}\) factor, while the finite
remainder fixes the scheme-dependent numerical constant in the
four-fermion amplitude.  Thus the physics calculation consists of the
regulated fluctuation determinant, the zero-mode source projection, and the
collective-coordinate integral as a single object.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a finite positive operator \(P\), the identity
\(\log\det P=-\int_{\epsilon}^{\infty}\dd t\,t^{-1}\operatorname{Tr}e^{-tP}\)
holds up to the subtraction convention at the lower endpoint.  Applying this
identity to the instanton and trivial-sector operators and adding the chosen
local counterterm gives
\eqref{eq:proper-time-instanton-determinant-ratio}.  Bosonic Gaussian
integration contributes the exponent \(-1/2\), ghost Berezin integration
contributes \(+1\), and fermionic nonzero modes contribute the Pfaffian or
determinant-line factor, giving
\eqref{eq:proper-time-instanton-Wnz}.  Zero modes have already been removed
from the traces and determinants, so their finite Berezin integral is exactly
the calculation in
Proposition~\ref{prop:qcd-thooft-determinant-zero-mode-amplitude}.  For the
four external wave packets this Berezin coefficient is
\(\det B_\eta(z)\).  Multiplication by the collective-coordinate density and
the classical instanton exponential gives
\eqref{eq:proper-time-four-fermion-instanton-amplitude}.  Finally, the
heat-kernel expansion of the trace difference at small \(t\) separates local
counterterms, the coefficient of \(\log(\mu\rho)\), and the finite
determinant constant; RG invariance identifies the universal logarithmic
coefficient with \(b_0\) in the displayed one-loop density.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Instanton zero-mode form factor and local limit]
\label{prop:instanton-zero-mode-tail-local-limit}
In a BPST \(Q=1\) background, after the spin and color tensors of one
fundamental zero-mode line are factored out, the radial scalar envelope which
weights a local mass, Yukawa, or bilinear source is
\begin{equation}
  h_\rho(y)
  =
  \frac{2\rho^2}{\pi^2(y^2+\rho^2)^3},
  \qquad
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y=1 .
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-envelope}
\end{equation}
Thus a zero-mode-paired source matrix entry has the form
\begin{equation}
  B_{ff'}^{(s)}(a,\rho,U)
  =
  \rho^{d_s}
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}
  h_\rho(y)\,
  \mathcal B_{ff'}(a+y;U)\,\dd^4y ,
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-source-convolution}
\end{equation}
where \(d_s=1\) for a mass source and \(d_s=3\) for the local quark-bilinear
source in \eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local}.  Equivalently, with
the Fourier convention
\[
  \widehat h_\rho(q)=
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}\ee^{\ii q\cdot y}h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y ,
\]
one has the exact form factor
\begin{equation}
  \widehat h_\rho(q)
  =
  zK_1(z),
  \qquad z=\rho |q|,
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-momentum-form-factor}
\end{equation}
where \(K_1\) is the modified Bessel function.  Hence a zero-mode-paired
source with Fourier momentum \(q\) is not represented by the local vertex
until \(z\ll1\).
The small-momentum expansion is
\begin{equation}
  zK_1(z)
  =
  1+
  \frac{z^2}{2}
  \left[
    \log\frac{z}{2}+\gamma_{\rm E}-\frac12
  \right]
  +O(z^4\log z),
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-form-factor-small-momentum}
\end{equation}
so the first nonlocal correction is \(q^2\rho^2\log(|q|\rho)\), not an
ordinary analytic Taylor coefficient.  At \(z\gg1\),
\begin{equation}
  zK_1(z)
  =
  \sqrt{\frac{\pi z}{2}}\,
  \ee^{-z}
  \left(1+\frac{3}{8z}+O(z^{-2})\right),
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-form-factor-large-momentum}
\end{equation}
which displays the suppression of source components varying on scales
shorter than the instanton size.  This is the source-profile factor inside
the one-instanton amplitude; it does not replace the determinant
normalization or the separate external-leg amputation data.  In position
space, for \(U_R=R/\rho\),
\begin{equation}
  \int_{|y|<R}h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y
  =
  \frac{U_R^4}{(1+U_R^2)^2},
  \qquad
  \int_{|y|>R}h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y
  =
  \frac{1+2U_R^2}{(1+U_R^2)^2}.
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-tail-mass}
\end{equation}
The first correction to a local Taylor expansion is more delicate.  The
truncated second moment is
\begin{equation}
  M_2(R)
  :=
  \int_{|y|<R}|y|^2h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y
  =
  2\rho^2
  \left[
    \log(1+U_R^2)
    +\frac{2}{1+U_R^2}
    -\frac{1}{2(1+U_R^2)^2}
    -\frac32
  \right],
  \label{eq:instanton-zero-mode-second-moment}
\end{equation}
and rotational invariance gives
\[
  \int_{|y|<R}y^\mu y^\nu h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y
  =
  \frac{\delta^{\mu\nu}}4M_2(R).
\]
Consequently the leading local term in
\eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-source-convolution} is
\(\rho^{d_s}\mathcal B_{ff'}(a;U)\), with a tail bounded by the second
quantity in \eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-tail-mass}.  But the would-be
two-derivative coefficient grows as
\(4\rho^2\log(R/\rho)+O(\rho^2)\) as \(R/\rho\to\infty\).  The local
't Hooft vertex is therefore the zeroth adiabatic term of the zero-mode
source convolution; higher-derivative terms require the external wave-packet,
mass, or infrared regulator that cuts off the zero-mode tail.  In momentum
space the same statement is the logarithm in
\eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-form-factor-small-momentum}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The normalized radial envelope in \eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-envelope} is
the scalar part of the product of two normalized fundamental zero-mode
wavefunctions in the BPST background.  Using
\(\dd^4y=2\pi^2r^3\dd r\) and \(u=r/\rho\),
\[
  \int_{\mathbb R^4}h_\rho(y)\,\dd^4y
  =
  4\int_0^\infty\frac{u^3}{(1+u^2)^3}\,\dd u
  =
  1.
\]
For the Fourier transform, use the Schwinger representation
\[
  \frac1{(y^2+\rho^2)^3}
  =
  \frac12\int_0^\infty s^2\ee^{-s(y^2+\rho^2)}\,\dd s .
\]
Then
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \widehat h_\rho(q)
  &=
  \rho^2\int_0^\infty
  \exp\!\left[-\rho^2s-\frac{q^2}{4s}\right]\dd s  \\
  &=
  \rho |q|\,K_1(\rho |q|),
\end{aligned}
\]
where the last step is the standard integral representation of the modified
Bessel function.  The small-\(z\) and large-\(z\) formulas are the
corresponding standard asymptotic expansions of \(K_1\); the logarithmic
small-\(z\) term is the momentum-space reflection of the logarithmically
divergent second moment computed below.

The same integral cut off at \(U_R\) gives
\[
  4\int_0^{U_R}\frac{u^3}{(1+u^2)^3}\,\dd u
  =
  1-\frac{2}{1+U_R^2}
  +\frac{1}{(1+U_R^2)^2}
  =
  \frac{U_R^4}{(1+U_R^2)^2},
\]
and subtracting from one gives the tail formula in
\eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-tail-mass}.  For the second moment,
\[
  M_2(R)
  =
  4\rho^2\int_0^{U_R}\frac{u^5}{(1+u^2)^3}\,\dd u .
\]
With \(t=1+u^2\), this antiderivative is
\[
  2\rho^2
  \left[
    \log t+\frac2t-\frac{1}{2t^2}-\frac32
  \right]_{t=1}^{1+U_R^2},
\]
which is \eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-second-moment}.  Rotational
invariance in four Euclidean dimensions gives the displayed
\(\delta^{\mu\nu}/4\) tensor moment.  The Taylor expansion of
\(\mathcal B(a+y;U)\) then yields the zeroth local term and the
\((M_2(R)/8)\partial^2\mathcal B(a;U)\) contribution inside the ball
\(|y|<R\), while the exterior contribution is controlled by the tail mass
times the appropriate supremum norm of the source.  Since
\(\log(1+U_R^2)=2\log(R/\rho)+o(1)\), the full second moment is
logarithmically infrared sensitive.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Amputation of the instanton source kernel]
\label{prop:instanton-external-leg-amputation-kernel}
Work in the finite-regulator \(Q=1\) chart of
Proposition~\ref{prop:instanton-proper-time-four-fermion-amplitude}, and
suppose an auxiliary infrared deformation or source scheme has been chosen in
which the external quark fields used to probe the kernel have isolated
nonzero external pole-residue factors.  After the spin and color external
covectors are fixed, let these adjacent unamputated leg factors be
\(R_{R,f}(p_{R,f})\) and \(R_{L,f'}(p_{L,f'})\).  For plane-wave packets in
the zero-mode channel, the zero-mode source matrix has the factorization
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  B^{\rm unamp}_{ff'}(z)
  &=
  R_{R,f}(p_{R,f})\,
  B^{\rm amp}_{ff'}(z)\,
  R_{L,f'}(p_{L,f'}),
\\
  B^{\rm amp}_{ff'}(z)
  &=
  \rho^3
  \ee^{\ii q_{ff'}\cdot a}\,
  \widehat h_\rho(q_{ff'})\,
  \mathcal T_{ff'}(U;p_{R,f},p_{L,f'}),
  \qquad
  q_{ff'}=p_{R,f}-p_{L,f'} .
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:instanton-amputated-source-matrix}
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathcal T_{ff'}\) is the remaining spin-color orientation tensor
after the external representatives have been contracted with the normalized
zero-mode line.  For \(N_f=2\), the unamputated and amputated source
determinants obey
\begin{equation}
  \det B^{\rm unamp}(z)
  =
  \left(\prod_{f=1}^{2}R_{R,f}\right)
  \left(\prod_{f'=1}^{2}R_{L,f'}\right)
  \det B^{\rm amp}(z).
  \label{eq:instanton-source-determinant-amputation}
\end{equation}
Consequently the source-amputated one-instanton four-fermion kernel is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal M^{(4),{\rm amp}}_{\Lambda,Q=1}
  =
  \int_{\mathcal U_{Q=1}}
  \exp[-S_\Lambda(A_z)+\ii\theta]\,
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda,{\rm pt}}^{\rm nz}(z;\mu)\,
  \det B^{\rm amp}(z)\,
  \dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}(z).
  \label{eq:instanton-amputated-four-fermion-kernel}
\end{equation}
Amputation removes only the adjacent external propagator residues.  It does
not remove the zero-mode form factor
\(\widehat h_\rho(q)=\rho|q|K_1(\rho|q|)\), the spin-color orientation tensor,
the nonzero-mode determinant, or the collective-coordinate integral.  Thus
the local 't Hooft operator is the small-\(\rho|q|\) limit of the amputated
instanton kernel, not the result of dividing out the instanton zero-mode
wavefunction itself.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The zero-mode source matrix is obtained by attaching the external source
wavefunctions to the normalized zero-mode line.  Before amputation, each row
of the flavor matrix carries the adjacent right-handed external residue
\(R_{R,f}\), and each column carries the adjacent left-handed external
residue \(R_{L,f'}\).  Removing these external legs multiplies the
unamputated matrix on the left and right by the inverse diagonal residue
matrices, leaving \(B^{\rm amp}\).

The determinant identity
\[
  \det(D_R B^{\rm amp} D_L)
  =
  \det D_R\,\det B^{\rm amp}\,\det D_L
\]
for the diagonal matrices \(D_R=\operatorname{diag}(R_{R,1},R_{R,2})\) and
\(D_L=\operatorname{diag}(R_{L,1},R_{L,2})\) gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-source-determinant-amputation}.  Multiplying the
unamputated Green kernel by the inverse product of the same residues therefore
gives \eqref{eq:instanton-amputated-four-fermion-kernel}.  The factors in
\(B^{\rm amp}\) are not external propagators: they are the Fourier transform
of the zero-mode density and the orientation tensor of the instanton
background.  They remain part of the hard instanton kernel.
\end{proof}

The qualification in
Proposition~\ref{prop:instanton-external-leg-amputation-kernel} is essential.
Colored quark legs in confining QCD do not define ordinary physical
asymptotic states.  The displayed object is an auxiliary source-amputated, or
partonic hard-kernel, coordinate useful for matching the finite-regulator
Green function to the local effective operator.  Gauge-invariant amplitudes
must contract the same instanton kernel with color-singlet external
operators, hadronic matrix elements, or a separately specified infrared
deformation.  This is the amplitude-level content of the original
one-instanton computation of 't Hooft
\cite{tHooft1976Pseudoparticle}: collective coordinates, nonzero-mode
fluctuation determinants, fermion zero-mode saturation, and external-leg
normalization are one calculation.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Hard-momentum size window]
\label{ca:hard-momentum-instanton-size-window}
Consider the \(N_f=2\) source-amputated four-fermion kernel
\eqref{eq:instanton-amputated-four-fermion-kernel} in a kinematic region
where the momentum transfers in the two zero-mode source entries of a
nonzero determinant term are
\[
  |q_1|=c_1 Q,\qquad |q_2|=c_2 Q,
  \qquad c_1,c_2>0,
\]
with \(Q\) much larger than the infrared scale of the theory.  Keep only the
one-loop universal density, the two zero-mode source powers
\(\rho^3\rho^3\), and the two BPST form factors.  Choosing
\(\mu=Q\), the corresponding size factor has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal I_{\rm hard}(Q)
  =
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(Q)}+\ii\theta\right]
  \left(\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(Q)}\right)^{2N_c}
  Q^{b_0}
  \int_0^\infty
  \rho^{\,b_0+1}
  \prod_{\ell=1}^{2}
  \bigl(c_\ell Q\rho\,K_1(c_\ell Q\rho)\bigr)\,\dd\rho
  \times\mathcal C_{\rm hard},
  \label{eq:hard-momentum-instanton-size-factor}
\end{equation}
up to finite determinant remainders, orientation tensors, source
normalizations, and higher-loop matching factors.  With \(s=Q\rho\),
\begin{equation}
  Q^{b_0}
  \int_0^\infty
  \rho^{\,b_0+1}
  \prod_{\ell=1}^{2}
  \bigl(c_\ell Q\rho\,K_1(c_\ell Q\rho)\bigr)\,\dd\rho
  =
  Q^{-2}
  \int_0^\infty
  s^{\,b_0+1}
  \prod_{\ell=1}^{2}
  \bigl(c_\ell sK_1(c_\ell s)\bigr)\,\dd s .
  \label{eq:hard-momentum-instanton-size-scaling}
\end{equation}
The small-\(\rho\) endpoint is finite in asymptotically free QCD because the
integrand behaves as \(\rho^{b_0+1}\).  The large-\(\rho\) endpoint is cut
off by
\[
  \prod_{\ell=1}^{2}c_\ell Q\rho\,K_1(c_\ell Q\rho)
  =
  O\!\left(\rho\,\exp[-(c_1+c_2)Q\rho]\right),
  \qquad Q\rho\to\infty .
\]
Thus a genuinely hard fermionic instanton amplitude is dominated by
instantons with \(\rho\) of order \(1/Q\), within a dimensionless
distribution set by the \(c_\ell\).  This is a physical cutoff supplied by
the external source profiles, not a cutoff on the instanton moduli space.
The approximation is controlled only when the dominant range also has
\(1/\rho\) in the weak-coupling regime and when the projection onto the
chosen hard channel removes determinant monomials whose source entries carry
no hard momentum transfer and hence no BPST form-factor cutoff.
\end{controlledapproximation}

This hard-size window is the amplitude counterpart of the local
't Hooft vertex.  The local operator records the \(Q\rho\ll1\) tensor and
selection rule, while \eqref{eq:hard-momentum-instanton-size-factor} records
how the same zero modes and fluctuation determinant enter an actual hard
correlator.  It also explains why the mass-saturated vacuum activity below is
a different object: a vacuum theta term has no external form factor to
exclude \(\rho\to\infty\), so its large-size behavior is an infrared
question, not a semiclassical prediction.

Thus the \(Q=1\) amplitude is the microscopic origin of the \(U(1)_A\)
selection rule.  The determinant in
\eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local} carries axial charge \(2N_f\);
equivalently, a mass-saturated instanton contribution depends on the CP-odd
combination \(\bar\theta=\theta+\arg\det M\) from
\eqref{eq:qcd-physical-theta}.  This gives the same anomalous Jacobian in the
coordinate system where the fermion zero modes of the instanton background
have been integrated.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Mass-saturated vacuum activity and the
size integral]
\label{ca:mass-saturated-instanton-vacuum-activity}
Assume vectorlike \(SU(N_c)\) QCD with \(N_f\) Dirac fundamentals, a diagonal
renormalized mass matrix \(M(\mu)\), and a one-instanton size window
\(\rho_-<\rho<\rho_+\) in which the one-loop density and the leading
zero-mode mass overlaps are valid.  In the same local normalization as
\eqref{eq:qcd-thooft-vertex-leading-local}, saturating one Dirac zero-mode
pair by the mass term supplies the dimensionless factor \(m_f(\mu)\rho\).
Thus the mass-saturated one-instanton vacuum activity in this window has the
form
\begin{equation}
  \zeta_m^{[\rho_-,\rho_+]}(\mu)
  =
  \mathcal C_{\mathcal S,N_c,N_f}\,
  \left(\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right)^{2N_c}
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right]
  |\det M(\mu)|\,\mu^{b_0}
  \int_{\rho_-}^{\rho_+}
  \rho^{\,b_0+N_f-5}\,\dd\rho ,
  \label{eq:mass-saturated-instanton-window-activity}
\end{equation}
up to the finite determinant remainder, orientation-volume convention, and
higher-loop/operator-renormalization factors of the chosen scheme.  The
corresponding \(Q=1\) vacuum term carries the phase
\begin{equation}
  V_4\zeta_m^{[\rho_-,\rho_+]}
  \exp\!\left[\ii(\theta+\arg\det M)\right],
  \label{eq:mass-saturated-instanton-vacuum-phase}
\end{equation}
while the anti-instanton term carries the complex conjugate phase.

The displayed size integral isolates the two endpoint questions:
\begin{equation}
  \int_{\rho_-}^{\rho_+}
  \rho^{\,b_0+N_f-5}\,\dd\rho
  =
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{\rho_+^{\,b_0+N_f-4}-\rho_-^{\,b_0+N_f-4}}
          {b_0+N_f-4},
      & b_0+N_f\ne4,\\[10pt]
    \log(\rho_+/\rho_-),
      & b_0+N_f=4 .
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:mass-saturated-size-window-integral}
\end{equation}
The small-size endpoint is locally finite precisely when
\(b_0+N_f>4\).  For \(SU(3)\), this margin is
\[
  b_0+N_f-4
  =
  7+\frac{N_f}{3},
\]
so the mass-saturated one-instanton vacuum term is not obstructed at
\(\rho=0\) in the asymptotically free range.  The same positive power means
that extending the one-loop formula to \(\rho_+=\infty\) would give an
infrared power divergence.  That divergence is not a new ultraviolet
counterterm; it says that the zero-temperature large-size region is outside
the controlled weak-coupling instanton calculation.  A finite dilute activity
therefore requires an additional physical infrared cutoff or mechanism, such
as a Higgs scale, a thermal screening scale, or a separately justified
confining/chiral-dynamics input.

If one quark is massless and no external source saturates its zero modes,
\(\det M=0\) and the vacuum activity
\eqref{eq:mass-saturated-instanton-window-activity} vanishes.  This is
compatible with nonzero instanton contributions to fermionic correlators:
those correlators use the source determinant of
\eqref{eq:proper-time-four-fermion-instanton-amplitude} instead of the mass
determinant in \eqref{eq:mass-saturated-instanton-window-activity}.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Dilute instanton gas as an amplitude expansion]
\label{ca:dilute-instanton-gas-amplitude}
Suppose the regulated one-instanton and one-anti-instanton amplitudes obtained
above admit finite, translation-invariant vacuum activities \(\zeta_\Lambda\)
per Euclidean four-volume after the size, orientation, nonzero-mode
determinant, and any required zero-mode mass-overlap factors have all been
included.  Assume further that the multi-instanton cluster expansion has a
window in which overlap corrections, instanton--anti-instanton interactions,
and boundary-face remainders contribute \(o(V_4)\) to \(\log Z\).  Then the
leading dilute-gas theta dependence is not a new moduli-space statement; it is
the Poisson sum of the already constructed amplitudes:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{Z_{\rm dig}(\bar\theta)}{Z_0}
  =
  \sum_{n_+,n_-\ge0}
  \frac{(V_4\zeta_\Lambda)^{n_+}}{n_+!}
  \frac{(V_4\zeta_\Lambda)^{n_-}}{n_-!}
  \ee^{\ii(n_+-n_-)\bar\theta}
  =
  \exp\!\left(2V_4\zeta_\Lambda\cos\bar\theta\right).
  \label{eq:dilute-instanton-gas-partition}
\end{equation}
Thus the vacuum-energy difference in this approximation is
\begin{equation}
  E_{\rm dig}(\bar\theta)-E_{\rm dig}(0)
  =
  2\zeta_\Lambda(1-\cos\bar\theta),
  \label{eq:dilute-instanton-gas-energy}
\end{equation}
and the topological-charge cumulants at \(\bar\theta=0\) obey
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_{2m}(Q)=2V_4\zeta_\Lambda,
  \qquad
  \kappa_{2m+1}(Q)=0,
  \qquad
  \chi_{\rm top}^{\rm dig}=2\zeta_\Lambda .
  \label{eq:dilute-instanton-gas-cumulants}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, writing
\[
  E_{\rm dig}(\bar\theta)-E_{\rm dig}(0)
  =
  \frac12\chi_{\rm top}^{\rm dig}\bar\theta^2
  \left(
    1+b_2^{\rm dig}\bar\theta^2+O(\bar\theta^4)
  \right)
\]
gives \(b_2^{\rm dig}=-1/12\).

This is a controlled semiclassical amplitude expansion only when the activity
is finite and dilute in the relevant correlation volume.  If a massless quark
zero mode is not saturated by a mass or source, the vacuum activity for this
term vanishes; instanton sectors may still contribute to fermionic correlators
such as \eqref{eq:proper-time-four-fermion-instanton-amplitude}.  If the
\(\rho\)-integral is infrared divergent, if small-instanton boundary terms are
not controlled, or if instanton interactions are not negligible, then the
Poisson form \eqref{eq:dilute-instanton-gas-partition} has not been derived.
In particular, this dilute four-dimensional gas is not a derivation of
confinement in zero-temperature Yang--Mills.  Its output is the conditional
theta-dependence and zero-mode-saturated amplitude data above.
\end{controlledapproximation}

For the electroweak \(SU(2)_L\) gauge theory, the same zero-mode logic applies
to every left-handed doublet.  For one generation there are three colored
quark doublets and one lepton doublet, hence four \(SU(2)_L\) doublet zero
modes.  For \(N_g\) generations the corresponding minimal fermion operator
contains
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal O_{\rm EW}
  \propto
  \prod_{r=1}^{N_g}
  \bigl(q_{Lr}q_{Lr}q_{Lr}\ell_{Lr}\bigr),
  \label{eq:electroweak-thooft-vertex}
\end{equation}
with weak, color, spinor, and flavor contractions fixed by the zero-mode
wavefunctions and by gauge invariance.  It changes baryon and lepton numbers
by
\[
  \Delta B=\Delta L=N_g,
  \qquad
  \Delta(B-L)=0,
  \qquad
  \Delta(B+L)=2N_g .
\]
At zero temperature the amplitude is exponentially suppressed by the instanton
action.  The real-time high-temperature process is instead organized around
the sphaleron barrier discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:gauge-higgs-classical-solitons}; both effects are controlled
by the same anomalous relation between topological charge and fermion-number
violation.

\section{Anomalous Pion Couplings}

The axial anomaly has observable low-energy consequences.  In two-flavor QCD,
the neutral pion couples to the electromagnetic topological density through
the anomalous divergence of the axial isospin current:
\[
  \partial_\mu J_{5,3}^\mu
  =
  \frac{e^2}{16\pi^2}
  \operatorname{tr}\bigl(T_3 Q^2\bigr)
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}
  +
  \text{mass terms}.
\]
Here \(Q\) is the quark charge matrix and \(T_3\) is the third generator of
flavor \(SU(2)\).  The same anomaly is reproduced in the pion effective theory
by the Wess--Zumino--Witten term introduced in the next chapter.
