\chapter{Chiral Axial Anomalies}
\label{ch:volume-ii-20}

\section{Classical Symmetry}

For massless Dirac fermions, the classical action may have vector and axial
symmetries:
\[
  \psi\mapsto \ee^{\ii\alpha}\psi,\qquad
  \psi\mapsto \ee^{\ii\beta\gamma_5}\psi.
\]
The corresponding classical axial current satisfies
\[
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu=0
\]
before coupling and regularization subtleties are considered.

\section{The Quantum Anomaly}

In a gauge background, no regulator preserves all formal symmetries
simultaneously. For a Dirac fermion coupled to electromagnetism,
\[
  \partial_\mu j_5^\mu
  =
  \frac{e^2}{16\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}
\]
up to convention-dependent signs and normalization of the current. The vector
current is kept conserved because gauge invariance is required for
consistency; the axial symmetry is anomalous.

\section{Path-Integral Measure}

In Fujikawa's viewpoint, the anomaly is the Jacobian of the fermion measure
under a chiral rotation. Expanding the fermion fields in eigenmodes of the
Dirac operator, the regulated trace of \(\gamma_5\) gives
\[
  \operatorname{Tr}\gamma_5\ee^{-\slashed D^2/M^2}
  \longrightarrow
  \frac{1}{32\pi^2}
  \int\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}.
\]
The anomaly is therefore not a failure of algebraic manipulation; it is a
property of the regulated infinite-dimensional measure.

\section{Consequences}

Anomalies constrain possible quantum theories. Gauge anomalies make a theory
inconsistent unless they cancel. Global anomalies are allowed, but they must be
matched along RG flows. This matching principle will become a powerful guide
to strongly coupled dynamics and symmetry breaking.
