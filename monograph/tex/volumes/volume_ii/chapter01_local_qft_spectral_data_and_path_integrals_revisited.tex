\ConventionsMixed


\section{Purpose of This Chapter}

The preceding part constructed the local Hilbert-space frameworks, the
K\"all\'en--Lehmann spectral representation, Haag--Ruelle scattering states,
and LSZ reduction.  This chapter does not rederive those constructions.  Its
role is to fix the notation used in the analytic scattering chapters:
Poincare-action conventions, one-particle normalizations, spectral-threshold
symbols, and Green-function boundary-value conventions.

\section{Status of Scalar Model Examples}
\label{sec:volume-ii-scalar-model-status}

Scalar Lagrangian densities appear in this part at three logically different
levels.  First, at finite ultraviolet cutoff they define regulated Green
functions and perturbative kernels.  Second, in low spacetime dimensions,
certain scalar models have constructive non-Gaussian continuum realizations, as
catalogued in Table~\ref{tab:constructive-qft-status-catalog} and recalled in
Chapter~\ref{ch:perturbative-green-functions}.  Third, a
four-dimensional \(\lambda\phi^4\) expression may be used as a renormalized
perturbative or effective-field-theory model below a cutoff, or as a
conditional notation for an exact massive local QFT if such a theory has been
separately constructed.

For the standard reflection-positive lattice \(\lambda\phi^4\) family in four
Euclidean dimensions with \(\lambda\ge0\), the known triviality theorems apply
to the usual critical or near-critical continuum scaling limits: the resulting
continuum limits are Gaussian, so the interacting connected correlations
vanish after the continuum field normalization.  This is a theorem about that
specified constructive scaling-limit problem.  It is a different statement from
the broader open question whether an ultraviolet-complete four-dimensional QFT
could have a small-coupling expansion agreeing with formal
\(\phi^4_4\) perturbation theory to all orders while differing
nonperturbatively.  The latter possibility would require its own construction
and verification of locality, positivity, spectrum, and scattering hypotheses.

Consequently, when the following chapters use \(D=4\) scalar amplitudes, they
are to be read either as finite-cutoff perturbative or effective descriptions,
or as conditional consequences of the stated Hilbert-space and analytic
hypotheses.  The scattering machinery derives consequences from a massive
local QFT with stable particles; it does not prove that a particular bare
four-dimensional scalar polynomial supplies such a QFT after removing the
cutoff.

\section{Poincare and Field Conventions}

The unitary representation is written
\[
  U(a,\Lambda),\qquad
  U(a,\mathbf 1)=\exp(\ii a^\mu P_\mu),
\]
with group law
\[
  U(a',\Lambda')U(a,\Lambda)
  =
  U(a'+\Lambda'a,\Lambda'\Lambda).
\]
The metric is mostly plus,
\[
  \eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(-,+,\ldots,+),
  \qquad
  p^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}p^\mu p^\nu,
\]
and the spectrum condition is
\[
  \operatorname{Spec}(P)\subset\overline V_+
  =
  \{p:p^0\ge0,\ p^2\le0\}.
\]
The vacuum vector is denoted by \(\vac\) and satisfies
\[
  U(a,\Lambda)\vac=\vac .
\]

For a local field transforming in a finite-dimensional Lorentz
representation \(R\),
\[
  U(a,\Lambda)^{-1}\widehat\Phi_\alpha(x)U(a,\Lambda)
  =
  R(\Lambda)_\alpha{}^\beta\widehat\Phi_\beta(\Lambda x+a).
\]
Thus translations give
\[
  \widehat\Phi_\alpha(x)
  =
  \ee^{-\ii x^\mu P_\mu}\widehat\Phi_\alpha(0)
  \ee^{\ii x^\mu P_\mu}.
\]
All such identities are identities of smeared operator-valued distributions on
the stated common domains.

\section{One-Particle Normalization}

For a stable scalar species of mass \(m>0\),
\[
  \Sigma_m^+
  =
  \{(\omega_{\vec p},\vec p):\omega_{\vec p}=(\vec p^{\,2}+m^2)^{1/2}\}
\]
is the positive mass shell.  Delta-normalized vectors and covariantly
normalized vectors are related by
\[
  \ket{\vec p}
  =
  \bigl((2\pi)^d2\omega_{\vec p}\bigr)^{1/2}\ket{\vec p}_\delta,
\]
with
\[
  {}_\delta\!\braket{\vec q}{\vec p}_\delta
  =
  \delta^{(d)}(\vec q-\vec p),
  \qquad
  \braket{\vec q}{\vec p}
  =
  (2\pi)^d2\omega_{\vec p}\delta^{(d)}(\vec q-\vec p).
\]
The invariant mass-shell measure is
\[
  \dd\mu_m(\vec p)
  =
  \frac{\dd^d\vec p}{(2\pi)^d2\omega_{\vec p}} .
\]

If a scalar interpolating field \(\widehat\phi\) creates the species, its
one-particle residue is fixed by
\[
  \bra{\vac}\widehat\phi(0)\ket{\vec p}=Z_\phi^{1/2},
  \qquad Z_\phi>0.
\]
This \(Z_\phi\) is the atom of the K\"all\'en--Lehmann measure at
\(\mu^2=m^2\), as derived in Chapter~\ref{ch:kallen-lehmann}.  The symbol
\(M_{\rm cont}\) denotes the lowest invariant mass in the continuous spectral
support with the same quantum numbers.  When the first continuum consists of
two particles of the same mass \(m\), \(M_{\rm cont}=2m\).

\section{Green-Function Boundary Values}

For local fields \(\widehat\Phi_{a_1},\ldots,\widehat\Phi_{a_N}\), the
time-ordered distribution is
\[
  G_{a_1\cdots a_N}(x_1,\ldots,x_N)
  =
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\{
    \widehat\Phi_{a_1}(x_1)\cdots\widehat\Phi_{a_N}(x_N)
  \}
  \ket{\vac}.
\]
Products at coincident points require a specified time-ordering prescription
or a renormalized composite-operator convention.

For a scalar two-point function, the positive spectral measure gives the
boundary-value formula
\[
  G_2(x)
  =
  \int_0^\infty \dd\rho_\phi(\mu^2)\,\Delta_F(x;\mu^2),
\]
where
\[
  \Delta_F(x;\mu^2)
  =
  -\ii\int\frac{\dd^D k}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{\ee^{\ii k\cdot x}}{k^2+\mu^2-\ii0}.
\]
The Euclidean two-point Schwinger function, when OS reconstruction hypotheses
are satisfied, is
\[
  S_2(x_E)
  =
  \int_0^\infty \dd\rho_\phi(\mu^2)\,
  \int\frac{\dd^D k_E}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{\ee^{\ii k_E\cdot x_E}}{k_E^2+\mu^2}.
\]
The passage among Wightman, time-ordered, and Euclidean functions is governed
by the tube-domain and OS hypotheses stated in the foundational chapters.  In
the present part these formulas serve only as notation and as inputs to LSZ,
dispersion theory, and threshold analysis.

\section{Regulated Functional Integrals as Downstream Data}

Regulated path integrals are used later to compute Green functions and their
renormalized limits.  If \(\Lambda\) is a regulator and \(J\) is a source, the
notation
\[
  Z_\Lambda[J]
  =
  \int_{\mathcal C_\Lambda}D_\Lambda\varphi\,
  \exp\left(
    -S_{\Lambda,E}[\varphi;g_I(\Lambda)]
    +\int \dd^D x_E\,J(x_E)\varphi(x_E)
  \right)
\]
means the finite-dimensional or otherwise regulated integral specified by the
chosen cutoff.  A continuum Schwinger function is a distributional limit of
source derivatives of \(Z_\Lambda\), after the cutoff dependence of the
coefficients \(g_I(\Lambda)\) has been chosen.  Existence of such a limit
requires a renormalization or construction theorem: the notation records the
regulated objects and the topology of the intended limit.
The systematic renormalized-source treatment begins in the renormalization and
effective-action chapters.
