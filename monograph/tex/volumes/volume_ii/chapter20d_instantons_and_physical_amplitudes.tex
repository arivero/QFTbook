\chapter{Instantons as Physical Amplitudes}
\label{ch:instantons-physical-amplitudes}

Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-20} developed the BPST solution, the
finite-regulator instanton density, the anomaly selection rules, and several
QCD amplitude benchmarks.  This chapter reorganizes those ingredients around
the physical object they are meant to compute.  The object is not the
instanton moduli space and not the universal density by itself.  It is a
regulated source or state amplitude, with zero modes saturated, nonzero
fluctuations divided by the chosen counterterms, endpoint faces controlled,
and a Lorentzian or Euclidean observable projection specified.

The distinction is operational.  Two channels may use exactly the same BPST
collective-coordinate space and the same one-loop density, while one channel
vanishes because its sources have rank one in the zero-mode space and the
other gives a nonzero four-fermion coefficient.  Or two Euclidean source
coefficients may agree after integrating over the collective variables, while
their spectral projections differ.  These are not refinements of moduli-space
geometry; they are the steps which turn a saddle contribution into a QFT
amplitude.

\section{The Density Gate: Fluctuations Before Channels}
\label{sec:instanton-density-gate}

The density in \eqref{eq:one-instanton-universal-density} is already a
fluctuation calculation.  The BPST collective-coordinate volume form supplies
one of its factors.  In the half-trace convention of
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-20}, its universal one-loop part is
\begin{equation}
  \dd\mu_1^{\rm univ}
  =
  C_{\mathcal S,N_c,N_f}\,
  \frac{\dd^4a\,\dd\rho}{\rho^5}\,
  \dd\Omega_{N_c}\,
  \left(\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right)^{2N_c}
  (\mu\rho)^{b_0}
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}+\ii\theta\right]
  \left(1+O(g_{\rm ht}^2)\right),
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-density-gate-universal}
\end{equation}
with \(b_0\) defined in
\eqref{eq:instanton-density-beta0-definition}.  The factors have different
origins.  The \(\dd^4a\,\dd\rho\,\rho^{-5}\dd\Omega_{N_c}\) part is the
collective-coordinate Jacobian and orientation convention.  The power
\((8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2)^{2N_c}\) comes from the \(4N_c\) bosonic zero-mode
normalizations.  The exponential is the classical BPST action.  The factor
\((\mu\rho)^{b_0}\) is the logarithmic remnant of the zero-mode-deleted
gauge, ghost, and matter fluctuation determinants after counterterm
subtraction.  The finite constant \(C_{\mathcal S,N_c,N_f}\) is not fixed by
dimensional analysis; it is part of the chosen determinant and orientation
normalization scheme.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[One-loop density gate for a channel]
\label{ca:instanton-one-loop-density-gate-channel}
The one-loop scale test which fixes the logarithmic density power is local:
\[
  \left(\mu\partial_\mu+\beta(g_{\rm ht})\partial_{g_{\rm ht}}\right)
  \log\!\left[
    (\mu\rho)^\alpha
    \exp\!\left(-\frac{8\pi^2}{g_{\rm ht}^2(\mu)}\right)
  \right]
  =
  \alpha-b_0+O(g_{\rm ht}^2).
\]
Thus the density gate passes only for \(\alpha=b_0\).  This test determines
the logarithmic fluctuation power, not the full physical amplitude.  Once the
density is inserted into a channel, the retained integrand can be organized in
the bookkeeping form
\begin{equation}
  \dd\mathcal A_{\mathcal C}^{\rm win}
  =
  \dd\rho\,
  \rho^{b_0-5}\,
  \mathcal W_{\rm fin}^{\rm nz}(\rho,U)\,
  \mathcal Z_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\rho,U)\,
  \mathcal M_{\mathcal C}^{\rm src}(\rho,U)\,
  \mathcal B_{\mathcal C}^{\rho}(\rho)\,
  \dd^4a\,\dd\Omega_{N_c}
  \times(\hbox{scheme and action factors}).
  \label{eq:instanton-density-gate-channel-integrand}
\end{equation}
If the zero-mode/source part starts as
\[
  \mathcal Z_{\mathcal C}^{0}\mathcal M_{\mathcal C}^{\rm src}
  =
  \rho^{\beta_{\mathcal C}}
  \bigl(Z_{\mathcal C}^{(0)}+O(\rho^\delta)\bigr),
\]
then the channel size power is
\(\rho^{b_0+\beta_{\mathcal C}-5}\dd\rho\).  The number
\(\beta_{\mathcal C}\) is physical source data.  It changes when masses
saturate zero modes, when external source derivatives are taken, when
amputation conventions are changed, or when the observable projection kills a
zero-mode component.  For the hard \(SU(3)\), \(N_f=2\), four-source channel
below, \(\beta_{\mathcal C}=6\), so the integrand begins as
\(\rho^{32/3}\dd\rho\), not as the density-only power
\(\rho^{14/3}\dd\rho\).

Consequently a same-theory ratio may cancel the finite determinant constant
and \(\Lambda_{\rm ht}^{b_0}\) only after the source frame, zero-mode
determinants, orientation projection, endpoint window, and physical
projection have been transported.  A computation which keeps
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-density-gate-universal} but drops
\(\mathcal Z_{\mathcal C}^{0}\), \(\mathcal M_{\mathcal C}^{\rm src}\), or
\(\mathcal B_{\mathcal C}^{\rho}\) has not computed the channel amplitude.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{The Channel Is Part of the Calculation}
\label{sec:instanton-physical-amplitude-channel}

The finite-regulator datum
of Definition~\ref{def:one-instanton-amplitude-datum} is the local input for
the present chapter.  We now package it as an observable channel.  This is a
change of emphasis, not a new convention: the BPST/ADHM data select the
domain of integration, while the channel records the determinant, zero-mode,
source, endpoint, and physical-projection data which decide the amplitude.

\begin{definition}[Instanton physical-amplitude channel]
\label{def:instanton-physical-amplitude-channel}
A one-instanton physical-amplitude channel at regulator \(\Lambda\) is a
tuple
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal C_{\Lambda}
  =
  \bigl(
  \mathcal U_{\rm win},
  \dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M},
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz},
  \mathcal Z_{\Lambda}^{0},
  \mathcal M_{\Lambda}^{\rm src},
  \mathcal B_{\Lambda}^{\rho},
  \mathfrak L_{\rm phys}
  \bigr).
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-amplitude-channel}
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathcal U_{\rm win}\) is the retained chart/window in center, size,
and orientation variables; \(\dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}\) is the
collective-coordinate density; \(\mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}\) is the
zero-mode-deleted fluctuation determinant with its counterterm convention;
\(\mathcal Z_{\Lambda}^{0}\) is the Berezin coefficient which saturates the
fermion zero modes; \(\mathcal M_{\Lambda}^{\rm src}\) is the finite source,
amputation, OPE, color-singlet, or hadron-overlap map;
\(\mathcal B_{\Lambda}^{\rho}\) is the endpoint, size-window, or screening
factor; and \(\mathfrak L_{\rm phys}\) is the final observable projection,
such as an LSZ residue, spectral-bin functional, Euclidean susceptibility,
OPE coefficient, or inclusive cut.
\end{definition}

The channel coefficient is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{\Lambda}(\mathcal C)
  =
  \mathfrak L_{\rm phys}
  \left[
  \int_{\mathcal U_{\rm win}}
  \ee^{-S_{\Lambda}(A_z)+\ii\theta}\,
  \mathcal W_{\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)\,
  \mathcal Z_{\Lambda}^{0}(z)\,
  \mathcal M_{\Lambda}^{\rm src}(z)\,
  \mathcal B_{\Lambda}^{\rho}(z)\,
  \dd\nu_{\Lambda,\mathcal M}(z)
  \right].
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-channel-functional}
\end{equation}
The physical statement is obtained only with a residual budget:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal A_{\rm phys}(\mathcal C)
  &=
  \mathcal A_{\Lambda}(\mathcal C)
  +R_{\rm det}+R_{\rm fluc}+R_{\rm zm}+R_{\rm src}+R_{\rm Schur}
   +R_{\rho}                                      \\
  &\qquad
  +R_{\rm sec}+R_{\rm cont}+R_{\rm spec}
   +R_{\rm IR}+R_{\rm cut}+R_{\rm scheme}.
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-channel-residual-budget}
\end{equation}
The names match the reusable slots in
Proposition~\ref{prop:instanton-amplitude-assembly-error-budget}.  A
calculation may set some of them to zero only by giving the corresponding
argument; a local BPST density does not set any of the source, endpoint, or
physical-projection terms to zero.

\begin{proposition}[Moduli-equivalent channels need not have the same amplitude]
\label{prop:instanton-moduli-equivalent-channel-separation}
Consider two channels with the same finite instanton chart, the same
collective-coordinate density, and the same nonzero-mode determinant
convention.  In a two-flavor zero-mode sector suppose that the only
channel-dependent local source input is a \(2\times2\) zero-mode source
matrix \(B(z)\), so that
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal Z_{\Lambda}^{0}(z)=
  \det B(z)
  =
  B_{uu}(z)B_{dd}(z)-B_{ud}(z)B_{du}(z).
  \label{eq:instanton-two-flavor-source-determinant}
\end{equation}
Then the common moduli measure and determinant convention do not determine
the amplitude.  If \(B(z)\) has rank one on the retained window, the
two-flavor source contribution vanishes identically, even when the
collective-coordinate density and nonzero-mode determinant are nonzero.  If
another channel has a full-rank \(B'(z)\) on a set of positive retained
weight, its coefficient in
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-channel-functional} can be nonzero with the same
moduli data.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At finite regulator, the fermion zero modes are ordinary Grassmann variables.
For two flavors the Berezin integral over the four source variables extracts
the determinant coefficient in
\eqref{eq:instanton-two-flavor-source-determinant}.  A rank-one source matrix
has zero determinant pointwise, so the whole zero-mode coefficient is zero
before the collective-coordinate integral is performed.  A full-rank source
matrix has nonzero determinant at the point under consideration.  Multiplying
by the same bosonic density and the same nonzero-mode determinant therefore
changes the channel coefficient only through the source determinant and the
subsequent observable projection.  The common moduli space has not changed.
\end{proof}

This proposition is the shortest way to state the physics lesson.  The
instanton solution supplies a saddle and a measure factor.  The physical
amplitude asks which external states or sources can see the zero-mode
directions, whether that source information survives projection, and whether
the remaining integral is controlled at its boundaries.

\section{Mass Saturation Versus Source Differentiation}
\label{sec:instanton-mass-source-differentiation}

The local 't Hooft determinant is useful precisely because it keeps the
zero-mode source algebra visible.  It becomes misleading when the determinant
is read as a single undifferentiated number, independent of how the physical
observable is selected.

\begin{proposition}[Two-flavor mass/source determinant coordinate]
\label{prop:two-flavor-source-mass-determinant-coordinate}
Let
\[
  M=\begin{pmatrix}m_u&0\\0&m_d\end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  B=\begin{pmatrix}B_{uu}&B_{ud}\\B_{du}&B_{dd}\end{pmatrix}
\]
be the mass and source restrictions to the instanton zero-mode space.  The
finite zero-mode Berezin coefficient is
\begin{equation}
  \det(M+B)
  =
  m_um_d
  +m_uB_{dd}+m_dB_{uu}
  +B_{uu}B_{dd}-B_{ud}B_{du}.
  \label{eq:two-flavor-mass-source-determinant-polynomial}
\end{equation}
Thus the mass-saturated vacuum activity, the two-source mass-assisted
coefficients, and the four-source differentiated coefficient are different
coordinates of the same zero-mode determinant.  They are not the same
physical observable.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is the determinant of a \(2\times2\) matrix:
\[
  \det(M+B)
  =(m_u+B_{uu})(m_d+B_{dd})-B_{ud}B_{du}.
\]
Expanding gives
\eqref{eq:two-flavor-mass-source-determinant-polynomial}.  Berezin
integration then selects the term with the required number of source
variables or mass insertions.  Setting \(B=0\) leaves the vacuum coordinate
\(m_um_d\); setting \(M=0\) leaves the four-source coefficient
\(\det B\).  Mixed mass/source observables select the linear-in-\(B\)
terms.
\end{proof}

This separation is what the original instanton amplitude calculation uses.
The four-fermion coefficient is obtained by differentiating the source
functional and retaining the zero-mode form factors, not by quoting the
mass-saturated vacuum activity and changing its interpretation.  The
coefficient must then be paired with the nonzero-mode determinant, the color
orientation tensor, external source residues or LSZ data, and a size-window
or infrared mechanism.

\section{A Hard Four-Fermion Benchmark}
\label{sec:instanton-hard-four-fermion-benchmark}

The cleanest test of the preceding distinction is the hard two-flavor
four-fermion channel.  Work in \(SU(3)\) with two light Dirac fundamentals,
choose a \(Q=1\) BPST sector, and insert four amputated external fermion
sources with momenta of size \(Q\).  The center integral first imposes the
momentum-conservation gate.  The color orientation integral then projects the
four source slots through a shared Haar tensor.  The fermion zero modes
produce two rank conditions, one for the right-handed overlap matrix and one
for the left-handed overlap matrix.  Only after these gates have been passed
does the size integral define the hard coefficient.

\begin{proposition}[Individual zero-mode slot in the hard channel]
\label{prop:instanton-hard-individual-zero-mode-slot}
The hard four-source channel uses the Fourier transform of individual BPST
fermion zero modes.  In the singular-gauge slot normalization of
Proposition~\ref{prop:instanton-individual-zero-mode-form-factor}, after the
spin-color tensor and the adjacent amputation convention have been separated,
the scalar slot profile is
\begin{equation}
  F_{\rm zm}(t)
  =
  -t\frac{\dd}{\dd t}
  \bigl[I_0(t)K_0(t)-I_1(t)K_1(t)\bigr]
  =
  2t\bigl[I_0(t)K_1(t)-I_1(t)K_0(t)\bigr]
  -2I_1(t)K_1(t),
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-individual-slot-form-factor}
\end{equation}
where \(t=\rho |p|/2\).  It has local limit \(F_{\rm zm}(0)=1\), while
\begin{equation}
  F_{\rm zm}(t)
  =
  \frac{3}{4t^3}+O(t^{-5})
  =
  \frac{6}{(\rho |p|)^3}
  +O((\rho |p|)^{-5}).
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-individual-slot-tail}
\end{equation}
Thus, for four hard slots with \(|p_\ell|=c_\ell Q\) and \(s=Q\rho\),
\begin{equation}
  \prod_{\ell=1}^4 F_{\rm zm}(c_\ell s/2)
  =
  6^4
  \left(\prod_{\ell=1}^4c_\ell^{-3}\right)
  s^{-12}
  +O(s^{-14})
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-four-slot-product-tail}
\end{equation}
when the \(c_\ell\)'s are held fixed away from zero.  A fused bilinear-density
source instead uses \(zK_1(z)\) and has exponential large-\(z\) decay.  The
two source types therefore define different endpoint problems before any
physical projection is taken.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first equality in
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-individual-slot-form-factor} is the
singular-gauge Fourier transform of the normalized fundamental zero mode
after its spin-color tensor has been factored out.  Differentiating
\(\Phi(t)=I_0(t)K_0(t)-I_1(t)K_1(t)\), using
\[
  I_0'=I_1,\qquad
  I_1'=I_0-\frac{I_1}{t},\qquad
  K_0'=-K_1,\qquad
  K_1'=-K_0-\frac{K_1}{t},
\]
gives the second expression in
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-individual-slot-form-factor}.  The small-\(t\)
expansions \(I_0=1+O(t^2)\), \(I_1=t/2+O(t^3)\), and
\(K_1=t^{-1}+O(t\log t)\) give \(F_{\rm zm}(0)=1\).

For the hard endpoint, use the large-\(t\) products
\[
\begin{aligned}
  I_0K_1
  &=
  \frac1{2t}
  \left(1+\frac1{2t}+\frac{3}{16t^3}+O(t^{-5})\right),\\
  I_1K_0
  &=
  \frac1{2t}
  \left(1-\frac1{2t}-\frac{3}{16t^3}+O(t^{-5})\right),\\
  I_1K_1
  &=
  \frac1{2t}
  \left(1-\frac{3}{8t^2}+O(t^{-4})\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
Then
\[
  2t(I_0K_1-I_1K_0)
  =
  \frac1t+\frac{3}{8t^3}+O(t^{-5}),
  \qquad
  2I_1K_1
  =
  \frac1t-\frac{3}{8t^3}+O(t^{-5}).
\]
The \(t^{-1}\) terms cancel in \(F_{\rm zm}\), leaving
\(3/(4t^3)+O(t^{-5})\).  Substituting \(t=c_\ell s/2\) gives the
single-slot coefficient \(6c_\ell^{-3}s^{-3}\), and multiplying the four
fixed hard slots gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-four-slot-product-tail}.
\end{proof}

Let \(Q_\ell=c_\ell Q\), \(c_\ell>0\), be the four hard source scales, and let
\(F_\ell(c_\ell Q\rho)\) denote the corresponding individual zero-mode slot
form factor, including any bounded source-window factor in the declared
source normalization.  After the center delta, common determinant
constant, Haar contraction, amputation convention, and source-overlap
determinants have been pulled into a prefactor \(\mathcal K_{\rm gate}\), the
stripped one-instanton hard coefficient has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{4}^{\rm hard}(Q)
  =
  \mathcal K_{\rm gate}\,
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}\,
  Q^{-35/3}\,
  H(c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4)
  +R_{4}^{\rm hard}(Q),
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-coefficient}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  H(c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4)
  =
  \int_0^\infty
  s^{32/3}\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}F_\ell(c_\ell s)\,\dd s .
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-window-integral}
\end{equation}
The residual \(R_{4}^{\rm hard}\) contains the determinant, source-frame,
amputation, endpoint, sector-isolation, continuation, and physical-projection
remainders from \eqref{eq:instanton-physical-channel-residual-budget}.  The
formula is a benchmark channel, not a universal local vertex: changing the
source wave packets, the orientation contraction, or the physical projection
changes \(\mathcal K_{\rm gate}\), \(H\), or \(R_{4}^{\rm hard}\).

\begin{proposition}[\(SU(3)\), \(N_f=2\) hard-source power and slow tail]
\label{prop:su3-nf2-hard-source-power-slow-tail}
In the preceding hard channel, \(b_0=29/3\).  The four differentiated
zero-mode slots contribute the local source power \(\rho^6\), so the size
integrand before hard form factors is
\begin{equation}
  \rho^{\,b_0+6-5}\,\dd\rho
  =
  \rho^{32/3}\,\dd\rho .
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-rho-power}
\end{equation}
After \(s=Q\rho\), the hard coefficient carries the scale power
\begin{equation}
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3},
  \qquad
  29/3-35/3=-2 ,
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-scale-power}
\end{equation}
which is the mass dimension of a four-fermion coefficient.  If all four
individual zero-mode form factors are the singular-gauge hard slots in
Proposition~\ref{prop:instanton-hard-individual-zero-mode-slot}, so that
\[
  F_\ell(c_\ell s)=6c_\ell^{-3}s^{-3}+O(s^{-5}),
\]
then the large-\(s\) endpoint of \eqref{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-window-integral}
has leading tail
\begin{equation}
  \int_R^\infty
  s^{32/3}\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}F_\ell(c_\ell s)\,\dd s
  =
  3\,6^4\!\left(\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}c_\ell^{-3}\right)R^{-1/3}
  +O(R^{-7/3}).
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-slow-tail}
\end{equation}
If one of the four slots is not hard, this power test no longer controls the
large-size endpoint by the same source profile.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(SU(3)\) with two Dirac fundamentals,
\[
  b_0=\frac{11}{3}3-\frac{2}{3}2=\frac{29}{3}.
\]
The universal one-loop size density contributes \(\rho^{b_0-5}\dd\rho\), and
the four differentiated zero-mode slots contribute \(\rho^6\).  This gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-rho-power}.  The change of variables
\(s=Q\rho\) gives
\[
  \dd\rho\,\rho^{32/3}
  =
  Q^{-35/3}s^{32/3}\,\dd s .
\]
At the hard subtraction point \(\mu=Q\), the one-loop density prefactor
\(\mu^{b_0}\) contributes \(Q^{29/3}\), so before rewriting the classical
exponential the coefficient scales as
\(\exp[-8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2(Q)]Q^{-2}\).  The one-loop RG-invariant trade
\[
  \exp[-8\pi^2/g_{\rm ht}^2(Q)]
  =
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-29/3}
\]
then gives the final power
\(\Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3}\).  The total mass dimension is
\(29/3-35/3=-2\), as required for a coefficient multiplying a dimension-six
four-fermion source.

For the endpoint, multiply the four large-\(s\) tails.  The product contributes
\[
  6^4\left(\prod_\ell c_\ell^{-3}\right)s^{-12}+O(s^{-14}).
\]
Multiplication by \(s^{32/3}\) gives
\[
  6^4\left(\prod_\ell c_\ell^{-3}\right)s^{-4/3}+O(s^{-10/3}).
\]
Integrating from \(R\) to infinity gives
\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-slow-tail}.  With only three hard slots
the leading endpoint power is \(s^{32/3-9}=s^{5/3}\), which is not integrable
at infinity.
\end{proof}

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Hard benchmark gate ledger]
\label{ca:instanton-hard-benchmark-gate-ledger}
A controlled comparison of two hard four-source instanton coefficients at
scales \(Q_1,Q_2\) may use the power in
\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-scale-power} only after the gate data are
held fixed:
\[
  \Delta_{\rm cen}\ne0,\qquad
  \mathcal P_{\rm Haar}\ne0,\qquad
  \det B_R\ne0,\qquad
  \det B_L\ne0,
\]
with the same amputation convention, source-frame normalization, determinant
scheme, sector projection, and dimensionless source-window shape.  If the
retained coefficients have the form
\[
  \mathcal A_j
  =
  \mathcal A_j^{(0)}(1+\varepsilon_j),
  \qquad
  |\varepsilon_j|\le e_j<1,
  \qquad j=1,2,
\]
and the leading gate data are the same at the two scales, then
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathcal A_2}{\mathcal A_1}
  =
  \left(\frac{Q_2}{Q_1}\right)^{-35/3}
  \frac{1+\varepsilon_2}{1+\varepsilon_1},
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-ratio}
\end{equation}
with residual multiplier bounded by
\begin{equation}
  \left|
  \frac{1+\varepsilon_2}{1+\varepsilon_1}-1
  \right|
  \le
  \frac{e_1+e_2}{1-e_1}.
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-ratio-bound}
\end{equation}
Thus the determinant constant and \(\Lambda_{\rm ht}\) cancel in a same-theory
hard-scale ratio, while the dimensionless source-window and rank-conditioning
data do not cancel unless they have actually been transported.  An off-shell
center gate, a rank-one zero-mode overlap, an omitted Haar projection, or
unamputated external residues change the amplitude coordinate before the power
law \((Q_2/Q_1)^{-35/3}\) can be tested.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{The Hard Kernel as a Wilsonian OPE Datum}
\label{sec:instanton-hard-wilsonian-ope-datum}

The hard coefficient in
\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-coefficient} is still a source
coefficient.  To use it as a local QFT input, one must decide which part of
the size integral is short-distance and which part is left for the matrix
element or the long-distance remainder.  This is the channel-level version of
the Wilsonian split in
Controlled Approximation~\ref{ca:short-instanton-ope-coefficient}.

Fix a dimensionless size split \(R\) in the hard variable \(s=Q\rho\), and
write
\[
  \rho_I=\frac{R}{Q},
  \qquad
  \mu_I=\frac{Q}{R}.
\]
Let \([O_4]_\mu\) be the renormalized local four-fermion operator obtained
from the same spin, flavor, and color-orientation projection as the hard
source channel.  The short-instanton coefficient is
\begin{equation}
  C_{4}^{<}(Q,R;\mu)
  =
  \ee^{\ii\theta}
  \mathcal K_{\rm gate}\,
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}\,
  Q^{-35/3}
  Z_{4}(R,\mu)
  \int_0^{R}
    s^{32/3}\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}F_\ell(c_\ell s)\,\dd s ,
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-short-ope-coefficient}
\end{equation}
where \(Z_4(R,\mu)\) is the finite matching matrix element from the source
projection to the chosen renormalized operator coordinate.  A physical
hadronic or inclusive observable then has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{\rm phys}^{I}
  =
  C_{4}^{<}(Q,R;\mu)\,
  \langle f|[O_4]_\mu|i\rangle_{\rm phys}
  +
  \mathcal A_{4}^{>}(Q,R)
  +
  R_{\rm match}+R_{\rm IR}+R_{\rm proj}+R_{\rm sec}.
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-ope-physical-assembly}
\end{equation}
The first term is local only in the Wilsonian sense.  The second term is the
same instanton source channel with \(\rho>\rho_I\), including whatever
screening, confinement, finite-volume, or source-window mechanism controls
that region.  The residuals record, respectively, operator matching, infrared
completion, final physical projection, and sector isolation.  Thus
\(C_4^{<}\) is not the physical amplitude unless the matrix element and the
long-size/remainder terms have been supplied.

The factorization-scale flow is a boundary flux of the same size integral:
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{\log\mu_I}C_4^{<}
  =
  -\ee^{\ii\theta}
  \mathcal K_{\rm gate}
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}
  Q^{-35/3}
  Z_{4}(R,\mu)\,
  R^{35/3}\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}F_\ell(c_\ell R)
  +
  C_4^{<}\,\partial_{\log\mu_I}\log Z_4 .
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-short-ope-boundary-flow}
\end{equation}
The first term is the explicit movement of the shell
\(\rho=R/Q\) between coefficient and remainder; the second is ordinary
operator-coordinate transport.  Changing the local operator basis changes
\(Z_4\), \(C_4^{<}\), and the matrix element inversely, not the completed
observable in \eqref{eq:instanton-hard-ope-physical-assembly}.

For the individual hard zero-mode slots of
Proposition~\ref{prop:instanton-hard-individual-zero-mode-slot}, the omitted
tail has the leading form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{4}^{>}(Q,R)
  =
  \ee^{\ii\theta}
  \mathcal K_{\rm gate}
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}
  Q^{-35/3}
  \left[
    3\,6^4
    \left(\prod_{\ell=1}^{4}c_\ell^{-3}\right)R^{-1/3}
    +O(R^{-7/3})
  \right]
  +R_{>}(Q,R).
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-ope-long-tail}
\end{equation}
The small power \(R^{-1/3}\) is the warning sign.  A hard source suppresses the
large-instanton endpoint, but it does so slowly for individual zero-mode
slots.  A reliable local 't Hooft-operator statement must therefore choose
\(R\), prove that the short coefficient is in the weak-size window, and keep
the long-size tail or show that the physical observable suppresses it.  Using
the full hard source coefficient as a local operator coefficient, or using the
short coefficient as a physical amplitude, skips this Wilsonian boundary
calculation.

\section{Normal Fluctuations Are Source Data}
\label{sec:instanton-normal-fluctuation-source-data}

The one-loop determinant normalizes the Gaussian integral over nonzero modes.
A selected source amplitude also asks how its insertion changes under those
same normal fluctuations.  This is one of the places where the original
instanton amplitude calculation is harder than a moduli-space calculation:
the determinant of the Hessian and the fluctuation average of the source
insertion are coupled through the same measure but are not the same number.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Nonzero-mode source quotient]
\label{ca:instanton-nonzero-mode-source-quotient}
Fix a retained instanton chart point \(z\), project out collective and gauge
zero modes, and let \(\eta\in\mathcal N_z\) denote the normal fluctuation
coordinate.  Let \(d\gamma_z(\eta)\) be the normalized Gaussian measure
defined by the zero-mode-deleted Hessian, and let
\(V_z(\eta)\) be the cubic-and-higher action together with finite counterterm
remainders in the same normal coordinates.  Write the selected source
insertion, after the explicit zero-mode coefficient has been separated, as
\[
  \mathcal Z_z(\eta;\mathcal J)
  =
  \mathcal Z_z(0;\mathcal J)
  \bigl(1+U_z(\eta;\mathcal J)\bigr),
  \qquad U_z(0;\mathcal J)=0 .
\]
On a chart where the leading zero-mode/source coefficient is nonzero, the
fluctuation factor multiplying the hard channel is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal R_{\rm fluc}(z;\mathcal J)
  =
  \frac{
    \left\langle
      \bigl(1+U_z(\eta;\mathcal J)\bigr)
      \ee^{-V_z(\eta)}
    \right\rangle_{\gamma_z}
  }{
    D_z
  },
  \qquad
  D_z=\left\langle\ee^{-V_z(\eta)}\right\rangle_{\gamma_z}.
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-quotient}
\end{equation}
The exact identity
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal R_{\rm fluc}-1
  =
  \langle U_z\rangle_{\gamma_z}
  +
  \frac{
    \left\langle
    \bigl(U_z-\langle U_z\rangle_{\gamma_z}\bigr)
    \bigl(\ee^{-V_z}-D_z\bigr)
    \right\rangle_{\gamma_z}
  }{D_z}
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-covariance}
\end{equation}
separates the Gaussian mean of the selected source from its covariance with
the same nonzero-mode interaction weight that produced the determinant
normalization.  A source-independent determinant calibration fixes \(D_z\).
It does not fix either term in
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-covariance}.

If the retained source variation begins as
\begin{equation}
  U_z(\eta;\mathcal J)
  =
  \ell_a(z;\mathcal J)\eta_a
  +\frac12Q_{ab}(z;\mathcal J)\eta_a\eta_b
  +U_{\ge3,z}(\eta;\mathcal J),
  \qquad
  C_{ab}(z)=\langle\eta_a\eta_b\rangle_{\gamma_z},
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-gaussian-source-expansion}
\end{equation}
then the Gaussian source mean is
\[
  \langle U_z\rangle_{\gamma_z}
  =
  \frac12\operatorname{Tr}(Q_zC_z)
  +\langle U_{\ge3,z}\rangle_{\gamma_z},
\]
because the linear normal-fluctuation term has zero Gaussian mean.  Suppose
on the retained source and size window
\[
  |D_z|\ge d_0,\qquad
  |\langle U_{\ge3,z}\rangle_{\gamma_z}|\le\epsilon_U,\qquad
  \|U_z-\langle U_z\rangle_{\gamma_z}\|_{L^2(\gamma_z)}\le M_U,
  \qquad
  \|\ee^{-V_z}-D_z\|_{L^2(\gamma_z)}\le M_V .
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    \mathcal R_{\rm fluc}(z;\mathcal J)
    -
    1
    -
    \frac12\operatorname{Tr}(Q_zC_z)
  \right|
  \le
  \epsilon_U+\frac{M_UM_V}{d_0}.
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-bound}
\end{equation}
After multiplying the hard kernel in
\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-four-source-coefficient}, the contribution of this
normal-fluctuation slot to the absolute window budget is bounded by the
absolute leading kernel mass times the right side of
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-bound}.  If
\(\mathcal Z_z(0;\mathcal J)\) loses rank, the relative quotient
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-quotient} is the wrong
coordinate; the fluctuation contribution must be kept as an absolute source
residual in \eqref{eq:instanton-physical-channel-residual-budget}.
\end{controlledapproximation}

Thus a hard instanton amplitude has three separate fluctuation entries.  The
universal logarithmic determinant supplies the \((\mu\rho)^{b_0}\) density
gate.  The finite determinant constant fixes a scheme-dependent Gaussian
normalization.  The quotient
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-quotient} tests the selected
source under that Gaussian and its interaction remainders.  Collapsing these
three entries into a single ``measure prefactor'' loses the amplitude
calculation.

\section{The Hard Channel As An Assembled Amplitude}
\label{sec:instanton-hard-amplitude-assembly}

The preceding gates are useful only when they are recombined in the same
observable coordinate.  For the \(SU(3)\), \(N_f=2\), hard four-source channel,
let \(s=Q\rho\), let \(\Omega\) denote the retained color-orientation
coordinate, and let \(\chi_{\rm phys}\) record the declared source window,
amputation convention, and physical projection after the center
momentum-conservation gate has been applied.  A finite-regulator assembly has
the form
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal A_{4,\Lambda}^{\rm hard}(Q;\mathcal J)
  &=
  \ee^{\ii\theta}
  C_{\mathcal S}\,
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3}
  \int_{\mathcal W}
  K_0(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)\,
  \mathcal R_{\rm det}(s,\Omega)\,                       \\
  &\qquad\qquad{}\times
  \mathcal R_{\rm fluc}(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)\,
  \mathcal R_{\rm zm}(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)\,
  \mathcal R_{\rm phys}(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)\,
  \dd s\,\dd\Omega
  +R_{\rm ext}(Q;\mathcal J),
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-assembled-channel}
\end{equation}
where
\[
\begin{aligned}
  K_0(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)
  &=
  s^{32/3}
  \mathcal P_{\rm Haar}(\Omega)\,
  \det B_R^0(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)\,
  \det B_L^0(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)              \\
  &\qquad{}\times
  \prod_{\ell=1}^4F_\ell(c_\ell s)\,
  \chi_{\rm phys}^{0}(s,\Omega;\mathcal J).
\end{aligned}
\]
The constant \(C_{\mathcal S}\) is the finite determinant and orientation
normalization in the chosen scheme.  The logarithmic determinant and running
action have already been traded for
\(\Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3}\).  The remaining factors have distinct
jobs: \(\mathcal R_{\rm det}\) is the finite determinant-shape mismatch after
scheme matching, \(\mathcal R_{\rm fluc}\) is the source-dependent quotient in
\eqref{eq:instanton-physical-source-fluctuation-quotient},
\(\mathcal R_{\rm zm}\) is the zero-mode/source-frame stability factor, and
\(\mathcal R_{\rm phys}\) is the finite projection or continuation mismatch.
The term \(R_{\rm ext}\) contains sector isolation, infrared, cut, endpoint,
and continuum errors not represented by the retained window.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Hard amplitude assembly ledger]
\label{ca:instanton-hard-amplitude-assembly-ledger}
Let
\begin{equation}
  H_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  =
  \int_{\mathcal W}K_0(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)\,\dd s\,\dd\Omega,
  \qquad
  M_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  =
  \int_{\mathcal W}|K_0(s,\Omega;\mathcal J)|\,\dd s\,\dd\Omega .
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-assembly-leading-window}
\end{equation}
Assume on the retained window that
\[
  \mathcal R_x=1+\delta_x,\qquad
  |\delta_x|\le e_x,
  \qquad x\in\{\det,{\rm fluc},{\rm zm},{\rm phys}\}.
\]
Then the leading assembled coefficient
\[
  \mathcal A_{4}^{0}(Q;\mathcal J)
  =
  \ee^{\ii\theta}
  C_{\mathcal S}
  \Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3}
  H_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J)
\]
has the absolute bound
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \left|
    \mathcal A_{4,\Lambda}^{\rm hard}(Q;\mathcal J)
    -
    \mathcal A_4^0(Q;\mathcal J)
  \right|
  &\le
  |C_{\mathcal S}|\Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3}
  \epsilon_{\rm asm}\,
  M_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  +|R_{\rm ext}(Q;\mathcal J)|,                         \\
  \epsilon_{\rm asm}
  &=
  (1+e_{\det})(1+e_{\rm fluc})(1+e_{\rm zm})(1+e_{\rm phys})-1 .
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:instanton-hard-assembly-error-bound}
\end{equation}
If a relative statement is wanted, a noncancellation margin must be supplied:
\[
  |H_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J)|
  \ge h_0 M_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J),
  \qquad h_0>0 .
\]
Only then does \eqref{eq:instanton-hard-assembly-error-bound} imply a
relative correction of size at most
\[
  \frac{\epsilon_{\rm asm}}{h_0}
  +
  \frac{|R_{\rm ext}(Q;\mathcal J)|}{
    |C_{\mathcal S}|\Lambda_{\rm ht}^{29/3}Q^{-35/3}
    |H_{\mathcal C}^{0}(\mathcal J)|
  } .
\]
Without this margin the correct control is absolute, not relative; a signed
window cancellation can make a tiny leading number even when the source,
determinant, and projection errors are individually small.
\end{controlledapproximation}

This assembly formula is the point at which the calculation becomes a
physical amplitude.  Quoting the BPST density tests only the logarithmic
fluctuation gate.  Quoting the zero-mode determinant tests only chirality and
source rank.  Quoting
\(\mathcal R_{\rm fluc}\) tests only the selected source under the normal
Gaussian.  The hard coefficient is obtained only after these pieces multiply
the same Haar-projected, amputated, windowed, and physically projected kernel.
Changing any of those data changes the amplitude coordinate, even though the
underlying instanton saddle is unchanged.

\section{A Source Kernel Still Needs a Physical Projection}
\label{sec:instanton-source-kernel-physical-projection}

The word ``amplitude'' should not hide the last map.  A finite Euclidean
instanton source kernel
\(\mathcal K_{E,\Lambda}[\mathcal J]\) is a source-functional coefficient in a
regulated path integral.  It becomes a scattering amplitude, an OPE
coefficient, a hadronic matrix element, or a spectral-bin contribution only
after the corresponding projection has been declared.  In a confining gauge
theory this point is especially important: a colored partonic instanton kernel
can be a useful auxiliary object, but it is not by itself an LSZ amplitude
unless a gauge-invariant external source, an infrared deformation, or a
separate partonic matching convention has been supplied.

For a stable gauge-invariant external channel the projection is a pole-window
operation.  If \(O_i,O_f\) are interpolating sources with nonzero overlap with
states \(|i\rangle,|f\rangle\), the large Euclidean-time window has the form
\begin{equation}
  G_{fi}^{I}(T,t)
  =
  Z_f Z_i\,
  \ee^{-E_f(T-t)}\ee^{-E_i t}\,
  \mathcal M_{fi}^{I}
  +R_{\rm exc}^{I}(T,t),
  \label{eq:instanton-hadronic-pole-window}
\end{equation}
after the instanton kernel has been assembled in the source channel.  The
matrix element \(\mathcal M_{fi}^{I}\) is obtained by isolating this pole and
then dividing by \(Z_fZ_i\).  Dividing a raw source correlator by guessed
overlaps before the pole has been isolated leaves excited-state leakage in the
answer, and a vanishing source overlap makes the extraction undefined.

For an inclusive or Euclidean spectral observable the projection is different.
A Euclidean source value has a Stieltjes form
\begin{equation}
  G_E^I(Q^2)
  =
  P_{\rm ct}(Q^2)
  +
  \int_0^\infty \frac{\dd\rho_I(\sigma)}{\sigma+Q^2},
  \qquad
  \mathfrak B_{\Delta}^I
  =
  \int_{\Delta}\dd\rho_I(\sigma),
  \label{eq:instanton-spectral-projection-window}
\end{equation}
where \(P_{\rm ct}\) is a local contact polynomial.  The bin
\(\mathfrak B_{\Delta}^I\) is a discontinuity or spectral functional; it is
not a single spacelike Euclidean value.  Contact polynomials can change
\(G_E^I(Q^2)\) while carrying no separated spectral weight in \(\Delta\), and
two positive spectral measures can agree at one Euclidean point while giving
different bin weights.  A physical spectral claim therefore needs an inversion
or window-stability datum, not just a source-kernel number.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Physical projection bridge for instanton
source kernels]
\label{ca:instanton-source-kernel-physical-projection}
Let \(\Pi_{\rm phys}\) be one declared physical projection: a stable-particle
pole residue, a spectral-bin functional, a discontinuity functional, an OPE
matching map, or an infrared-safe inclusive measurement.  Applying it to the
assembled source
kernel gives a leading physical coordinate
\[
  \mathcal A_{\rm phys}^{0}
  =
  \Pi_{\rm phys}^{0}[\mathcal K_{E,\Lambda}^{0}].
\]
The comparison with the actual physical quantity must budget the bridge
residuals
\[
  \Delta_{\rm phys}
  =
  \Delta_{\rm reg}
  +\Delta_{\rm cont}
  +\Delta_{\rm pole/bin}
  +\Delta_{\rm IR}
  +\Delta_{\rm cut}
  +\Delta_{\rm match}
  +\Delta_{\rm end},
\]
where the entries denote, respectively, regulator transport, analytic
continuation, pole or spectral-bin isolation, infrared completion, unitarity
cut or discontinuity normalization, operator/source matching, and retained
size-window endpoint error.  If
\[
  |\Delta_x|\le B_x
  \qquad
  x\in\{{\rm reg,cont,pole/bin,IR,cut,match,end}\},
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    \mathcal A_{\rm phys}
    -
    \Pi_{\rm phys}^{0}[\mathcal K_{E,\Lambda}^{0}]
  \right|
  \le
  B_{\rm reg}+B_{\rm cont}+B_{\rm pole/bin}+B_{\rm IR}
  +B_{\rm cut}+B_{\rm match}+B_{\rm end}.
  \label{eq:instanton-physical-bridge-residual-bound}
\end{equation}

The negative controls are immediate.  A color-nonsinglet auxiliary kernel has
no standalone physical LSZ map in a confining theory.  A pole residue cannot
be read before the source overlap and excited-state window are controlled.  A
spectral bin cannot be inferred from one Euclidean Stieltjes value.  An OPE
coefficient changes when the operator basis or matching scheme is changed.  A
real-time rate requires a retarded/KMS continuation rather than a Euclidean
static source value.  Thus the projection bridge is a separate physical
calculation, not a relabelling of the instanton density or the hard source
kernel.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{First Cluster Corrections Are Amplitude Data}
\label{sec:instanton-first-cluster-amplitude-correction}

Equation~\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-assembled-channel} is a \(Q=1\) source
coefficient.  Taken alone, it leaves sector isolation from neighboring
topological sectors unproved.  The next obstruction lies in the connected
two-body amplitude in the same regulator, source frame, and physical
projection.

Let \(\sigma,\tau\in\{+,-\}\) denote instanton and anti-instanton charges,
and write \(z_1,z_2\) for their collective coordinates after the common
center has been separated.  In a retained two-body chart, the source-channel
cluster coefficient has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm conn}(\mathcal J)
  =
  \ee^{\ii(\sigma+\tau)\bar\theta}
  \int_{\mathcal W_{\sigma\tau}}
  \left[
    K_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm full}(z_1,z_2;\mathcal J)
    -
    K_{\sigma}^{(1)}(z_1;\mathcal J)
    K_{\tau}^{(1)}(z_2;\mathcal J)
  \right]\,
  \dd\nu_{\sigma\tau}(z_1,z_2).
  \label{eq:instanton-pair-connected-source-coefficient}
\end{equation}
The full two-body kernel includes the relative quasi-zero-mode interaction,
the zero-mode overlap matrix, the nonzero-mode determinant in the pair
background, the source insertion, and the final projection.  The second term
is the disconnected product of the two already-counted one-body channels.
Removing it is part of the amplitude calculation, not an aesthetic
normalization choice.  Without this subtraction the cluster coefficient
double counts the Poisson term.

For \(\sigma+\tau=0\), the pair is theta-neutral.  That does not make it
irrelevant.  An instanton--anti-instanton molecule may change a source
correlator through zero-mode overlap singular values or through the
source-dependent nonzero-mode quotient even though it contributes no
\(\bar\theta\)-curvature at this order.  For \(\sigma+\tau=\pm2\), the
same-charge pair carries a second theta harmonic; in a vacuum handoff it is
the source of the first \(\cos(2\bar\theta)\) correction described in
ControlledApproximation~\ref{ca:first-cluster-correction-dilute-instantons}.
Thus theta curvature, hard source coefficients, and neutral-pair source
correlators again read different pieces of the same finite-regulator
instanton ensemble.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[First cluster correction to a source amplitude]
\label{ca:instanton-first-cluster-amplitude-correction}
Fix a one-instanton source channel \(\mathcal C\) and its anti-instanton
conjugate in the same scheme.  A first cluster approximation to the selected
source amplitude has the ordered form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{\rm cl}^{(2)}(\mathcal J)
  =
  \mathcal A_+^{(1)}(\mathcal J)
  +\mathcal A_-^{(1)}(\mathcal J)
  +\sum_{\sigma,\tau\in\{+,-\}}
    \mathcal A_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm conn}(\mathcal J)
  +R_{\ge3}^{\rm cl}(\mathcal J).
  \label{eq:instanton-source-first-cluster-assembly}
\end{equation}
Let \(C_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(z_1,z_2;\mathcal J)\) be the retained leading
connected pair kernel after the disconnected one-body product in
\eqref{eq:instanton-pair-connected-source-coefficient} has been subtracted,
and set
\[
  H_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  =
  \int_{\mathcal W_{\sigma\tau}}
  C_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(z_1,z_2;\mathcal J)\,\dd\nu_{\sigma\tau},
  \qquad
  M_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  =
  \int_{\mathcal W_{\sigma\tau}}
  |C_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(z_1,z_2;\mathcal J)|\,\dd|\nu_{\sigma\tau}|.
\]
Assume the retained pair kernel differs from \(C_{\sigma\tau}^{0}\) by
multiplicative corrections
\[
  \mathcal R_y=1+\delta_y,\qquad |\delta_y|\le e_y,
  \qquad
  y\in\{{\rm qzm},{\rm ov},{\rm nz},{\rm src},{\rm phys}\},
\]
where the entries are, respectively, the relative quasi-zero-mode
interaction, zero-mode overlap determinant, nonzero-mode determinant mismatch,
source-kernel correction, and physical-projection mismatch.  Let
\[
  \epsilon_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm pair}
  =
  \prod_y(1+e_y)-1,
\]
and let \(R_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm tail}\) denote the absolute remainder from
relative-coordinate tails, collision faces, and the boundary of the two-body
window.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \left|
  \mathcal A_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm conn}(\mathcal J)
  -
  \ee^{\ii(\sigma+\tau)\bar\theta}
  H_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  \right|
  \le
  \epsilon_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm pair}
  M_{\sigma\tau}^{0}(\mathcal J)
  +R_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm tail}.
  \label{eq:instanton-source-first-cluster-bound}
\end{equation}
The relative-coordinate finiteness needed to make
\(R_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm tail}\) small is the integrability condition of
ControlledApproximation~\ref{ca:instanton-two-body-cluster-integrability},
transported to the present source and projection data.

This ledger has three immediate negative controls.  First, replacing
\(K_{\sigma\tau}^{\rm full}\) by the product
\(K_{\sigma}^{(1)}K_{\tau}^{(1)}\) gives zero connected cluster data and only
recovers the Poisson approximation.  Second, using theta curvature to bound a
neutral \(I\bar I\) source coefficient misses the case
\(\sigma+\tau=0\), where the theta phase is trivial but the source kernel or
zero-mode overlap can be nonzero.  Third, a one-instanton sector-isolation
claim must budget the absolute size of the retained pair terms in
\eqref{eq:instanton-source-first-cluster-assembly}; finite one-body density,
by itself, does not control \(R_{\rm sec}\).
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{Observable Handoffs Are Not Interchangeable}
\label{sec:instanton-observable-handoffs}

The assembled coefficient in
\eqref{eq:instanton-hard-assembled-channel} is still not a universal
``instanton contribution.''  It is a coefficient in a chosen source and
projection coordinate.  The same \(Q=\pm1\) saddle data can feed several
different QFT observables only after different final maps have been applied.
Those maps have different dimensions, positivity properties, and limiting
procedures.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Observable handoff ledger]
\label{ca:instanton-observable-handoff-ledger}
Let \(\mathcal X_{+}\) be the finite-regulator \(Q=1\) one-instanton
amplitude density after the collective density, nonzero-mode determinant,
zero-mode saturation, source matching, and endpoint window have all been
assembled.  Let \(\mathcal X_{-}\) be the corresponding anti-instanton
density.  Four common handoffs are these.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item A hard source coefficient extracts
\(\mathfrak L_{\rm hard}[\mathcal X_+]\) and still needs the Haar projector,
amputation convention, and spectral or OPE projection.
\item A theta curvature extracts the vacuum activity
\(\zeta_+\ee^{\ii\bar\theta}+\zeta_-\ee^{-\ii\bar\theta}\) and still needs
zero-mode saturation and a cluster window.
\item A \(U(1)_A\)-odd susceptibility extracts the zero-mode overlap singular
values \(s_\alpha\) from an \(I\bar I\) ensemble and still needs the ensemble
weight and the thermodynamic/chiral order of limits.
\item A real-time axial relaxation rate extracts \(\Gamma_{\rm CS}\) from the
retarded zero-frequency slope and still needs KMS continuation and the axial
susceptibility.
\end{enumerate}
In a CP-symmetric dilute vacuum window, \(\zeta_+=\zeta_-=\zeta\) gives
\[
  \frac{Z_{\rm dig}(\bar\theta)}{Z_0}
  =
  \exp\!\left(2V_4\zeta\cos\bar\theta\right),
  \qquad
  \chi_{\rm top}^{\rm dig}=2\zeta,
  \qquad
  b_2^{\rm dig}=-\frac1{12},
\]
as in
\eqref{eq:dilute-instanton-gas-partition}--\eqref{eq:dilute-instanton-gas-cumulants}.
This handoff is a vacuum partition-function statement.  If one light Dirac
flavor has an unsaturated zero mode, the corresponding mass-saturated vacuum
activity is zero, even though a differentiated fermionic source coefficient
such as \eqref{eq:instanton-hard-assembled-channel} can be nonzero.

The \(U(1)_A\)-odd susceptibility handoff is different.  In an
instanton--anti-instanton ensemble with zero-mode overlap singular values
\(s_\alpha\), the zero-mode-zone contribution is governed by
\[
  \Delta_{\pi-\delta}^{\rm zm}(m)
  =
  4m^2\int_0^\infty
  \frac{\rho_{\rm zm}(s)}{(m^2+s^2)^2}\,\dd s
\]
after the exact-topology density has been removed in the thermodynamic
order of limits; see
\eqref{eq:instanton-zero-mode-zone-u1a-kernel}.  It is controlled by the
near-zero singular-value law, not by the one-body Poisson activity alone.
Likewise the real-time axial relaxation handoff is
\[
  \gamma_5^{\rm top}
  =
  \frac{(2N_f)^2\Gamma_{\rm CS}}{2\chi_5T},
\]
as in \eqref{eq:qcd-massless-topological-axial-relaxation}; it needs the
retarded Chern--Simons diffusion slope, not only the Euclidean theta
curvature.

Consequently a valid instanton calculation must name the handoff functional.
A hard four-source coefficient is not a topological susceptibility; a
Euclidean topological susceptibility is not a real-time sphaleron or axial
relaxation rate; and a dilute instanton curvature is not the
Witten--Veneziano pure-Yang--Mills curvature unless a same-scheme curvature
comparison and the pseudoscalar pole-normalization residuals have also been
supplied.
\end{controlledapproximation}

The physics content is the non-interchangeability.  The BPST saddle fixes the
topological charge, the zero-mode chirality, and the local semiclassical
weights.  The observable decides which part of that data survives: a
differentiated source sees fermion zero-mode slots; a theta curvature sees a
vacuum activity with all zero modes saturated; a \(U(1)_A\) susceptibility sees
the near-zero spectrum of an ensemble; and a real-time rate sees a retarded
low-frequency slope.

\section{Finite-Cell Control Model}
\label{sec:finite-cell-instanton-channel-control}

A continuum proof of an instanton amplitude is analytic.  Nevertheless, the
logic which prevents a moduli-only shortcut is already visible in a finite
cell model.  Let the retained instanton window be decomposed into finitely
many cells \(i\).  Let
\[
  c_i
  =
  w_i\,d_i\,\det B_i\,p_i
\]
denote the leading cell contribution after the positive collective weight
\(w_i\), determinant factor \(d_i\), zero-mode source determinant
\(\det B_i\), and physical projection weight \(p_i\) have all been inserted.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Finite-cell instanton channel control]
\label{ca:finite-cell-instanton-channel-control}
In a finite retained-cell channel, suppose each leading cell contribution
\(c_i\) is replaced by \(c_i(1+\delta_i)\), with
\(|\delta_i|\le\epsilon_i\), and suppose the omitted projection, sector,
infrared, cut, and scheme remainders have absolute majorant \(R_{\rm ext}\).
Then
\begin{equation}
  \left|
  \mathcal A_{\rm phys}
  -\sum_i c_i
  \right|
  \le
  \sum_i |c_i|\,\epsilon_i+R_{\rm ext}.
  \label{eq:finite-cell-instanton-channel-bound}
\end{equation}
If the source matrix on a cell is perturbed by
\(B_i\mapsto B_i+E_i\), with \(B_i\) invertible and
\(\eta_i=\|B_i^{-1}E_i\|\) in an operator norm for which
\(|\operatorname{tr}X|\le2\|X\|\) and \(|\det X|\le\|X\|^2\) on
\(2\times2\) matrices, then
\begin{equation}
  \frac{|\det(B_i+E_i)-\det B_i|}{|\det B_i|}
  \le
  2\eta_i+\eta_i^2 .
  \label{eq:finite-cell-source-determinant-stability}
\end{equation}
The first estimate is the triangle inequality applied after the channel has
already been projected to the same observable coordinate.  It would be false
as a relative statement without a noncancellation margin for
\(\sum_i c_i\).  For the determinant estimate, write
\[
  \det(B_i+E_i)=\det B_i\,
  \det(1+B_i^{-1}E_i).
\]
For a \(2\times2\) matrix \(X\),
\(\det(1+X)-1=\operatorname{tr}X+\det X\).  The stated norm bounds give
\eqref{eq:finite-cell-source-determinant-stability}.
\end{controlledapproximation}

The finite-cell model has two uses.  First, it gives a transparent negative
control: deleting \(\det B_i\), \(p_i\), or \(R_{\rm ext}\) changes the
physical statement, even if the collective-coordinate weights are unchanged.
Second, it records why the continuum estimates in
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-20} carry so many named residuals.  They
are not decorative error terms.  They are the places where a Euclidean saddle
coefficient becomes, or fails to become, a physical amplitude.
