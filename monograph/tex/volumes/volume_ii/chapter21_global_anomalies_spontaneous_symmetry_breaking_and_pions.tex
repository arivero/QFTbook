\section{Background Fields and Anomaly Data}

Let a quantum field theory have an exact internal global symmetry
\(G_{\mathrm f}\).  The symmetry acts on local fields and local operators, and
on Hilbert spaces obtained after choosing a phase.  To test the symmetry as a
property of local correlation functions, introduce a nondynamical
\(G_{\mathrm f}\)-connection \(B\) on spacetime \(M\).  The background-field
generating functional is
\[
  Z[B]
  =
  \int [D\Phi]\,
  \exp\{\ii S[\Phi;B]\},
\]
where \(\Phi\) denotes the dynamical fields and \(B\) is held fixed as an
external datum.
Let \(\mathcal B\) be the space of allowed background fields and
\(\mathcal G_{\mathrm f}\) the group of background gauge transformations.  If
\(g\in\mathcal G_{\mathrm f}\), write \(B^g\) for the transformed connection.

The background anomaly is the local-counterterm equivalence class of phases
\(\mathcal A_g[B]\) in the transformation law
\begin{equation}
  Z[B^g]
  =
  \exp\{\ii\mathcal A_g[B]\}\,Z[B],
  \label{eq:finite-background-anomaly}
\end{equation}
where \(g\in\mathcal G_{\mathrm f}\).  The zero class is the class for which
there exists a local counterterm \(C[B]\) making the transformed functional
invariant under all background gauge transformations.  Multiplying \(Z[B]\) by
\(\exp\{\ii C[B]\}\) changes the representative by
\[
  \mathcal A_g[B]
  \longmapsto
  \mathcal A_g[B]+C[B^g]-C[B].
\]
Thus the anomaly is represented by the equivalence class of
\(\mathcal A_g[B]\) under local-counterterm shifts.

The phases in \eqref{eq:finite-background-anomaly} obey a cocycle identity.
Applying \(h\) and then \(g\) gives the same transformed background as applying
\(gh\), hence
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{gh}[B]
  =
  \mathcal A_g[B^h]+\mathcal A_h[B]
  \quad
  \operatorname{mod} 2\pi .
  \label{eq:background-anomaly-cocycle}
\end{equation}
The infinitesimal part of this cocycle is the local Wess--Zumino consistency
condition developed in the preceding chapter.  A finite anomaly may also have a
purely finite part: its differential on the identity component of
\(\mathcal G_{\mathrm f}\) vanishes, but the cocycle
\eqref{eq:background-anomaly-cocycle} is nontrivial on another component or on
a noncontractible loop in background-field space.

Geometrically, the background-field construction assigns a complex line to each
gauge-equivalence class of \(B\), and \(Z[B]\) is a section of this anomaly
line.  Local counterterms change a trivialization of the line.  When the anomaly is
represented by an invertible field theory on a five-dimensional manifold \(Y\)
with \(\partial Y=M\), equality of boundary variations identifies the phase in
\eqref{eq:finite-background-anomaly}.  The local Chern--Simons version of this
bulk-boundary identity was described in Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-20}; below
we use mapping tori to state the finite version needed for global anomalies.

\section{Anomaly Matching}

Let a microscopic theory with exact global symmetry \(G_{\mathrm f}\) flow to
an infrared description.  Couple both descriptions to the same background
\(B\).  If the ultraviolet generating functional transforms as in
\eqref{eq:finite-background-anomaly}, then the infrared functional must obey
\[
  Z_{\mathrm{IR}}[B^g]
  =
  \exp\{\ii\mathcal A_g[B]\}\,Z_{\mathrm{IR}}[B]
\]
after possible local counterterms have been matched.  The equality of the
equivalence class of \(\mathcal A_g[B]\) modulo counterterms is 't Hooft
anomaly matching.

The matching object is the cocycle class
\([\mathcal A]\) defined by \eqref{eq:background-anomaly-cocycle}.  Different
infrared descriptions may carry the same class: massless fermions can produce
it through their determinant or Pfaffian line, Goldstone fields can produce it
through a Wess--Zumino--Witten functional, and a topological sector can produce
it through an invertible response.  The assumptions are that the symmetry
remains exact along the flow, the background-field coupling is kept fixed, and
any modes removed between two scales contribute only local background
counterterms to the long-distance functional.  Under these assumptions the
cocycle class is unchanged by RG flow.

\section{A Perturbative Example of a Global Anomaly}

The local triangle computation already distinguishes two uses of a current.
Consider a four-dimensional massless Dirac fermion with vector and axial
currents
\[
  j^\mu=\ii\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi,
  \qquad
  j_A^\mu=\ii\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\gamma_5\psi .
\]
The preceding chapter fixed the regularization convention in which the vector
current coupled to an ordinary \(U(1)\) gauge field is preserved and the axial
current carries the \(j_Ajj\) anomaly.  To test the axial \(U(1)\) itself as a
background symmetry, couple a background gauge field \(A_\mu\) to \(j_A^\mu\),
\[
  \Delta S=\int\dd^4x\,A_\mu j_A^\mu .
\]
Gauge invariance of this background coupling would require the Ward identity
for the vector-current divergence in the three-point function with two axial
insertions:
\[
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j^\mu(x)j_A^\nu(y)j_A^\rho(z)
  \ket{\vac} .
\]
The same oriented triangle graphs as in the local axial anomaly appear, with
two \(\gamma_5\) insertions.  In the \(D=4-\epsilon\) regulator, split
\(\ell=\ell_\parallel+\ell_\perp\) and keep \(\gamma_5\) four-dimensional.
The trace term that remains after imposing the two axial-vector external Ward
identities is proportional to
\[
  \ell_\perp^2(-4\ii)\epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}
  (k-2\ell)_\alpha p_\beta .
\]
The loop integral is symmetric under permutations of the three external
momenta \(k,p,-k-p\).  Its finite local part is linear in the external
momenta, hence
\[
  \epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}(k-2\ell)_\alpha p_\beta
  \longmapsto
  \frac13\epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}k_\alpha p_\beta
\]
inside the regulated contact term.  With the conventions of the preceding
chapter this gives
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu
  \bra{\vac}
  \operatorname T\,j^\mu(x)j_A^\nu(y)j_A^\rho(z)
  \ket{\vac}
  =
  -\frac1{6\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\nu\rho\alpha\beta}
  \partial_\alpha\delta^{(4)}(x-z)
  \partial_\beta\delta^{(4)}(y-z).
  \label{eq:vector-current-two-axial-contact-term}
\end{equation}
Expanding the background-field effective action
\[
  \ee^{\ii\Gamma[A]}
  =
  \int D\psi\,
  \exp\left\{\ii S_0[\psi]
    +\ii\int\dd^4x\,A_\mu j_A^\mu\right\}
\]
to second order in \(A\), equation
\eqref{eq:vector-current-two-axial-contact-term} implies
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma[A+\dd\zeta]-\Gamma[A]
  =
  \int\dd^4x\,\zeta(x)\,
  \frac1{48\pi^2}
  \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}.
  \label{eq:axial-background-gauge-variation}
\end{equation}
Thus the axial \(U(1)\) is an exact global symmetry only if one does not
require a gauge-invariant functional of an axial background field.  Its
background response is the anomaly class.  Promoting the same background to a
dynamical gauge field would demand invariance under \(A\mapsto A+\dd\zeta\)
and is obstructed by \eqref{eq:axial-background-gauge-variation}.

\section{Example: A Finite SU(2) Anomaly}

Let \((M,\sigma_M)\) be a compact spin four-manifold and let
\(G_{\mathrm f}=SU(2)\).  The spin structure \(\sigma_M\) is part of the
background datum: a Weyl fermion is not defined on a merely oriented
four-manifold.  A change of \(\sigma_M\), when several spin structures exist,
is a change of the fermionic QFT background and may change the Pfaffian line
and its anomaly.  Consider one left-handed Weyl fermion valued in the
fundamental representation of \(SU(2)\).  For an \(SU(2)\) background
connection \(B\), the Euclidean Weyl operator is
\[
  D_{B,\sigma_M}^+:
  \Gamma(S_+\otimes E_B)
  \longrightarrow
  \Gamma(S_-\otimes E_B),
\]
where \(S_\pm\) are the positive and negative chirality spin bundles and
\(E_B\) is the associated rank-two complex vector bundle.  The fundamental
representation of \(SU(2)\) is pseudoreal, so the fermion functional integral
defines a Pfaffian line over the space of structured backgrounds
\((M,\sigma_M,B)\).

Let \(g:M\to SU(2)\) be a background gauge transformation, and choose a smooth
path of background connections \(B_t\), \(t\in[0,1]\), from \(B_0=B\) to
\(B_1=B^g\).  Identifying the two ends of \(M\times[0,1]\) by \(g\) gives the
mapping torus
\[
  X_g
  =
  (M\times[0,1])/\bigl((x,1)\sim(x,0)\hbox{ by }g\bigr)
\]
with an induced \(SU(2)\) bundle \(E_g\).  The spin structure on
\(M\times[0,1]\) is the product one from \(\sigma_M\); gluing the two ends
by the identity diffeomorphism of \(M\) and the internal gauge transformation
\(g\) gives a spin structure \(\sigma_g\) on \(X_g\).  Thus the invariant
below is a function of \((X_g,\sigma_g,E_g)\), not just of the oriented
manifold and the vector bundle.  If the spin structure on \(M\) is changed, or
if the mapping-torus spin structure is twisted by an allowed
\(\mathbb Z_2\) flat line, the mod-two index may change.

Let \(\mathcal D_{X_g,\sigma_g,E_g}\) be the five-dimensional real Dirac
operator coupled to this bundle, and define its mod-two index by
\[
  \zeta(X_g,\sigma_g,E_g)
  =
  \dim\ker\mathcal D_{X_g,\sigma_g,E_g}
  \quad \operatorname{mod}2 .
\]
The analytic framework behind this statement is the Dai--Freed formulation of
fermion anomalies.  For a family of chiral Dirac operators, the determinant or
Pfaffian is a section of a line over background-field space, and the holonomy
of this line around a closed loop of backgrounds is computed by the
Atiyah--Patodi--Singer eta-invariant of the Dirac operator on the associated
mapping torus.  In the present pseudoreal \(SU(2)\) fundamental case the real
structure refines the Dai--Freed holonomy to the mod-two-index sign
\[
  \operatorname{Hol}_{\mathrm{DF}}(X_g,\sigma_g,E_g)
  =
  (-1)^{\zeta(X_g,\sigma_g,E_g)} .
  \label{eq:su2-dai-freed-holonomy}
\]
Equivalently, cutting the mapping torus open gives the APS-boundary
description of the same Pfaffian transport.  Therefore the Pfaffian transforms
by
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\operatorname{Pf}(D_{B^g,\sigma_M}^+)}
       {\operatorname{Pf}(D_{B,\sigma_M}^+)}
  =
  (-1)^{\zeta(X_g,\sigma_g,E_g)} .
  \label{eq:su2-global-anomaly-pfaffian}
\end{equation}
For \(M=S^4\) and \(g\) representing the nontrivial class in
\(\pi_4(SU(2))\simeq\mathbb Z_2\), the mod-two index is \(1\) for one
fundamental Weyl doublet.  Thus an odd number of such doublets has a finite
\(SU(2)\) anomaly.  The local cubic anomaly vanishes because \(SU(2)\) has no
independent symmetric cubic invariant; equation
\eqref{eq:su2-global-anomaly-pfaffian} detects an obstruction invisible to the
infinitesimal descent calculation.

If this \(SU(2)\) symmetry is gauged, the sign in
\eqref{eq:su2-global-anomaly-pfaffian} prevents the fermion measure from
descending to a function on gauge-equivalence classes of backgrounds.  If the
same \(SU(2)\) is kept as a global symmetry, the sign is an anomaly class that
must be reproduced by the infrared theory.

\section{Anomaly Inflow and Mapping Tori}

The finite anomaly in \eqref{eq:finite-background-anomaly} is represented by a
five-dimensional invertible response.  Its holonomy on a mapping torus gives
the finite transformation phase.  In the form needed here, an invertible
anomaly theory assigns a phase
\[
  \mathcal I_5[Y,\sigma_Y,B_Y]\in U(1)
\]
to every closed five-manifold \(Y\) equipped with a chosen spin structure
\(\sigma_Y\), global-symmetry background \(B_Y\), and any additional
geometric structures required by the fermions.  If
\((M,\sigma_M)=\partial(Y,\sigma_Y)\), the same theory assigns an anomaly line
over the space of boundary backgrounds, and the four-dimensional partition
function \(Z[M,\sigma_M,B]\) is a vector in that line.  Choosing a filling
\((Y,\sigma_Y,B_Y)\) trivializes the line by multiplying by the bulk phase.
Changing the filling changes the trivialization by the phase of the closed
structured five-manifold obtained by gluing the two fillings.

For a finite background gauge transformation \(g\), choose a path \(B_t\) from
\(B\) to \(B^g\) and identify the two ends by \(g\).  The resulting mapping
torus \(X_g\) carries the induced background \(B_g\).  The inflow statement is
the equality
\begin{equation}
  \frac{Z[M,\sigma_M,B^g]}{Z[M,\sigma_M,B]}
  =
  \mathcal I_5[X_g,\sigma_g,B_g],
  \label{eq:mapping-torus-inflow-phase}
\end{equation}
after a choice of local counterterm convention.  On the identity component of
the background gauge group, cutting the mapping torus open reduces
\eqref{eq:mapping-torus-inflow-phase} to the descent formula of the preceding
chapter.  Infinitesimally, the five-dimensional Chern--Simons response varies
by the boundary term \(-\int_M I_4^{(1)}\), while the boundary generating
functional varies by \(+\int_M I_4^{(1)}\).  For a disconnected component, the
mapping-torus phase can remain nontrivial even when the local polynomial
anomaly vanishes.

The \(SU(2)\) example above is exactly of this finite type.  Its anomaly
theory assigns
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal I^{SU(2)}_5[X_g,\sigma_g,E_g]
  =
  (-1)^{\zeta(X_g,\sigma_g,E_g)}
  \label{eq:su2-invertible-anomaly-theory}
\end{equation}
to the mapping torus with the induced rank-two bundle.  Equation
\eqref{eq:su2-global-anomaly-pfaffian} is therefore the mapping-torus inflow
law \eqref{eq:mapping-torus-inflow-phase}.  An odd number of fundamental Weyl
doublets has nontrivial \(\mathcal I^{SU(2)}_5\); an even number has the
trivial product phase for this test, and the Pfaffian line has no sign
holonomy around the generator detected by \(X_g\).

This language is also the precise version of the symmetry-protected
topological interpretation of anomalies.  A gapped short-range-entangled
five-dimensional phase with symmetry \(G_{\mathrm f}\) and no intrinsic
topological order has an invertible response \(\mathcal I_5\).  A
four-dimensional theory with anomaly \(\mathcal I_5\) can occur as a boundary
whose anomalous transformation is cancelled by the inverse bulk response.  In
mathematical treatments, deformation classes of fully extended
reflection-positive invertible responses with a specified tangential and
\(G_{\mathrm f}\)-background structure are often organized by characters of
the corresponding bordism groups, together with possible free Chern--Simons or
theta-type terms.  This cobordism organization is a classification framework
for invertible responses; each use in QFT still requires specifying the
background fields, spin or pin structure, locality assumptions, and the
normalization of the fermion determinant or Pfaffian line.

\section{Low-Degree Homotopy Groups in Four-Dimensional Applications}

The preceding example used \(\pi_4(SU(2))\) because a gauge transformation on
the compactified four-space \(S^4\) is a map \(S^4\to G\).  Other nearby
homotopy groups enter different constructions.  If a principal \(G\)-bundle on
\(S^4\) is described by transition data on the equator, the transition function
is a map \(S^3\to G\), so the instanton number is controlled by \(\pi_3(G)\)
and by the normalization of the corresponding characteristic class.  If a
four-dimensional nonlinear sigma model has target \(G\) and its Wess--Zumino
term is written by extending a field \(U:M^4\to G\) to a five-manifold, the
quantization is governed by the integral cohomology class of the closed
five-form used in the Wess--Zumino functional.  For the standard simple-group
case \(G=SU(N)\), \(N\ge3\), this quantization is tested on maps
\(S^5\to G\), hence by \(\pi_5(G)\simeq\mathbb Z\).  Thus
\(\pi_3,\pi_4,\pi_5\) play different roles and should not be conflated.

The following table records the low-degree groups most often met for compact
connected classical gauge groups.  The entries for \(SO(N)\) with \(N\ge7\),
\(U(N)\) with \(N\ge3\), \(SU(N)\) with \(N\ge3\), and \(Sp(N)\) with \(N\ge2\)
are the stable values in the displayed range, as given by Bott periodicity.
For \(d\ge2\), \(SO(N)\) and \(Spin(N)\) have the same \(\pi_d\); their
\(\pi_1\) differs because \(Spin(N)\to SO(N)\) is the universal double cover.
\[
\begin{array}{c|ccccc}
G & \pi_1(G) & \pi_2(G) & \pi_3(G) & \pi_4(G) & \pi_5(G) \\
\hline
U(1) & \mathbb Z & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
U(N),\ N\ge3 & \mathbb Z & 0 & \mathbb Z & 0 & \mathbb Z \\
SU(2)\simeq Sp(1) & 0 & 0 & \mathbb Z & \mathbb Z_2 & \mathbb Z_2 \\
SU(N),\ N\ge3 & 0 & 0 & \mathbb Z & 0 & \mathbb Z \\
SO(N),\ N\ge7 & \mathbb Z_2 & 0 & \mathbb Z & 0 & 0 \\
Spin(N),\ N\ge7 & 0 & 0 & \mathbb Z & 0 & 0 \\
Sp(N),\ N\ge2 & 0 & 0 & \mathbb Z & \mathbb Z_2 & \mathbb Z_2
\end{array}
\]
The low-rank orthogonal groups are exceptional because of accidental
isomorphisms:
\[
\begin{array}{c|ccc}
G & \pi_3(G) & \pi_4(G) & \pi_5(G) \\
\hline
Spin(3)\simeq SU(2) & \mathbb Z & \mathbb Z_2 & \mathbb Z_2 \\
Spin(4)\simeq SU(2)\times SU(2)
  & \mathbb Z^2 & \mathbb Z_2^2 & \mathbb Z_2^2 \\
Spin(5)\simeq Sp(2) & \mathbb Z & \mathbb Z_2 & \mathbb Z_2 \\
Spin(6)\simeq SU(4) & \mathbb Z & 0 & \mathbb Z .
\end{array}
\]
For simply connected exceptional compact groups
\[
  G=G_2,F_4,E_6,E_7,E_8
\]
one has
\[
  \pi_1(G)=\pi_2(G)=0,\qquad
  \pi_3(G)\simeq\mathbb Z,\qquad
  \pi_4(G)=\pi_5(G)=0 .
\]
There are further low-degree torsion groups in special cases, for example
\(\pi_6(SU(2))\simeq\mathbb Z_{12}\),
\(\pi_6(SU(3))\simeq\mathbb Z_6\), and
\(\pi_6(G_2)\simeq\mathbb Z_3\).  These become relevant in higher-dimensional
global-anomaly questions.

This table is a guide to the first topological obstructions, not a replacement
for the anomaly itself.  A modern finite anomaly is a phase of the fermion
determinant or Pfaffian line, and in its most invariant formulation it is an
invertible field theory on one higher dimension.  Homotopy groups detect
important test families of backgrounds; the complete torsion classification can
depend on spin structure, global form of the group, matter representation, and
bordism data.

\section{Vacuum Sectors and Locality}

Spontaneous symmetry breaking is a statement about phases of an infinite-volume
quantum system.  A useful comparison is a one-dimensional quantum mechanical
Hamiltonian
\[
  H=\frac{P^2}{2}+V(x),
  \qquad
  V(x)=V(-x),
\]
where \(V\) has two degenerate minima at \(x=\pm a\).

  \label{fig:double-well-finite-volume}
\end{figure}
The localized semiclassical states \(\ket{+a}\) and \(\ket{-a}\) combine into
Hamiltonian eigenstates
\[
  \ket{\psi_\pm}
  =
  \frac1{\sqrt2}\bigl(\ket{+a}\pm\ket{-a}\bigr).
\]
The splitting between them is exponentially small in the Euclidean tunneling
action,
\[
  \Delta E\sim \ee^{-S_0},
  \qquad
  -\ddot x+V'(x)=0,
  \qquad
  x(\pm\infty)=\pm a .
\]
For a local quantum field theory with the analogous set of homogeneous
minima, the tunneling action is proportional to the spatial volume.  In the
infinite-volume limit the transition amplitude between distinct homogeneous
phases is suppressed as
\[
  \exp(-S_0\,\operatorname{Vol})
  \longrightarrow 0 .
\]
The pure vacua therefore define distinct phases rather than two nearly
degenerate levels of one finite-dimensional Hamiltonian.

Locality gives the corresponding superselection statement.  Let
\(\{\ket{\Omega_i}\}\) be translation-invariant pure vacua in infinite volume.
For any local operator \(\mathcal A(x)\), the vacuum matrix
\[
  A_{ij}:=\bra{\Omega_i}\mathcal A(0)\ket{\Omega_j}
\]
can be chosen diagonal in the phase label.  One way to see the constraint is
to compare two local operators \(\mathcal A(x)\) and \(\mathcal B(y)\) at
large spacelike separation.  Insert the vacuum sector decomposition and use
cluster decomposition in a pure phase:
\[
  \bra{\Omega_i}\mathcal A(x)\mathcal B(y)\ket{\Omega_j}
  =
  \sum_k A_{ik}B_{kj}+\hbox{terms vanishing as }|x-y|\to\infty .
\]
For spacelike separation, locality gives
\([\mathcal A(x),\mathcal B(y)]=0\), hence the vacuum matrices satisfy
\[
  \sum_k A_{ik}B_{kj}=\sum_k B_{ik}A_{kj}
\]
for all local \(\mathcal A,\mathcal B\).  The commuting family of vacuum
matrices may be diagonalized simultaneously.  In that basis, local operators
do not connect distinct pure phases:
\[
  \bra{\Omega_i}\mathcal A(x)\ket{\Omega_j}=0
  \qquad (i\ne j).
\]

\section{Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking}

Consider a Lorentz-invariant quantum theory in infinite spatial volume with an
exact continuous internal symmetry group \(G\).  Choose one pure phase and let
\(\ket{\vac}\) be its vacuum vector.  The unbroken subgroup is
\[
  H
  =
  \{h\in G:U(h)\ket{\vac}=\ket{\vac}\}.
\]
The symmetry is spontaneously broken from \(G\) to \(H\) when \(H\) is a proper
subgroup.  Let \(\mathfrak g=\operatorname{Lie}(G)\) and
\(\mathfrak h=\operatorname{Lie}(H)\).  Choose an \(H\)-stable complement
\(\mathfrak m\) so that
\[
  \mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\oplus\mathfrak m,
  \qquad
  [\mathfrak h,\mathfrak m]\subset\mathfrak m .
\]
Elements \(T_i\in\mathfrak h\) generate unbroken transformations and elements
\(X_a\in\mathfrak m\) generate broken directions.

Goldstone fields are local coordinates on the coset \(G/H\).  A coset
representative may be written
\begin{equation}
  \xi(\pi(x))
  =
  \exp\left\{\frac{\ii}{f}\pi^a(x)X_a\right\}.
  \label{eq:goldstone-coset-coordinate}
\end{equation}
For \(g\in G\), the defining nonlinear action is
\begin{equation}
  g\,\xi(\pi)
  =
  \xi(\pi')\,h(g,\pi),
  \qquad
  h(g,\pi)\in H .
  \label{eq:ccwz-nonlinear-action}
\end{equation}
The compensator \(h(g,\pi)\) is determined by the chosen coset section and by
the requirement that \(g\xi(\pi)\) be written again in the form
\(\xi(\pi')H\).

The Maurer--Cartan form decomposes as
\begin{equation}
  \xi^{-1}\dd\xi
  =
  \ii e^a X_a+\ii\omega^i T_i .
  \label{eq:ccwz-maurer-cartan}
\end{equation}
The one-forms \(e^a=e^a_{\mu}\dd x^\mu\) are the covariant vielbein on the
Goldstone target \(G/H\), while \(\omega^i\) is an \(H\)-connection.  If
\(G/H\) carries an invariant metric \(G_{ab}\) on the broken tangent space, the
leading Goldstone action is
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{Goldstone}}
  =
  \frac{f^2}{2}
  \int G_{ab}\,
  e^a_\mu e^{b\mu}\,\dd^4x
  +\cdots .
  \label{eq:goldstone-leading-action}
\end{equation}
The ellipsis denotes higher-derivative terms and explicit symmetry-breaking
terms.  No potential appears for exact Goldstone coordinates because constant
maps into \(G/H\) label symmetry-related vacua.

\section{Goldstone Theorem as a Spectral Statement}

The spectral input behind \eqref{eq:goldstone-leading-action} is as follows.
Assume a conserved current \(j_A^\mu\), a local operator \(\mathcal O\), and a
nonzero order parameter
\begin{equation}
  \langle\vac,[Q_A,\mathcal O(0)]\vac\rangle
  =
  \langle\vac,\delta_A\mathcal O(0)\vac\rangle
  \ne0 ,
  \label{eq:goldstone-order-parameter}
\end{equation}
where \(Q_A\) is obtained as the infinite-volume limit of smeared charges.
The Ward identity for the time-ordered distribution is
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu
  \langle\vac,T\{j_A^\mu(x)\mathcal O(0)\}\vac\rangle
  =
  -\ii\delta^{(4)}(x)
  \langle\vac,\delta_A\mathcal O(0)\vac\rangle .
  \label{eq:goldstone-ward-distribution}
\end{equation}
Fourier transforming gives a distribution \(\widehat G_A^\mu(p)\) satisfying
\[
  p_\mu\widehat G_A^\mu(p)
  =
  \langle\vac,\delta_A\mathcal O(0)\vac\rangle .
\]
Lorentz covariance implies that the singular part of
\(\widehat G_A^\mu(p)\) is of the form \(p^\mu F_A(p^2)\).  Since the
right-hand side is nonzero at \(p=0\), \(F_A(p^2)\) must contain a
\(1/p^2\) singularity.  In the K\"all\'en--Lehmann language this singularity is a
massless contribution to the current-operator spectral measure.

Equivalently, for each broken direction there are zero-mass one-particle
states \(\ket{\pi_a(p)}\) created by the current:
\begin{equation}
  \bra{\vac}j_A^\mu(0)\ket{\pi_a(p)}
  =
  \ii F_{Aa}p^\mu,
  \qquad p^2=0 .
  \label{eq:goldstone-current-matrix-element}
\end{equation}
If the translation generators have spectrum contained in the closed forward
light cone and the vacuum is the unique translation-invariant vector in the
chosen phase, the matrix \(F_{Aa}\) has rank equal to the number of broken
generators.  These massless states are the quanta described by the Goldstone
coordinates \(\pi^a(x)\).

\begin{remark}[Goldstone theorem and gauged directions]
\label{rem:goldstone-gauge-brst-higgs}
The spectral argument above is a theorem about a global symmetry current acting
on the physical positive Hilbert space.  It assumes that \(Q_A\) is a charge
defined on physical states and that \(\delta_A\mathcal O\) is the variation of
a gauge-invariant local observable or of a local field in an ordinary
positive-metric representation.

Gauge redundancy is different.  In a covariant gauge-fixed description the
state space before the physical quotient is a Krein space, as in
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-18}, and physical states are BRST cohomology
classes.  A scalar coordinate that moves along a gauged orbit is then a
would-be Goldstone coordinate, not by itself a physical massless particle.  At
quadratic order in the Abelian Higgs model, write
\[
  \phi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\bigl(v+h(x)+\ii\pi(x)\bigr),
  \qquad
  m_A=gv .
\]
The scalar kinetic term contains
\begin{equation}
  |D_\mu\phi|^2
  =
  \frac12(\partial_\mu h)^2
  +
  \frac12(\partial_\mu\pi-m_A A_\mu)^2
  +\text{higher powers of }h,\pi,A .
  \label{eq:abelian-higgs-quadratic-mixing}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, away from zeros of \(v+h\), the gauge-invariant vector
\[
  \mathcal A_\mu
  =
  A_\mu-\frac{1}{m_A}\partial_\mu\pi
\]
has the Proca mass term \(\frac12m_A^2\mathcal A_\mu\mathcal A^\mu\) at
quadratic order.  In an \(R_\xi\) gauge one instead uses the gauge-fixing
functional
\[
  F_\xi=\partial^\mu A_\mu-\xi m_A\pi ,
\]
which cancels the \(A_\mu\partial^\mu\pi\) mixing and assigns the unphysical
fields \(\pi\) and the Faddeev--Popov ghosts the gauge-dependent mass
\(\sqrt{\xi}\,m_A\).  The longitudinal gauge polarization, the would-be
Goldstone coordinate, and the ghost-antighost variables form BRST doublet or
quartet sectors, so they have no independent positive-norm particle states in
the BRST cohomology.  The physical spectrum contains a massive vector particle
with three polarizations and the gauge-invariant scalar excitation \(h\), plus
any Goldstone particles associated with broken global symmetries that remain
after the gauge redundancy has been quotiented.
\end{remark}

\]

  =

  -V(r)
  -r^2\partial_\mu\theta\,\partial^\mu\theta,
  \qquad

  -\frac12V''(v)\rho^2
  -v^2\partial_\mu\theta\,\partial^\mu\theta .

\section{Chiral Symmetry of Massless QCD}

Let \(q_L^I=P_Lq^I\) and \(q_R^I=P_Rq^I\), \(I=1,\ldots,N_f\), be massless
quark fields in the fundamental representation of the color gauge group.  The
connected continuous flavor symmetry acting faithfully on local gauge-invariant
operators has Lie algebra
\[
  \mathfrak{su}(N_f)_L\oplus
  \mathfrak{su}(N_f)_R\oplus
  \mathfrak u(1)_B .
\]
For local current algebra computations this Lie algebra is represented by
\[
  SU(N_f)_L\times SU(N_f)_R\times U(1)_B .
\]
The precise global form includes finite quotients by common center actions;
those quotients matter for global background fields and are developed later.
The anomaly derived in the preceding chapter removes the axial \(U(1)_A\)
rotation from the exact continuous quantum symmetry group.

The left and right transformations act by
\[
  q_L\longmapsto Lq_L,
  \qquad
  q_R\longmapsto Rq_R,
  \qquad
  (L,R)\in SU(N_f)_L\times SU(N_f)_R .
\]

\begin{conjecture}[Chiral symmetry breaking in QCD]
\label{conj:qcd-chiral-symmetry-breaking}
For four-dimensional \(SU(N_c)\) QCD with \(N_f\) light fundamental quarks in
the range where the infrared theory is confining rather than conformal, the
infinite-volume vacuum selected by equal positive quark masses has a nonzero
chiral order parameter in the subsequent massless limit:
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{m\downarrow0}\lim_{V\to\infty}
  \langle \bar q_{R,J}q_{L,I}\rangle_{m,V}
  =
  \Sigma_0\,\delta_{IJ},
  \qquad
  \Sigma_0\neq0 .
  \label{eq:qcd-chiral-condensate-conjecture}
\end{equation}
The unbroken continuous nonabelian flavor symmetry in this phase is the
diagonal vector subgroup \(SU(N_f)_V\).
\end{conjecture}

Conjecture~\ref{conj:qcd-chiral-symmetry-breaking} is a dynamical assertion
about the QCD vacuum.  It is not presently a theorem derived from the
four-dimensional continuum QCD Lagrangian or from a completed continuum limit
of lattice QCD.  Lattice simulations and phenomenology give strong evidence
for the small-\(N_f\) theory, while semiclassical instanton calculations do
not supply a controlled derivation of the infrared condensate.  The pion
effective theory below is the low-energy theory conditional on this chiral
breaking phase.

In the phase described by
Conjecture~\ref{conj:qcd-chiral-symmetry-breaking}, the bilinear condensate has
an orientation that can be chosen as
\[
  \Sigma_{IJ}
  =
  \langle \bar q_{R,J}q_{L,I}\rangle
  =
  \Sigma_0\,\delta_{IJ},
\]
so that the stabilizer is the diagonal subgroup.  More explicitly, before
choosing the orientation of the vacuum, write
\[
  A_{IJ}:=\langle\bar q_{R,J}q_{L,I}\rangle .
\]
Under \(SU(N_f)_L\times SU(N_f)_R\),
\[
  A\longmapsto R^\dagger A L .
\]
A choice of \(L\) and \(R\) can diagonalize \(A\).  If the vacuum is invariant
under the non-chiral subgroup, then \(A\) is proportional to the identity.
The unbroken transformations are precisely \(L=R\).  The breaking pattern is
therefore
\begin{equation}
  SU(N_f)_L\times SU(N_f)_R
  \longrightarrow
  SU(N_f)_V .
  \label{eq:qcd-chiral-breaking-pattern}
\end{equation}
Given this dynamical input, the number of Goldstone bosons is \(N_f^2-1\).

At \(\theta=0\), the non-chiral flavor symmetry is constrained by the
positivity structure of the Euclidean vectorlike theory.  The statement has a
precise theorem boundary.

\begin{hypothesis}[Euclidean measure needed for Vafa--Witten positivity]
\label{hyp:vafa-witten-measure-data}
For the vectorlike theory with equal positive quark masses \(m>0\), assume that
there is a family of gauge-invariant Euclidean finite-regulator measures
\(\dd\nu_{m,\Lambda}(A)\).  If a continuum statement is claimed, assume in
addition that the gauge-invariant Schwinger functions and the source
functionals for the order parameters used below have a continuum limit, denoted
\(\dd\nu_m(A)\) in this paragraph, with the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*.]
\item expectations of gauge-invariant polynomially bounded fermion bilinears and
their source deformations are defined at the regulator level and converge in
the limit in which the Vafa--Witten conclusion is asserted;
\item the full regulator measure, including the gauge action and the vectorlike
fermion discretization or determinant, is reflection positive for a Euclidean
time reflection, and this positivity is preserved by the limiting Schwinger
functions.  Thus OS reconstruction applies to the observables used in the
argument;
\item the Dirac operator and fermion regulator have vectorlike determinant
positivity in each fixed gauge-field background.  In the continuum notation
this is the anti-Hermiticity and eigenvalue pairing statement
\[
  \det(\not D_A+m)
  =
  \prod_{\lambda>0}(\lambda^2+m^2)\,m^{n_0(A)}
  \geq0;
\]
on a lattice it is replaced by the corresponding positivity property of the
chosen vectorlike fermion action;
\item the infinite-volume and small-mass limits used to select a phase exist
for the order parameters under consideration.
\end{enumerate}
\end{hypothesis}

At finite lattice spacing, these hypotheses are properties to verify for the
chosen lattice gauge and fermion action.  The Wilson plaquette gauge action is
reflection positive in the sense described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:lattice-yang-mills-nonperturbative-definition}, and standard
vectorlike fermion discretizations must be checked with their own
reflection-positivity and determinant-positivity conditions.  These properties
are not automatic for an arbitrary regulator or gauge-fixed expression.  For
the four-dimensional continuum gauge theory, the existence of \(\dd\nu_m(A)\)
with the required locality, reflection positivity, and scaling limits is part
of the Yang--Mills/QCD construction problem; the finite-lattice positivity
argument is therefore not, by itself, a construction of continuum QCD.

Under Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:vafa-witten-measure-data}, the determinant factor in
each fixed gauge-field background is nonnegative, and for equal masses the
fermion determinant and gauge measure are invariant under \(SU(N_f)_V\).  A
vector-flavor-breaking external source may then be averaged over the compact
vector flavor group without decreasing the Euclidean functional integral.  In
the infinite-volume limit this convexity/positivity comparison rules out a
lower vacuum energy for a vector-flavor-breaking orientation.  Taking the
small-mass limit therefore selects, when the limit exists, a vacuum preserving
\(SU(N_f)_V\).  This is the Vafa--Witten positivity argument in the form
needed here.  It does not by itself prove the existence of the chiral
condensate; it constrains the orientation of the condensate once the
vectorlike massive regulator and its reflection-positive measure have been
constructed.

The complex phase of \(\Sigma_0\) is tied to the topological angle.  A
flavor-singlet axial rotation would rotate \(\Sigma_0\), but the same
transformation is anomalous and shifts \(\theta\).  Thus the invariant data
are the condensate orientation together with the anomaly-invariant
combination of \(\theta\) and the quark-mass phase discussed in the preceding
chapter.

Choose Hermitian flavor generators \(T^a\) with
\(\operatorname{Tr}(T^aT^b)=\frac12\delta^{ab}\).  The pion field is the
\(SU(N_f)\)-valued matrix
\begin{equation}
  U(x)
  =
  \exp\left\{\frac{2\ii}{f_\pi}\pi^a(x)T^a\right\},
  \qquad
  U(x)\in SU(N_f),
  \label{eq:pion-matrix-field}
\end{equation}
transforming as
\begin{equation}
  U(x)
  \longmapsto
  L\,U(x)\,R^{-1}.
  \label{eq:pion-chiral-transformation}
\end{equation}
The axial-current normalization is
\[
  \bra{\vac}A^{a\mu}(0)\ket{\pi^b(p)}
  =
  \ii f_\pi p^\mu\delta^{ab}.
\]
With this convention, the leading two-derivative chiral Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_2
  =
  \frac{f_\pi^2}{4}
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(D_\mu U^\dagger D^\mu U\bigr),
  \label{eq:leading-chiral-lagrangian}
\end{equation}
where the covariant derivative in external left and right flavor backgrounds
\(\ell_\mu,r_\mu\) is
\begin{equation}
  D_\mu U
  =
  \partial_\mu U-\ii\ell_\mu U+\ii U r_\mu .
  \label{eq:pion-external-covariant-derivative}
\end{equation}
Setting \(\ell_\mu=r_\mu=0\) gives the ordinary pion kinetic term and its
derivative self-interactions.

\section{Low-Energy Pion Scattering}

The two-derivative chiral Lagrangian is not merely a parametrization of
massless fields.  It determines the leading low-energy \(S\)-matrix of the
Goldstone bosons, because every interaction is derivative and therefore
ordered by powers of external momentum.  For the \(N_f=2\) theory it is useful
to write \(SU(2)\simeq S^3\) in stereographic coordinates
\(\vec\xi=(\xi_1,\xi_2,\xi_3)\):
\begin{equation}
  U(\xi)
  =
  \frac{1-\vec\xi^{\,2}+2\ii\,\vec\xi\cdot\vec\sigma}
       {1+\vec\xi^{\,2}} .
  \label{eq:su2-stereographic-pion-field}
\end{equation}
Let \(F_{\rm st}\) denote the normalization used in these stereographic
coordinates:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_2
  =
  -\frac{F_{\rm st}^2}{16}
  \operatorname{Tr}(\partial_\mu U\,\partial^\mu U^\dagger)
  =
  -\frac{F_{\rm st}^2}{2}
  \frac{\partial_\mu\vec\xi\cdot\partial^\mu\vec\xi}
       {(1+\vec\xi^{\,2})^2}.
  \label{eq:su2-stereographic-lagrangian}
\end{equation}
With canonical fields
\[
  \vec\pi=F_{\rm st}\vec\xi,
\]
the expansion begins as
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_2
  =
  -\frac12\partial_\mu\vec\pi\cdot\partial^\mu\vec\pi
  +
  \frac1{F_{\rm st}^2}
  \vec\pi^{\,2}\,
  \partial_\mu\vec\pi\cdot\partial^\mu\vec\pi
  +O(\pi^6/F_{\rm st}^4).
  \label{eq:su2-stereographic-expansion}
\end{equation}
The infinitesimal \(SU(2)_L\times SU(2)_R\) action in this chart is useful for
organizing local terms.  Write
\[
  L=\exp\!\left(\frac{\ii}{2}\vec\theta_L\cdot\vec\sigma\right),
  \qquad
  R=\exp\!\left(\frac{\ii}{2}\vec\theta_R\cdot\vec\sigma\right),
  \qquad
  \vec\theta_V=\frac{\vec\theta_L+\vec\theta_R}{2},
  \qquad
  \vec\theta_A=\frac{\vec\theta_L-\vec\theta_R}{2}.
\]
For \(U\mapsto LUR^{-1}\), a direct computation from
\eqref{eq:su2-stereographic-pion-field} gives
\begin{equation}
  \delta\vec\xi
  =
  -\vec\theta_V\times\vec\xi
  +\frac12(1-\vec\xi^{\,2})\vec\theta_A
  +\vec\xi(\vec\xi\cdot\vec\theta_A).
  \label{eq:su2-stereographic-chiral-transformation}
\end{equation}
Thus the diagonal subgroup acts linearly on \(\vec\xi\), while the axial
directions act by a nonlinear shift plus higher powers of \(\vec\xi\).
Define
\begin{equation}
  \vec D_\mu
  =
  \frac{\partial_\mu\vec\xi}{1+\vec\xi^{\,2}} .
  \label{eq:su2-stereographic-d-field}
\end{equation}
For constant transformation parameters,
\begin{equation}
  \delta\vec D_\mu
  =
  -\vec\theta_V\times\vec D_\mu
  -(\vec\xi\times\vec\theta_A)\times\vec D_\mu .
  \label{eq:su2-stereographic-d-transformation}
\end{equation}
The two independent four-derivative invariants relevant to four-pion
scattering can be written as
\[
  (\vec D_\mu\cdot\vec D^\mu)^2,
  \qquad
  (\vec D_\mu\cdot\vec D_\nu)(\vec D^\mu\cdot\vec D^\nu),
\]
up to integrations by parts and terms that first affect higher-point
amplitudes.
This coordinate choice is only a local chart on \(S^3\); the scattering
amplitude obtained from it is coordinate independent.  In the axial-current
normalization above, \(T^a=\sigma^a/2\) and therefore
\[
  \exp\left(\frac{2\ii}{f_\pi}\pi_{\rm ax}^aT^a\right)
  =
  1+\frac{\ii}{f_\pi}\pi_{\rm ax}^a\sigma^a+O(\pi_{\rm ax}^2).
\]
The stereographic chart has
\[
  \frac{1-\vec\xi^{\,2}+2\ii\,\vec\xi\cdot\vec\sigma}
       {1+\vec\xi^{\,2}}
  =
  1+2\ii\,\xi^a\sigma^a+O(\xi^2),
  \qquad
  \pi_{\rm st}^a=F_{\rm st}\xi^a .
\]
Identifying the one-pion field defined by the axial current with the
canonical stereographic field,
\(\pi_{\rm ax}^a=\pi_{\rm st}^a\), gives the conversion
\begin{equation}
  F_{\rm st}=2f_\pi .
  \label{eq:fst-fpi-conversion}
\end{equation}
A convention that rescales the stereographic coordinate rescales
\(F_{\rm st}\) and the coordinate field together; in the fixed chart
\eqref{eq:su2-stereographic-pion-field} the numerical factor in
\eqref{eq:fst-fpi-conversion} is part of the convention.

For
\[
  \pi^a(k_1)+\pi^b(k_2)\to \pi^c(k_3)+\pi^d(k_4),
\]
use the Mandelstam variables
\[
  s=-(k_1+k_2)^2,\qquad
  t=-(k_1-k_3)^2,\qquad
  u=-(k_1-k_4)^2 .
\]
The metric is mostly plus, \(k_1,k_2\) are incoming, and \(k_3,k_4\) are
outgoing.  In the chiral limit used for the two-derivative Goldstone
calculation, \(k_i^2=0\), so
\[
  s+t+u=0 .
\]
For equal nonzero pion masses the same convention gives
\(s+t+u=4m_\pi^2\).
The \(SU(2)_V\)-invariant amplitude has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{ab\to cd}
  =
  \delta_{ab}\delta_{cd}A(s,t,u)
  +\delta_{ac}\delta_{bd}A(u,t,s)
  +\delta_{ad}\delta_{bc}A(t,s,u).
  \label{eq:pion-scattering-isospin-decomposition}
\end{equation}
At order \(E^2/F_{\rm st}^2\), the four-pion vertex from
\eqref{eq:su2-stereographic-expansion} gives
\begin{equation}
  A(s,t,u)
  =
  \frac{4s}{F_{\rm st}^2}
  +O(E^4/F_{\rm st}^4).
  \label{eq:pion-leading-scattering-amplitude}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, after converting to the convention in which the axial current
matrix element defines \(f_\pi\), this is the standard leading chiral theorem
\[
  A(s,t,u)=\frac{s}{f_\pi^2}+O(E^4/f_\pi^4).
\]

At order \(E^4/F_{\rm st}^4\), the effective Lagrangian contains local
four-derivative terms.  In the stereographic \(SU(2)\) chart these may be
written, to the order needed for four-pion scattering, as
\[
  -\frac{C_4}{4F_{\rm st}^4}
  (\partial_\mu\vec\pi\cdot\partial^\mu\vec\pi)^2
  -
  \frac{C'_4}{4F_{\rm st}^4}
  (\partial_\mu\vec\pi\cdot\partial_\nu\vec\pi)
  (\partial^\mu\vec\pi\cdot\partial^\nu\vec\pi).
\]
The same order also receives one-loop contributions from two insertions of
the two-derivative interaction.  A regulator that does not preserve the
nonlinearly realized symmetry may generate power terms that look like a
potential on \(S^3\); such terms must be absent in a symmetry-preserving
scheme or canceled by symmetry-restoring local counterterms.  After this
local renormalization, the logarithmic part has the form
\begin{align}
  A(s,t,u)
  &=
  \frac{4s}{F_{\rm st}^2}
  +
  \frac1{F_{\rm st}^4}
  \biggl\{
  \frac1{12\pi^2}
    \biggl[
      3s^2\log\frac{\mu^2}{-s}
      \notag\\
  &\hspace{2.4cm}
      +(u^2-s^2+3t^2)\log\frac{\mu^2}{-t}
      \notag\\
  &\hspace{2.4cm}
      +(t^2-s^2+3u^2)\log\frac{\mu^2}{-u}
    \biggr]
    \notag\\
  &\hspace{1.6cm}
    -\frac12 C_4(\mu)s^2
    -\frac12 C'_4(\mu)(t^2+u^2)
  \biggr\}
  +O(E^6).
  \label{eq:pion-one-loop-chiral-log-amplitude}
\end{align}
Equivalently, in a Wilsonian cutoff notation in which the logarithms are
written as \(\log(\Lambda^2/-s)\), \(\log(\Lambda^2/-t)\), and
\(\log(\Lambda^2/-u)\), the remaining cutoff dependence is absorbed by
\[
  C_4(\Lambda)
  =
  C_4(\mu)+\frac{2}{3\pi^2}\log\frac{\Lambda^2}{\mu^2},
  \qquad
  C'_4(\Lambda)
  =
  C'_4(\mu)+\frac{4}{3\pi^2}\log\frac{\Lambda^2}{\mu^2}.
\]
The finite constants \(C_4(\mu)\) and \(C'_4(\mu)\) are low-energy constants.
Their values depend on the microscopic dynamics and on the subtraction
convention, but the logarithmic dependence and the required \(\mu\)-dependence
are fixed by the low-energy effective theory.

\section{Mass Spurions and Pseudo-Goldstone Bosons}

Quark masses explicitly break the chiral symmetry.  Introduce a complex mass
matrix \(M\) through
\[
  \mathcal L_{\mathrm{mass}}^{\mathrm{QCD}}
  =
  -\bar q_L M q_R-\bar q_RM^\dagger q_L .
\]
Treating \(M\) as a background spurion with
\[
  M\longmapsto LMR^{-1}
\]
makes the microscopic mass term formally invariant under
\((L,R)\).  The leading mass-dependent invariant in the pion Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_{\mathrm{mass}}
  =
  \frac{f_\pi^2}{4}
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(\chi U^\dagger+U\chi^\dagger\bigr),
  \qquad
  \chi=2B_0M .
  \label{eq:chiral-mass-spurion}
\end{equation}
The low-energy constant \(B_0\) is fixed by the chiral-limit condensate
convention
\[
  \langle\bar q q\rangle_0
  =
  -N_f f_\pi^2B_0
\]
at leading order.  For two light flavors,
\[
  m_{\pi^\pm}^2
  =
  B_0(m_u+m_d)
\]
up to higher-order chiral and electromagnetic corrections.  For degenerate
light quarks \(m_u=m_d=m_q\), this becomes \(m_\pi^2=2B_0m_q\).
The condensate notation in this chapter is flavor-summed:
\[
  \Sigma_{\mathrm{sum}}
  :=
  \langle\bar q q\rangle_0
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^{N_f}\langle\bar q_iq_i\rangle_0,
  \qquad
  \Sigma_{\mathrm{fl}}
  :=
  \frac{1}{N_f}\Sigma_{\mathrm{sum}} .
\]
The leading-order identity following from the two preceding equations is the
flavor-summed form
\begin{equation}
  m_{\pi^\pm}^2f_\pi^2
  =
  -(m_u+m_d)\,
  \frac{\langle\bar q q\rangle_0}{N_f}.
  \label{eq:gmor-flavor-summed}
\end{equation}
The conversion to the per-flavor condensate is
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma_{\mathrm{fl}}
  =
  \frac{\langle\bar q q\rangle_0}{N_f}
  =
  \langle\bar u u\rangle_0
  =
  \langle\bar d d\rangle_0
  \qquad
  (N_f=2\hbox{ isospin-symmetric vacuum}).
  \label{eq:gmor-condensate-conversion}
\end{equation}
Using \eqref{eq:gmor-condensate-conversion}, the same leading-order statement is
the per-flavor Gell-Mann--Oakes--Renner relation
\begin{equation}
  m_{\pi}^2f_\pi^2
  =
  -(m_u+m_d)\langle\bar u u\rangle_0 .
  \label{eq:gmor-per-flavor}
\end{equation}
The factor of \(2\) is accounted for by the conversion between the
flavor-summed condensate and the per-flavor condensate.

The same normalization can be seen directly in the \(SU(2)\) stereographic
chart.  Let
\[
  M=\begin{pmatrix}m_u&0\\0&m_d\end{pmatrix}.
\]
In terms of the quark bilinear \(\Phi^I{}_{J}=\bar q_L^Iq_{R,J}\), the
microscopic deformation has the form
\[
  \Delta\mathcal L
  =
  -\frac{m_u+m_d}{2}
  \operatorname{Tr}(\Phi+\Phi^\dagger)
  -\frac{m_u-m_d}{2}
  \operatorname{Tr}\bigl(\sigma_3(\Phi+\Phi^\dagger)\bigr).
\]
Restricting the pion field to the \(U(1)\subset SU(2)\) generated by
\(\sigma_3\),
\[
  U(\xi_3)
  =
  1+2\ii\xi_3\sigma_3+\cdots
  =
  \begin{pmatrix}\ee^{\ii\theta}&0\\0&\ee^{-\ii\theta}\end{pmatrix}
  +\cdots ,
  \qquad
  \theta=2\xi_3+\cdots .
\]
Since the kinetic term in \eqref{eq:su2-stereographic-lagrangian} gives
\[
  \mathcal L_2=-\frac{F_{\rm st}^2}{2}\partial_\mu\vec\xi\cdot
  \partial^\mu\vec\xi+\cdots,
\]
the \(U(1)\) comparison has radius \(R=F_{\rm st}/2\).  Expanding the mass
deformation therefore gives
\[
  -\frac12 m_{ab}^2\pi_a\pi_b,
  \qquad
  m_{ab}^2
  =
  \delta_{ab}\frac{4}{F_{\rm st}^2}(m_u+m_d)
  \left\langle-\frac{\bar q q}{2}\right\rangle_0 ,
\]
which is the same leading relation as the \(B_0\)-notation after converting
normalizations.  Empirically,
\[
  m_{\pi^0}\simeq135\,\mathrm{MeV},
  \qquad
  m_{\pi^\pm}\simeq140\,\mathrm{MeV},
\]
with the splitting produced by isospin breaking and electromagnetism.

The chiral expansion counts \(D_\mu U=O(p)\), external vector and axial
backgrounds as \(O(p)\), and \(M\) as \(O(p^2)\).  The functional obtained from
\(\mathcal L_2+\mathcal L_{\mathrm{mass}}\) is therefore the leading
generating functional for current Green functions at momenta small compared
with the chiral-symmetry-breaking scale.

\(U:M_4\to SU(N_f)\) to \(\widetilde U:B_5\to SU(N_f)\).

The ungauged Wess--Zumino functional is
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{\mathrm{WZ}}[U]
  =
  -\frac{\ii}{240\pi^2}
  \int_{B_5}
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(\widetilde U^{-1}\dd \widetilde U\bigr)^5 .
  \label{eq:wess-zumino-functional}
\end{equation}
The five-form is real after the factor \(-\ii\), because
\(\widetilde U^{-1}\dd\widetilde U\) is anti-Hermitian.  If two extensions are
glued along \(M_4\), the difference of \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{WZ}}\) is the
integral over a closed five-manifold.  With the normalization in
\eqref{eq:wess-zumino-functional}, this difference is \(2\pi\) times an
integer for maps representing \(\pi_5(SU(N_f))\simeq\mathbb Z\).  Hence
\[
  \exp\{\ii n\Gamma_{\mathrm{WZ}}[U]\}
\]
is independent of the extension precisely when \(n\in\mathbb Z\).  The value of
this integer in QCD is fixed by matching the infinitesimal background anomaly.
It is enough to gauge a left flavor background \(A=A_\mu\dd x^\mu\), set the
right background to zero, and retain the part quadratic in \(A\).  Matrix
multiplication and wedge product are implicit in the following formulas.  A
single left-handed quark multiplet gives the consistent variation
\[
  \delta_\alpha\Gamma_{\mathrm{1\,color}}[A]
  =
  \frac1{48\pi^2}
  \int_{M_4}\operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(\alpha\,\dd A\,\dd A\bigr)
  +O(A^3),
\]
where \(\alpha=\alpha^aT^a\).  QCD has \(N_c\) identical color copies of this
flavor representation, hence
\[
  \delta_\alpha\Gamma_{\mathrm{quark}}[A]
  =
  \frac{N_c}{48\pi^2}
  \int_{M_4}\operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(\alpha\,\dd A\,\dd A\bigr)
  +O(A^3).
\]
The gauged Wess--Zumino functional at level \(n\) has the corresponding
Goldstone variation
\[
  \delta_\alpha\bigl(n\Gamma_{\mathrm{WZW}}[U;A,0]\bigr)
  =
  \frac{n}{48\pi^2}
  \int_{M_4}\operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(\alpha\,\dd A\,\dd A\bigr)
  +O(A^3).
\]
Equality of the anomaly phases for arbitrary infinitesimal
\(\alpha\) and background \(A\) gives
\[
  n=N_c .
\]

When the flavor symmetry is coupled to background gauge fields, the
Wess--Zumino functional is completed by local four-dimensional terms involving
\(U,\ell_\mu,r_\mu\).  The completed Wess--Zumino--Witten functional
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{WZW}}[U;\ell,r]\) satisfies
\[
  \delta_{\alpha_L,\alpha_R}
  \Gamma_{\mathrm{WZW}}[U;\ell,r]
  =
  \mathcal A_{\mathrm{quark}}[\alpha_L,\alpha_R;\ell,r],
\]
where \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{quark}}\) is the consistent flavor anomaly of the
microscopic quarks.  Thus the pion functional supplies exactly the anomaly
phase required by \eqref{eq:finite-background-anomaly}.  Equivalently, the
gauged Wess--Zumino--Witten functional is a Goldstone-field realization of the
same inflow class: its five-dimensional extension transgresses the microscopic
anomaly polynomial, and its four-dimensional gauge variation is the boundary
term that matches the quark anomaly.

\section{Conceptual Output of the Gauge-Dynamics Sequence}

The dependencies established in this sequence are as follows.  Gauge fields are
local connections.  Gauge fixing supplies local coordinates and ghosts.  BRST
cohomology defines physical states, local operators, counterterms, and anomaly
candidates in the gauge-fixed formalism.  QCD renormalization gives
asymptotically free short-distance variables.  Local anomalies are constrained
by Wess--Zumino consistency and descent.  Finite background anomalies are
encoded by cocycles on background-field space.  Exact anomaly classes are
preserved along RG flow.  Spontaneous symmetry breaking realizes these
constraints through Goldstone fields and the Wess--Zumino--Witten functional.
