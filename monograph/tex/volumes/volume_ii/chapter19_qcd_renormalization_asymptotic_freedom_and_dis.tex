\chapter{QCD Renormalization, Asymptotic Freedom, and Deep Inelastic Scattering}

\section{QCD as a Renormalized Gauge Theory}

Quantum chromodynamics in four dimensions is Yang--Mills theory with gauge
group \(SU(N_c)\) coupled to \(N_f\) Dirac fermions in the fundamental
representation.  Write the quark fields as
\[
  q^i_{I\alpha}(x),
  \qquad
  i=1,\ldots,N_c,\quad I=1,\ldots,N_f,
\]
where \(i\) is color, \(I\) is flavor, and \(\alpha\) is a spinor index.  We
use the fundamental normalization
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_{\square}(t^a t^b)=T_F\delta^{ab},
  \qquad
  T_F=\frac12 .
\]
The group-theoretic constants used in renormalization are defined by
\begin{equation}
  f^{acd}f^{bcd}=C_A\delta^{ab},
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}_{R}(t_R^a t_R^b)=T_R\delta^{ab},
  \qquad
  t_R^a t_R^a=C_R\mathbf 1_R .
  \label{eq:qcd-casimir-definitions}
\end{equation}
For \(SU(N_c)\),
\[
  C_A=N_c,\qquad
  T_F=\frac12,\qquad
  C_F=\frac{N_c^2-1}{2N_c}.
\]

The renormalized coupling \(g(\mu)\) depends on a scale \(\mu\) and on the
renormalization scheme.  The beta function is
\[
  \beta(g):=\mu\frac{\dd g}{\dd\mu}.
\]
The first coefficient is obtained below from the background-field 1PI
effective action.  In a mass-independent scheme the result is
\begin{equation}
  \beta(g)
  =
  -\frac{g^3}{16\pi^2}
  \left(
  \frac{11}{3}C_A-\frac{4}{3}T_F N_f
  \right)
  +O(g^5).
  \label{eq:qcd-one-loop-beta-general}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  \beta(g)
  =
  -\frac{g^3}{16\pi^2}
  \left(
  \frac{11}{3}N_c-\frac{2}{3}N_f
  \right)
  +O(g^5)
  \label{eq:qcd-one-loop-beta-fundamental}
\end{equation}
for fundamental quarks.

\section{Background-Field Derivation of the One-Loop Beta Function}

The coupling in a nonabelian gauge theory is most cleanly extracted from the
coefficient of the background curvature term in the 1PI effective action.  Let
\(A_\mu=\widetilde A_\mu+a_\mu\), where \(\widetilde A_\mu\) is a background
connection and \(a_\mu\) is the quantum fluctuation.  The background covariant
derivative in a representation \(R\) is denoted
\[
  \widetilde D_\mu=\partial_\mu-\ii \widetilde A_\mu^a t_R^a ,
  \qquad
  \widetilde F_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \partial_\mu\widetilde A_\nu-\partial_\nu\widetilde A_\mu
  -\ii[\widetilde A_\mu,\widetilde A_\nu].
\]
In the adjoint representation,
\[
  (\widetilde D_\mu X)^a
  =
  \partial_\mu X^a+f^{a}{}_{bc}\widetilde A_\mu^bX^c .
\]
Choose the background-field gauge condition
\[
  \widetilde D^\mu a_\mu=0 .
\]
Under a background gauge transformation with parameter
\(\zeta=\zeta^a t^a\),
\[
  \delta \widetilde A_\mu=\widetilde D_\mu\zeta,\qquad
  \delta a_\mu=-\ii[a_\mu,\zeta],
\]
and the ghosts and quarks transform in the adjoint and \(R\) representations,
respectively.  Since this transformation acts linearly on the variables
integrated over in the path integral and as a connection transformation on
\(\widetilde A\), the resulting 1PI functional
\(\Gamma[\widetilde A]\) is invariant under background gauge transformations.
Its dimension-four term therefore has the form
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma[\widetilde A]
  \supset
  -\frac1{4g^2(\mu)}
  \int\dd^4x\,
  \widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^{a\mu\nu}
  \label{eq:qcd-background-effective-coupling}
\end{equation}
in Lorentzian signature, up to terms with more powers of
\(\widetilde F\), covariant derivatives, or quark fields.  The problem is
therefore reduced to computing one coefficient in a gauge-invariant
functional, rather than separately renormalizing gauge-fixed vertices.

Set the background quark and ghost fields to zero and use background Feynman
gauge.  The part of the action quadratic in \(a_\mu,q,\bar q,c,\bar c\) has
the form
\begin{equation}
  S^{(2)}
  =
  \int\dd^4x\,
  \left[
    -\frac1{4g_0^2}a^\mu_a(D_A)_{a\mu,b\nu}a^\nu_b
    +\bar q D_\psi q
    +\bar c^aD_{\rm gh}^{ab}c^b
  \right],
  \label{eq:qcd-background-quadratic-action}
\end{equation}
where \(g_0\) is the bare coupling.  The operators are differential operators
whose coefficients are built from \(\widetilde D_\mu\) and
\(\widetilde F_{\mu\nu}\).  In background Feynman gauge their principal
pieces are
\[
  D_\psi=-\gamma^\mu\widetilde D_\mu,\qquad
  D_{\rm gh}=\widetilde D_\mu\widetilde D^\mu,
\]
and \(D_A\) is the adjoint vector operator obtained from the quadratic part of
\((\widetilde F+\widetilde D a-\widetilde D a+[a,a])^2\) together with
the gauge-fixing term.  The Gaussian integral gives
\begin{equation}
  \ii\Gamma^{(1)}[\widetilde A]
  =
  \log
  \left[
    \frac{(\det D_\psi)^{N_f}\det D_{\rm gh}}
    {(\det D_A)^{1/2}}
  \right].
  \label{eq:qcd-background-determinant-ratio}
\end{equation}
The numerator reflects Grassmann integration for quarks and ghosts; the
denominator is the square root from the bosonic gauge-field Gaussian.

To extract the logarithmic coefficient, take \(\widetilde A_\mu\) to be
approximately constant inside a large box and to vanish smoothly outside.
The box size supplies an infrared scale \(L\sim\mu^{-1}\).  In the constant
region, the functional trace of a function of a differential operator reduces
to an ordinary momentum integral:

\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Tr} f(D)
  =
  \int\dd^Dx
  \int\frac{\dd^Dk}{(2\pi)^D}\,
  \operatorname{tr} f(M(k)).
  \label{eq:qcd-constant-background-functional-trace}
\end{equation}
Here \(\operatorname{Tr}\) includes integration over spacetime and trace over
all indices, while \(\operatorname{tr}\) is the finite-dimensional trace over
spin, Lorentz, and color indices of the symbol \(M(k)\).

For the quark determinant, the symbol is
\[
  M_\psi(k)=\ii\left(\not k-\gamma^\mu\widetilde A_\mu^a t_R^a\right).
\]
Expanding \(\operatorname{tr}\log M_\psi(k)\) to fourth order in the constant
background and using rotational invariance in \(D=4-\epsilon\),
\begin{equation}
  \frac{k^{\nu_1}k^{\nu_2}k^{\nu_3}k^{\nu_4}}{(k^2)^4}
  \longrightarrow
  \frac1{(k^2)^2D(D+2)}
  \left(
    \delta^{\nu_1\nu_2}\delta^{\nu_3\nu_4}
    +\delta^{\nu_1\nu_3}\delta^{\nu_2\nu_4}
    +\delta^{\nu_1\nu_4}\delta^{\nu_2\nu_3}
  \right),
  \label{eq:qcd-k-four-rotational-average}
\end{equation}
one obtains
\begin{equation}
  \left.
  \int\frac{\dd^Dk}{(2\pi)^D}\operatorname{tr}\log M_\psi(k)
  \right|_{\widetilde A^4}
  =
  \frac13
  \operatorname{tr}_R
  \left(
    [\widetilde A_\mu,\widetilde A_\nu]
    [\widetilde A^\mu,\widetilde A^\nu]
  \right)
  \int\frac{\dd^Dk}{(2\pi)^D}\frac1{(k^2)^2}.
  \label{eq:qcd-fermion-quartic-background}
\end{equation}
Background gauge invariance completes this quartic expression to the full
curvature invariant.  With the infrared cutoff \(|k|>\mu\),
\begin{equation}
  I_\epsilon(\mu)
  :=
  \int_{|k|>\mu}
  \frac{\dd^{4-\epsilon}k}{(2\pi)^{4-\epsilon}}\frac1{(k^2)^2}
  =
  \frac1{8\pi^2}\frac{\mu^{-\epsilon}}{\epsilon}
  +\hbox{finite}.
  \label{eq:qcd-basic-log-integral}
\end{equation}
Thus one quark flavor in representation \(R\) contributes
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_\psi^{(1)}[\widetilde A]
  \supset
  -\frac13 T_R I_\epsilon(\mu)
  \int\dd^Dx\,
  \widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^{a\mu\nu}.
  \label{eq:qcd-quark-determinant-f2}
\end{equation}

The ghost and gauge-field determinants are evaluated by the same
constant-background method.  Their logarithmic parts are
\begin{align}
  \operatorname{Tr}\log D_{\rm gh}
  &\supset
  \frac1{12}C_A I_\epsilon(\mu)
  \int\dd^Dx\,
  \widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^{a\mu\nu},
  \notag\\
  \operatorname{Tr}\log D_A
  &\supset
  -\frac53 C_A I_\epsilon(\mu)
  \int\dd^Dx\,
  \widetilde F^a_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^{a\mu\nu}.
  \label{eq:qcd-ghost-vector-determinants-f2}
\end{align}
Combining these terms with the determinant weights in
\eqref{eq:qcd-background-determinant-ratio} gives the coefficient
\begin{equation}
  b
  =
  -\frac{N_f}{3}T_R
  +
  \left[
    -\frac12\left(-\frac53\right)+\frac1{12}
  \right]C_A
  =
  \frac{11}{12}C_A-\frac{N_f}{3}T_R .
  \label{eq:qcd-background-b-coefficient}
\end{equation}
The term \(5C_A/6\) comes from the vector determinant after the bosonic
factor \(-1/2\), the term \(C_A/12\) from the Faddeev--Popov determinant, and
the term \(-N_fT_R/3\) from the quark determinants.

Comparing the logarithmic part of \(\Gamma[\widetilde A]\) with
\eqref{eq:qcd-background-effective-coupling} gives
\begin{equation}
  -\frac1{4g^2(\mu)}
  =
  -\frac1{4g_0^2}
  +
  \frac{b}{8\pi^2}\frac{\mu^{-\epsilon}}{\epsilon}
  +\hbox{finite}.
  \label{eq:qcd-background-bare-renormalized-relation}
\end{equation}
In \(D=4-\epsilon\), write the dimensionless coupling as
\[
  \lambda^2(\mu)=\mu^\epsilon g^2(\mu).
\]
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  \frac1{g_0^2}
  =
  \mu^\epsilon
  \left[
    \frac1{\lambda^2(\mu)}
    +
    \frac{b}{2\pi^2}\frac1{\epsilon}
    +\hbox{finite}
    +O(\lambda^2)
  \right].
  \label{eq:qcd-background-bare-coupling-ms}
\end{equation}
The bare coupling \(g_0\) is independent of the auxiliary scale \(\mu\).
Differentiating \eqref{eq:qcd-background-bare-coupling-ms} at fixed \(g_0\)
and then taking \(\epsilon\to0\) gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd}{\dd\log\mu}\frac1{\lambda^2(\mu)}
  =
  \frac{b}{2\pi^2}+O(\lambda^2),
  \qquad
  \beta(\lambda)
  =
  -\frac{b}{4\pi^2}\lambda^3+O(\lambda^5).
  \label{eq:qcd-background-beta-lambda}
\end{equation}
Setting \(\epsilon=0\) and writing \(g\) for the four-dimensional
dimensionless coupling,
\[
  \frac{b}{4\pi^2}
  =
  \frac1{16\pi^2}
  \left(
    \frac{11}{3}C_A-\frac{4}{3}T_RN_f
  \right),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:qcd-one-loop-beta-general}.  For fundamental quarks of
\(SU(N_c)\), \(T_R=T_F=1/2\) and \(C_A=N_c\), giving
\eqref{eq:qcd-one-loop-beta-fundamental}.

\section{Dimensional Transmutation}

Assume
\[
  b_0:=\frac{11}{3}N_c-\frac{2}{3}N_f>0 .
\]
At one loop,
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd g}{\dd\mu}
  =
  -\frac{b_0}{16\pi^2}g^3 .
\]
Integrating between \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu\) gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac1{g^2(\mu)}
  =
  \frac1{g^2(\mu_0)}
  +
  \frac{b_0}{8\pi^2}\log\frac{\mu}{\mu_0}.
  \label{eq:qcd-one-loop-running}
\end{equation}
Thus \(g(\mu)\) decreases at large \(\mu\).  This property is asymptotic
freedom.

The integration constant may be traded for a scale.  At one-loop accuracy,
\begin{equation}
  \Lambda
  =
  \mu\,
  \exp\left\{
  -\frac{8\pi^2}{b_0 g^2(\mu)}
  \right\}.
  \label{eq:qcd-lambda-one-loop}
\end{equation}
Higher-loop definitions multiply the right side by powers and series in
\(g(\mu)\), and the numerical value depends on the renormalization scheme.
The structural point is scheme independent: a dimensionless renormalized
coupling has been replaced by a dimensionful RG-invariant scale, together
with the quark masses.  This is dimensional transmutation.

Perturbative predictions at external momentum scale \(Q\) are organized by
the small parameter \(g(Q)\) when \(Q\gg\Lambda\).  At scales comparable to
\(\Lambda\), the perturbative expansion is no longer a controlled expansion in
small coupling.  Statements about the spectrum, confinement, and the mass gap
then require nonperturbative input.

\section{Color and Physical External States}

The gauge-fixed quark and gluon fields are useful local variables in
perturbation theory.  Physical states are BRST cohomology classes, and local
physical operators are represented by gauge-invariant or BRST-closed
operators.  This has an immediate consequence for scattering: LSZ reduction
applied to the elementary quark or gluon fields does not by itself define a
physical hadron \(S\)-matrix.  Physical external states in QCD are created by
color-singlet operators, such as
\[
  \bar q_I q_J,\qquad
  \bar q_I\gamma^\mu q_J,\qquad
  \epsilon_{i_1\cdots i_{N_c}}
  q^{i_1}_{I_1}\cdots q^{i_{N_c}}_{I_{N_c}},
  \qquad
  \operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}).
\]
The first three are mesonic current, vector-current, and baryonic examples;
the last is a gluonic example.

Confinement is the nonperturbative property that isolated finite-energy
asymptotic particles carry no color.  The monograph uses it in this precise
spectral sense when discussing QCD phenomenology: the asymptotic states are
hadrons, while the short-distance operator expansion is expressed in terms of
quark and gluon fields because those are the local variables of the
renormalized gauge theory.

\section{Current Correlators and the Hadronic Tensor}

Deep inelastic scattering probes a hadron by a local current.  Let
\(\ket{P}\) be a one-hadron momentum eigenstate with \(P^2=M^2\).  For an
electromagnetic current
\[
  J^\mu(x)=\sum_I e_I\,\bar q_I(x)\gamma^\mu q_I(x),
\]
define the hadronic tensor
\begin{equation}
  W^{\mu\nu}(P,q)
  =
  \frac1{4\pi}
  \int \dd^4 x\,
  \ee^{\ii q\cdot x}
  \bra{P}
  [J^\mu(x),J^\nu(0)]
  \ket{P}.
  \label{eq:hadronic-tensor-definition}
\end{equation}
The momentum transfer is spacelike:
\[
  Q^2:=-q^2>0,
  \qquad
  x_B:=\frac{Q^2}{2P\cdot q}.
\]
The Bjorken limit is
\[
  Q^2\to\infty,
  \qquad
  x_B\ \text{fixed}.
\]
The short-distance region \(x\to0\) in
\eqref{eq:hadronic-tensor-definition} is then controlled by the operator
product expansion of two currents:
\begin{equation}
  J^\mu(x)J^\nu(0)
  \sim
  \sum_a C_a^{\mu\nu}(x,\mu)\,\mathcal O_a(0,\mu).
  \label{eq:dis-current-ope}
\end{equation}
The Wilson coefficients \(C_a^{\mu\nu}\) are determined by short-distance
renormalization and can be computed in perturbation theory at large \(Q\).  The
matrix elements
\(\bra{P}\mathcal O_a(0,\mu)\ket{P}\) are long-distance hadronic data.

\section{Twist, Parton Distributions, and Factorization}

For a local operator of spin \(s\) and scaling dimension \(\Delta\), its twist
is
\[
  \tau:=\Delta-s .
\]
In the Bjorken limit, the leading terms in the OPE are the smallest-twist
operators compatible with the quantum numbers of the currents.  A convenient
way to collect the tower of leading-twist matrix elements is by light-ray
operators.  Let \(n^\mu\) be a lightlike vector, \(n^2=0\), and let
\[
  W(\lambda n,0)
  =
  \mathcal P
  \exp\left\{\ii\int_0^\lambda
  n^\mu A_\mu(sn)\,\dd s\right\}
\]
be the Wilson line in the appropriate color representation.  With the
normalization of one-particle hadron states fixed as in the scattering
chapters, a quark parton distribution is defined by the renormalized light-ray
matrix element
\begin{equation}
  f_{q/H}(x,\mu)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd\lambda}{2\pi}\,
  \ee^{-\ii x\lambda P\cdot n}
  \bra{P}
  \bar q(\lambda n) W(\lambda n,0)\gamma\cdot n\,q(0)
  \ket{P}_{\mu}.
  \label{eq:pdf-light-ray-definition}
\end{equation}
The Wilson line makes the nonlocal operator gauge covariant, and the
subscript \(\mu\) indicates the renormalization scale of the light-ray
operator.

Factorization expresses a structure function \(F_i\) in the form
\begin{equation}
  F_i(x_B,Q)
  =
  \sum_{a}
  \int_{x_B}^{1}\frac{\dd z}{z}\,
  C_{i,a}\!\left(\frac{x_B}{z},\frac{Q}{\mu},g(\mu)\right)
  f_{a/H}(z,\mu)
  +
  O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^p}{Q^p}\right),
  \label{eq:dis-factorization}
\end{equation}
where \(a\) runs over quark, antiquark, and gluon parton species, and the
power \(p>0\) depends on the first omitted higher-twist operators.  The
coefficients \(C_{i,a}\) are short-distance kernels; the distributions
\(f_{a/H}\) are universal hadronic matrix elements for the chosen
factorization scheme.

The independence of \(F_i\) from the auxiliary scale \(\mu\) gives the
evolution equation
\begin{equation}
  \mu\frac{\dd}{\dd\mu} f_{a/H}(x,\mu)
  =
  \sum_b\int_x^1\frac{\dd z}{z}\,
  P_{ab}\!\left(\frac{x}{z},g(\mu)\right)f_{b/H}(z,\mu),
  \label{eq:dglap-evolution}
\end{equation}
where \(P_{ab}\) are splitting kernels, equivalently anomalous dimensions of
the light-ray operators.  This is the precise sense in which short-distance
quark and gluon variables control high-energy hadronic observables while
physical asymptotic states remain color singlets.

\section{Local Twist-Two Operators and Integer Moments}

The light-ray definition packages an infinite family of local operators.  For
quarks, the symmetric traceless spin-\(N\) operators have the form
\[
  \mathcal O_{q}^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_N}
  =
  \bar q\,
  \gamma^{(\mu_1}
  \ii D^{\mu_2}\cdots \ii D^{\mu_N)}
  q
  -
  \text{traces},
  \qquad N\ge1,
\]
with gauge-covariant derivatives in the appropriate representation.  The
parentheses denote symmetrization over Lorentz indices.  The gluon operators
are
\[
  \mathcal O_{g}^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_N}
  =
  F^{(\mu_1}{}_{\rho}\,
  \ii D^{\mu_2}\cdots\ii D^{\mu_{N-1}}
  F^{\mu_N)\rho}
  -
  \text{traces},
  \qquad N\ge2 .
\]
Both have twist two after subtracting traces.  Their hadron matrix elements
are fixed by Lorentz covariance up to scalar reduced matrix elements:
\[
  \bra{P}\mathcal O_{a}^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_N}(\mu)\ket{P}
  =
  2\,a_{a,N}(\mu)
  \bigl(P^{\mu_1}\cdots P^{\mu_N}-\text{traces}\bigr),
  \qquad a=q,g .
\]

Expanding the light-ray operator near zero separation gives these local
operators contracted with the lightlike vector \(n^\mu\).  Consequently the
integer moments of the parton distributions are the same reduced matrix
elements, with the precise flavor and charge-conjugation combination fixed by
the current channel:
\[
  a_{a,N}(\mu)
  =
  \int_0^1 \dd x\,x^{N-1} f_{a/H}(x,\mu)
\]
for the corresponding parton combination.  The local OPE and the nonlocal
light-ray factorization are therefore two presentations of the same
short-distance data.

Renormalization mixes operators with the same spin, twist, and quantum
numbers.  Taking integer moments of \eqref{eq:dglap-evolution} gives
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd}{\dd\mu}a_{a,N}(\mu)
  =
  \sum_b P_{ab,N}(g(\mu))\,a_{b,N}(\mu),
  \qquad
  P_{ab,N}(g)=\int_0^1\dd x\,x^{N-1}P_{ab}(x,g).
\]
The coefficient functions in the current OPE obey the dual evolution so that
the physical structure functions are independent of the auxiliary
factorization scale.  This is the local-operator form of perturbative
scaling violation in deep inelastic scattering.
