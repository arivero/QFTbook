\chapter{Gauge Fixing, Ghosts, and BRST Cohomology}
\label{ch:volume-ii-18}

\section{Gauge Orbits and Local Coordinates}

Let \(M\) be the spacetime manifold and let \(G\) be the compact gauge group
whose Lie algebra conventions were fixed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:vol2-classical-yang-mills-matter}.  On a fixed local
trivialization, the space of gauge potentials is the affine space
\[
  \mathcal A=\Omega^1(M,\mathfrak g).
\]
Here \(\mathfrak g=i\,\mathfrak g_{\mathrm{ah}}\) is the Hermitian coordinate
space associated with the anti-Hermitian Lie algebra
\(\mathfrak g_{\mathrm{ah}}=\operatorname{Lie}(G)\) in a unitary
representation.  If \(X,Y\in\mathfrak g\), the compact real Lie bracket is
\[
  [X,Y]_{\mathrm H}:=-\ii[X,Y],
\]
where the bracket on the right is the matrix commutator in a unitary
representation.
The local gauge group is
\[
  \mathcal G=C^\infty(M,G),
\]
acting on \(A\in\mathcal A\) by
\[
  A_\mu\longmapsto A_\mu^g
  =
  gA_\mu g^{-1}+\ii g\partial_\mu g^{-1}.
\]
The infinitesimal action associated with
\(\zeta=\zeta^a t^a\in C^\infty(M,\mathfrak g)\) is
\[
  \delta_\zeta A_\mu=D_\mu(A)\zeta,
  \qquad
  D_\mu(A)\zeta=\partial_\mu\zeta+[A_\mu,\zeta]_{\mathrm H},
  \qquad
  (D_\mu(A)\zeta)^a
  =
  \partial_\mu\zeta^a+f^{bc}{}_{a}A_\mu^b\zeta^c .
\]
Gauge-invariant classical functionals are constant along \(\mathcal G\)-orbits
in \(\mathcal A\).  A formal path integral over gauge fields therefore
contains a redundant integral over orbit directions unless a coordinate on the
orbit space is chosen.

A gauge condition is a map
\[
  F:\mathcal A\longrightarrow C^\infty(M,\mathfrak g),
  \qquad
  A\longmapsto F(A)=F^a(A)t^a ,
\]
with the intended local slice \(F(A)=0\).  Its infinitesimal variation along a
gauge orbit defines the Faddeev--Popov operator
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal M^{ab}_F(A;x,y)
  :=
  \left.
  \frac{\delta F^a(A^{\exp(\ii\zeta)})(x)}
       {\delta\zeta^b(y)}
  \right|_{\zeta=0}.
  \label{eq:fp-operator-definition}
\end{equation}
In a region of field space where the slice is transverse to the orbit, the
operator \(\mathcal M_F(A)\) is invertible after zero modes associated with
unfixed global transformations have been separated.  The formal identity
\begin{equation}
  1
  =
  \Delta_F(A)
  \int_{\mathcal G}Dg\,
  \delta\bigl(F(A^g)\bigr),
  \qquad
  \Delta_F(A)=\det \mathcal M_F(A),
  \label{eq:fp-identity}
\end{equation}
is then the change-of-variables formula from orbit coordinates to a local
slice.  Global obstructions to a single smooth slice are real features of
nonabelian gauge theory; the perturbative construction around a chosen
background uses the local form \eqref{eq:fp-identity}.

\section{Covariant Gauges and Ghost Fields}

The covariant linear gauge condition is
\[
  F^a(A)=\partial^\mu A_\mu^a .
\]
For this choice,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal M^{ab}(A)
  =
  \partial^\mu D_\mu^{ab}(A),
  \qquad
  (D_\mu c)^a
  =
  \partial_\mu c^a+f^{bc}{}_{a}A_\mu^b c^c .
  \label{eq:covariant-fp-operator}
\end{equation}
The determinant of \(\mathcal M(A)\) is represented by Grassmann-valued scalar
fields \(c^a(x)\) and \(\bar c^a(x)\) in the adjoint representation:
\begin{equation}
  \det\mathcal M(A)
  =
  \int [D\bar c\,Dc]\,
  \exp\left\{-\ii\int_M \bar c^a\mathcal M^{ab}(A)c^b\,\dd^D x\right\}.
  \label{eq:ghost-determinant}
\end{equation}
This equality is understood up to a field-independent normalization and phase:
in a finite-dimensional Grassmann Gaussian, changing the sign multiplying
\(\bar c\,\mathcal M c\) multiplies the integral by a power of \(-1\) and
\(\ii\), but does not change its dependence on \(A\).  The sign in
\eqref{eq:ghost-determinant} is chosen so that the ghost term agrees with the
BRST gauge-fixing fermion below in the Minkowski weight \(\exp(\ii S)\).
The field \(c\) is called the ghost and \(\bar c\) the antighost.  They are
Lorentz scalars with fermionic statistics.  Their role is determined by
\eqref{eq:ghost-determinant}: they encode the Jacobian of gauge fixing and
produce the loop contributions required by gauge covariance of the
renormalized theory.

It is useful to introduce an auxiliary real field \(B^a(x)\), the
Nakanishi--Lautrup field.  For a real parameter \(\xi\), and for the
Yang--Mills normalization
\[
  \mathcal L_{\mathrm{YM}}
  =
  -\frac1{4g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2}F^a_{\mu\nu}F^{a\mu\nu},
\]
the gauge-fixing and ghost Lagrangian density can be written
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_{\mathrm{gf+gh}}
  =
  B^a\partial^\mu A_\mu^a
  +\frac{g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2\xi}{2}B^aB^a
  -\bar c^a\,\partial^\mu D_\mu^{ab}(A)c^b .
  \label{eq:gf-ghost-lagrangian}
\end{equation}
Eliminating \(B\) by its algebraic equation of motion gives
\[
  B^a=-\frac1{g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2\xi}\partial^\mu A_\mu^a,
  \qquad
  \mathcal L_{\mathrm{gf}}
  =
  -\frac1{2g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2\xi}
  (\partial^\mu A_\mu^a)(\partial^\nu A_\nu^a),
\]
with the usual limiting interpretation of Landau gauge as \(\xi\to0\).
Setting \(g_{\mathrm{YM}}=1\), or rescaling the gauge potential to canonical
quadratic normalization, gives the more common version without the displayed
factor of \(g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2\) in the \(B^2\) term.

\section{The BRST Differential}

The gauge-fixed field algebra is a graded-commutative algebra generated by
\[
  A_\mu^a,\qquad c^a,\qquad \bar c^a,\qquad B^a,
\]
and by matter fields when present.  Assign ghost numbers
\[
  \operatorname{gh}(A_\mu)=0,\qquad
  \operatorname{gh}(B)=0,\qquad
  \operatorname{gh}(c)=1,\qquad
  \operatorname{gh}(\bar c)=-1 .
\]
The BRST differential \(s\) is an odd derivation of ghost number \(+1\).  On
the Yang--Mills sector it is defined by
\begin{equation}
  sA_\mu^a=(D_\mu c)^a,\qquad
  sc^a=-\frac12 f^{a}{}_{bc}c^b c^c,\qquad
  s\bar c^a=B^a,\qquad
  sB^a=0 .
  \label{eq:brst-transformations}
\end{equation}
The ghost \(c=c^a t^a\) is an odd field valued in the same Hermitian coordinate
space as the infinitesimal gauge parameter.  For an odd \(\mathfrak g\)-valued
field, the expression \([c,c]_{\mathrm H}\) is defined by combining the Lie
bracket with the Grassmann product:
\[
  [c,c]_{\mathrm H}^a=f^a{}_{bc}c^b c^c .
\]
Thus \(sc=-\frac12[c,c]_{\mathrm H}\).  The antighost \(\bar c\) is an
independent odd adjoint-valued scalar of ghost number \(-1\), and \(B\) is an
even adjoint-valued scalar of ghost number \(0\).
For a matter field \(\psi\in V_R\), with representation matrices \(t_R^a\),
\begin{equation}
  s\psi=\ii c^a t_R^a\psi,
  \qquad
  s\bar\psi=-\ii\bar\psi\,c^a t_R^a .
  \label{eq:brst-matter-transformations}
\end{equation}
The derivation rule is graded:
\[
  s(XY)=(sX)Y+(-1)^{|X|}X(sY),
\]
where \(|X|\) is the Grassmann parity of \(X\).

Nilpotency is an algebraic consequence of the Jacobi identity.  For the ghost,
using the graded derivation rule gives
\[
  s^2c^a
  =
  \frac14 f^a{}_{bc} f^b{}_{de} c^d c^e c^c
  -\frac14 f^a{}_{bc} f^c{}_{de} c^b c^d c^e .
\]
After antisymmetrizing over the three odd ghost factors, this is
\[
  s^2c^a
  =
  -\frac1{12}
  \left(
    f^a{}_{be}f^e{}_{cd}
    +f^a{}_{ce}f^e{}_{db}
    +f^a{}_{de}f^e{}_{bc}
  \right)c^b c^c c^d
  =
  0 .
\]
For the gauge field, the matrix form of the same computation is
\[
  s^2A_\mu
  =
  D_\mu(sc)+[D_\mu c,c]_{\mathrm H}
  =
  -\frac12D_\mu[c,c]_{\mathrm H}+[D_\mu c,c]_{\mathrm H}
  =
  0,
\]
because \(D_\mu[c,c]_{\mathrm H}=2[D_\mu c,c]_{\mathrm H}\) for odd \(c\).
For a matter field, the representation identity
\([t_R^a,t_R^b]=\ii f^{ab}{}_{c}t_R^c\) gives
\[
  s^2\psi
  =
  \ii(sc^a)t_R^a\psi+c^a c^b t_R^a t_R^b\psi
  =
  -\frac{\ii}{2} f^a{}_{bc}c^b c^c t_R^a\psi
  +\frac12 c^a c^b[t_R^a,t_R^b]\psi
  =
  0,
\]
and similarly for \(\bar\psi\).  Hence
\begin{equation}
  s^2=0.
  \label{eq:brst-nilpotency}
\end{equation}
Thus \(s\) is the infinitesimal gauge-orbit differential with the even gauge
parameter replaced by the odd field \(c\), combined with the
Chevalley--Eilenberg differential of the gauge algebra and the nonminimal
contractible pair \((\bar c,B)\).

Let
\[
  \Psi
  =
  \int_M
  \bar c^a\left(
    \partial^\mu A_\mu^a+\frac{g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2\xi}{2}B^a
  \right)\dd^D x .
\]
Using \eqref{eq:brst-transformations},
\begin{equation}
  s\Psi
  =
  \int_M
  \left[
  B^a\partial^\mu A_\mu^a
  +\frac{g_{\mathrm{YM}}^2\xi}{2}B^aB^a
  -\bar c^a\partial^\mu D_\mu^{ab}(A)c^b
  \right]\dd^D x .
  \label{eq:gauge-fixing-fermion}
\end{equation}
The full gauge-fixed action is therefore
\[
  S_{\mathrm{gf}}
  =
  S_{\mathrm{YM}}+S_{\mathrm{matter}}+s\Psi .
\]
Since \(S_{\mathrm{YM}}+S_{\mathrm{matter}}\) is gauge invariant and \(s^2=0\),
one obtains \(sS_{\mathrm{gf}}=0\).  The gauge-fixed action is therefore
controlled by a nilpotent fermionic symmetry acting on the enlarged field
space, including ghosts, antighosts, and the Nakanishi--Lautrup field.

\section{Local BRST Cohomology}

The BRST differential acts on local expressions before any functional integral
is evaluated.  Let \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{loc}}\) denote the graded-commutative
algebra of local functions: polynomials or formal power series in the fields
\[
  A_\mu,\quad c,\quad \bar c,\quad B,\quad \psi,\quad \bar\psi
\]
and finitely many of their spacetime derivatives, with coefficients smooth in
the spacetime point when explicit \(x\)-dependence is allowed.  The differential
\(s\) is extended to derivatives by
\[
  s(\partial_{\nu_1}\cdots\partial_{\nu_r}\Phi)
  =
  \partial_{\nu_1}\cdots\partial_{\nu_r}(s\Phi)
\]
for every generator \(\Phi\).  The local BRST cohomology at ghost number \(g\)
is
\[
  H^g(s;\mathcal F_{\mathrm{loc}})
  =
  \frac{\{a\in\mathcal F_{\mathrm{loc}}:\operatorname{gh}(a)=g,\ sa=0\}}
       {\{a=sb:\operatorname{gh}(b)=g-1\}} .
\]
At ghost number zero, this cohomology is the local algebraic form of
gauge-invariant composite operators, with BRST-exact representatives removed.

Integrated local functionals require a second quotient because Lagrangian
densities differing by total derivatives define the same action on manifolds
without boundary, or the same bulk functional after boundary data have been
specified.  Let \(\Omega^p_{\mathrm{loc}}\) be the space of local \(p\)-forms and
let
\[
  d=dx^\mu\partial_\mu
\]
be the spacetime exterior derivative.  With the convention that \(s\) and \(d\)
are odd derivations on local forms, they anticommute:
\[
  sd+ds=0 .
\]
For a \(D\)-dimensional spacetime, an integrated local functional is represented
by a local \(D\)-form \(a\).  The cohomology relevant for local functionals is
the relative cohomology \(H^{g,D}(s\mid d)\).  Its cocycles and coboundaries are
\[
  sa+dm=0,
  \qquad
  a\sim a+sb+dn ,
\]
where
\[
  a\in\Omega^D_{\mathrm{loc}},\quad
  m,n\in\Omega^{D-1}_{\mathrm{loc}},\quad
  b\in\Omega^D_{\mathrm{loc}},
\]
with ghost numbers chosen so that all displayed terms have ghost number \(g+1\)
in the first equation and ghost number \(g\) in the equivalence relation.  Thus
\(H^{0,D}(s\mid d)\) classifies BRST-invariant local counterterm densities
modulo field redefinitions and total derivatives, while \(H^{1,D}(s\mid d)\)
is the algebraic home of possible local gauge anomalies.  The anomaly chapters
will compute the relevant representatives in four-dimensional chiral gauge
theories; the present chapter fixes the cohomological language.

The pair \((\bar c,B)\) belongs to the nonminimal gauge-fixing sector.  Since
\[
  s\bar c^a=B^a,\qquad sB^a=0,
\]
and the BRST transformations of the minimal variables \(A,c,\psi,\bar\psi\) do
not depend on \(\bar c\) or \(B\), this pair is contractible in local BRST
cohomology.  The doublet lemma below gives the explicit homotopy.  Consequently,
the nonminimal variables are essential for writing local propagators and
Slavnov--Taylor identities, but the cohomology classes of gauge-invariant local
operators, counterterms, and anomalies may be represented in the minimal sector
under the standard regularity assumptions used in perturbative Yang--Mills
theory.

\section{Cohomology of States and Observables}

In canonical quantization of the gauge-fixed theory, the state space before
the BRST quotient is an indefinite inner-product space \(\mathcal K\).  The
BRST differential is represented by a charge \(Q\) of ghost number \(+1\):
\[
  Q^2=0,
  \qquad
  [Q,A_\mu^a]=\ii sA_\mu^a
\]
with the graded commutator understood for fermionic fields.  The physical
state space is the cohomology
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal H_{\mathrm{phys}}
  =
  H^0(Q,\mathcal K)
  =
  \frac{\ker(Q:\mathcal K^0\to\mathcal K^1)}
       {\operatorname{im}(Q:\mathcal K^{-1}\to\mathcal K^0)} .
  \label{eq:brst-physical-state-cohomology}
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathcal K^n\) is the subspace of ghost number \(n\).  The numerator
selects BRST-closed states, and the denominator identifies states differing by
a BRST-exact vector.  Gauge-invariant local operators define classes in the
ghost-number-zero cohomology of \(s\), while BRST-exact insertions have
vanishing matrix elements between physical states when the BRST symmetry is
preserved by the regulator and renormalization.

The perturbative \(S\)-matrix of a gauge-fixed nonabelian theory is therefore
an operator first constructed on \(\mathcal K\) and then descended to
\(\mathcal H_{\mathrm{phys}}\).  This statement keeps the construction aligned
with the earlier order of the monograph: asymptotic states and LSZ are the
conceptual definition of scattering, while gauge fixing is a local
computational realization whose output must be independent of the chosen
coordinate on gauge orbits.

\section{Positivity of the BRST Quotient}

The quotient in \eqref{eq:brst-physical-state-cohomology} is a Hilbert-space
construction only after positivity has been checked.  Assume the indefinite
inner product on \(\mathcal K\) is nondegenerate and that \(Q\) is symmetric
with respect to it:
\[
  \langle Q\chi,\psi\rangle=\langle\chi,Q\psi\rangle .
\]
If \(\psi\in\ker Q\) and \(\eta=Q\chi\), then
\[
  \langle\eta,\psi\rangle
  =
  \langle Q\chi,\psi\rangle
  =
  \langle\chi,Q\psi\rangle
  =
  0.
\]
Thus exact states are orthogonal to all closed states, including themselves.
The inner product therefore descends to the quotient
\(\ker Q/\operatorname{im}Q\).  The remaining requirement is that the induced
form on ghost number zero cohomology be positive and that zero-norm closed
states represent the zero cohomology class.  When this holds, completing the
quotient gives the physical Hilbert space.

Perturbatively, the unphysical polarizations, ghosts, antighosts, and
Nakanishi--Lautrup modes organize into \(Q\)-doublets.  A doublet has the form
\[
  Q u=v,\qquad Qv=0 .
\]
Any state obtained by acting with such paired excitations is either exact or
paired with an exact state and does not contribute to cohomology.  The
cohomology is then represented by transverse physical polarizations and
gauge-invariant matter states.  This is the cohomological version of the
statement that the physical content of the gauge-fixed construction is carried
by \(Q\)-cohomology classes.

\section{The Doublet Lemma}

The decoupling of unphysical variables has a simple algebraic form.  Suppose a
graded commutative differential algebra contains pairs
\[
  u^i,\qquad v^i,
\]
with
\[
  su^i=v^i,\qquad sv^i=0.
\]
Let \(N\) count the total number of \(u\)'s and \(v\)'s in a polynomial, and
define a homotopy operator \(h\) by
\[
  h v^i=u^i,\qquad h u^i=0,
\]
extended as a graded derivation with the appropriate signs.  On the subalgebra
generated by the doublets one has
\[
  sh+hs=N .
\]
If \(X\) is \(s\)-closed and homogeneous with \(NX=rX\), \(r>0\), then
\[
  X=\frac1r(sh+hs)X=s\left(\frac1r hX\right).
\]
Thus every \(s\)-closed expression with positive doublet number is
\(s\)-exact.  The cohomology is represented by expressions independent of the
doublet variables.

In perturbative gauge theory the longitudinal gauge modes, antighosts,
Nakanishi--Lautrup variables, and their paired ghost excitations are organized
in precisely this way after linearization around the perturbative vacuum.  The
lemma explains why physical cohomology depends on gauge-invariant transverse
and matter data, while the doublet variables remain necessary in intermediate
local formulas for propagators, counterterms, and Slavnov--Taylor identities.

\section{Slavnov--Taylor Identities and Counterterms}

BRST symmetry gives functional identities for the generating functional and
for the 1PI effective action.  In a compact notation, introduce sources
\(K^{a\mu}\) and \(L^a\) coupled to \(sA_\mu^a\) and \(sc^a\).  The 1PI
effective action \(\Gamma[A,c,\bar c,B;K,L]\) obeys the Slavnov--Taylor
identity
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal S(\Gamma)
  :=
  \int_M
  \left[
  \frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta K^{a\mu}}
  \frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta A_\mu^a}
  +
  \frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta L^a}
  \frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta c^a}
  +
  B^a\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\bar c^a}
  \right]\dd^D x
  =
  0 .
  \label{eq:slavnov-taylor-identity}
\end{equation}
The sources \(K^{a\mu}\) and \(L^a\) have ghost numbers \(-1\) and \(-2\),
respectively, so that the source terms \(K^{a\mu}sA^a_\mu\) and \(L^a sc^a\)
have ghost number zero.  They are introduced because the BRST transformations
of \(A_\mu\) and \(c\) are nonlinear composite fields.  The
Batalin--Vilkovisky formulation replaces this case-by-case source assignment:
for every field \(\Phi^A\), one introduces an antifield \(\Phi^*_A\) of
opposite Grassmann parity and ghost number
\[
  \operatorname{gh}(\Phi^*_A)=-1-\operatorname{gh}(\Phi^A),
\]
and encodes gauge symmetry by an odd Poisson bracket, the antibracket, and a
master equation.  The present Slavnov--Taylor identity is the gauge-fixed
Yang--Mills specialization of that homological structure.  A later chapter
will develop the BV formalism as the natural framework for gauge-theory
1PI effective actions, Wilsonian effective actions, and renormalization
compatible with gauge symmetry.
The displayed formula suppresses the sign refinements required by left and
right functional derivatives for odd variables; its content is that the
quantum effective action is invariant under the BRST differential after the
nonlinear variations have been sourced.

Renormalization is compatible with gauge theory when counterterms can be
chosen so that \eqref{eq:slavnov-taylor-identity} remains valid order by
order.  Algebraically, the possible invariant counterterms are classified by
ghost-number-zero local BRST cohomology.  A gauge anomaly is an obstruction at
ghost number \(+1\): it is a local functional \(\mathcal A\) satisfying the
linearized consistency condition
\[
  \mathcal B_\Gamma\mathcal A=0
\]
that cannot be written as \(\mathcal A=\mathcal B_\Gamma X\) for a local
functional \(X\).  The axial anomaly and the nonabelian gauge-anomaly
cancellation conditions are developed in the anomaly chapters below.
