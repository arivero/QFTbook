\section{Local Correlators as the Primary Data}

Let \(\widehat{\mathcal O}_I(x)\) denote a Lorentzian local operator-valued
distribution on \(\R^{1,D-1}\).  The label \(I\) includes all internal and
spin indices.  Local commutativity means that for spacelike separated points
\[
  [\widehat{\mathcal O}_I(x),\widehat{\mathcal O}_J(y)]_\pm=0,
  \qquad
  (x-y)^2>0,
\]
where the graded commutator is used when fermionic operators are present.
For a vacuum vector \(\ket{\Omega}\), the unordered Lorentzian correlation
functions are the Wightman distributions
\[
  W_{I_1\cdots I_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
  =
  \bra{\Omega}
  \widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_1}(x_1)\cdots
  \widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_n}(x_n)
  \ket{\Omega}.
\]
Time-ordered Green functions are
\[
  G^T_{I_1\cdots I_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
  =
  \bra{\Omega}
  T\{\widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_1}(x_1)\cdots
  \widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_n}(x_n)\}
  \ket{\Omega}.
\]
The corresponding Euclidean Schwinger functions are denoted by
\[
  S_{I_1\cdots I_n}(x^E_1,\ldots,x^E_n)
  =
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O_{I_1}(x^E_1)\cdots\mathcal O_{I_n}(x^E_n)
  \right\rangle_E .
\]
Throughout this chapter, the state--operator construction is used under the
following hypotheses: the Euclidean correlators are reflection positive for
reflection through a sphere, the radial Hamiltonian obtained from dilatations
has discrete finite-multiplicity eigenspaces on the finite-energy domain, the
Euclidean and Lorentzian distributions are related by the spectral
tube-domain analytic continuation, and vacuum correlators obey clustering in
the flat-space limit.  These assumptions are precisely the inputs needed to
turn radial reflection positivity into a Hilbert-space inner product and to
identify local insertions at the origin with finite-energy states.

These three versions are related by analytic continuation whenever the
Lorentzian theory satisfies the spectral and locality hypotheses needed for
the tube-domain construction.  For an ordered Wightman product, continue
\[
  x^0_k=-\ii x^D_{E,k}
\]
through a domain in which the imaginary parts remain ordered.  With the
convention \(x^0=-\ii\tau\), the ordering
\[
  \operatorname{Im}x^0_1<\operatorname{Im}x^0_2<\cdots<
  \operatorname{Im}x^0_n
\]
corresponds to
\[
  \tau_1>\tau_2>\cdots>\tau_n .
\]
The Euclidean correlator is the boundary value obtained after this rotation.
Time-ordered Green functions arise from a contour whose real-time projection
places the insertions in chronological order.

When a path-integral representation is available, this analytic continuation
can be represented by a contour \(C\) in complex time:
\[
  S_C[\Phi]
  =
  \int_C \dd x^0\int\dd^{D-1}\mathbf x\,
  \mathcal L(\Phi,\partial\Phi),
  \qquad
  \left\langle\prod_i\mathcal O_i(x_i)\right\rangle_C
  =
  \int[D\Phi]\,\ee^{\ii S_C[\Phi]}\prod_i\mathcal O_i(x_i).
\]
The contour formula is a representation of the same distributional analytic
continuation; it is not an additional axiom of the conformal theory.

\begin{remark}[Observables at a conformal fixed point]
At a conformal fixed point, the data used in this volume are local operators,
their correlation functions, and the Hilbert-space structure obtained from
radial quantization.  A separate scattering theory requires asymptotic
particle states.  Many conformal theories have no mass gap and no isolated
massive particle sectors, so their intrinsic observable data are organized by
local correlations rather than an \(S\)-matrix.  Four-dimensional Yang--Mills
theory illustrates a different situation: the classical action has no
dimensionful coupling, but quantum renormalization produces
\(\Lambda_{\rm QCD}\) and a nonzero trace anomaly, so the quantum theory is
not a conformal field theory.
\end{remark}

\section{The Weyl Map to the Cylinder}

Let \(x^\mu\) be Euclidean coordinates on \(\R^D\), and write
\[
  r=|x|,\qquad
  x^\mu=r n^\mu,\qquad
  n^\mu n_\mu=1.
\]
The flat metric is
\[
  \dd s^2
  =
  \dd r^2+r^2\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2,
\]
where \(\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2\) is the unit metric on \(S^{D-1}\).  Define
\[
  \tau=\log r .
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd s^2}{r^2}
  =
  \dd\tau^2+\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2.
  \label{eq:radial-cylinder-metric}
\end{equation}
Thus the punctured space \(\R^D\setminus\{0\}\) is Weyl equivalent to the
Euclidean cylinder
\[
  \R_\tau\times S^{D-1}.
\]
The origin corresponds to \(\tau=-\infty\), and infinity corresponds to
\(\tau=+\infty\).

The use of this Weyl map assumes that the flat-space conformal theory has
been extended to background metrics with a specified Weyl transformation law
for renormalized local operators.  In even dimensions a Weyl anomaly may add
local curvature terms to the generating functional.  On the punctured flat
space and cylinder comparison below, such terms affect contact and vacuum
normalization data; the separated-point operator map is fixed after a choice
of renormalized operator basis.

\section{Operators Under the Cylinder Map}

For a scalar primary \(\mathcal O(x)\) of dimension \(\Delta\), define the
cylinder operator
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}(\tau,n)
  =
  \ee^{\Delta\tau}\mathcal O(\ee^\tau n).
  \label{eq:scalar-cylinder-operator-map}
\end{equation}
This is the ordinary Weyl transformation of a scalar primary under
\(\dd s^2\mapsto r^{-2}\dd s^2\).  Operators with spin are transformed using
the same Weyl factor together with the orthonormal-frame rotation induced by
the coordinate change \(x^\mu=r n^\mu\).

For a general renormalized local operator basis, the map can mix operators
with the same quantum numbers when local counterterms are changed.  The
statement used in radial quantization is that, after fixing this basis, the
correlation functions on the two Weyl-related backgrounds agree:
\begin{equation}
  \left\langle
    \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
  \right\rangle_{\R^D}
  =
  \left\langle
    \widetilde{\mathcal O}_1(\tau_1,n_1)\cdots
    \widetilde{\mathcal O}_n(\tau_n,n_n)
  \right\rangle_{\R\times S^{D-1}},
  \label{eq:cylinder-flat-correlator-equivalence}
\end{equation}
for separated points \(x_i=\ee^{\tau_i}n_i\), up to the specified local
curvature and contact terms.

\section{States from Local Operators}

Let \(\mathcal O(0)\) be a local operator inserted at the origin.  In the
Euclidean path integral on a ball of radius \(R\), the insertion defines a
state on the boundary sphere \(S_R^{D-1}\):
\[
  \ket{\mathcal O;R}
  =
  \int_{\Phi|_{S_R}=\varphi}
  [D\Phi]_{B_R}\,
  \ee^{-S[\Phi]}\,
  \mathcal O(0).
\]
The right side is a wavefunctional of the boundary field configuration
\(\varphi\).  After the Weyl map to the cylinder, changing \(R\) translates
the state in \(\tau\).  The state associated with \(\mathcal O\) is denoted
\[
  \ket{\mathcal O}.
\]
Conversely, a state on \(S^{D-1}\) evolved to \(\tau=-\infty\) determines a
local insertion at the origin when the limit exists within the local operator
space.  This gives the state-operator map
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal V_{\mathrm{loc}}
  \longleftrightarrow
  \mathcal H_{S^{D-1}},
  \qquad
  \mathcal O\longmapsto\ket{\mathcal O}.
  \label{eq:state-operator-map}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, for insertions ordered by cylinder time
\(\tau_1>\tau_2>\cdots>\tau_n\),
\begin{equation}
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O(0)\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
  \right\rangle_{\R^D}
  =
  \bra{\Omega}
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_1(\tau_1,n_1)\cdots
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_n(\tau_n,n_n)
  \ket{\mathcal O}_{\rm cyl}.
  \label{eq:state-operator-correlation-formula}
\end{equation}
The state \(\ket{\Omega}\) is the ground state on \(S^{D-1}\).

The vacuum state corresponds to the identity operator:
\[
  \mathbf 1\longleftrightarrow\ket{\Omega}.
\]
Products of local operators inserted at the origin are defined by the
short-distance operator product expansion and correspond to the corresponding
states after radial ordering.

\section{Dilatation as the Cylinder Hamiltonian}

The dilatation vector field on \(\R^D\) is
\[
  x^\mu\partial_\mu=r\partial_r=\partial_\tau.
\]
Therefore the dilatation charge maps to the Hamiltonian generating
translations in cylinder time.  Let \(\widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}\) be defined
by Euclidean evolution
\[
  \ket{\psi(\tau+a)}
  =
  \ee^{-a\widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}}\ket{\psi(\tau)} .
\]
In radial-quantization conventions the factors of \(\ii\) in the Lorentzian
Hermitian conformal charges are absorbed, and
\begin{equation}
  \widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}=\widehat D_{\rm rad}.
  \label{eq:dilatation-cylinder-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
The eigenvalue of \(\widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}\) is the scaling dimension.

If \(\mathcal O\) has scaling dimension \(\Delta\), then
\[
  \widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}\ket{\mathcal O}
  =
  \Delta\ket{\mathcal O}.
\]
Thus the spectrum of scaling dimensions in flat space is the energy spectrum
of the theory on the spatial sphere.  Unitarity implies \(\Delta\ge0\) for
states above the vacuum in a unitary CFT.

\section{Primary and Descendant States}

A local operator at the origin is called a conformal primary if the associated
state is annihilated by all special conformal generators:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat K_\mu\ket{\mathcal O}=0 .
  \label{eq:primary-state-condition}
\end{equation}
If \(\mathcal O_\alpha\) transforms in a finite-dimensional spin
representation with Hermitian matrices \((S_{\mu\nu})_\alpha{}^\beta\), so
that a finite rotation near the identity is represented by
\(\exp(-\frac{\ii}{2}\omega^{\mu\nu}S_{\mu\nu})\), then a primary state obeys
\[
  \widehat D_{\rm rad}\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha}
  =
  \Delta\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha},
  \qquad
  \widehat J_{\mu\nu}\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha}
  =
  -(S_{\mu\nu})_\alpha{}^\beta\ket{\mathcal O_\beta},
  \qquad
  \widehat K_\mu\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha}=0 .
\]
Translations generate descendants:
\[
  \widehat P_{\mu_1}\cdots\widehat P_{\mu_n}\ket{\mathcal O}
  \longleftrightarrow
  \partial_{\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_n}\mathcal O(0).
\]
The commutator
\[
  [\widehat D_{\rm rad},\widehat P_\mu]=\widehat P_\mu
\]
shows that each derivative raises the scaling dimension by one.

\section{Reflection Positivity and Conjugation}

Radial quantization gives a natural conjugation.  In Euclidean flat space,
reflection through the unit sphere is inversion:
\[
  x^\mu\mapsto \frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
\]
On the cylinder this is time reflection \(\tau\mapsto-\tau\).  For a scalar
operator of dimension \(\Delta\), the conjugate insertion at infinity is
\[
  \mathcal O^\dagger(\infty)
  =
  \lim_{r\to\infty}r^{2\Delta}\mathcal O(r n)
\]
when the limit is independent of \(n\).  The two-point function becomes the
inner product
\[
  \braket{\mathcal O}{\mathcal O}
  =
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O^\dagger(\infty)\mathcal O(0)
  \right\rangle .
\]
For unitary theories this inner product is positive semidefinite and becomes
positive definite after null states are quotiented.  This positivity is the
source of unitarity bounds, developed after primary transformation laws and
two-point functions have been fixed.

\section{Adjoints of the Conformal Generators}

In radial quantization, Hermitian conjugation is reflection through the unit
sphere followed by the internal adjunction of the operator.  This fixes the
adjoints of the conformal generators.  Dilatations are translations in
cylinder time, so
\[
  \widehat D^\dagger=\widehat D
\]
in the cylinder Hilbert-space convention.  Rotations are unitary symmetries
of the spatial sphere:
\[
  \widehat J_{\mu\nu}^\dagger=\widehat J_{\mu\nu}.
\]
Translations and special conformal transformations are exchanged by inversion,
hence
\begin{equation}
  \widehat P_\mu^\dagger=\widehat K_\mu,
  \qquad
  \widehat K_\mu^\dagger=\widehat P_\mu .
  \label{eq:radial-adjoint-p-k}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:radial-adjoint-p-k} is the algebraic input behind
unitarity bounds.  If \(\ket{\mathcal O}\) is a primary state, then the norm
of a first descendant is
\[
  \|\widehat P_\mu\ket{\mathcal O}\|^2
  =
  \bra{\mathcal O}\widehat K_\mu\widehat P_\mu\ket{\mathcal O},
\]
with summation over \(\mu\) when a rotational scalar norm is taken.  Commuting
\(\widehat K_\mu\) through \(\widehat P_\nu\) and using
\(\widehat K_\mu\ket{\mathcal O}=0\) expresses the result in terms of
\(\Delta\) and the spin representation of the primary.  Positivity of all
descendant norms is therefore a representation-theoretic constraint on
allowed primary data.

\section{The Free Scalar Check}

Let \(\phi\) be a free massless scalar primary with
\[
  \Delta_\phi=\frac{D-2}{2}.
\]
The flat action is equivalent, by the Weyl transformation already described
in Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-iii-stress-tensor-weyl}, to the
conformally coupled action on the cylinder.  If
\[
  \widetilde\phi(\tau,n)=\ee^{\Delta_\phi\tau}\phi(\ee^\tau n),
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm cyl}[\widetilde\phi]
  =
  \frac12\int_{\R\times S^{D-1}}\dd\tau\,\dd\Omega_{D-1}
  \left[
    (\partial_\tau\widetilde\phi)^2
    +
    \nabla_{S^{D-1}}\widetilde\phi\cdot
    \nabla_{S^{D-1}}\widetilde\phi
    +
    \xi_\ast R_{\rm cyl}\widetilde\phi^2
  \right].
  \label{eq:free-scalar-cylinder-action}
\end{equation}
Here \(\xi_\ast=(D-2)/(4(D-1))\).  The cylinder metric is the product metric on
\(\mathbb R_\tau\times S^{D-1}\).  The \(\mathbb R_\tau\) factor has zero scalar
curvature, and the unit sphere \(S^{D-1}\) has scalar curvature
\((D-1)(D-2)\); hence the scalar curvature of the unit cylinder is
\[
  R_{\rm cyl}=(D-1)(D-2).
\]
The conformal mass term is
\[
  m_{\mathrm{cyl}}^2=\xi_\ast R_{\rm cyl}=\frac{(D-2)^2}{4}.
\]
For a one-particle mode on \(S^{D-1}\) with angular momentum \(\ell\),
\[
  -\nabla^2_{S^{D-1}}Y_{\ell}=\ell(\ell+D-2)Y_{\ell}.
\]
The cylinder energy is
\[
  E_\ell
  =
  \sqrt{\ell(\ell+D-2)+\frac{(D-2)^2}{4}}
  =
  \ell+\frac{D-2}{2}.
\]
The \(\ell=0\) mode corresponds to the primary field \(\phi\), whose scaling
dimension is
\[
  \Delta_\phi=\frac{D-2}{2}.
\]
Modes with \(\ell>0\) correspond to derivative descendants of \(\phi\).  This
explicit spectrum realizes the state-operator correspondence in a theory where
all local operators can be built from normal-ordered products of \(\phi\) and
its derivatives, modulo the equation of motion.

\section{The Operator Partition Function of the Free Scalar}

At a fixed point, it is often useful to count linearly independent local
operators by their scaling dimension.  Let
\[
  Z_{\rm op}(q)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal V_{\rm loc}}q^{\widehat D_{\rm rad}}
\]
be the formal character of the local-operator space.  In the free scalar
theory, the independent single-letter operators are the symmetric traceless
derivatives
\[
  \partial_{(\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_\ell)}\phi
  \quad\text{with traces removed},
  \qquad
  \ell=0,1,2,\ldots .
\]
The trace removal is the equation of motion \(\partial^2\phi=0\) and all of
its derivatives.  Counting all symmetric derivatives and subtracting the
traces gives the single-letter character
\begin{equation}
  f_\phi(q)
  =
  q^{\frac{D-2}{2}}
  \frac{1-q^2}{(1-q)^D}.
  \label{eq:free-scalar-letter-partition}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\[
  f_\phi(q)
  =
  q^{\frac{D-2}{2}}
  \sum_{\ell=0}^\infty d_{\ell,D}q^\ell,
  \qquad
  d_{\ell,D}
  =
  \binom{D+\ell-1}{\ell}
  -
  \binom{D+\ell-3}{\ell-2},
\]
with the second binomial interpreted as zero for \(\ell<2\).  These
\(d_{\ell,D}\) are the dimensions of scalar spherical harmonics of degree
\(\ell\) on \(S^{D-1}\).

Normal-ordered products of the letters form the bosonic symmetric algebra of
single-particle letters.  If
\[
  f_\phi(q)=\sum_\Delta a_\Delta q^\Delta,
\]
then the free scalar operator partition function is
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\rm op}^{\phi}(q)
  =
  \prod_\Delta
  \frac1{(1-q^\Delta)^{a_\Delta}}
  =
  \exp\left(
    \sum_{m=1}^{\infty}\frac1m f_\phi(q^m)
  \right).
  \label{eq:free-scalar-operator-partition}
\end{equation}
For \(D=3\), \(d_{\ell,3}=2\ell+1\), and therefore
\[
  f_\phi(q)
  =
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}(2\ell+1)q^{\ell+\frac12}
  =
  q^{1/2}\frac{1-q^2}{(1-q)^3}.
\]
This is the same counting obtained from the one-particle spectrum of
\(\widehat H_{\rm cyl}=\sqrt{-\nabla_{S^2}^2+1/4}\) on \(S^2\).
