\section{Local Correlators as the Primary Data}

Let \(\widehat{\mathcal O}_I(x)\) denote a Lorentzian local operator-valued
distribution on \(\R^{1,D-1}\).  The label \(I\) includes all internal and
spin indices.  Local commutativity means that for spacelike separated points
\[
  [\widehat{\mathcal O}_I(x),\widehat{\mathcal O}_J(y)]_\pm=0,
  \qquad
  (x-y)^2>0,
\]
where the graded commutator is used when fermionic operators are present.
For a vacuum vector \(\ket{\vac}\), the unordered Lorentzian correlation
functions are the Wightman distributions
\[
  W_{I_1\cdots I_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
  =
  \bra{\vac}
  \widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_1}(x_1)\cdots
  \widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_n}(x_n)
  \ket{\vac}.
\]
Time-ordered Green functions are
\[
  G^T_{I_1\cdots I_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
  =
  \bra{\vac}
  T\{\widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_1}(x_1)\cdots
  \widehat{\mathcal O}_{I_n}(x_n)\}
  \ket{\vac}.
\]
The corresponding Euclidean Schwinger functions are denoted by
\[
  S_{I_1\cdots I_n}(x^E_1,\ldots,x^E_n)
  =
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O_{I_1}(x^E_1)\cdots\mathcal O_{I_n}(x^E_n)
  \right\rangle_E .
\]
\begin{hypothesis}[Radial reconstruction data]
\label{hyp:radial-reconstruction-data}
The state--operator construction in this chapter is used for a Euclidean
Schwinger hierarchy \(S_{I_1\cdots I_n}\) satisfying the following conditions.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \(S_n\) are distributional correlation functions of renormalized
  local operators on \(\R^D\), with Euclidean covariance, graded permutation
  symmetry, and stated contact-term prescriptions at coincident points.
  \item Let \(\vartheta(x)=x/|x|^2\) be reflection through the unit sphere.  The
  operator basis carries an antilinear reflection map \(\Theta_{\rm rad}\) that
  acts on a smeared insertion supported in the unit ball by applying
  \(\vartheta\), the appropriate Weyl factor, and the spin representation of
  the reflected orthonormal frame.  For every finite linear combination \(F\)
  of such inside-supported insertions,
  \[
    \langle F,F\rangle_{\rm rad}
    =
    \left\langle \Theta_{\rm rad}F\,F\right\rangle_E
    \geq0 .
  \]
  The radial Hilbert space is the completion of this positive semidefinite
  form after quotienting by its null space.
  \item The one-parameter group generated by dilatations acts on this Hilbert
  space by a nonnegative self-adjoint operator \(D_{\rm rad}\).  Its spectrum
  on the finite-energy subspace is discrete, and for every \(E<\infty\) the
  spectral subspace with \(D_{\rm rad}\le E\) is finite-dimensional.  In
  particular every eigenspace has finite multiplicity, and the eigenvalue
  \(0\) subspace is spanned by the vacuum vector.  Thus the conformally
  invariant vacuum in the radial Hilbert space is unique.
  \item For each local operator \(\mathcal O_I\) used below, the radial limit of
  the insertion at the origin defines a finite-energy vector.  Descendant
  vectors are obtained by applying the translation generators, equivalently by
  derivatives of the insertion.  The local operator basis is radially complete:
  after quotienting by the null radial two-point pairing, origin insertions of
  local operators and their descendants span precisely the finite-energy
  subspace
  \[
    \mathcal H_{\rm fin}
    =
    \bigoplus_{\lambda<\infty}\ker(D_{\rm rad}-\lambda).
  \]
  Equivalently, every finite-energy eigenvector has a representative as a local
  scaling field at the origin, unique modulo null operators.
  \item The Euclidean correlators are related to Lorentzian Wightman
  distributions by the standard tube-domain analytic continuation, with the
  Lorentzian distributions satisfying the spectrum condition and local
  commutativity.  The flat-space vacuum correlators obey cluster decomposition:
  for two finite products of separated local insertions,
  \[
    A(x_1,\ldots,x_m)=\mathcal O_{I_1}(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_{I_m}(x_m),
    \qquad
    B(y_1,\ldots,y_n)=\mathcal O_{J_1}(y_1)\cdots\mathcal O_{J_n}(y_n),
  \]
  and for a Euclidean vector \(a\) such that no coincident-point singularity is
  crossed along the translated configuration,
  \[
    \lim_{\lambda\to+\infty}
    \left\langle
      A(x_1+\lambda a,\ldots,x_m+\lambda a)\,
      B(y_1,\ldots,y_n)
    \right\rangle_E
    =
    \langle A(x_1,\ldots,x_m)\rangle_E\,
    \langle B(y_1,\ldots,y_n)\rangle_E .
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{hypothesis}

Under Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:radial-reconstruction-data}, radial reflection
positivity gives the inner product, \(D_{\rm rad}\) gives the cylinder
Hamiltonian, and local insertions at the origin give the complete
finite-energy state space.

These three versions are related by analytic continuation whenever the
Lorentzian theory satisfies the spectral and locality hypotheses needed for
the tube-domain construction.  For an ordered Wightman product, continue
\[
  x^0_k=-\ii x^D_{E,k}
\]
through a domain in which the imaginary parts remain ordered.  With the
convention \(x^0=-\ii\tau_E\), the ordering
\[
  \operatorname{Im}x^0_1<\operatorname{Im}x^0_2<\cdots<
  \operatorname{Im}x^0_n
\]
corresponds to
\[
  \tau_{E,1}>\tau_{E,2}>\cdots>\tau_{E,n}.
\]
Here \(\tau_E\) denotes Euclidean time inherited from Lorentzian analytic
continuation.  It is distinct from the cylinder coordinate \(\tau=\log r\)
introduced below.  The Euclidean correlator is the boundary value obtained
after this rotation.
Time-ordered Green functions arise from a contour whose real-time projection
places the insertions in chronological order.

When a path-integral representation is available, this analytic continuation
can be represented by a contour \(C\) in complex time:
\[
  S_C[\Phi]
  =
  \int_C \dd x^0\int\dd^{D-1}\mathbf x\,
  \mathcal L(\Phi,\partial\Phi),
  \qquad
  \left\langle\prod_i\mathcal O_i(x_i)\right\rangle_C
  =
  \int[D\Phi]\,\ee^{\ii S_C[\Phi]}\prod_i\mathcal O_i(x_i).
\]
The contour formula is a representation of the same distributional analytic
continuation; it is not an additional axiom of the conformal theory.

\begin{remark}[Observables at a conformal fixed point]
At a conformal fixed point, the data used in this volume are local operators,
their correlation functions, and the Hilbert-space structure obtained from
radial quantization.  A separate scattering theory requires asymptotic
particle states.  Many conformal theories have no mass gap and no isolated
massive particle sectors, so their intrinsic observable data are organized by
local correlations rather than an \(S\)-matrix.  Four-dimensional Yang--Mills
theory illustrates a different situation: the classical action has no
dimensionful coupling, but quantum renormalization produces
\(\Lambda_{\rm QCD}\) and a nonzero trace anomaly, so the quantum theory is
not a conformal field theory.
\end{remark}

\section{The Weyl Map to the Cylinder}

Let \(x^\mu\) be Euclidean coordinates on \(\R^D\), and write
\[
  r=|x|,\qquad
  x^\mu=r n^\mu,\qquad
  n^\mu n_\mu=1.
\]
The flat metric is
\[
  \dd s^2
  =
  \dd r^2+r^2\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2,
\]
where \(\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2\) is the unit metric on \(S^{D-1}\).  Define
\[
  \tau=\log r .
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd s^2}{r^2}
  =
  \dd\tau^2+\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2.
  \label{eq:radial-cylinder-metric}
\end{equation}
Thus the punctured space \(\R^D\setminus\{0\}\) is Weyl equivalent to the
Euclidean cylinder
\[
  \R_\tau\times S^{D-1}.
\]
The origin corresponds to \(\tau=-\infty\), and infinity corresponds to
\(\tau=+\infty\).

The use of this Weyl map assumes that the flat-space conformal theory has
been extended to background metrics with a specified Weyl transformation law
for renormalized local operators.  In even dimensions a Weyl anomaly may add
local curvature terms to the generating functional.  On the punctured flat
space and cylinder comparison below, such terms affect contact and vacuum
normalization data; the separated-point operator map is fixed after a choice
of renormalized operator basis.

\section{Operators Under the Cylinder Map}

For a scalar primary \(\mathcal O(x)\) of dimension \(\Delta\), define the
cylinder operator
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}(\tau,n)
  =
  \ee^{\Delta\tau}\mathcal O(\ee^\tau n).
  \label{eq:scalar-cylinder-operator-map}
\end{equation}
This is the ordinary Weyl transformation of a scalar primary under
\(\dd s^2\mapsto r^{-2}\dd s^2\).

The spin factor is the local orthonormal-frame rotation induced by the same
map.  Work on a coordinate patch \(U\subset S^{D-1}\) with a smooth oriented
orthonormal tangent frame
\[
  E_A^\mu(n),
  \qquad
  A=1,\ldots,D-1,
  \qquad
  n_\mu E_A^\mu(n)=0,
  \qquad
  E_A^\mu(n)E_{B\mu}(n)=\delta_{AB}.
\]
The cylinder metric is \(\widehat g=r^{-2}\delta_{\mu\nu}\dd x^\mu\dd x^\nu\).
An orthonormal coframe for \(\widehat g\), pulled back to the punctured flat
space, is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat e^{\hat\tau}{}_\mu(x)=r^{-1}n_\mu,
  \qquad
  \widehat e^{\hat A}{}_\mu(x)=r^{-1}E_A{}_\mu(n).
  \label{eq:cylinder-local-orthonormal-coframe}
\end{equation}
Removing the common Weyl scale gives the \(SO(D)\) rotation from the flat
Cartesian orthonormal frame to the cylinder orthonormal frame,
\begin{equation}
  R^{\hat a}{}_\mu(n)
  :=
  r\,\widehat e^{\hat a}{}_\mu(x),
  \qquad
  R^{\hat\tau}{}_\mu(n)=n_\mu,
  \qquad
  R^{\hat A}{}_\mu(n)=E_A{}_\mu(n),
  \label{eq:cylinder-frame-rotation}
\end{equation}
so \(R^{\hat a}{}_\mu R^{\hat b\mu}=\delta^{\hat a\hat b}\).  On overlaps of
two patches the frames \(E_A\) differ by an \(SO(D-1)\) rotation; the formulas
below transform by the corresponding tensor or spin representation.

For a covariant vector primary \(V_\mu(x)\) of dimension \(\Delta\), the
cylinder orthonormal-frame components are
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde V_{\hat a}(\tau,n)
  =
  \ee^{\Delta\tau}
  R_{\hat a}{}^\mu(n)\,
  V_\mu(\ee^\tau n).
  \label{eq:vector-cylinder-operator-map}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\[
  V_\mu(\ee^\tau n)
  =
  \ee^{-\Delta\tau}
  R_\mu{}^{\hat a}(n)\,
  \widetilde V_{\hat a}(\tau,n).
\]
The associated spin-one state map is obtained by inserting the flat operator at
the origin:
\begin{equation}
  \ket{V_\mu}:=V_\mu(0)\ket{\vac},
  \qquad
  \lim_{\tau\to-\infty}
  \ee^{-\Delta\tau}\,
  \widetilde V_{\hat a}(\tau,n)\ket{\vac}
  =
  R_{\hat a}{}^\mu(n)\ket{V_\mu}.
  \label{eq:vector-primary-state-map}
\end{equation}
The direction dependence in the last expression is exactly the rotation between
the local cylinder frame at \(n\) and the fixed flat spin index at the origin.
For a primary in a representation \(\rho\) of \(SO(D)\), replace
\(R_{\hat a}{}^\mu\) in \eqref{eq:vector-cylinder-operator-map} by
\(\rho(R(n))\).

For a general renormalized local operator basis, the map can mix operators
with the same quantum numbers when local counterterms are changed.  The
statement used in radial quantization is that, after fixing this basis, the
correlation functions on the two Weyl-related backgrounds agree:
\begin{equation}
  \left\langle
    \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
  \right\rangle_{\R^D}
  =
  \left\langle
    \widetilde{\mathcal O}_1(\tau_1,n_1)\cdots
    \widetilde{\mathcal O}_n(\tau_n,n_n)
  \right\rangle_{\R\times S^{D-1}},
  \label{eq:cylinder-flat-correlator-equivalence}
\end{equation}
for separated points \(x_i=\ee^{\tau_i}n_i\), up to the specified local
curvature and contact terms.

\section{States from Local Operators}

Let \(\mathcal O(0)\) be a local operator inserted at the origin.  In the
Euclidean path integral on a ball of radius \(R\), the insertion defines a
state on the boundary sphere \(S_R^{D-1}\):
\[
  \ket{\mathcal O;R}
  =
  \int_{\Phi|_{S_R}=\varphi}
  [D\Phi]_{B_R}\,
  \ee^{-S[\Phi]}\,
  \mathcal O(0).
\]
The right side is a wavefunctional of the boundary field configuration
\(\varphi\).  After the Weyl map to the cylinder, changing \(R\) translates
the state in \(\tau\).  The state associated with \(\mathcal O\) is denoted
\[
  \ket{\mathcal O}.
\]

\begin{construction}[cylinder sewing and Heisenberg operators]
\label{constr:cylinder-sewing-heisenberg}
Let
\[
  \Sigma_\tau=\{\tau\}\times S^{D-1}
\]
be a constant-cylinder-time slice, and write
\[
  H_{\rm cyl}=D_{\rm rad}
\]
for the nonnegative self-adjoint generator of translations in \(\tau\).
For a cylinder local operator \(X(n)\) placed on the slice \(\Sigma_0\), its
Euclidean Heisenberg translate is the operator on the common finite-energy
domain
\begin{equation}
  X(\tau,n)=
  \ee^{\tau H_{\rm cyl}}X(0,n)\ee^{-\tau H_{\rm cyl}} .
  \label{eq:cylinder-heisenberg-operator}
\end{equation}
For separated insertions
\[
  \tau_+>\tau_1>\tau_2>\cdots>\tau_n>\tau_- ,
\]
and boundary states \(v_-\) on \(\Sigma_{\tau_-}\) and \(v_+\) on
\(\Sigma_{\tau_+}\), the cylinder amplitude obtained by cutting along all
intermediate slices is
\begin{align}
  &\mathcal A_{\tau_+,\tau_-}
  (v_+;X_1,\ldots,X_n;v_-)
  \nonumber\\
  &\quad =
  \left\langle
    v_+,
    \ee^{-(\tau_+-\tau_1)H_{\rm cyl}}
    X_1(0,n_1)
    \ee^{-(\tau_1-\tau_2)H_{\rm cyl}}
    \cdots
    X_n(0,n_n)
    \ee^{-(\tau_n-\tau_-)H_{\rm cyl}}
    v_-
  \right\rangle .
  \label{eq:cylinder-slab-sewing}
\end{align}
Equation \eqref{eq:cylinder-slab-sewing} is the Hilbert-space form of
Euclidean sewing.  In a functional-integral presentation it is obtained by
integrating over the boundary field data on each cut \(\Sigma_{\tau_j}\); in
the radial reconstruction presentation it is the composition law for the
positive-time semigroup on the null quotient.  The two descriptions give the
same sesquilinear form because both are defined by the same Schwinger
correlation hierarchy.

Taking \(v_+=\vac\) and using
\[
  \bra{\vac}\ee^{-sH_{\rm cyl}}=\bra{\vac},
  \qquad s\ge0,
\]
turns \eqref{eq:cylinder-slab-sewing} into the Euclidean Heisenberg matrix
element
\begin{equation}
  \bra{\vac}
  X_1(\tau_1,n_1)\cdots X_n(\tau_n,n_n)
  \ket{v}
  =
  \mathcal A_{\tau_+,\tau_-}
  \bigl(\vac;X_1,\ldots,X_n;\ee^{-\tau_-H_{\rm cyl}}v\bigr),
  \label{eq:cylinder-sewing-heisenberg-matrix-element}
\end{equation}
for every finite-energy vector \(v\) and every lower cut
\(\tau_-<\tau_n\).  The right side is independent of the auxiliary cuts after
the boundary state is transported as displayed.  Thus the cylinder correlation
function with a state prepared in the far past is computed by an ordered
product of Heisenberg cylinder operators.
\end{construction}

\begin{theorem}[State--operator correspondence]
\label{thm:state-operator-correspondence}
Assume the radial reconstruction data of
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:radial-reconstruction-data}.  Let
\(\mathcal V_{\rm loc}\) be the complex vector space of renormalized local
operators at the origin, including descendants, modulo operators whose radial
two-point pairing with every local operator vanishes.  Let
\(\mathcal H_{\rm fin}\subset\mathcal H_{S^{D-1}}\) be the finite-energy
subspace of the radial Hilbert space.  Then insertion at the origin defines an
isometric linear isomorphism
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal V_{\mathrm{loc}}
  \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}
  \mathcal H_{\rm fin},
  \qquad
  \mathcal O\longmapsto\ket{\mathcal O}.
  \label{eq:state-operator-map}
\end{equation}
After completing \(\mathcal V_{\rm loc}\) in the radial inner product, the map
extends uniquely to a Hilbert-space isomorphism onto
\(\mathcal H_{S^{D-1}}\).  Equivalently, for insertions ordered by cylinder time
\(\tau_1>\tau_2>\cdots>\tau_n\),
\begin{equation}
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O(0)\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
  \right\rangle_{\R^D}
  =
  \bra{\vac}
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_1(\tau_1,n_1)\cdots
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_n(\tau_n,n_n)
  \ket{\mathcal O}_{\rm cyl}.
  \label{eq:state-operator-correlation-formula}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Radial reflection positivity supplies the positive semidefinite inner product
on local insertions inside a sphere.  Quotienting its null space makes the map
\(\mathcal O\mapsto\ket{\mathcal O}\) injective, and the path-integral
construction of the wavefunctional on \(S_R^{D-1}\) identifies its norm with
the radial two-point pairing; hence the map is isometric.  Dilatation
covariance transports the state from radius \(R\) to the cylinder and places
it in the finite-energy subspace by
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:radial-reconstruction-data}.  The radial-completeness
clause of the same hypothesis gives surjectivity onto
\(\mathcal H_{\rm fin}\).  Since \(D_{\rm rad}\) is nonnegative
self-adjoint, the spectral projections \(P_{[0,E]}(D_{\rm rad})\) converge
strongly to the identity as \(E\to\infty\).  Hence
\(\mathcal H_{\rm fin}\) is dense in \(\mathcal H_{S^{D-1}}\), and the
isometry extends by completion to the Hilbert-space isomorphism stated in the
theorem.

It remains to derive the correlation formula.  Put
\[
  X_j(0,n_j)=\widetilde{\mathcal O}_j(0,n_j),
  \qquad
  X_j(\tau_j,n_j)=
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_j(\tau_j,n_j),
\]
where the second equality is the Heisenberg translation
\eqref{eq:cylinder-heisenberg-operator}.  Choose cuts
\(\tau_+>\tau_1\) and \(\tau_-<\tau_n\).  By
\eqref{eq:cylinder-sewing-heisenberg-matrix-element} with
\(v=\ket{\mathcal O}_{\rm cyl}\),
\[
  \bra{\vac}
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_1(\tau_1,n_1)\cdots
  \widetilde{\mathcal O}_n(\tau_n,n_n)
  \ket{\mathcal O}_{\rm cyl}
\]
is the cylinder Schwinger functional on the punctured cylinder with:
\begin{itemize}
\item the incoming boundary state prepared by the local insertion
  \(\mathcal O(0)\), transported to the lower cut;
\item insertions \(X_n,\ldots,X_1\) placed in the slabs ordered by increasing
  cylinder time; and
\item the outgoing boundary projected to the vacuum by the limit
  \(\tau_+\to+\infty\).
\end{itemize}
The vacuum projection exists because \(H_{\rm cyl}\ge0\) and the zero-energy
subspace is \(\mathbb C\vac\).  The sewing identity is unchanged if the
auxiliary cuts are moved without crossing an insertion.

Finally apply the Weyl map \(x_j=\ee^{\tau_j}n_j\).  The operator definition
\eqref{eq:scalar-cylinder-operator-map}, with the corresponding spin-frame
factor for spinning operators, identifies the cylinder Schwinger functional
at separated points with the flat-space radially ordered correlator.  Since
\(\tau_1>\cdots>\tau_n\), radial ordering places
\(\mathcal O_1(x_1),\ldots,\mathcal O_n(x_n)\) outside the origin insertion in
the displayed order.  This gives
\eqref{eq:state-operator-correlation-formula}.

\end{proof}

The state \(\ket{\vac}\) is the ground state on \(S^{D-1}\).

The vacuum state corresponds to the identity operator:
\[
  \mathbf 1\longleftrightarrow\ket{\vac}.
\]
Products of local operators inserted at the origin are defined by the
short-distance operator product expansion and correspond to the corresponding
states after radial ordering.

\section{Dilatation as the Cylinder Hamiltonian}

The dilatation vector field on \(\R^D\) is
\[
  x^\mu\partial_\mu=r\partial_r=\partial_\tau.
\]
Therefore the dilatation charge maps to the Hamiltonian generating
translations in cylinder time.  Let \(\widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}\) be defined
by Euclidean evolution
\[
  \ket{\psi(\tau+a)}
  =
  \ee^{-a\widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}}\ket{\psi(\tau)} .
\]
In radial-quantization conventions the factors of \(\ii\) in the Lorentzian
Hermitian conformal charges are absorbed, and
\begin{equation}
  \widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}=\widehat D_{\rm rad}.
  \label{eq:dilatation-cylinder-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
The eigenvalue of \(\widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}\) is the scaling dimension.

The relation between the Hermitian Lorentzian convention and the radial
Euclidean convention is as follows.  Let \(X\) be a real conformal generator and
let \(\widehat X_{\rm L}\) be the corresponding Hermitian Lorentzian charge, so
that a real Lorentzian group parameter \(a\) acts by
\[
  U_{\rm L}(\exp aX)=\exp(-\ii a\widehat X_{\rm L}).
\]
After analytic continuation of the group parameter, \(a=-\ii a_E\), the same
complexified group element acts in radial Euclidean quantization by
\[
  \exp(-a_E\widehat X_{\rm rad})
\]
whenever the continuation maps the relevant surface to another Euclidean
surface.  For dilatations this gives
\[
  \widehat D_{\rm rad}=\widehat H_{\rm cyl},
  \qquad
  \ee^{-a\widehat D_{\rm rad}}\ket{\mathcal O}
  =
  \ee^{-a\Delta}\ket{\mathcal O}.
\]
For commutator formulas, the radial chapters write the complexified conformal
algebra without the explicit Lorentzian factor of \(\ii\).  Thus the
Hermitian-convention relation
\[
  [\widehat D_{\rm L},\widehat P^{\rm L}_\mu]=\ii\widehat P^{\rm L}_\mu
\]
is recorded in radial notation as
\[
  [\widehat D_{\rm rad},\widehat P_\mu]=\widehat P_\mu.
\]
This is a convention for the complexified Lie-algebra action on local
operators; positivity statements use the Hilbert-space adjoint
\(\widehat P_\mu^\dagger=\widehat K_\mu\) in the radial Hilbert space.

If \(\mathcal O\) has scaling dimension \(\Delta\), then
\[
  \widehat H_{\mathrm{cyl}}\ket{\mathcal O}
  =
  \Delta\ket{\mathcal O}.
\]
Thus the spectrum of scaling dimensions in flat space is the energy spectrum
of the theory on the spatial sphere.  Unitarity implies \(\Delta\ge0\) for
states above the vacuum in a unitary CFT.

\section{Primary and Descendant States}

A local operator at the origin is called a conformal primary if the associated
state is annihilated by all special conformal generators:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat K_\mu\ket{\mathcal O}=0 .
  \label{eq:primary-state-condition}
\end{equation}
If \(\mathcal O_\alpha\) transforms in a finite-dimensional spin
representation with Hermitian matrices \((S_{\mu\nu})_\alpha{}^\beta\), so
that a finite rotation near the identity is represented by
\(\exp(-\frac{\ii}{2}\omega^{\mu\nu}S_{\mu\nu})\), then a primary state obeys
\[
  \widehat D_{\rm rad}\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha}
  =
  \Delta\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha},
  \qquad
  \widehat J_{\mu\nu}\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha}
  =
  -(S_{\mu\nu})_\alpha{}^\beta\ket{\mathcal O_\beta},
  \qquad
  \widehat K_\mu\ket{\mathcal O_\alpha}=0 .
\]
Translations generate descendants:
\[
  \widehat P_{\mu_1}\cdots\widehat P_{\mu_n}\ket{\mathcal O}
  \longleftrightarrow
  \partial_{\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_n}\mathcal O(0).
\]
The commutator
\[
  [\widehat D_{\rm rad},\widehat P_\mu]=\widehat P_\mu
\]
shows that each derivative raises the scaling dimension by one.

\section{Reflection Positivity and Conjugation}

Radial quantization gives a natural conjugation.  In Euclidean flat space,
reflection through the unit sphere is inversion:
\[
  x^\mu\mapsto \frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
\]
On the cylinder this is time reflection \(\tau\mapsto-\tau\).  For a scalar
operator of dimension \(\Delta\), the conjugate insertion at infinity is
\[
  \mathcal O^\dagger(\infty)
  =
  \lim_{r\to\infty}r^{2\Delta}\mathcal O(r n)
\]
when the limit is independent of \(n\).  The two-point function becomes the
inner product
\[
  \braket{\mathcal O}{\mathcal O}
  =
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O^\dagger(\infty)\mathcal O(0)
  \right\rangle .
\]
For unitary theories this inner product is positive semidefinite and becomes
positive definite after null states are quotiented.  This positivity is the
source of unitarity bounds, developed after primary transformation laws and
two-point functions have been fixed.

\section{Adjoints of the Conformal Generators}

In radial quantization, Hermitian conjugation is reflection through the unit
sphere followed by the internal adjunction of the operator.  This fixes the
adjoints of the conformal generators.  Dilatations are translations in
cylinder time, so
\[
  \widehat D^\dagger=\widehat D
\]
in the cylinder Hilbert-space convention.  Rotations are unitary symmetries
of the spatial sphere:
\[
  \widehat J_{\mu\nu}^\dagger=\widehat J_{\mu\nu}.
\]
Translations and special conformal transformations are exchanged by inversion,
hence
\begin{equation}
  \widehat P_\mu^\dagger=\widehat K_\mu,
  \qquad
  \widehat K_\mu^\dagger=\widehat P_\mu .
  \label{eq:radial-adjoint-p-k}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:radial-adjoint-p-k} is the algebraic input behind
unitarity bounds.  If \(\ket{\mathcal O}\) is a primary state, then the norm
of a first descendant is
\[
  \|\widehat P_\mu\ket{\mathcal O}\|^2
  =
  \bra{\mathcal O}\widehat K_\mu\widehat P_\mu\ket{\mathcal O},
\]
with summation over \(\mu\) when a rotational scalar norm is taken.  Commuting
\(\widehat K_\mu\) through \(\widehat P_\nu\) and using
\(\widehat K_\mu\ket{\mathcal O}=0\) expresses the result in terms of
\(\Delta\) and the spin representation of the primary.  Positivity of all
descendant norms is therefore a representation-theoretic constraint on
allowed primary data.

\section{The Free Scalar Check}

Let \(\phi\) be a free massless scalar primary with
\[
  \Delta_\phi=\frac{D-2}{2}.
\]
The flat action is equivalent, by the Weyl transformation already described
in Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-iii-stress-tensor-weyl}, to the
conformally coupled action on the cylinder.  If
\[
  \widetilde\phi(\tau,n)=\ee^{\Delta_\phi\tau}\phi(\ee^\tau n),
\]
then
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm cyl}[\widetilde\phi]
  =
  \frac12\int_{\R\times S^{D-1}}\dd\tau\,\dd\Omega_{D-1}
  \left[
    (\partial_\tau\widetilde\phi)^2
    +
    \nabla_{S^{D-1}}\widetilde\phi\cdot
    \nabla_{S^{D-1}}\widetilde\phi
    +
    \xi_\ast R_{\rm cyl}\widetilde\phi^2
  \right].
  \label{eq:free-scalar-cylinder-action}
\end{equation}
Here \(\xi_\ast=(D-2)/(4(D-1))\).  The cylinder metric is
\[
  \dd s_{\rm cyl}^2=\dd\tau^2+\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2,
\]
where \(\dd\Omega_{D-1}^2=\gamma_{ab}\dd\theta^a\dd\theta^b\) is the unit
round metric on \(S^{D-1}\).  The Levi-Civita connection of this product
metric splits.  Thus
\[
  R_{\tau\tau}^{\rm cyl}=0,
  \qquad
  R_{ab}^{\rm cyl}=R_{ab}^{S^{D-1}}=(D-2)\gamma_{ab},
\]
and contraction with \(g_{\rm cyl}^{\tau\tau}=1\) and
\(g_{\rm cyl}^{ab}=\gamma^{ab}\) gives
\[
  R_{\rm cyl}
  =
  g_{\rm cyl}^{MN}R_{MN}^{\rm cyl}
  =
  \gamma^{ab}(D-2)\gamma_{ab}
  =
  (D-1)(D-2).
\]
The conformal mass term is
\[
  m_{\mathrm{cyl}}^2=\xi_\ast R_{\rm cyl}=\frac{(D-2)^2}{4}.
\]
For a one-particle mode on \(S^{D-1}\) with angular momentum \(\ell\),
\[
  -\nabla^2_{S^{D-1}}Y_{\ell}=\ell(\ell+D-2)Y_{\ell}.
\]
The cylinder energy is
\[
  E_\ell
  =
  \sqrt{\ell(\ell+D-2)+\frac{(D-2)^2}{4}}
  =
  \ell+\frac{D-2}{2}.
\]
The \(\ell=0\) mode corresponds to the primary field \(\phi\), whose scaling
dimension is
\[
  \Delta_\phi=\frac{D-2}{2}.
\]
Modes with \(\ell>0\) correspond to derivative descendants of \(\phi\).  This
explicit spectrum realizes the state-operator correspondence in a theory where
all local operators can be built from normal-ordered products of \(\phi\) and
its derivatives, modulo the equation of motion.

\section{The Operator Partition Function of the Free Scalar}

At a fixed point, it is often useful to count linearly independent local
operators by their scaling dimension.  Let
\[
  Z_{\rm op}(q)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal V_{\rm loc}}q^{\widehat D_{\rm rad}}
\]
be the formal character of the local-operator space.  In the free scalar
theory, the independent single-letter operators are the symmetric traceless
derivatives
\[
  \partial_{(\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_\ell)}\phi
  \quad\text{with traces removed},
  \qquad
  \ell=0,1,2,\ldots .
\]
The trace removal is the equation of motion \(\partial^2\phi=0\) and all of
its derivatives.  Counting all symmetric derivatives and subtracting the
traces gives the single-letter character
\begin{equation}
  f_\phi(q)
  =
  q^{\frac{D-2}{2}}
  \frac{1-q^2}{(1-q)^D}.
  \label{eq:free-scalar-letter-partition}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\[
  f_\phi(q)
  =
  q^{\frac{D-2}{2}}
  \sum_{\ell=0}^\infty d_{\ell,D}q^\ell,
  \qquad
  d_{\ell,D}
  =
  \binom{D+\ell-1}{\ell}
  -
  \binom{D+\ell-3}{\ell-2},
\]
with the second binomial interpreted as zero for \(\ell<2\).  These
\(d_{\ell,D}\) are the dimensions of scalar spherical harmonics of degree
\(\ell\) on \(S^{D-1}\).

Normal-ordered products of the letters form the bosonic symmetric algebra of
single-particle letters.  If
\[
  f_\phi(q)=\sum_\Delta a_\Delta q^\Delta,
\]
then the free scalar operator partition function is
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\rm op}^{\phi}(q)
  =
  \prod_\Delta
  \frac1{(1-q^\Delta)^{a_\Delta}}
  =
  \exp\left(
    \sum_{m=1}^{\infty}\frac1m f_\phi(q^m)
  \right).
  \label{eq:free-scalar-operator-partition}
\end{equation}
For \(D=3\), \(d_{\ell,3}=2\ell+1\), and therefore
\[
  f_\phi(q)
  =
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}(2\ell+1)q^{\ell+\frac12}
  =
  q^{1/2}\frac{1-q^2}{(1-q)^3}.
\]
This is the same counting obtained from the one-particle spectrum of
\(\widehat H_{\rm cyl}=\sqrt{-\nabla_{S^2}^2+1/4}\) on \(S^2\).

\section{High-Temperature Operator Entropy and the Cardy Limit}
\label{sec:cardy-operator-entropy}

For a CFT satisfying Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:radial-reconstruction-data}, assume
in addition that the spectral projection of \(D_{\rm rad}\) onto
\([0,\Delta]\) has finite trace for every finite \(\Delta\).  The cylinder
thermal trace is then
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\rm cyl}(\beta)
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_{S^{D-1}}}
  \ee^{-\beta D_{\rm rad}}
  =
  \int_{0}^{\infty}\ee^{-\beta E}\,dN(E),
  \qquad
  \beta>0,
  \label{eq:cylinder-thermal-trace}
\end{equation}
where
\[
  N(\Delta)
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_{S^{D-1}}}
  \mathbf 1_{[0,\Delta]}(D_{\rm rad}).
\]
If the spectrum is pure point with finite degeneracies, this integral is
\(\sum_\Delta d(\Delta)\ee^{-\beta\Delta}\), where
\(d(\Delta)=\dim\ker(D_{\rm rad}-\Delta)\).  The operator partition function
\(Z_{\rm op}(q)\) is this same trace with \(q=\ee^{-\beta}\) when the local
operator basis is complete and the trace converges.  The large-\(\Delta\)
density should therefore be understood as the integrated spectral counting
function \(N(\Delta)\), or as a smoothed density obtained from it, rather than
as an everywhere-defined ordinary function.

Assume that the high-temperature cylinder free energy has the leading form
\begin{equation}
  \log Z_{\rm cyl}(\beta)
  =
  A\,\beta^{-(D-1)}+o(\beta^{-(D-1)}),
  \qquad
  A>0 .
  \label{eq:cylinder-high-temp-free-energy}
\end{equation}
This hypothesis is a thermodynamic input about the CFT on
\(S^{D-1}\): in \(D>2\) the constant \(A\) is not fixed by the stress-tensor
two-point coefficient alone.  It is the finite-volume version of the flat
thermal pressure coefficient.  The precise positivity input is the following
Hardy--Littlewood Tauberian implication: if \(dN\) is a positive spectral
measure on \([0,\infty)\) and
\(\log\int_0^\infty\ee^{-\beta E}dN(E)=A\beta^{-\alpha}+o(\beta^{-\alpha})\)
with \(A,\alpha>0\), then
\[
  \log N(E)
  =
  (\alpha+1)A^{1/(\alpha+1)}
  \left(\frac{E}{\alpha}\right)^{\alpha/(\alpha+1)}
  +o(E^{\alpha/(\alpha+1)}).
\]
Applying this with \(\alpha=D-1\) gives the leading microcanonical entropy
\begin{equation}
  \log N(\Delta)
  =
  D\,A^{1/D}
  \left(\frac{\Delta}{D-1}\right)^{\frac{D-1}{D}}
  +o(\Delta^{\frac{D-1}{D}}).
  \label{eq:general-cft-entropy-dimension-asymptotic}
\end{equation}
The saddle calculation is explicit.  Extremize
\[
  \Phi(\beta)=\beta\Delta+A\beta^{-(D-1)}.
\]
The stationary point satisfies
\[
  \Delta=(D-1)A\beta_\ast^{-D},
  \qquad
  \beta_\ast=\left(\frac{(D-1)A}{\Delta}\right)^{1/D}.
\]
Substitution gives \eqref{eq:general-cft-entropy-dimension-asymptotic}.  Thus
the power \(\Delta^{(D-1)/D}\) is fixed by extensivity of high-temperature
thermal physics in \(D-1\) spatial dimensions, while the coefficient records
the specific CFT thermal free energy.

In \(D=2\) the torus supplies a stronger statement because modular invariance
relates the high-temperature trace to the low-temperature vacuum.  Let the
spatial circle have circumference \(2\pi\), and let
\[
  H_{\rm cyl}=L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c_L+c_R}{24},
  \qquad
  \Delta=h+\bar h,
  \qquad
  J=h-\bar h .
\]
For a compact unitary two-dimensional CFT with discrete finite-multiplicity
spectrum, unique vacuum, and modular-invariant torus partition function, the
thermal trace
\[
  Z(\beta)
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \exp\left[-\beta\left(L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c_L+c_R}{24}\right)\right]
\]
obeys \(Z(\beta)=Z(4\pi^2/\beta)\) for the rectangular torus.  As
\(\beta\to0^+\), the transformed trace is dominated by the vacuum, and
\begin{equation}
  \log Z(\beta)
  =
  \frac{\pi^2(c_L+c_R)}{6\beta}
  +o(\beta^{-1}).
  \label{eq:modular-high-temp-cardy}
\end{equation}
If \(c_L=c_R=c\), then \(A=\pi^2c/3\) in
\eqref{eq:cylinder-high-temp-free-energy}.  Equation
\eqref{eq:general-cft-entropy-dimension-asymptotic} gives
\begin{equation}
  \log N(\Delta)
  =
  2\pi\sqrt{\frac{c\,\Delta}{3}}
  +o(\sqrt{\Delta}).
  \label{eq:cardy-total-dimension-asymptotic}
\end{equation}
This is the Cardy entropy-dimension relation for total scaling dimension.
Keeping left and right energies separately gives the spin-resolved form
\begin{equation}
  \log \rho(h,\bar h)
  =
  2\pi\sqrt{\frac{c_L h}{6}}
  +
  2\pi\sqrt{\frac{c_R\bar h}{6}}
  +o(\sqrt h+\sqrt{\bar h}),
  \label{eq:cardy-spin-resolved}
\end{equation}
for \(h,\bar h\to\infty\) with \(h/\bar h\) remaining in a compact subinterval
of \((0,\infty)\).  If the lowest left or right conformal weight is not the
vacuum value, the same derivation replaces \(c_L,c_R\) by the corresponding
effective central charges \(c_L-24h_{\min}\) and
\(c_R-24\bar h_{\min}\).
