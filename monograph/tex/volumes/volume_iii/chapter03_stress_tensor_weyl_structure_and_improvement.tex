\section{The Stress Tensor from Background Metrics}

Let a Euclidean local theory be coupled to a smooth background metric
\(g_{\mu\nu}\) on a \(D\)-dimensional manifold \(M\).  The stress tensor is
defined by the first variation of the action or of the renormalized generating
functional with respect to the metric.  This chapter uses the
contravariant-metric convention
\begin{equation}
  \delta S[\Phi;g]
  =
  \frac12
  \int_M \dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,
  T_{\mu\nu}(x)\,\delta g^{\mu\nu}(x),
  \label{eq:stress-tensor-metric-variation}
\end{equation}
where \(\Phi\) denotes the dynamical fields and
\(g=\det(g_{\mu\nu})\).  Since
\(\delta g^{\mu\nu}=-g^{\mu\rho}g^{\nu\sigma}\delta g_{\rho\sigma}\), the same
convention is equivalently
\[
  \delta S[\Phi;g]
  =
  -\frac12
  \int_M \dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,
  T^{\mu\nu}(x)\,\delta g_{\mu\nu}(x).
\]
Equivalently, at the level of functional derivatives with all other sources
held fixed,
\begin{equation}
  T^{\mu\nu}(x)
  =
  -\frac{2}{\sqrt g(x)}
  \frac{\delta}{\delta g_{\mu\nu}(x)}
  =
  \frac{2}{\sqrt g(x)}
  g^{\mu\rho}(x)g^{\nu\sigma}(x)
  \frac{\delta}{\delta g^{\rho\sigma}(x)} .
  \label{eq:stress-tensor-covariant-contravariant-derivatives}
\end{equation}
The first differential operator acts on the functional written as a function of
\(g_{\mu\nu}\), and the second acts on the same functional written as a function
of \(g^{\mu\nu}\).  This identity is only the chain rule applied to
\(\delta g^{\mu\nu}=-g^{\mu\rho}g^{\nu\sigma}\delta g_{\rho\sigma}\).
This is the convention used by the metric derivative of the Euclidean
source functional.  At a regulator scale for which the metric-dependent
functional integral is a well-defined regulated integral, let
\[
  Z[g]=\int \mathcal D_g\Phi\,\exp\{-S[\Phi;g]\},
  \qquad
  \mathcal W[g]=-\log Z[g].
\]
For variations supported away from boundary components, or with boundary
conditions fixed so that no boundary term is produced, differentiating the
regulated integral gives
\[
  \delta\mathcal W[g]
  =
  \langle\delta S[\Phi;g]\rangle_g
  =
  -\frac12
  \int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,
  \langle T^{\mu\nu}(x)\rangle_g\,\delta g_{\mu\nu}(x).
\]
Therefore
\[
  \langle T^{\mu\nu}(x)\rangle_g
  =
  -\frac{2}{\sqrt g(x)}
  \frac{\delta\mathcal W[g]}{\delta g_{\mu\nu}(x)}.
\]
After subtracting local metric counterterms, the same identity defines the
renormalized source chart used in
\eqref{eq:cft-source-derivative-conventions}.  If instead one writes
\(K[g]=\log Z[g]\), the right-hand side carries the opposite sign; the
difference is only the choice between the logarithm of the partition function
and the Euclidean effective action.
With this sign convention, evaluating \(T_{\mu\nu}\) on the flat metric gives
the translation Noether tensor after adding the divergence term that makes the
Noether tensor symmetric.

A flat classical action can have several curved extensions.  For example,
\[
  S[\phi]
  =
  \int_{\R^D}\dd^Dx\,
  \left(
  \frac12\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
  +
  \frac12m^2\phi^2
  \right)
\]
may be extended as
\[
  S[\phi;g]
  =
  \int_M\dd^Dx\,\sqrt g\,
  \left(
  \frac12g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi
  +
  \frac12m^2\phi^2
  +
  \text{local curvature couplings}
  \right).
\]
Curvature couplings that vanish on flat space still affect \(T_{\mu\nu}\),
because \(T_{\mu\nu}\) is defined by varying \(g_{\mu\nu}\) before setting
\(g_{\mu\nu}=\delta_{\mu\nu}\).

If the theory is invariant under diffeomorphisms of the background, then an
infinitesimal vector field \(\xi^\mu\) gives
\[
  \delta_\xi g^{\mu\nu}
  =
  -\nabla^\mu\xi^\nu-\nabla^\nu\xi^\mu .
\]
Substituting this variation into
\eqref{eq:stress-tensor-metric-variation} and integrating by parts gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0
  \label{eq:stress-tensor-covariant-conservation}
\end{equation}
as an operator equation away from insertions.  In flat space this becomes
\(\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0\).  Symmetry of \(T_{\mu\nu}\) follows from
variation with respect to the symmetric tensor \(g_{\mu\nu}\).

\section{Metric Perturbations and Stress Insertions}

Set \(g_{\mu\nu}=\delta_{\mu\nu}+\delta g_{\mu\nu}\), with \(\delta g\) smooth
and compactly supported.  To first order, the Euclidean path integral changes
by the insertion
\begin{equation}
  \exp[-\delta_g S]
  =
  \exp\left[
  -\frac12\int_{\R^D}\dd^Dx\,
  \delta g^{\mu\nu}(x)T_{\mu\nu}(x)
  \right],
  \label{eq:stress-insertion-small-metric}
\end{equation}
where indices on \(\delta g^{\mu\nu}\) are raised with the flat metric at this
order.  Thus, for an operator product \(X\) supported away from
\(\operatorname{supp}(\delta g)\),
\[
  \langle X\rangle_{\delta+\delta g}
  =
  \langle X\rangle_{\delta}
  -
  \frac12\int\dd^Dx\,\delta g^{\mu\nu}(x)
  \langle T_{\mu\nu}(x)X\rangle_{\delta}
  +
  O(\delta g^2).
\]
Higher metric variations require a prescription for coincident products of
stress tensors.  The short-distance singularities in
\(T_{\mu\nu}(x)T_{\rho\sigma}(y)\) and the corresponding contact terms are part
of the renormalized definition of the theory on curved backgrounds.  This is a
local ultraviolet statement: in a massless theory the distributional extension
of stress-tensor products is used only after the infrared state or regulator
has made the separated-point correlators meaningful on the test functions
under consideration.  In even dimensions these contact terms include the Weyl
anomaly data.

\section{Weyl Transformations}

A Weyl transformation is a local rescaling of the background metric:
\[
  g_{\mu\nu}(x)\mapsto \ee^{2\omega(x)}g_{\mu\nu}(x),
\]
where \(\omega\) is a smooth real function.  Infinitesimally,
\[
  \delta_\omega g_{\mu\nu}=2\omega g_{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  \delta_\omega g^{\mu\nu}=-2\omega g^{\mu\nu}.
\]
The corresponding metric variation gives
\[
  \delta_\omega S
  =
  -
  \int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,
  \omega(x)\,T^\mu{}_\mu(x).
\]
\begin{theorem}[Flat-space trace condition from Weyl invariance]
\label{thm:flat-space-weyl-trace-condition}
Assume that the renormalized theory is coupled to a background metric, that
the stress tensor is defined by metric variation as in
\eqref{eq:stress-insertion-small-metric}, and that a choice of field Weyl
transformations and allowed improvements has been made for which the
renormalized functional is invariant under compactly supported Weyl
rescalings in flat space, apart from curvature anomaly terms and contact terms
on coincident insertions.  Then, at separated flat-space points,
\begin{equation}
  T^\mu{}_\mu=0
  \label{eq:weyl-trace-zero}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The action may assign Weyl transformations to the dynamical fields \(\Phi\).
After including these field variations and the chosen improvement terms, Weyl
invariance says that the first variation of every separated correlator under
\(\delta_\omega g_{\mu\nu}=2\omega g_{\mu\nu}\) vanishes, modulo the stated
anomaly and contact contributions.  The metric-variation formula above gives
\[
  \delta_\omega\langle X\rangle
  =
  -\int\dd^Dx\,\omega(x)
  \left\langle T^\mu{}_\mu(x)X\right\rangle
\]
for every operator product \(X\) supported away from
\(\operatorname{supp}(\omega)\).  Since \(\omega\) is an arbitrary smooth test
function in the separated region, the distributional insertion of
\(T^\mu{}_\mu\) vanishes there.  This is the local operator equation
\eqref{eq:weyl-trace-zero} at separated flat-space points.
\end{proof}

The background metric is an external source for stress-tensor insertions and
Ward identities.

In even dimensions, the renormalized generating functional may transform
under a Weyl rescaling by a local curvature functional.  At separated
flat-space points the conformal Ward identities are governed by
\eqref{eq:weyl-trace-zero}; the anomaly contributes contact terms and
curved-space response data.  The present volume records this contact-term
distinction; the special two-dimensional extension of conformal symmetry is
deferred to a separate treatment.

\section{Weyl Anomaly Density and Contact Ward Identities}

Define the trace anomaly density by
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\omega\mathcal W[g]
  =
  -\int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,\omega(x)\,\mathcal A[g](x),
  \qquad
  \mathcal A[g](x)=\left\langle T^\mu{}_\mu(x)\right\rangle_g .
  \label{eq:weyl-anomaly-density-definition}
\end{equation}
This sign follows from the convention
\(\langle T^{\mu\nu}\rangle_g=-2g^{-1/2}\delta\mathcal W/\delta g_{\mu\nu}\)
and \(\delta_\omega g_{\mu\nu}=2\omega g_{\mu\nu}\).  The anomaly density is a
local scalar of Weyl weight \(D\), defined modulo the Weyl variation of local
metric counterterms.  Its functional derivatives are contact terms in
stress-tensor Ward identities.  More explicitly, let
\[
  X_{n-1}
  =
  T^{\rho_1\sigma_1}(x_1)\cdots
  T^{\rho_{n-1}\sigma_{n-1}}(x_{n-1}).
\]
Tracing one stress tensor in an \(n\)-point distribution gives, away from
separated-point terms,
\[
  \begin{aligned}
  g_{\mu\nu}(x)
  \left\langle T^{\mu\nu}(x)X_{n-1}\right\rangle
  =
  \left.
  \frac{\delta^{\,n-1}\mathcal A[g](x)}
       {\prod_{j=1}^{n-1}\delta g_{\rho_j\sigma_j}(x_j)}
  \right|_{g=\delta}
  +\text{index-variation contact terms}.
  \end{aligned}
\]
Here the denominator denotes the iterated functional derivative with respect
to the displayed metric components.  Thus anomaly coefficients are part of
the metric-source definition of the stress-tensor correlators, even though
\(T^\mu{}_\mu=0\) at separated flat-space points.

\section{Classification of Conformal Anomalies}

The local anomaly density is constrained because Weyl transformations form an
abelian group.  Let
\[
  \mathbf A_\omega[g]
  :=
  \int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,\omega(x)\mathcal A[g](x).
\]
The identity
\([\delta_{\omega_1},\delta_{\omega_2}]\mathcal W[g]=0\) gives the
Wess--Zumino consistency condition
\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\omega_1}\mathbf A_{\omega_2}[g]
  -
  \delta_{\omega_2}\mathbf A_{\omega_1}[g]
  =
  0.
  \label{eq:weyl-wess-zumino-consistency}
\end{equation}
Changing the renormalized source functional by a local metric counterterm
\(\mathcal W[g]\mapsto\mathcal W[g]+B[g]\) changes
\(\mathbf A_\omega[g]\) by \(-\delta_\omega B[g]\).  Therefore the intrinsic
trace anomaly is the cohomology of the Weyl differential on local metric
functionals: solutions of
\eqref{eq:weyl-wess-zumino-consistency} modulo Weyl variations of local
counterterms.

In the parity-even metric sector on a closed manifold without boundary, the
bulk solutions in even dimension \(D=2n\) have the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{2n}[g]
  =
  -a_{2n}E_{2n}
  +
  \sum_\alpha c_\alpha I_\alpha
  +
  \nabla_\mu J^\mu .
  \label{eq:weyl-anomaly-type-abd-general}
\end{equation}
Here
\begin{equation}
  E_{2n}
  =
  \frac{1}{2^n}
  \delta^{\mu_1\nu_1\cdots\mu_n\nu_n}
         _{\rho_1\sigma_1\cdots\rho_n\sigma_n}
  R^{\rho_1\sigma_1}{}_{\mu_1\nu_1}\cdots
  R^{\rho_n\sigma_n}{}_{\mu_n\nu_n}
  \label{eq:euler-density-general}
\end{equation}
is the Euler density in the curvature convention used in this chapter.  The
term \(E_{2n}\) is the type-A anomaly.  Its integral is topological:
\(\int_M\sqrt g\,E_{2n}\) is proportional to the Euler characteristic on a
closed \(2n\)-manifold.  Consequently its Weyl variation is a total
derivative, so it obeys
\eqref{eq:weyl-wess-zumino-consistency}.  The scalar densities \(I_\alpha\)
are type-B anomalies: \(\sqrt g\,I_\alpha\) is locally Weyl invariant in
dimension \(2n\).  The divergence \(\nabla_\mu J^\mu\) denotes type-D, or
trivial, terms; these are Weyl variations of local metric counterterms and
are scheme dependent.

This classification is local.  It classifies the contact terms in
stress-tensor Ward identities produced by varying a smooth background metric.
Global terms, boundaries, defects, and parity-odd orientation-dependent
candidates require additional data and are not part of the parity-even bulk
list in \eqref{eq:weyl-anomaly-type-abd-general}.  In odd dimension there is
no parity-even local bulk trace anomaly of this type on a closed smooth
manifold, although finite nonlocal data such as the sphere free energy can be
important conformal data.

In two dimensions, with the convention above, write
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_2[g]=-\frac{c_{2d}}{24\pi}R .
  \label{eq:two-dimensional-weyl-anomaly}
\end{equation}
This is purely type A: \(E_2=R\), and there is no independent parity-even
type-B scalar in two dimensions.

In four dimensions, define
\[
  E_4
  =
  R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  -4R_{\mu\nu}R^{\mu\nu}
  +R^2,
  \qquad
  W^2
  =
  W_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}W^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma},
\]
where \(W_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\) is the Weyl tensor.  A parity-even CFT has
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_4[g]
  =
  \frac{1}{16\pi^2}
  \left(
    c_{\rm W} W^2
    -a_{\rm W} E_4
    +b_{\rm D}\nabla^2 R
  \right).
  \label{eq:four-dimensional-weyl-anomaly}
\end{equation}
The coefficient \(b_{\rm D}\) is changed by adding a local \(R^2\) counterterm
to \(\mathcal W[g]\).  The coefficients \(a_{\rm W}\) and \(c_{\rm W}\) are
unchanged by such counterterms at a conformal fixed point.
\(E_4\) is the type-A term, \(W^2\) is the unique parity-even type-B scalar,
and \(\nabla^2R\) is type D.  More explicitly,
\[
  \delta_\omega\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt g\,R^2
  =
  -12\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt g\,\omega\,\nabla^2R
\]
on a closed four-manifold, so the coefficient of \(\nabla^2R\) is shifted by
a choice of local \(R^2\) counterterm.
The completeness of this parity-even four-dimensional list follows from the
dimension-four curvature basis
\(R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}^2\), \(R_{\mu\nu}^2\), \(R^2\), and \(\nabla^2R\):
the two nontrivial Wess--Zumino classes are the Euler combination \(E_4\) and
the Weyl-invariant combination \(W^2\), while the remaining scalar can be
changed by the \(R^2\) counterterm just displayed.

In six dimensions, modulo type-D total-derivative terms, the parity-even bulk
classification has one type-A coefficient and three type-B coefficients:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_6[g]
  =
  \frac{1}{(4\pi)^3}
  \left(
    -a_6E_6+c_1I_1+c_2I_2+c_3I_3
    +\nabla_\mu J^\mu
  \right).
  \label{eq:six-dimensional-weyl-anomaly-classification}
\end{equation}
A convenient representative of the three type-B invariants is
\begin{align}
  I_1
  &=
  W_{\mu\rho\sigma\nu}
  W^{\mu\alpha\beta\nu}
  W_{\alpha}{}^{\rho\sigma}{}_{\beta},
  \notag\\
  I_2
  &=
  W_{\mu\nu}{}^{\rho\sigma}
  W_{\rho\sigma}{}^{\alpha\beta}
  W_{\alpha\beta}{}^{\mu\nu},
  \notag\\
  I_3
  &\equiv
  W_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
  \left(
    \nabla^2\delta^\mu{}_\alpha
    +4R^\mu{}_\alpha
    -\frac65R\,\delta^\mu{}_\alpha
  \right)
  W^{\alpha\nu\rho\sigma}
  \quad
  \bmod \nabla_\mu J^\mu .
  \label{eq:six-dimensional-type-b-invariants}
\end{align}
The equivalence sign in the definition of \(I_3\) is essential: different
representatives differ by type-D terms, while the coefficient \(c_3\) of the
cohomology class is invariant under local counterterm changes.  In dimensions
higher than six the same rule applies, but the number of independent Weyl
invariants grows because more contractions of Weyl tensors and conformally
covariant derivatives are available.
For \(D=6\), dimension counting and the algebraic Bianchi identities leave
two independent cubic Weyl contractions and one independent
two-Weyl-tensor invariant with two derivatives; Ricci-dependent representatives
either combine with these into the conformally covariant operator in \(I_3\),
enter the Euler density \(E_6\), or are type-D counterterm variations.

\section{Anomaly Coefficients and Stress-Tensor Correlators}

The same number \(c_{2d}\) in \eqref{eq:two-dimensional-weyl-anomaly} is the
Virasoro central charge in the holomorphic stress-tensor OPE normalization
\[
  \langle T(z)T(0)\rangle=\frac{c_{2d}}{2z^4}.
\]
The equality of these two definitions is obtained by taking two metric
variations of \eqref{eq:two-dimensional-weyl-anomaly} around a flat metric and
then rewriting the resulting contact Ward identity in complex coordinates.

With the two-point
normalization of \eqref{eq:stress-tensor-two-point}, the Weyl-squared
coefficient is fixed by the separated stress-tensor two-point coefficient:
\begin{equation}
  C_T=\frac{40}{\pi^4}\,c_{\rm W}.
  \label{eq:four-dimensional-ct-cw-relation}
\end{equation}
This relation is a normalization statement.  It follows by taking two metric
variations of the \(W^2\) term in
\eqref{eq:four-dimensional-weyl-anomaly}, Fourier transforming the resulting
quadratic curvature contact term, and matching the logarithmic scale
dependence of the flat-space \(TT\) correlator to
\eqref{eq:stress-tensor-two-point}.  The Euler term begins to contribute
independent information at the next metric variation.

The parity-even separated \(TTT\) correlator in \(D=4\) is described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-iii-correlation-functions-conformal-frames} by three
coefficients, denoted there by \(a,b,c\) in
\eqref{eq:stress-tensor-three-point-structures}.  To avoid notation collision,
call them \(a_{\rm OP},b_{\rm OP},c_{\rm OP}\) in this paragraph.  The Ward
identity relation \eqref{eq:ttt-ward-ct-relation} gives, at \(D=4\),
\begin{equation}
  C_T
  =
  \frac{\pi^2}{3}
  \left(
    14a_{\rm OP}-2b_{\rm OP}-5c_{\rm OP}
  \right),
  \label{eq:op-coefficients-ct-four-d}
\end{equation}
so \(c_{\rm W}\) is this same linear combination through
\eqref{eq:four-dimensional-ct-cw-relation}.  The Euler coefficient
\(a_{\rm W}\) is a second, independent linear functional of
\((a_{\rm OP},b_{\rm OP},c_{\rm OP})\), obtained by taking three metric
variations of the \(E_4\) term in
\eqref{eq:four-dimensional-weyl-anomaly} and projecting the resulting trace
Ward identity onto the three separated tensor structures.  Equivalently, one
may use the free-field basis of four-dimensional stress-tensor structures:
\begin{equation}
  \langle TTT\rangle
  =
  n_s\langle TTT\rangle_{\rm real\ scalar}
  +
  n_f\langle TTT\rangle_{\rm Dirac\ fermion}
  +
  n_v\langle TTT\rangle_{\rm Maxwell\ vector}.
  \label{eq:ttt-free-field-basis}
\end{equation}
In that basis,
\begin{equation}
  a_{\rm W}
  =
  \frac{n_s}{360}
  +
  \frac{11n_f}{360}
  +
  \frac{31n_v}{180},
  \qquad
  c_{\rm W}
  =
  \frac{n_s}{120}
  +
  \frac{n_f}{20}
  +
  \frac{n_v}{10}.
  \label{eq:four-dimensional-free-basis-anomaly-map}
\end{equation}
Here \(n_f\) multiplies the Dirac-fermion stress-tensor structure; using a
Weyl fermion instead halves the fermion entries.  The third independent
linear combination of \(a_{\rm OP},b_{\rm OP},c_{\rm OP}\) is genuine \(TTT\)
data not fixed by \(a_{\rm W}\) and \(c_{\rm W}\).  After \(C_T\) has fixed
the overall stress-tensor normalization, Chapter~\ref{ch:cft-light-ray-energy-correlators}
uses the conformal-collider coordinates \(t_2,t_4\) as detector coordinates on
the remaining separated \(TTT\) data.

\section{Improvement of the Stress Tensor}

The stress tensor has improvement freedom.  If \(L(x)\) is a local scalar
operator, then
\begin{equation}
  T_{\mu\nu}
  \longmapsto
  T'_{\mu\nu}
  =
  T_{\mu\nu}
  +
  \left(
  \delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2-\partial_\mu\partial_\nu
  \right)L
  \label{eq:scalar-stress-improvement}
\end{equation}
preserves conservation in flat space.  More general improvements are possible,
but \eqref{eq:scalar-stress-improvement} is the case needed for the free
scalar and many scalar fixed points.  Its trace is
\[
  T'^\mu{}_\mu
  =
  T^\mu{}_\mu
  +(D-1)\partial^2L .
 \]
If \(T^\mu{}_\mu\) is a total Laplacian of a local scalar, an improvement can
make the trace vanish in flat space.

The improvement can be represented by adding a curvature coupling to the
background-metric action.  For a scalar field \(\phi\), the term
\[
  \Delta S_\xi
  =
  \frac{\xi}{2}
  \int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,R(g)\phi^2
\]
vanishes in flat space when \(R(g)=0\), but its metric variation contributes
to \(T_{\mu\nu}\) at flat \(g_{\mu\nu}\).  Thus a term absent from the
flat-space action can change the flat-space stress tensor.

\section{The Conformally Coupled Free Scalar}

Consider a real massless scalar field in Euclidean signature.  The minimally
coupled curved-space action is
\[
  S_0[\phi;g]
  =
  \frac12\int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,
  g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi .
\]
Its metric variation is
\[
  \delta_g S_0
  =
  \frac12\int_M\dd^Dx\,\sqrt g\,
  \delta g^{\mu\nu}
  \left(
  \partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi
  -
  \frac12g_{\mu\nu}g^{\rho\sigma}
  \partial_\rho\phi\,\partial_\sigma\phi
  \right).
\]
On the flat background this gives
\[
  T_{\mu\nu}^{(0)}
  =
  \partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi
  -
  \frac12\delta_{\mu\nu}(\partial\phi)^2 .
\]
Its trace is
\[
  T^{(0)\mu}{}_\mu
  =
  \left(1-\frac D2\right)(\partial\phi)^2 .
\]
For \(D>2\) this trace is a nonzero local operator before improvement.  Using
the equation of motion \(\partial^2\phi=0\), the identity
\[
  \partial^2\phi^2
  =
  2(\partial\phi)^2+2\phi\,\partial^2\phi
\]
shows that the trace is a Laplacian modulo the equation of motion.

Now add the curvature functional
\[
  S_1[\phi;g]
  =
  \frac12\int_M\dd^Dx\,\sqrt g\,R(g)\phi^2 .
\]
The scalar curvature variation with respect to \(g^{\mu\nu}\) is
\[
  \delta R
  =
  R_{\mu\nu}\delta g^{\mu\nu}
  +
  \nabla_\rho
  \left(
  g^{\mu\nu}\delta\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu}
  -
  g^{\mu\rho}\delta\Gamma^\nu_{\mu\nu}
  \right),
\]
where \(\delta\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu}\) is the variation of the Levi-Civita
connection.  Integrating the total derivative by parts and then setting
\(g_{\mu\nu}=\delta_{\mu\nu}\) gives
\[
  \delta_g S_1\big|_{g=\delta}
  =
  \frac12\int_{\R^D}\dd^Dx\,\delta g^{\mu\nu}
  \left(
  -\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi^2
  +
  \delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\phi^2
  \right).
\]
The remaining terms in the full curved-space variation are proportional to
curvature and vanish after the metric is set to \(\delta_{\mu\nu}\).

For
\[
  S[\phi;g]=S_0[\phi;g]+aS_1[\phi;g],
\]
the flat-space stress tensor is
\begin{equation}
  T_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi
  -
  \frac12\delta_{\mu\nu}(\partial\phi)^2
  +
  a
  \left(
  \delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2-\partial_\mu\partial_\nu
  \right)\phi^2,
  \label{eq:improved-free-scalar-stress}
\end{equation}
The total translation generators
\(P^\nu=\int_\Sigma\dd^{D-1}x\,T^{0\nu}\) are unchanged by the improvement
term when spatial total derivatives integrate to zero on the constant-time
slice.  The trace changes:
\[
  T^\mu{}_\mu
  =
  \left(1-\frac D2\right)(\partial\phi)^2
  +
  a(D-1)\partial^2\phi^2
\]
and therefore
\[
  T^\mu{}_\mu
  =
  \left(1-\frac D2+2(D-1)a\right)(\partial\phi)^2
  +
  2(D-1)a\,\phi\,\partial^2\phi .
\]
The trace vanishes modulo the scalar equation of motion if and only if
\begin{equation}
  a=\xi_\ast=\frac{D-2}{4(D-1)}.
  \label{eq:conformal-scalar-xi}
\end{equation}
The corresponding curved-space action is
\begin{equation}
  S[\phi;g]
  =
  \frac12
  \int_M\dd^D x\,\sqrt g\,
  \left[
  g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\partial_\nu\phi
  +
  \xi_\ast R(g)\phi^2
  \right].
  \label{eq:conformal-scalar-curved-action}
\end{equation}
With the Weyl transformation
\[
  g_{\mu\nu}\mapsto \ee^{2\omega}g_{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  \phi\mapsto \ee^{-\frac{D-2}{2}\omega}\phi,
\]
this action is Weyl invariant at the classical level.  In the corresponding
free massless scalar QFT, the improved trace is
\[
  \widehat T^\mu{}_\mu(x)
  =
  2(D-1)\xi_\ast\,\widehat\phi(x)\,\partial^2\widehat\phi(x).
\]
It represents the zero class in the local-operator vector space modulo the
free equation-of-motion ideal.  Equivalently, for local insertions
\(\mathcal O_i(y_i)\) with \(x\ne y_i\),
\[
  \left\langle
    \widehat T^\mu{}_\mu(x)\,
    \prod_i\mathcal O_i(y_i)
  \right\rangle
  =
  0 .
\]
At coincident points the factor \(\partial^2\widehat\phi\) generates the
Schwinger--Dyson contact terms of the free Gaussian measure.

\section{Ward Identities with Stress-Tensor Insertions}

Let
\[
  X=
  \mathcal O_{A_1}(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_{A_n}(x_n),
  \qquad
  X_i[B]=
  \mathcal O_{A_1}(x_1)\cdots B(x_i)\cdots
  \mathcal O_{A_n}(x_n),
\]
where \(B(x_i)\) replaces the \(i\)-th operator.  Suppose first that each
\(\mathcal O_{A_i}\) belongs to a finite-dimensional source representation of
linearized diffeomorphisms, with matrices
\((\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)})_{A_i}{}^{B_i}\) defined by the linear term
\[
  \delta_\xi \mathcal O_{A_i}(x_i)
  =
  \xi^\rho(x_i)\partial_\rho\mathcal O_{A_i}(x_i)
  +
  \partial_\mu\xi_\nu(x_i)
  (\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)})_{A_i}{}^{B_i}
  \mathcal O_{B_i}(x_i).
\]
For scalar operators the matrices \(\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)}\) vanish.  For
operators carrying only orthonormal-frame spin, the antisymmetric part of
\(\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)}\) is the usual spin matrix
\(\mathsf S^{\mu\nu}_{(i)}=-\mathsf S^{\nu\mu}_{(i)}\), and the symmetric
traceless part is absent.  The source-functional identity
\eqref{eq:cft-source-diffeomorphism-ward}, differentiated with respect to the
corresponding sources and then evaluated at zero source, gives the smeared
flat-space Ward identity
\begin{equation}
  \int\dd^D x\,
  \partial_\mu\xi_\nu(x)
  \left\langle T^{\mu\nu}(x)X\right\rangle
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^n
  \xi^\nu(x_i)\partial_{x_i^\nu}\langle X\rangle
  +
  \sum_{i=1}^n
  \partial_\mu\xi_\nu(x_i)
  \left\langle
    X_i[
    (\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)})_{A_i}{}^{B_i}\mathcal O_{B_i}]
  \right\rangle .
  \label{eq:stress-ward-spinning-smeared}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, as an identity of distributions in \(x\),
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu
  \left\langle
  T^{\mu\nu}(x)X
  \right\rangle
  =
  -\sum_{i=1}^n
  \delta(x-x_i)
  \partial_{x_i^\nu}
  \langle X\rangle
  +
  \sum_{i=1}^n
  \partial_\mu
  \left[
    \delta(x-x_i)
    \left\langle
      X_i[
      (\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)})_{A_i}{}^{B_i}\mathcal O_{B_i}]
    \right\rangle
  \right].
  \label{eq:stress-ward-spinning-local}
\end{equation}
There is no omitted spin term in
\eqref{eq:stress-ward-spinning-local}.  For scalar insertions
\(\mathsf R^{\mu\nu}_{(i)}=0\), and the familiar translation Ward identity is
obtained by deleting the second line.  For spinning insertions that second
line is part of the identity.  To see its relation to the conformal Ward
identities used later, let the \(i\)-th operator be a primary in an
orthonormal-frame spin representation \(\rho_i\), with infinitesimal spin
matrices \(S^{(i)}_{\mu\nu}\) in the convention of
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-iii-primary-operators-finite-transformations}.  If
\(\epsilon^\mu\) is a conformal Killing vector and
\[
  J^\mu_\epsilon=T^\mu{}_\nu\epsilon^\nu,
  \qquad
  \sigma_\epsilon=\frac1D\partial_\rho\epsilon^\rho,
  \qquad
  \omega_{\epsilon,\mu\nu}
  =\frac12(\partial_\nu\epsilon_\mu-\partial_\mu\epsilon_\nu),
\]
then smearing \eqref{eq:stress-ward-spinning-smeared} with
\(\xi=\epsilon\) and adding the trace Ward identity gives
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu
  \left\langle J^\mu_\epsilon(x)X\right\rangle
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^n
  \delta^{(D)}(x-x_i)
  \left\langle
    X_i[\mathcal D^{(i)}_\epsilon\mathcal O_{A_i}]
  \right\rangle
  +
  \sigma_\epsilon(x)\mathcal A(x;X),
  \label{eq:stress-ward-primary-spin-current}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal D^{(i)}_\epsilon\mathcal O_{i,a_i}(x_i)
  =
  -\epsilon^\rho(x_i)\partial_{x_i^\rho}\mathcal O_{i,a_i}(x_i)
  -\Delta_i\sigma_\epsilon(x_i)\mathcal O_{i,a_i}(x_i)
  -\frac{\ii}{2}\omega_\epsilon^{\mu\nu}(x_i)
  (S^{(i)}_{\mu\nu})_{a_i}{}^{b_i}
  \mathcal O_{i,b_i}(x_i).
  \label{eq:stress-ward-primary-spin-contact-operator}
\end{equation}
Equations \eqref{eq:stress-ward-primary-spin-current} and
\eqref{eq:stress-ward-primary-spin-contact-operator} are the same source
Ward identity as \eqref{eq:primary-spin-current-ward}; the derivative
contact term in \eqref{eq:stress-ward-spinning-local} is precisely what
becomes the local rotation term proportional to
\(\omega_\epsilon^{\mu\nu}S^{(i)}_{\mu\nu}\).  Thus one may use the scalar
translation formula only after verifying that all insertions are scalars, or
after the spin contact term has been carried along separately.
For a basis of composite operators that mixes under the linearized
diffeomorphism, the displayed representation matrix is replaced by the
corresponding source-chart representation matrix.  The trace Ward identity
similarly records the response under Weyl transformations.  At a flat-space
conformal fixed point, the separated-point trace insertion vanishes, while
contact terms encode scaling dimensions and conformal transformations of the
inserted operators.

The charge form of the Ward identity follows by integrating
\(T^\mu{}_\nu\epsilon^\nu\) over a small sphere around each insertion.  This
is the bridge between the local stress-tensor equation and the conformal
algebra acting on local operators.
