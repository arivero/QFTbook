\chapter{Fixed Points and Conformal Data}
\label{ch:volume-iii-fixed-points-conformal-data}

\section{Fixed Points of the Renormalization Group}

Let \(\mathcal T\) be a local quantum field theory in \(D\) spacetime
dimensions and let \(\{g_I(\mu)\}_{I\in\mathcal I}\) be a set of
dimensionless renormalized coordinates on a space of local deformations.  The
scale \(\mu>0\) is the renormalization scale, and \(\mathcal I\) labels local
operators included in the coordinate chart.  The renormalization-group vector
field is
\begin{equation}
  \mu\frac{\dd g_I}{\dd\mu}
  =
  \beta_I(g).
  \label{eq:cft-rg-vector-field}
\end{equation}
An RG fixed point in this chart is a point \(g_\ast\) satisfying
\begin{equation}
  \beta_I(g_\ast)=0
  \qquad
  \text{for all }I\in\mathcal I .
  \label{eq:cft-rg-fixed-point}
\end{equation}
At such a point, correlation functions have no dependence on an auxiliary
renormalization scale except through explicit operator normalizations and
possible background anomalies.

The infinitesimal behavior near a fixed point is controlled by the matrix
\begin{equation}
  M_{IJ}
  =
  \left.
  \frac{\partial\beta_I}{\partial g_J}
  \right|_{g=g_\ast}.
  \label{eq:cft-linearized-rg-matrix}
\end{equation}
If \(v^{(a)}_J\) is an eigenvector of \(M\),
\[
  \sum_J M_{IJ}v^{(a)}_J=y_a v^{(a)}_I,
\]
then the corresponding infinitesimal coupling
\(\lambda_a=\sum_I v_I^{(a)}(g_I-g_{\ast I})\) obeys
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd\lambda_a}{\dd\mu}
  =
  y_a\lambda_a+O(\lambda^2).
\]
The number \(y_a\) is the RG eigenvalue of the deformation.  If the associated
local operator has scaling dimension \(\Delta_a\), then
\begin{equation}
  y_a=D-\Delta_a .
  \label{eq:rg-eigenvalue-scaling-dimension}
\end{equation}
Thus relevant, marginal, and irrelevant deformations are characterized by
\(y_a>0\), \(y_a=0\), and \(y_a<0\), respectively, with the convention that
the flow parameter is the momentum scale.

\section{Conformal Field Theory as Local Fixed-Point Data}

The local data of a conformal field theory consist of the following objects.
First, there is a vector space \(\mathcal V_{\mathrm{loc}}\) of local
operators.  Its elements are denoted
\[
  \mathcal O_A(x),
  \qquad
  A\in\mathcal A,
\]
where \(\mathcal A\) is an index set labeling an operator basis or a collection
of operator multiplets.  Second, there is an action of the conformal algebra
on \(\mathcal V_{\mathrm{loc}}\).  Third, there are Euclidean correlation
functions
\begin{equation}
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O_{A_1}(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_{A_n}(x_n)
  \right\rangle,
  \qquad
  x_i\in\R^D,
  \label{eq:cft-euclidean-correlators}
\end{equation}
defined as distributions away from coincident points and completed by local
contact-term prescriptions when necessary.  Fourth, there is a stress tensor
\(T_{\mu\nu}\) whose conserved charges generate spacetime conformal
transformations.

When the theory is unitary in Lorentzian signature, these Euclidean data are
the analytic continuation of Wightman functions satisfying positivity,
locality, and the spectral condition.  The present volume develops the
Euclidean operator language first because it is the most efficient coordinate
system for conformal symmetry, while the relation to Lorentzian local quantum
fields remains the same relation developed earlier for Green functions.

A scalar primary operator \(\mathcal O\) has a scaling dimension
\(\Delta_{\mathcal O}\).  Under a uniform scale transformation
\[
  x^\mu\mapsto \lambda x^\mu,
  \qquad
  \lambda>0,
\]
its separated-point correlation functions transform as
\begin{equation}
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O_1(\lambda x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(\lambda x_n)
  \right\rangle
  =
  \lambda^{-\sum_i\Delta_i}
  \left\langle
  \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
  \right\rangle
  \label{eq:cft-scale-covariance}
\end{equation}
when all \(\mathcal O_i\) are scalar primaries.  Operators with spin carry
additional finite-dimensional rotation matrices.  The systematic treatment of
spin appears below when primary operators are defined by the full conformal
algebra.

\section{Ising Universality as a Fixed-Point Example}

The statistical Ising model in \(D\) Euclidean dimensions assigns to each
lattice site \(x\) a spin \(s_x\in\{+1,-1\}\).  With nearest-neighbor
interaction and inverse temperature \(\beta_{\mathrm{th}}\), the partition
function is
\[
  Z_{\mathrm{Ising}}
  =
  \sum_{\{s_x=\pm1\}}
  \exp\left\{
  \beta_{\mathrm{th}}\sum_{\langle x y\rangle}s_xs_y
  \right\}.
\]
The connected spin correlator determines a correlation length
\(\xi(\beta_{\mathrm{th}})\) away from criticality:
\[
  \langle s_xs_y\rangle_{\mathrm c}
  \sim
  \exp\{-|x-y|/\xi\}
  \qquad
  (|x-y|\gg1).
\]
At the critical inverse temperature \(\beta_{\mathrm c}\), the correlation
length diverges.  The long-distance scaling limit is described by a conformal
field theory, the Ising CFT.  In that continuum theory, the lattice spin
approaches a scalar primary \(\sigma(x)\), and the energy-density perturbation
approaches another scalar primary \(\varepsilon(x)\).

The leading separated two-point functions at criticality take the form
\[
  \langle\sigma(x)\sigma(0)\rangle
  =
  \frac{C_\sigma}{|x|^{2\Delta_\sigma}},
  \qquad
  \langle\varepsilon(x)\varepsilon(0)\rangle
  =
  \frac{C_\varepsilon}{|x|^{2\Delta_\varepsilon}},
\]
with positive normalization constants \(C_\sigma,C_\varepsilon\) in a unitary
normalization.  The thermal exponent \(\nu\) is related to the scaling
dimension of the energy operator by
\[
  \Delta_\varepsilon=D-\frac1{\nu}.
\]
These relations are definitions inside the continuum fixed-point description:
the critical exponents of the lattice model are recovered from scaling
dimensions of operators in the CFT.

\section{Trace Equation at a Fixed Point}

Let \(T_{\mu\nu}\) be the symmetric conserved stress tensor.  Away from a
fixed point, the trace Ward identity has the renormalized local form
\begin{equation}
  T^\mu{}_\mu
  =
  \sum_I\beta_I(g)[\mathcal O_I]_\mu
  +
  \partial_\mu V^\mu
  +
  \text{curvature terms in a background metric}.
  \label{eq:cft-trace-beta-local-form}
\end{equation}
The vector operator \(V^\mu\) records possible improvement ambiguities in flat
space.  At a fixed point, the beta-function terms vanish.  A theory is
conformal in flat space when the remaining flat-space trace can be removed by
an allowed improvement so that
\begin{equation}
  T^\mu{}_\mu=0
  \label{eq:cft-flat-traceless-stress}
\end{equation}
as a local operator equation, up to contact terms in Ward identities.  The
next chapters derive the conformal charges from
\eqref{eq:cft-flat-traceless-stress} and then explain the role of Weyl
transformations and stress-tensor improvements.
