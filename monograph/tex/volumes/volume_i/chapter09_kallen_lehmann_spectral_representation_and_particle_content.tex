\chapter{K\"all\'en--Lehmann Spectral Representation}
\label{ch:kallen-lehmann}
\ConventionsLorentzian


\section{Spectral Data of a Scalar Field}

Let \(\Hilb\) carry a strongly continuous unitary representation of spacetime
translations with commuting self-adjoint generators \(P^\mu\). Let
\[
  E(\Delta)
\]
be the joint projection-valued spectral measure of \(P^\mu\), where
\(\Delta\subset\mathbb R^D\) is Borel. Assume the spectrum condition
\[
  \operatorname{Spec}(P)\subset\overline V_+
  =
  \{p\in\mathbb R^D:p^0\ge0,\ p^2\le0\},
\]
with \(p^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}p^\mu p^\nu\). Let \(\vac\) be a translation-invariant
vacuum vector in the pure vacuum sector,
\[
  E(\{0\})\Hilb=\mathbb C\vac .
\]

Let \(\widehat\phi\) be a scalar operator-valued distribution. If
\[
  \langle\vac|\widehat\phi(x)|\vac\rangle
\]
is nonzero, replace \(\widehat\phi\) by
\(\widehat\phi-\langle\vac|\widehat\phi|\vac\rangle\). This removes the
vacuum atom from the two-point function and keeps the connected spectral data:
because the zero-momentum spectral projection is \(|\vac\rangle\langle\vac|\),
there is no remaining translation-invariant component after this subtraction.
Point-field notation below is distributional notation; after smearing, the
matrix elements are ordinary Hilbert-space pairings.

The Wightman two-point distribution is
\[
  W_2(x-y)
  =
  \langle\vac|
  \widehat\phi(x)\widehat\phi(y)
  |\vac\rangle.
\]
Translation covariance gives
\[
  W_2(x-y)
  =
  \int_{\overline V_+}\ee^{\ii p\cdot(x-y)}\,\dd\nu_\phi(p),
\]
where the positive measure \(\nu_\phi\) is
\[
  \dd\nu_\phi(p)
  =
  \langle\vac|
  \widehat\phi(0)E(\dd p)\widehat\phi(0)
  |\vac\rangle.
\]
Positivity is immediate from the spectral theorem: for a Borel set
\(\Delta\),
\[
  \nu_\phi(\Delta)
  =
  \|E(\Delta)\widehat\phi(0)\vac\|^2
  \ge0.
\]
Equivalently, if one writes the same statement in generalized
energy-momentum eigenvector notation, with
\[
  P^\mu|\alpha\rangle=p_\alpha^\mu|\alpha\rangle,
\]
then
\[
  W_2(x-y)
  =
  \int \dd\alpha\,
  \ee^{\ii p_\alpha\cdot(x-y)}
  \left|
    \langle\alpha|\widehat\phi(0)|\vac\rangle
  \right|^2 .
\]
This formula is only notation for the spectral measure above; the measure
\(d\nu_\phi\) is the invariant object.

\section{Invariant-Mass Decomposition}

For a scalar field and invariant vacuum, \(\nu_\phi\) is invariant under the
proper orthochronous Lorentz group. The Lorentz-invariant parameter on the
forward cone is the invariant mass squared
\[
  \mu^2=-p^2\ge0.
\]
Let
\[
  \pi:\overline V_+\to[0,\infty),
  \qquad
  \pi(p)=-p^2,
\]
which is a Borel map between standard Borel spaces.  The spectral density
measure below is a quotient measure for the Lorentz orbits of this map, not the
literal pushforward \(\pi_*\nu_\phi\): a single massive shell has infinite
Lorentz-invariant volume, so the raw pushforward would assign infinite mass to
an isolated one-particle shell.  The normalization of the invariant measure on
each shell must be fixed first.

\begin{proposition}[Invariant mass-shell disintegration]
\label{prop:kallen-lehmann-mass-shell-disintegration}
Let \(\nu\) be a positive locally finite Borel measure on
\(\overline V_+\) that is invariant under \(SO^+(1,D-1)\).  Assume that
\(\nu\) has no atom at \(p=0\).  Then there is a positive Borel measure
\(\rho\) on \([0,\infty)\) such that for every
\(F\in C_c(\overline V_+)\),
\[
  \int_{\overline V_+}F(p)\,\dd\nu(p)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\dd\rho(\mu^2)
  \int\frac{\dd^Dp}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  \theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+\mu^2)F(p).
\]
The measure \(\rho\) is the spectral density measure after the normalization of
the invariant measure on each shell has been fixed.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let
\[
  G=SO^+(1,D-1),\qquad X=\overline V_+\setminus\{0\}.
\]
The excluded point has zero \(\nu\)-measure by hypothesis, so it may be
removed throughout the proof.  The space \(X\) is locally compact, second
countable, \(\sigma\)-compact, and standard Borel.  The group \(G\) is a
locally compact second countable Lie group, and its action on \(X\) is
continuous.

We first verify the regular-orbit hypotheses in the Mackey--Effros
decomposition theorem.  The \(G\)-orbits in \(X\) are
\[
  \Sigma_s^+
  =
  \{p\in\overline V_+: -p^2=s\},
  \qquad s>0,
\]
together with the punctured future light cone
\[
  \Sigma_0^{+\times}
  =
  \{p\in\overline V_+:p^2=0,\ p\ne0\}.
\]
For \(s>0\), a representative is \(k_s=(\sqrt s,0,\ldots,0)\), and the
stabilizer is the compact group \(SO(D-1)\).  For the null orbit, a
representative is \(\ell=(1,1,0,\ldots,0)\), and the stabilizer is the
Euclidean group
\[
  ISO(D-2)=\mathbb R^{D-2}\rtimes SO(D-2)
\]
embedded as the usual massless little group.  These stabilizers are closed
subgroups of \(G\), and the orbit maps identify the orbits with the
homogeneous spaces
\[
  \Sigma_s^+\simeq G/SO(D-1),
  \qquad
  \Sigma_0^{+\times}\simeq G/ISO(D-2).
\]
The orbit space is the standard Borel space \([0,\infty)\), with orbit map
\(\pi(p)=-p^2\), after the origin has been removed from the fiber over
\(0\).  A Borel cross-section is
\[
  c(0)=\ell,\qquad c(s)=k_s\quad(s>0).
\]
Thus the action is smooth in Mackey's sense: the orbit equivalence relation is
regularly embedded, the quotient is standard Borel, and a Borel section
exists.  Since \(G\), \(SO(D-1)\), and \(ISO(D-2)\) are unimodular, each
homogeneous orbit carries a nonzero \(G\)-invariant Radon measure, unique up
to a positive scalar.  The Mackey--Effros disintegration theorem for smooth
Borel actions of locally compact second countable groups therefore applies to
the \(\sigma\)-finite invariant Radon measure \(\nu|_X\).

Consequently there are conditional invariant Radon measures \(\nu_s\),
supported on the orbit \(\pi^{-1}(s)\cap X\), and a positive Borel measure
\(\lambda\) on \([0,\infty)\) such that
\[
  \int F(p)\,\dd\nu(p)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \left(\int_{\pi^{-1}(s)}F(p)\,\dd\nu_s(p)\right)
  \dd\lambda(s)
\]
for all \(F\in C_c(X)\).  This \(\lambda\) is an orbit-space measure; it is
determined by the chosen normalization of conditional orbit measures.  A
single massive orbit has infinite invariant volume, so this normalization is
part of the data entering the quotient measure.

It remains to identify the orbit measures and fix their normalization.  For
\(s>0\), the standard invariant measure on the positive mass shell is
\[
  \dd\sigma_s(p)
  =
  \frac{\dd^Dp}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  \theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+s).
\]
Indeed, the distribution \(\theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+s)\dd^Dp\) is Lorentz
invariant, and in coordinates \(p=(\omega_{\vec p},\vec p)\),
\(\omega_{\vec p}=(\vec p^{\,2}+s)^{1/2}\), it is
\[
  \dd\sigma_s(p)
  =
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec p}} .
\]
For \(s=0\) the same formula gives the invariant measure on the punctured
future light cone,
\[
  \dd\sigma_0(p)
  =
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2|\vec p|},
  \qquad \vec p\ne0,
\]
which is locally finite on \(X\).  The origin would give a separate orbit and
a possible atom; it was removed by hypothesis.

The uniqueness of invariant Radon measure on each homogeneous orbit gives
\[
  \nu_s=c(s)\sigma_s
\]
for \(\lambda\)-almost every \(s\), with \(c(s)\ge0\).  The family
\(s\mapsto\sigma_s\) is measurable: for \(F\in C_c(X)\),
\[
  s\longmapsto \int_{\pi^{-1}(s)}F(p)\,\dd\sigma_s(p)
\]
is Borel, and it is continuous on \((0,\infty)\) by the displayed
\(\vec p\)-coordinate formula and dominated convergence on compact supports;
the \(s=0\) value is the corresponding null-shell limit whenever the support
stays away from the origin.  The measurable scalar \(c(s)\) is then fixed
\(\lambda\)-almost everywhere by testing \(\nu_s\) against any countable
measure-determining family of compactly supported continuous functions on
\(X\).  Absorbing \(c(s)\) into \(\dd\lambda(s)\) defines the positive Borel
measure \(\dd\rho(s):=c(s)\dd\lambda(s)\).  Substituting
\(\nu_s=c(s)\sigma_s\) gives the stated formula for every
\(F\in C_c(X)\), and hence for every \(F\in C_c(\overline V_+)\) because the
removed origin has zero \(\nu\)-measure.
\end{proof}

Applying Proposition~\ref{prop:kallen-lehmann-mass-shell-disintegration} to
\(\nu_\phi\), with the vacuum atom removed as above, gives
\[
  \dd\nu_\phi(p)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \dd\rho(\mu^2)\,
  \frac{\dd^Dp}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  \theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+\mu^2).
\]
Here \(d\rho\) is a positive Borel measure on \([0,\infty)\). When it is
absolutely continuous one writes \(\dd\rho(\mu^2)=\rho(\mu^2)\dd\mu^2\);
when it has atoms, the atomic weights are part of the same measure.
In the special case in which \(d\nu_\phi\) has a density with respect to
\(\dd^Dp\), scalar covariance and the spectrum condition say equivalently that
the density is of the form
\[
  \frac{\theta(p^0)}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\rho(-p^2),
\]
with support on \(-p^2\ge0\). The measure notation is retained because the
one-particle contribution is an atom, not an ordinary function.

Define the positive-frequency scalar two-point function of mass \(\mu\) by
\[
  \Delta_+(x;\mu^2)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^Dp}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  \theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+\mu^2)\,
  \ee^{\ii p\cdot x}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
  \Delta_+(x;\mu^2)
  =
  \int
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  \frac{
    \ee^{\ii\vec p\cdot\vec x-\ii\sqrt{\vec p^{\,2}+\mu^2}\,x^0}
  }{2\sqrt{\vec p^{\,2}+\mu^2}} .
\]
The Wightman two-point function is then
\[
  W_2(x)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \dd\rho(\mu^2)\,
  \Delta_+(x;\mu^2).
\]
This is the K\"all\'en--Lehmann representation for the Wightman two-point
function.

For each \(\mu^2\), the distribution \(\Delta_+(x;\mu^2)\) is even at
spacelike separation. Indeed, if \(x^2>0\), one may choose a Lorentz frame in
which \(x^0=0\); in that frame the integral is
\[
  \int
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}
  \frac{\ee^{\ii\vec p\cdot\vec x}}{2\sqrt{\vec p^{\,2}+\mu^2}},
\]
which is unchanged by \(\vec p\mapsto-\vec p\). Therefore
\[
  \Delta_+(x;\mu^2)=\Delta_+(-x;\mu^2),
  \qquad x^2>0,
\]
and hence \(W_2(x)=W_2(-x)\) for spacelike \(x\). No such equality follows for
timelike \(x\); the ordering of operators is then genuine data.

\section{Time-Ordered and Euclidean Forms}

The time-ordered two-point function is
\[
  G_T(x)
  =
  \langle\vac|
  \operatorname{T}\{
    \widehat\phi(x)\widehat\phi(0)
  \}
  |\vac\rangle.
\]
Using the definition of time ordering,
\[
  G_T(x)
  =
  \theta(x^0)W_2(x)+\theta(-x^0)W_2(-x).
\]
Therefore
\[
  G_T(x)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \dd\rho(\mu^2)\,
  \Delta_F(x;\mu^2),
\]
where
\[
  \Delta_F(x;\mu^2)
  =
  -\ii
  \int\frac{\dd^Dk}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{\ee^{\ii k\cdot x}}{k^2+\mu^2-\ii\epsilon}.
\]
To see explicitly that this \(i\epsilon\) prescription gives the
time-ordered expression, set
\[
  \omega_{\vec k,\mu}=\sqrt{\vec k^{\,2}+\mu^2}.
\]
Since \(k^2+\mu^2=-(k^0)^2+\omega_{\vec k,\mu}^2\), the \(k^0\)-integral in
\(\Delta_F\) is
\[
  I(x^0,\vec k;\mu^2)
  =
  \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
  \frac{\dd k^0}{2\pi\ii}\,
  \frac{\ee^{-\ii k^0x^0}}
  {-(k^0)^2+\omega_{\vec k,\mu}^2-\ii\epsilon}.
\]
The two poles are displaced to
\[
  k^0=+\omega_{\vec k,\mu}-\ii0,
  \qquad
  k^0=-\omega_{\vec k,\mu}+\ii0.
\]
For \(x^0>0\) the exponential damps in the lower half-plane, and the closed
contour has clockwise orientation; for \(x^0<0\) the contour closes in the
upper half-plane. Thus
\[
  I(x^0,\vec k;\mu^2)
  =
  \theta(x^0)\frac{\ee^{-\ii\omega_{\vec k,\mu}x^0}}
  {2\omega_{\vec k,\mu}}
  +
  \theta(-x^0)\frac{\ee^{+\ii\omega_{\vec k,\mu}x^0}}
  {2\omega_{\vec k,\mu}}.
\]
After the remaining spatial Fourier transform this is exactly
\[
  \Delta_F(x;\mu^2)
  =
  \theta(x^0)\Delta_+(x;\mu^2)
  +
  \theta(-x^0)\Delta_+(-x;\mu^2).
\]

Equivalently,
\[
  G_T(x)
  =
  -\ii
  \int\frac{\dd^Dk}{(2\pi)^D}\,
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot x}
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \frac{\dd\rho(\mu^2)}{k^2+\mu^2-\ii\epsilon}.
\]

The Euclidean function is obtained by analytic continuation of the Wightman
functions in the time variable. For
\[
  z=x-y,\qquad z^0\in\mathbb C,\qquad \operatorname{Im}z^0<0,
\]
the factor
\(\exp(-\ii p^0z^0)\) is damped by the spectrum condition \(p^0\ge0\), so
\(W_2(z)\) is analytic in this tube after smearing in the spatial variables.
Set \(x^0=-\ii\tau_x\), \(y^0=-\ii\tau_y\). If
\(\tau_x>\tau_y\), then \(z^0=-\ii(\tau_x-\tau_y)\) lies in the lower half
plane and the Euclidean two-point function is the continuation of
\(W_2(x-y)\). If \(\tau_x<\tau_y\), the time-ordered function instead continues
\(W_2(y-x)\). This gives a single symmetric Euclidean distribution.

The Euclidean two-point function has the same spectral measure:
\[
  G_E(x_E)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \dd\rho(\mu^2)\,
  \Delta_E(x_E;\mu^2),
\]
with
\[
  \Delta_E(x_E;\mu^2)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^Dk_E}{(2\pi)^D}
  \frac{\ee^{\ii k_E\cdot x_E}}{k_E^2+\mu^2}.
\]
The spectral measure \(d\rho\) is thus common to the Wightman, time-ordered,
and Euclidean two-point functions. The different Green functions are different
boundary values or analytic continuations of the same positive spectral data.

\section{Stieltjes Positivity of the Euclidean Propagator}

The Euclidean momentum-space two-point function contains the same information
in a form that is often the most robust for constructive and lattice
arguments.  In this section \(z\) denotes the complex continuation of the
Euclidean invariant \(Q^2\).  On the physical Euclidean axis set
\[
  Q^2=k_E^2\ge0 .
\]
If the spectral measure obeys the Stieltjes integrability condition
\begin{equation}
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\frac{\dd\rho(s)}{1+s}<\infty ,
  \label{eq:stieltjes-integrability-condition}
\end{equation}
then, after Fourier transform,
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde G_E(Q^2)
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}
  \frac{\dd\rho(s)}{Q^2+s}.
  \label{eq:euclidean-stieltjes-propagator}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:stieltjes-integrability-condition} is automatic if
\(\rho([0,\infty))<\infty\), as in the canonically normalized elementary scalar
case discussed below.  It is not automatic for arbitrary local composite
operators; for them the two-point function may require subtractions, and
polynomial ambiguities in \(Q^2\) are precisely the momentum-space form of
contact terms at coincident Euclidean points.

Under \eqref{eq:stieltjes-integrability-condition}, \(\widetilde G_E\) is a
Stieltjes transform of a positive measure.  More precisely,
\[
  F(z)=\int_{[0,\infty)}\frac{\dd\rho(s)}{z+s}
\]
converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets of
\(\mathbb C\setminus(-\infty,0]\): for every compact
\(K\subset\mathbb C\setminus(-\infty,0]\) there is \(c_K>0\) such that
\(|z+s|\ge c_K(1+s)\) for \(z\in K\) and \(s\ge0\).  Dominated convergence
then permits differentiation under the integral sign, so \(F\) is holomorphic
on this cut plane and its restriction to \(z=Q^2>0\) is the Euclidean function in
\eqref{eq:euclidean-stieltjes-propagator}.  The endpoint \(z=0\) is part of
the cut if the spectrum has support arbitrarily near \(s=0\); no continuation
through that endpoint is being assumed.  If the spectral support begins at a
positive mass gap \(s=m_0^2>0\), the cut instead begins at \(z=-m_0^2\), and
the function is analytic in a neighborhood of \(z=0\).
Consequently, for every \(Q^2>0\),
\[
  (-1)^r
  \frac{\dd^r}{\dd (Q^2)^r}\widetilde G_E(Q^2)
  =
  r!\int_{[0,\infty)}
  \frac{\dd\rho(s)}{(Q^2+s)^{r+1}}
  \ge0,
  \qquad r=0,1,2,\ldots .
\]
These inequalities are not perturbative statements.  They are Hilbert-space
positivity written in Euclidean momentum variables.

The measure can be recovered from the discontinuity across the negative real
\(z\)-axis.  The continuation is performed by treating \(z\) as a complex
variable on the cut plane and approaching the point \(z=-s\) from the two
sides of the cut.  If the absolutely continuous part has density
\(\rho_{\rm ac}(s)\), then
\[
  \rho_{\rm ac}(s)
  =
  -\frac{1}{2\pi\ii}
  \operatorname{Disc}_{z=-s}\widetilde G_E(z),
  \qquad s>0,
\]
with atoms appearing as simple poles at \(z=-s_0\) of positive residue.  This
is the Euclidean form of the statement that stable particles are isolated
positive atoms of the spectral measure and multiparticle states are continuous
spectral support.

For a more singular local field, the spectral measure may have only tempered
growth, by which we mean here that there is an integer \(N\ge0\) such that
\[
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\frac{\dd\rho(s)}{(1+s)^{N+1}}<\infty .
\]
The unsubtracted Stieltjes integral need not define an ordinary function.  The
nonlocal part of the Euclidean momentum-space distribution is nevertheless
characterized by its \((N+1)\)-st derivative,
\[
  F^{(N+1)}(z)
  =
  (-1)^{N+1}(N+1)!
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\frac{\dd\rho(s)}{(z+s)^{N+2}} .
\]
The right-hand side is holomorphic on the same cut plane.  Integrating this
equation back determines a representative \(F(z)\) only up to a polynomial of
degree \(N\), which is exactly the freedom to add contact terms.  The
discontinuity across the cut, and therefore the spectral measure away from
contact terms, is unaffected by this polynomial ambiguity.

\section{Canonical Normalization and Spectral Weight}

The measure \(d\rho\) depends on the normalization of the local field.  A
canonical equal-time normalization consists of a regularity datum on the
operator-valued distribution together with a smeared commutator identity.
Let \(d=D-1\).  Assume there is a dense common invariant domain
\(\mathcal D\subset\Hilb\), with \(\vac\in\mathcal D\), such that for every
\(f\in\mathcal S(\mathbb R^d)\) the smeared equal-time fields
\[
  \widehat\phi_t(f)=\int\dd^d\vec x\,f(\vec x)\widehat\phi(t,\vec x),
  \qquad
  \widehat\pi_t(f)=\int\dd^d\vec x\,f(\vec x)\widehat\pi(t,\vec x)
\]
preserve \(\mathcal D\), and
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd t}\widehat\phi_t(f)\psi
  =
  \widehat\pi_t(f)\psi,
  \qquad \psi\in\mathcal D,
\]
where the derivative is taken in the Hilbert norm.  This is \(C^1\)
distributional time-regularity of \(\widehat\phi\).  The canonical
normalization is the domain identity
\[
  [\widehat\phi_t(f),\widehat\pi_t(g)]\psi
  =
  \ii\int_{\mathbb R^d} f(\vec x)g(\vec x)\,\dd^d\vec x\,\psi,
  \qquad f,g\in\mathcal S(\mathbb R^d),\quad \psi\in\mathcal D .
\]
The formal notation
\[
  [\widehat\phi(t,\vec x),\partial_t\widehat\phi(t,\vec y)]
  =
  \ii\,\delta^{(d)}(\vec x-\vec y)\mathbf 1
\]
means precisely this smeared statement with
\(\widehat\pi_t=\partial_t\widehat\phi_t\) as an operator-valued distribution.
An interacting local field used as an interpolating operator need not satisfy
this canonical identity.  If an analogous equal-time commutator exists with a
central normalization \(C_\phi\ge0\),
\[
  [\widehat\phi_t(f),\widehat\pi_t(g)]\psi
  =
  \ii C_\phi
  \int_{\mathbb R^d} f(\vec x)g(\vec x)\,\dd^d\vec x\,\psi ,
\]
then the same argument below gives \(\int\dd\rho=C_\phi\).  If no such
equal-time normalization is part of the field data, the K\"all\'en--Lehmann
representation by itself gives positivity and support, but not a total-weight
sum rule.

The commutator two-point distribution is
\[
  C(x):=
  \langle\vac|[\widehat\phi(x),\widehat\phi(0)]|\vac\rangle
  =
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\dd\rho(s)\,
  \bigl(\Delta_+(x;s)-\Delta_+(-x;s)\bigr).
\]
The sign of the equal-time derivative is fixed by commutator antisymmetry.
The canonical identity gives, in smeared form,
\([\widehat\phi_0(\vec r),\widehat\pi_0(\vec0)]
=\ii\delta^{(d)}(\vec r)\mathbf1\).  Since the derivative
\(\partial_t C(t,\vec r)|_{t=0}\) differentiates the first field in
\([\widehat\phi(t,\vec r),\widehat\phi(0,\vec0)]\), it produces
\[
  [\widehat\pi_0(\vec r),\widehat\phi_0(\vec0)]
  =
  -[\widehat\phi_0(\vec0),\widehat\pi_0(\vec r)]
  =
  -\ii\delta^{(d)}(\vec r)\mathbf1,
\]
where the last equality uses the symmetry of the spatial delta distribution.
The \(C^1\) hypothesis gives the equal-time derivative as a spatial tempered
distribution:
\begin{equation}
  \left.\partial_t C(t,\vec r)\right|_{t=0}
  =
  \langle\vac|[\widehat\pi_0(\vec r),\widehat\phi_0(\vec 0)]|\vac\rangle
  =
  -\ii\,\delta^{(d)}(\vec r).
  \label{eq:kallen-lehmann-equal-time-commutator-derivative}
\end{equation}

It remains to justify the spectral calculation of the same derivative.  Let
\(\rho_R\) be the restriction of \(\rho\) to \([0,R]\), and let
\[
  C_R(t,\vec r)=
  \int_{[0,R]}\dd\rho(s)\,
  \bigl(\Delta_+(t,\vec r;s)-\Delta_+(-t,-\vec r;s)\bigr).
\]
The measure \(\rho_R\) is finite by local finiteness.  For a spatial test
function \(h\in\mathcal S(\mathbb R^d)\), set
\[
  F_s(t;h)=
  \int_{\mathbb R^d}\dd^d\vec r\,h(\vec r)
  \bigl(\Delta_+(t,\vec r;s)-\Delta_+(-t,-\vec r;s)\bigr).
\]
Writing \(\omega_{\vec p,s}=\sqrt{\vec p^{\,2}+s}\), the spatial Fourier
representation gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \frac{\dd}{\dd t}F_s(t;h)
  &=
  -\frac{\ii}{2}
  \int\frac{\dd^d\vec p}{(2\pi)^d}
  \left(
    \widetilde h(\vec p)\ee^{-\ii\omega_{\vec p,s}t}
    +
    \widetilde h(-\vec p)\ee^{+\ii\omega_{\vec p,s}t}
  \right),
\end{aligned}
\]
where
\[
  \widetilde h(\vec p)
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R^d}\dd^d\vec r\,
  h(\vec r)\ee^{\ii\vec p\cdot\vec r}.
\]
The bound
\[
  \left|\frac{\dd}{\dd t}F_s(t;h)\right|
  \le
  \int\frac{\dd^d\vec p}{(2\pi)^d}\,|\widetilde h(\vec p)|
\]
is uniform in \(s\ge0\) and \(t\).  Hence, for every finite \(R\), dominated
convergence permits the derivative to pass through the \(s\)-integral.  At
\(t=0\),
\[
  \left.\frac{\dd}{\dd t}F_s(t;h)\right|_{t=0}
  =
  -\ii h(\vec0).
\]
Therefore
\[
  \left\langle
    \left.\partial_t C_R(t,\cdot)\right|_{t=0},
    h
  \right\rangle
  =
  -\ii\,\rho([0,R])\,h(\vec0).
\]

The final limiting step is part of the equal-time regularity assumption: the
spectral truncations \(C_R\) converge to \(C\) after applying the operation
\(\partial_t|_{t=0}\) in \(\mathcal S'(\mathbb R^d)\).  Comparing with
\eqref{eq:kallen-lehmann-equal-time-commutator-derivative} gives
\[
  \rho([0,R])\longrightarrow 1
  \qquad (R\to\infty),
\]
and monotone convergence yields the canonical sum rule
\begin{equation}
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\dd\rho(s)=1.
  \label{eq:kallen-lehmann-canonical-sum-rule}
\end{equation}
It fixes the chosen field normalization; changing the field normalization
rescales the spectral measure.

If the same canonically normalized field has a one-particle atomic contribution
\(Z\,\delta_{m^2}(\dd s)\), where \(\delta_{m^2}\) is the unit point measure at
\(s=m^2\), positivity of \(d\rho\) and
\eqref{eq:kallen-lehmann-canonical-sum-rule} imply
\[
  0\le Z\le1.
\]
The remainder \(1-Z\) is the total spectral weight carried by all continuum
states and by any other atoms in this normalization.  For a noncanonical
interpolating field, a composite operator, or a renormalized field in a
different convention, the same K\"all\'en--Lehmann representation holds, but
\eqref{eq:kallen-lehmann-canonical-sum-rule} is replaced, when an equal-time
commutator normalization exists, by
\[
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\dd\rho(s)=C_\phi .
\]
Then positivity gives \(0\le Z\le C_\phi\).  Without such a finite
normalization, the coefficient \(Z\) is still a positive spectral weight of the
chosen interpolating field, but it has no universal upper bound; rescaling the
field rescales \(Z\).  The canonical free field used later in LSZ is the
asymptotic field on the one-particle Fock space, normalized separately from the
interpolating local field whose correlators contain the pole.

\begin{remark}[Support hypothesis for a single massive scalar channel]
\label{rem:kallen-lehmann-single-massive-support}
The lower endpoint \(0\) in the K\"all\'en--Lehmann integral is conventional:
it is the possible lower endpoint of invariant mass squared under the spectrum
condition, not a claim that the chosen field has spectral weight at zero.  In
the special situation used by the elementary scalar LSZ discussion, assume:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item the theory has no massless particle seen by \(\widehat\phi\), so
    \(d\rho_\phi(\{0\})=0\);
  \item the field has nonzero overlap with a stable scalar one-particle mass
    \(m>0\), giving the atom \(Z_\phi\delta_{m^2}(\dd s)\);
  \item there are no lighter massive particles or bound-state atoms in this
    channel; and
  \item the non-one-particle part created by \(\widehat\phi\) begins with
    states containing at least two particles of mass \(m\).
\end{enumerate}

Then the non-vacuum spectral measure has the support decomposition
\[
  \operatorname{supp}d\rho_\phi
  \subset
  \{m^2\}\cup[4m^2,\infty),
  \qquad
  \dd\rho_\phi(s)
  =
  Z_\phi\delta_{m^2}(\dd s)+\dd\rho_{\phi,\mathrm c}(s),
\]
with \(\operatorname{supp}d\rho_{\phi,\mathrm c}\subset[4m^2,\infty)\).  This
gap between the atom and the rest of the support is the spectral input used
later by Haag--Ruelle and LSZ.  If massless particles, infraparticle
thresholds, or additional bound-state atoms are present, the displayed support
decomposition must be replaced by the corresponding spectral statement.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Existence of an isolated mass shell is a spectral statement]
\label{rem:kallen-lehmann-mass-shell-existence-status}
The K\"all\'en--Lehmann representation does not imply the existence of a
one-particle state.  For a fixed scalar local field \(\widehat\phi\), a term
\[
  \dd\rho_\phi(s)=Z_\phi\,\delta_{m^2}(\dd s)+\dd\rho_{\phi,\mathrm c}(s),
  \qquad Z_\phi>0,
\]
is exactly the statement that \(\widehat\phi\vac\) has nonzero spectral
projection onto the positive mass shell of invariant mass \(m\).  A positive
measure whose support begins at \(m^2\) but has no atom at \(m^2\) gives a
threshold, not a normalizable Wigner one-particle vector created by that
field.

The field-independent Hilbert-space assertion needed by Haag--Ruelle theory is
stronger and has two parts.  With \(E\) the joint spectral measure of
\(P^\mu\) and
\[
  \Sigma_m^+=\{p:p^0>0,\ p^2=-m^2\},
\]
one must have
\[
  E(\Sigma_m^+)\Hilb\ne\{0\},
  \qquad
  \operatorname{Spec}(P)\cap N=\Sigma_m^+
\]
for some open neighborhood \(N\) of \(\Sigma_m^+\).  The first condition says
that the one-particle subspace is nonzero; the second says that the shell is
isolated from the rest of the energy-momentum spectrum.  If the first condition
fails, the notation \(E(\Sigma_m^+)\Hilb\) denotes the zero subspace and the
corresponding scattering construction is vacuous.  If the second condition
fails, the sharp Wigner particle hypothesis required by ordinary
Haag--Ruelle estimates is absent even when spectral weight accumulates at the
same invariant mass.

An atom may be invisible to a particular interpolating field if that field has
zero matrix element to the corresponding superselection sector or internal
channel.  Conversely, absence of an atom in every physical local
interpolating field is evidence for absence of that stable particle sector, but
the invariant statement is the vanishing of the spectral projection on the
physical Hilbert space.  The nonzero isolated-shell property is a theorem in
free massive fields and in those constructive massive models for which the
Wightman theory, the mass gap, and a separated one-particle spectral component
have actually been proved.  Formal perturbation theory alone supplies
candidate pole data rather than this Hilbert-space spectral theorem.
\end{remark}

\section{Scalar One-Particle States and Normalization}

Assume now that the Hilbert space contains an isolated positive-mass
one-particle subspace with mass \(m>0\). In this section ``scalar particle''
means that the corresponding massive Wigner representation has trivial
little-group representation and no internal degeneracy. This is a property of
the one-particle representation; it is not the same statement as saying that a
particular local field is a Lorentz scalar.

Let
\[
  k_R^\mu=(m,\vec 0),
  \qquad
  k^\mu=(\omega_{\vec k},\vec k),
  \qquad
  \omega_{\vec k}=\sqrt{\vec k^{\,2}+m^2}.
\]
Choose, for every \(\vec k\), a standard boost \(L(\vec k)\in SO^+(1,D-1)\)
such that
\[
  L(\vec k)k_R=k.
\]
With generalized momentum eigenvectors normalized by
\[
  {}_\delta\langle\vec k\,{}'|\vec k\rangle_\delta
  =
  \delta^{(D-1)}(\vec k\,{}'-\vec k),
\]
write
\[
  |\vec k\rangle_\delta
  =
  N(\vec k)\,U(L(\vec k))|\vec 0\rangle_\delta,
  \qquad
  N(\vec 0)=1.
\]

For a Lorentz transformation \(\Lambda\), define the Wigner rotation
\[
  W(\Lambda,k)=L(\Lambda k)^{-1}\Lambda L(k).
\]
It fixes \(k_R\), hence lies in the little group \(SO(D-1)\). For the scalar
one-particle representation, \(U(W(\Lambda,k))\) acts trivially on the rest
state. Therefore
\[
  U(\Lambda)|\vec k\rangle_\delta
  =
  \frac{N(\vec k)}{N(\Lambda\vec k)}
  |\Lambda\vec k\rangle_\delta,
\]
where \(\Lambda\vec k\) denotes the spatial part of \(\Lambda k\).
Unitarity fixes \(N\). Since the on-shell measure
\[
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec k}{2\omega_{\vec k}}
\]
is Lorentz invariant,
\[
  \delta^{(D-1)}(\Lambda\vec k\,{}'-\Lambda\vec k)
  =
  \frac{k^0}{(\Lambda k)^0}
  \delta^{(D-1)}(\vec k\,{}'-\vec k).
\]
Comparing
\[
  {}_\delta\langle\vec k\,{}'|\vec k\rangle_\delta
  =
  {}_\delta\langle\vec k\,{}'|U(\Lambda)^\dagger U(\Lambda)|\vec k\rangle_\delta
\]
gives
\[
  |N(\Lambda\vec k)|^2
  =
  |N(\vec k)|^2\,\frac{k^0}{(\Lambda k)^0}.
\]
Taking \(k=k_R\) and \(\Lambda=L(\vec k)\), together with \(N(\vec0)=1\),
yields
\[
  N(\vec k)=\sqrt{\frac{m}{\omega_{\vec k}}},
\]
after fixing the harmless phase convention.

It is often better to use invariantly normalized states
\[
  |\vec k\rangle_{\rm inv}
  =
  \sqrt{(2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec k}}\,
  |\vec k\rangle_\delta,
\]
for which
\[
  {}_{\rm inv}\langle\vec k\,{}'|\vec k\rangle_{\rm inv}
  =
  (2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec k}\,
  \delta^{(D-1)}(\vec k\,{}'-\vec k),
  \qquad
  U(\Lambda)|\vec k\rangle_{\rm inv}=|\Lambda\vec k\rangle_{\rm inv}.
\]

If \(\widehat\phi\) is a scalar local field and the vacuum is invariant, then
the matrix element
\[
  A(\vec k)
  =
  {}_\delta\langle\vec k|\widehat\phi(0)|\vac\rangle
\]
has the same \(\vec k\)-dependence as \(N(\vec k)\). Indeed,
\(U(\Lambda)\widehat\phi(0)U(\Lambda)^{-1}=\widehat\phi(0)\) and
\(U(\Lambda)\vac=\vac\) give
\[
  A(\vec k)
  =
  \frac{N(\vec k)}{N(\Lambda\vec k)}
  A(\Lambda\vec k).
\]
Choosing \(\Lambda=L(\vec k)^{-1}\) gives
\[
  A(\vec k)=N(\vec k)A(\vec 0),
\]
up to the phase convention already fixed in the definition of the
one-particle states. Thus there is a nonnegative number \(Z\), depending on the
chosen field, such that
\[
  {}_\delta\langle\vec k|\widehat\phi(0)|\vac\rangle
  =
  \left[
    \frac{Z}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec k}}
  \right]^{1/2},
\]
or equivalently
\[
  {}_{\rm inv}\langle\vec k|\widehat\phi(0)|\vac\rangle
  =
  Z^{1/2}.
\]

\section{One-Particle Atoms}

Assume the spectrum contains an isolated scalar one-particle mass \(m>0\), and
assume the local field \(\widehat\phi\) has nonzero projection onto that
one-particle subspace. With the invariant normalization from the one-particle
construction,
\[
  \langle\vec p\,{}'|\vec p\rangle
  =
  (2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec p}\,
  \delta^{(D-1)}(\vec p\,{}'-\vec p),
  \qquad
  \omega_{\vec p}=\sqrt{\vec p^{\,2}+m^2},
\]
define \(Z\ge0\) by
\[
  {}_{\rm inv}\langle\vec p|\widehat\phi(0)|\vac\rangle=Z^{1/2}
\]
for scalar one-particle states. Lorentz covariance makes the right-hand side
independent of \(\vec p\) in this normalization. Let \(\delta_{m^2}\) denote
the unit point measure at \(\mu^2=m^2\). The one-particle contribution to the
spectral measure is
\[
  \dd\rho_1(\mu^2)=Z\,\delta_{m^2}(\dd\mu^2).
\]
Thus
\[
  \dd\rho(\mu^2)
  =
  Z\,\delta_{m^2}(\dd\mu^2)
  +
  \dd\rho_{\mathrm c}(\mu^2),
\]
where \(d\rho_{\mathrm c}\) is the remaining positive measure.
At the level of the momentum-space measure, using the delta-normalized states
in the previous section, the same statement is the calculation
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \dd\nu_{\phi,1}(p)
  &=
  \int\dd^{D-1}\vec k\,
  \left|
    {}_\delta\langle\vec k|\widehat\phi(0)|\vac\rangle
  \right|^2
  \delta^{(D)}(p-k)\,\dd^Dp
  \\
  &=
  \int\dd^{D-1}\vec k\,
  \frac{Z}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec k}}\,
  \delta^{(D)}(p-k)\,\dd^Dp
  =
  \frac{Z\,\dd^Dp}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  \theta(p^0)\delta(p^2+m^2).
\end{aligned}
\]
Comparison with the invariant-mass decomposition then gives
\(\dd\rho_1(\mu^2)=Z\,\delta_{m^2}(\dd\mu^2)\).

The number \(Z\) is attached to the chosen local field. Rescaling the field

matrix element and the one-particle pole residue of its two-point function.
It is therefore not an unphysical datum in the following precise sense: it is
not invariant under changing the operator whose two-point function is being
studied, but for a fixed operator it is an unambiguous spectral weight.

\section{Role in the Construction}

The K\"all\'en--Lehmann representation is nonperturbative. It states that
Hilbert-space positivity and the spectrum condition force the scalar
two-point function to be a positive superposition of free massive two-point
functions. Isolated atoms of the measure identify stable one-particle mass
shells seen by the local field. Continuous spectral support records the
remaining invariant-mass spectrum.

The local field \(\widehat\phi\) in this chapter is an interpolating local
field.  Its time-ordered two-point function contains the full spectral measure
\[
  G_T^\phi(x)
  =
  Z\,\Delta_F(x;m^2)
  +
  \int_{[0,\infty)}\dd\rho_{\mathrm c}(s)\,\Delta_F(x;s)
\]
whenever an isolated one-particle atom of mass \(m\) is present.  The
asymptotic scalar field associated with that atom is a different object: it is
the canonical free scalar field on the asymptotic Fock space generated by the
one-particle Hilbert space \(\Hilb_1\), and its two-point function is
\[
  G_T^{\mathrm{as}}(x)=\Delta_F(x;m^2)
\]
with unit one-particle residue and no continuum part.  The relation between the
two fields is the one-particle projection and normalization
\[
  P_1\widehat\phi(f)\vac
  =
  Z^{1/2}\psi_f,
\]
where, in the Fourier convention
\[
  f_-(p)=\int\dd^D x\,f(x)\ee^{\ii p\cdot x},
\]
\[
  \psi_f=
  \int_{\Sigma_m^+}\dd\mu_m(p)\,f_-(p)|p\rangle
  \in\Hilb_1 .
\]
Haag--Ruelle constructs the asymptotic Fock space from such vectors, and LSZ
extracts the corresponding pole coefficient from correlation functions of the
interpolating local field.

\begin{remark}[Infraparticle spectrum in charged QED]
The word ``atom'' here means a literal point mass in the positive spectral
measure, for example \(Z\,\delta_{m^2}(\dd s)\) with \(Z>0\).  This is exactly
the input needed later by Haag--Ruelle theory and by LSZ pole-coefficient
extraction.  The
charged sector of QED with a massless photon realizes a different spectral
structure.  It contains the charged threshold together with states differing by
photons of arbitrarily small energy; the energy-momentum spectral support
therefore accumulates at the threshold instead of leaving an open neighborhood
containing only \(\Sigma_m^+\).  Equivalently, the charged particle is an
infraparticle: threshold spectral support replaces the simple one-particle
pole, and the charged sector has no normalizable one-particle Wigner subspace
obtained from \(P_1=E(\Sigma_m^+)\).

Gauge-fixed perturbative notation must be interpreted through this physical
spectral statement.  A gauge-dependent electron propagator is not, by itself,
the positive K\"all\'en--Lehmann measure of a compactly localized physical
observable in the Hilbert space.  Physical charged asymptotic data require
either inclusive probabilities or dressed charged states.  The later infrared
chapters develop the corresponding Bloch--Nordsieck cancellation and
Faddeev--Kulish-type dressing constructions in
Chapter~\ref{ch:qed-external-states} and
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-infrared-inclusive-qed}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Confinement and physical spectral atoms]
In a confining nonabelian gauge theory the positive Hilbert-space spectral
measure is attached to fields or observables that act on the physical Hilbert
space.  Gauge-invariant color-singlet interpolating fields have
K\"all\'en--Lehmann measures supported on hadronic states; when such a measure
has an isolated atom, the atom corresponds to a stable hadron or glueball seen
by that interpolating field.  Gauge-dependent quark and gluon fields are not
local physical observables with positive spectral measures on the physical
Hilbert space, and confinement excludes isolated quark or gluon mass-shell
atoms from the physical asymptotic spectrum.  Consequently the Haag--Ruelle and
LSZ constructions later in this volume apply in a confining theory only to
stable color-singlet particles, after the corresponding physical interpolating
operators and isolated hadronic atoms have been identified.
\end{remark}

The next step in the construction is the perturbative computation of Green
functions. The construction of scattering states and the S-matrix comes later,
after the spectral one-particle data have been separated from the local
two-point functions.
