\chapter{Symmetries, Noether Currents, and Stress Tensors}
\label{ch:symmetries-noether-stress}
\ConventionsLorentzian


\section{Variational Framework}

Work on \(D\)-dimensional Minkowski spacetime \(\mathbb M^D\), with mostly-plus
metric \(\eta_{\mu\nu}\). Let \(A\) range over a finite set of bosonic field
components. A classical field configuration is written
\[
  \phi=\{\phi^A\}.
\]
In this chapter the Lagrangian density is a first-derivative local expression
\[
  \mathcal L=\mathcal L(\phi^A,\partial_\mu\phi^A).
\]
The action over a spacetime region \(M\subset\mathbb M^D\) is
\[
  S_M[\phi]=\int_M\dd^Dx\,\mathcal L(\phi,\partial\phi).
\]
Variations are assumed smooth. When no boundary term is being studied, the
variation has compact support in \(M\).

Define the Euler--Lagrange expression
\[
  E_A(\mathcal L)
  =
  \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\phi^A}
  -
  \partial_\mu
  \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi^A)} .
\]
For a variation \(\delta\phi^A\),
\[
  \delta\mathcal L
  =
  E_A(\mathcal L)\,\delta\phi^A
  +
  \partial_\mu\theta^\mu(\delta\phi),
\]
where repeated \(A\) and spacetime indices are summed and
\[
  \theta^\mu(\delta\phi)
  =
  \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi^A)}
  \delta\phi^A .
\]
Thus
\[
  \delta S_M
  =
  \int_M\dd^Dx\,E_A(\mathcal L)\,\delta\phi^A
  +
  \int_{\partial M}\dd\Sigma_\mu\,\theta^\mu(\delta\phi),
\]
where \(\dd\Sigma_\mu\) is the oriented hypersurface measure on
\(\partial M\). A classical solution is a field configuration satisfying
\[
  E_A(\mathcal L)=0
\]
for every component \(A\), with whatever boundary conditions are part of the
problem.

\section{Infinitesimal Symmetries}

An infinitesimal field-space transformation is a rule
\[
  \delta_R\phi^A=R^A[\phi],
\]
where \(R^A[\phi]\) is a local expression in \(x\), the fields, and finitely
many derivatives of the fields. The transformation is a variational symmetry
of the action when there exists a local vector \(K_R^\mu\) such that
\[
  \delta_R\mathcal L=\partial_\mu K_R^\mu .
\]
This equation is the definition of the symmetry in the present classical
Lagrangian framework. It is local in the fields and is stated before imposing
the Euler--Lagrange equation.

Substituting \(\delta_R\phi^A=R^A\) into the variational identity gives
\[
  E_A(\mathcal L)R^A
  +
  \partial_\mu
  \left(
    \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi^A)}R^A
    -
    K_R^\mu
  \right)
  =
  0.
\]
The Noether current associated to the chosen representative \(R\) is therefore
\[
  j_R^\mu
  =
  \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi^A)}R^A
  -
  K_R^\mu .
\]
On solutions,
\[
  \partial_\mu j_R^\mu=0.
\]
The same current can be identified by localizing the symmetry parameter. Let
\(\epsilon\in C_c^\infty(M)\), replace the constant infinitesimal parameter by
\(\epsilon(x)\), and use
\[
  \delta_{\epsilon R}\phi^A=\epsilon R^A .
\]
Modulo the compactly supported total derivative
\(\partial_\mu(\epsilon K_R^\mu)\), the variation of the action is
\[
  \delta_{\epsilon R}S_M
  =
  \int_M\dd^Dx\,(\partial_\mu\epsilon)\,j_R^\mu .
\]
If the field is a solution, the compactly supported variation has zero first
variation; hence integration by parts gives
\[
  0
  =
  \delta_{\epsilon R}S_M
  =
  \int_M\dd^Dx\,(\partial_\mu\epsilon)\,j_R^\mu
  =
  -\int_M\dd^Dx\,\epsilon\,\partial_\mu j_R^\mu .
\]
Since \(\epsilon\) is arbitrary with compact support, this is equivalent to the
on-shell conservation law. This local-parameter test is often the cleanest way
to read off the current, because \(j_R^\mu\) is precisely the coefficient of
\(\partial_\mu\epsilon\) after the constant-parameter total derivative has been
separated.

The current is a representative of a local equivalence class. If
\[
  B_R^{\nu\mu}=-B_R^{\mu\nu},
\]
then
\[
  j_R^\mu\longmapsto j_R^\mu+\partial_\nu B_R^{\nu\mu}
\]
has the same local divergence, because
\(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu B_R^{\nu\mu}=0\). The corresponding charge is
unchanged when the surface integral of \(B_R\) at spatial boundary vanishes.

\section{Charges and Generators}

Let \(\Sigma_t=\{x^0=t\}\) be a constant-time slice. If \(j_R^\mu\) is
conserved and the spatial integral exists, define
\[
  Q_R(t)=\int_{\Sigma_t}\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,j_R^0(t,\vec x).
\]
For a spatial region \(V\subset\mathbb R^{D-1}\),
\[
  Q_R^V(t)=\int_V\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,j_R^0(t,\vec x).
\]
Current conservation gives
\[
  Q_R^V(t_2)-Q_R^V(t_1)
  =
  -\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\dd x^0
  \int_{\partial V}\dd S_i\,j_R^i(x^0,\vec x).
\]
Thus \(Q_R(t)\) is time independent when the flux through spatial boundary is
zero in the limit \(V\nearrow\mathbb R^{D-1}\).

The equal-time Poisson bracket convention is the one used in the scalar-field
construction:
\[
  \{F,G\}
  =
  \int\dd^{D-1}\vec x
  \left[
    \frac{\delta F}{\delta\phi^A(\vec x)}
    \frac{\delta G}{\delta\Pi_A(\vec x)}
    -
    \frac{\delta F}{\delta\Pi_A(\vec x)}
    \frac{\delta G}{\delta\phi^A(\vec x)}
  \right].
\]
It gives
\[
  \{\phi^A(\vec x),\Pi_B(\vec y)\}
  =
  \delta^A{}_B\,\delta^{(D-1)}(\vec x-\vec y).
\]
A charge \(Q_R\) generates the infinitesimal transformation \(R\) on canonical
observables when
\[
  \delta_R F=\{F,Q_R\}
\]
for functionals \(F\) on phase space in the domain where the functional
derivatives and boundary terms are defined. In particular,
\[
  R^A(t,\vec x)=\{\phi^A(t,\vec x),Q_R\}.
\]

For the common first-order case in which \(K_R^0=0\) and \(R^A\) is a function
of canonical coordinates and spatial derivatives but not of velocities, the
charge has the form
\[
  Q_R=\int_{\Sigma_t}\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,\Pi_A R^A ,
\]
up to possible spatial-divergence improvements. Then
\(\delta Q_R/\delta\Pi_A=R^A\), and the displayed generator identity follows
directly from the equal-time bracket. When \(R^A\) or \(K_R^0\) contains
velocities, the Noether charge is first rewritten as a phase-space functional.
For example, a time translation by \(\tau\) gives
\[
  R^A=-\tau\dot\phi^A,\qquad Q_R=-\tau H,
\]
so Hamilton's equation yields
\[
  \{\phi^A,-\tau H\}=-\tau\dot\phi^A .
\]
Thus the generator statement is a statement on phase space, with the Noether
charge expressed in canonical variables.

\section{Poincare Transformations of Scalar Fields}

For a scalar field, a finite Poincare transformation is described by
\[
  x'^\mu=\Lambda^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu+a^\mu,\qquad
  \phi'(x')=\phi(x),
\]
where \(\Lambda^T\eta\Lambda=\eta\). Write
\(\Lambda^\mu{}_\nu=\delta^\mu{}_\nu+\omega^\mu{}_\nu+O(\omega^2)\), with
\(\omega_{\mu\nu}=-\omega_{\nu\mu}\). The fixed-coordinate infinitesimal
variation is
\[
  \delta\phi(x)
  =
  -\left(a^\mu+\omega^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu\right)\partial_\mu\phi(x).
\]
The translation and Lorentz currents below are the Noether currents for the
two independent parameters \(a^\mu\) and \(\omega_{\mu\nu}\).

\section{Translation Symmetry and the Stress Tensor}

Assume that \(\mathcal L\) has no explicit dependence on \(x\). For a constant
vector \(\xi^\nu\), use the fixed-coordinate field variation
\[
  \delta_\xi\phi^A(x)=-\xi^\nu\partial_\nu\phi^A(x).
\]
Since \(\mathcal L\) is a scalar local expression,
\[
  \delta_\xi\mathcal L=-\xi^\nu\partial_\nu\mathcal L
  =
  \partial_\mu K^\mu_\xi,
  \qquad
  K^\mu_\xi=-\xi^\mu\mathcal L.
\]
The corresponding Noether current is written
\[
  j^\mu_\xi=\xi^\nu T^\mu{}_\nu,
\]
where
\[
  T^\mu{}_\nu
  =
  -
  \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi^A)}
  \partial_\nu\phi^A
  +
  \delta^\mu{}_\nu\,\mathcal L .
\]
On solutions,
\[
  \partial_\mu T^\mu{}_\nu=0.
\]
Equivalently, let \(\xi^\nu(x)\) be a compactly supported vector field and use
\(\delta_\xi\phi^A=-\xi^\nu\partial_\nu\phi^A\). After separating the total
derivative \(-\partial_\mu(\xi^\mu\mathcal L)\), the action variation is
\[
  \delta_\xi S_M
  =
  \int_M\dd^Dx\,(\partial_\mu\xi^\nu)\,T^\mu{}_\nu .
\]
For arbitrary compactly supported \(\xi^\nu\), integration by parts on a
solution is equivalent to \(\partial_\mu T^\mu{}_\nu=0\). Thus
\(T^\mu{}_\nu\) is the response coefficient for a localized translation.
The translation charges are
\[
  P_\nu=\int\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,T^0{}_\nu,
  \qquad
  P^\nu=\eta^{\nu\rho}P_\rho
  =
  \int\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,T^{0\nu}.
\]

For the real scalar field
\[
  \mathcal L
  =
  -\frac12\partial_\rho\phi\,\partial^\rho\phi
  -
  V(\phi),
\]
the stress tensor is
\[
  T^\mu{}_\nu
  =
  \partial^\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi
  -
  \delta^\mu{}_\nu
  \left(
    \frac12\partial_\rho\phi\,\partial^\rho\phi+V(\phi)
  \right).
\]
Raising the second index,
\[
  T^{\mu\nu}=\eta^{\nu\rho}T^\mu{}_\rho,
\]
gives a symmetric tensor in this example. Its \(00\)-component is
\[
  T^{00}
  =
  \frac12(\partial_0\phi)^2
  +
  \frac12\sum_{i=1}^{D-1}(\partial_i\phi)^2
  +
  V(\phi).
\]
Thus for the free scalar field, where
\(V(\phi)=\frac12m^2\phi^2\), \(T^{00}\) is the Hamiltonian density already
obtained by canonical quantization.

\section{Metric Variation and Improvement}

Suppose the flat-space action is obtained by restricting a generally covariant
background-metric action \(S_g[\phi]\) to \(g_{\mu\nu}=\eta_{\mu\nu}\). The
Hilbert stress tensor is defined by
\[
  T_{\mathrm H}^{\mu\nu}(x)
  =
  -\frac{2}{\sqrt{|g|}}
  \frac{\delta S_g}{\delta g_{\mu\nu}(x)}
  \bigg|_{g=\eta}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
  \delta_g S_g
  =
  -\frac12
  \int\dd^Dx\,\sqrt{|g|}\,
  T_{\mathrm H}^{\mu\nu}\,\delta g_{\mu\nu}.
\]
Using
\(\delta g^{\mu\nu}=-g^{\mu\rho}g^{\nu\sigma}\delta g_{\rho\sigma}\), this is
equivalently
\[
  T_{\mathrm H}^{\mu\nu}(x)
  =
  \frac{2}{\sqrt{|g|}}
  g^{\mu\rho}g^{\nu\sigma}
  \frac{\delta S_g}{\delta g^{\rho\sigma}(x)}
  \bigg|_{g=\eta}.
\]
The Euclidean source-functional chapters use the same conversion between
covariant and contravariant metric variations, with \(\sqrt g\) in place of
\(\sqrt{|g|}\).
For the minimally coupled scalar action this definition gives the symmetric
stress tensor displayed above.

Stress tensors that differ by an improvement term
\[
  T^{\mu\nu}\longmapsto
  T^{\mu\nu}+\partial_\rho B^{\rho\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  B^{\rho\mu\nu}=-B^{\mu\rho\nu},
\]
have the same conservation law. They define the same translation charges when
the resulting surface integrals vanish. The choice of representative matters
for local operator identities and for couplings to background geometry, so the
representative must be stated as part of the framework.

\section{Lorentz Currents}

For a scalar field, an infinitesimal Lorentz transformation is specified by
\(\omega_{\mu\nu}=-\omega_{\nu\mu}\) and
\[
  \delta_\omega\phi(x)
  =
  -\omega^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu\partial_\mu\phi(x).
\]
If \(T^{\mu\nu}=T^{\nu\mu}\), the Lorentz current may be written
\[
  j^\mu_\omega
  =
  \frac12\omega_{\rho\sigma}M^{\mu,\rho\sigma},
\]
with
\[
  M^{\mu,\rho\sigma}
  =
  x^\rho T^{\mu\sigma}-x^\sigma T^{\mu\rho}.
\]
Using \(\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0\),
\[
  \partial_\mu M^{\mu,\rho\sigma}
  =
  T^{\rho\sigma}-T^{\sigma\rho}.
\]
Thus \(M^{\mu,\rho\sigma}\) is conserved when the stress tensor is symmetric.
The corresponding Lorentz charges are
\[
  J^{\rho\sigma}
  =
  \int\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,
  \left(
    x^\rho T^{0\sigma}-x^\sigma T^{0\rho}
  \right).
\]

For fields carrying Lorentz indices or spinor indices, the current includes an
additional spin term. In such cases one either keeps the canonical spin current
explicitly or passes to a Belinfante-improved symmetric stress tensor, provided
the improvement is defined and the boundary conditions make the charges
equivalent.

\section{Quantum Currents and Charges}

In a quantum field theory, a current is a vector of operator-valued
distributions \(j^\mu_R\) on a common dense domain \(\mathcal D\subset\Hilb\).
For \(f\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb M^D)\), the smeared current is denoted
\[
  j^\mu_R(f)=\int\dd^Dx\,f(x)j^\mu_R(x).
\]
Current conservation is a distributional statement:
\[
  \partial_\mu j_R^\mu=0.
\]
Equivalently, all matrix elements between vectors in \(\mathcal D\) obey the
corresponding distributional conservation law.

A quantum charge is defined as a limit of smeared local charges when that limit
exists. Choose a compactly supported time smearing \(f_t(x^0)\) concentrated
near \(t\) and spatial cutoff functions \(\chi_R(\vec x)\) with
\(\chi_R\to1\) as \(R\to\infty\). The regulated charge is
\[
  Q_R[f_t,\chi_R]
  =
  \int\dd^Dx\,f_t(x^0)\chi_R(\vec x)j_R^0(x).
\]
If these operators converge on a specified domain, the limit is the charge
operator \(Q_R\). If \(Q_R\) is essentially self-adjoint or has a chosen
self-adjoint extension, it exponentiates to a one-parameter unitary group.

With the fixed-coordinate Noether convention, the infinitesimal quantum action
on an operator \(\widehat{\mathcal O}\) is
\[
  \delta_R\widehat{\mathcal O}
  =
  \frac{\ii}{\hbar}
  [Q_R,\widehat{\mathcal O}]
\]
on the common domain where the commutator is defined. For spacetime
translations the covariance convention of the earlier chapters is
\[
  U(a)^{-1}\widehat\Phi_A(x)U(a)
  =
  \widehat\Phi_A(x+a),
  \qquad
  U(a)=\exp\left(\frac{\ii}{\hbar}a^\nu P_\nu\right).
\]
This is equivalent to
\[
  [P_\nu,\widehat\Phi_A(x)]
  =
  \ii\hbar\,\partial_\nu\widehat\Phi_A(x)
\]
in distributional notation.

When a renormalized stress tensor exists as a local operator-valued
distribution, the Poincare generators are represented by
\[
  P^\nu=\int\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,T^{0\nu}(x),
\]
and, for a symmetric stress tensor in the scalar case,
\[
  J^{\rho\sigma}
  =
  \int\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,
  \left(
    x^\rho T^{0\sigma}(x)-x^\sigma T^{0\rho}(x)
  \right),
\]
with the same regulator-and-limit interpretation as for general charges.

The classical Noether construction supplies the local densities for
Lagrangian models. The quantum theory must specify the operator domains,
renormalization prescription, conservation law, and boundary behavior under
which those densities define charges. Once those data are fixed, the stress
tensor is the local bridge between spacetime symmetry and the generators that
act on states and fields.
