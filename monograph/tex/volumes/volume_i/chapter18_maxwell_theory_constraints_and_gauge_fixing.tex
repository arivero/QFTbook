\chapter{Maxwell Theory, Constraints, and Gauge Fixing}
\label{ch:maxwell-theory-constraints-gauge-fixing}

\section{Representative Fields and Field Strength}

Work on four-dimensional Minkowski space with mostly-plus metric. A Maxwell
configuration in this local discussion is represented by a real one-form
\[
  A=A_\mu(x)\,\dd x^\mu .
\]
Two representatives related by
\[
  A_\mu(x)\longmapsto A_\mu(x)+\partial_\mu\xi(x)
\]
describe the same electromagnetic field. Here \(\xi\) is a real scalar
gauge parameter in the same boundary-condition class as \(A_\mu\). We assume
throughout this chapter that fields are either smeared with compactly
supported test functions or obey boundary conditions for which integrations
by parts have no boundary term. Zero modes of the gauge-fixing operators are
then removed separately; this is part of the definition of the local gauge
slice used below.

The local gauge-invariant field strength is
\[
  F_{\mu\nu}
  =
  \partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu .
\]
The free Maxwell action is
\[
  S[A]
  =
  -\frac14\int\dd^4x\,F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}.
\]
Its Euler--Lagrange equation is
\[
  \partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu}=0 .
\]

With \(x^0=t\) and spatial indices \(i,j=1,2,3\), the Lagrangian density is
\[
  \mathcal L
  =
  \frac12\sum_{i=1}^3(\partial_0A_i-\partial_iA_0)^2
  -
  \frac14\sum_{i,j=1}^3(\partial_iA_j-\partial_jA_i)^2 .
\]
The absence of \(\partial_0A_0\) is the first sign that the vector
potential is a redundant representative rather than four independent
propagating fields.

\section{Canonical Constraint Analysis}

At fixed time let \(A_\mu(t,\vec x)\) be canonical coordinates. For
functionals \(F,G\) of \(A_\mu,\Pi^\mu\), use the equal-time Poisson bracket
\[
  \{F,G\}_{\mathrm P}
  =
  \int\dd^3\vec x\,
  \left(
    \frac{\delta F}{\delta A_\mu(\vec x)}
    \frac{\delta G}{\delta\Pi^\mu(\vec x)}
    -
    \frac{\delta F}{\delta\Pi^\mu(\vec x)}
    \frac{\delta G}{\delta A_\mu(\vec x)}
  \right).
\]
Equivalently,
\[
  \{A_\mu(\vec x),\Pi^\nu(\vec y)\}_{\mathrm P}
  =
  \delta_\mu{}^\nu\delta^3(\vec x-\vec y),
\]
with all \(A\)-\(A\) and \(\Pi\)-\(\Pi\) brackets zero.

The canonical momenta are
\[
  \Pi^\mu(\vec x)
  =
  \frac{\partial\mathcal L}
       {\partial(\partial_0A_\mu(t,\vec x))},
\]
so
\[
  \Pi^0(\vec x)=0,
  \qquad
  \Pi^i(\vec x)=\partial_0A_i-\partial_iA_0 .
\]
The equation
\[
  \chi_1(\vec x)=\Pi^0(\vec x)=0
\]
is the primary constraint. The canonical Hamiltonian is
\[
  H_{\mathrm c}
  =
  \int\dd^3\vec x\,
  \left[
    \frac12\Pi^i\Pi^i
    +
    \frac14F_{ij}F_{ij}
    -
    A_0\,\partial_i\Pi^i
  \right],
\]
after integrating \(\Pi^i\partial_iA_0\) by parts. Preserving the primary
constraint under time evolution gives
\[
  \dot\Pi^0(\vec x)
  =
  \{\Pi^0(\vec x),H_{\mathrm c}\}_{\mathrm P}
  =
  \partial_i\Pi^i(\vec x),
\]
hence the secondary constraint
\[
  \chi_2(\vec x)=\partial_i\Pi^i(\vec x)=0 .
\]
This is Gauss' law in the absence of charges.

The constraints obey
\[
  \{\chi_a(\vec x),\chi_b(\vec y)\}_{\mathrm P}=0,
  \qquad
  a,b=1,2.
\]
They are first-class constraints. Their relation to gauge redundancy is
direct. For a spacetime function \(\zeta\), define at time \(t\)
\[
  G[\zeta]
  =
  \int\dd^3\vec y\,
  \left[
    (\partial_0\zeta)(t,\vec y)\,\Pi^0(\vec y)
    -
    \zeta(t,\vec y)\,\partial_i\Pi^i(\vec y)
  \right].
\]
Then
\[
  \{A_0(\vec x),G[\zeta]\}_{\mathrm P}
  =
  \partial_0\zeta(t,\vec x),
  \qquad
  \{A_i(\vec x),G[\zeta]\}_{\mathrm P}
  =
  \partial_i\zeta(t,\vec x).
\]
Thus the first-class constraints generate
\[
  \delta_\zeta A_\mu(t,\vec x)=\partial_\mu\zeta(t,\vec x)
\]
on the vector-potential representative. The gauge-invariant phase space is
obtained by restricting to the constraint surface and quotienting by these
gauge orbits, or equivalently by choosing a regular slice through the orbits.

\section{Axial Gauge and Reduced Phase Space}

Axial gauge chooses the representative satisfying
\[
  A_3=0 .
\]
Locally along an orbit,
\[
  A_3\longmapsto A_3+\partial_3\xi,
\]
so this is a regular gauge condition for modes on which \(\partial_3\) is
invertible. Modes with \(\partial_3\xi=0\) are residual gauge parameters for
this slice and must be treated separately by boundary conditions or an
additional gauge condition.

There are two equivalent local descriptions. In canonical language the
condition \(A_3=0\) pairs with Gauss' law and allows one to solve
\[
  \partial_3\Pi^3=-\partial_a\Pi^a,
  \qquad a=1,2,
\]
for the longitudinal momentum \(\Pi^3\), again modulo \(\partial_3\) zero
modes. In Lagrangian language one imposes \(A_3=0\) before eliminating the
non-dynamical field \(A_0\). The gauge-fixed Lagrangian density is
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal L_{\mathrm{ax}}
  &=
  \frac12\sum_{a=1}^2(\partial_0A_a-\partial_aA_0)^2
  +
  \frac12(\partial_3A_0)^2  \\
  &\quad
  -
  \frac12(\partial_1A_2-\partial_2A_1)^2
  -
  \frac12\sum_{a=1}^2(\partial_3A_a)^2 .
\end{aligned}
\]
The \(A_0\) equation is
\[
  \sum_{a=1}^2\partial_a(\partial_aA_0-\partial_0A_a)
  +\partial_3^2A_0=0.
\]
With
\[
  \Pi^a=\partial_0A_a-\partial_aA_0,
\]
this becomes
\[
  \sum_{a=1}^2\partial_a\Pi^a+\partial_3^2A_0=0.
\]
The pair
\[
  \Pi^0(\vec x)=0,
  \qquad
  \sum_{a=1}^2\partial_a\Pi^a(\vec x)+\partial_3^2A_0(\vec x)=0
\]
is second-class for nonzero \(k_3\), since
\[
  \left\{
    \Pi^0(\vec x),
    \sum_{a=1}^2\partial_a\Pi^a(\vec y)+\partial_3^2A_0(\vec y)
  \right\}_{\mathrm P}
  =
  \partial_3^2\delta^3(\vec x-\vec y).
\]
Hence one may either introduce the corresponding Dirac bracket or solve
\[
  \partial_3^2A_0
  =
  -\sum_{a=1}^2\partial_a\Pi^a
\]
and eliminate \(A_0,\Pi^0\). For the regular modes of the axial slice the
remaining canonical variables are
\[
  A_a(\vec x),\Pi^a(\vec x),
  \qquad a=1,2.
\]
They are the two canonical degrees of freedom of the free photon.

\section{Gauge Slices and the Faddeev--Popov Factor}

The formal expression
\[
  Z_{\mathrm{formal}}
  =
  \int DA_\mu\,\ee^{\ii S[A]}
\]
integrates over all representatives on each gauge orbit. Let
\(\mathcal G(A)\) be a gauge condition. Locally in field space, and after
removing zero modes, the Faddeev--Popov identity is
\[
  1
  =
  \Delta_{\mathcal G}[A]
  \int D\xi\,\delta\bigl(\mathcal G(A+\partial\xi)\bigr),
\]
where
\[
  \Delta_{\mathcal G}[A]
  =
  \det M_{\mathcal G}[A],
  \qquad
  M_{\mathcal G}[A]\xi
  =
  \left.
  \frac{\dd}{\dd s}
  \mathcal G(A+s\,\partial\xi)
  \right|_{s=0}.
\]
For abelian Maxwell theory with a linear gauge condition, \(M_{\mathcal G}\)
is independent of \(A_\mu\). Its determinant is then an overall
normalization in the free theory. This special simplification is not a
definition of gauge fixing; it is a property of the abelian linear examples
used in this chapter.

A finite-dimensional model isolates the logic. Let
\[
  S(x,y)=(x-y)^2
\]
on \(\R^2\), with gauge orbits
\[
  (x,y)\longmapsto(x+\alpha,y+\alpha).
\]
The ungauged Gaussian integral diverges by the orbit volume. Choose the
linear slice
\[
  \ell(x,y)=y+ax+b=0,
  \qquad a\ne -1.
\]
The Faddeev--Popov factor is the absolute value of the derivative of
\(\ell(x+\alpha,y+\alpha)\) with respect to \(\alpha\),
\[
  \Delta_\ell=|1+a|.
\]
For a gauge-invariant observable \(O(x,y)=O(x+\alpha,y+\alpha)\),
\[
  \frac{
    \int\dd x\,\Delta_\ell\,
    \ee^{-(x+ax+b)^2}O(x,-ax-b)
  }{
    \int\dd x\,\Delta_\ell\,
    \ee^{-(x+ax+b)^2}
  }
\]
is independent of \(a,b\). Indeed, setting
\[
  u=(1+a)x+b
\]
turns the numerator and denominator into the same Gaussian average over
\(u=x-y\); the Jacobian and \(\Delta_\ell\) cancel in the ratio. This is the
finite-dimensional form of gauge-slice independence for gauge-invariant
quantities in a region where the slice is regular.

\section{Axial-Gauge Gaussian and Propagator}

For axial gauge,
\[
  \mathcal G_{\mathrm{ax}}(A)=A_3,
  \qquad
  M_{\mathrm{ax}}=\partial_3 .
\]
After removing the \(\partial_3\) zero modes, the determinant is independent
of \(A_\mu\), and the gauge-fixed Maxwell integral may be written as
\[
  Z_{\mathrm{ax}}
  =
  \int DA_\mu\,\delta(A_3)\,\ee^{\ii S[A]} .
\]
Integrating the Maxwell action by parts gives
\[
  S[A]
  =
  \frac12\int\dd^4x\,
  A_\mu
  \left(\eta^{\mu\nu}\Box-\partial^\mu\partial^\nu\right)
  A_\nu ,
  \qquad
  \Box=\partial^\rho\partial_\rho .
\]
The unfixed quadratic operator has zero directions \(A_\mu=\partial_\mu\xi\).
On the axial slice \(A_3=0\), the remaining block has indices
\(\mu,\nu=0,1,2\). With
\[
  A_\mu(x)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^4k}{(2\pi)^4}\,
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot x}\widetilde A_\mu(k),
\]
the quadratic form is
\[
  S_{\mathrm{ax}}
  =
  \frac12
  \int\frac{\dd^4k}{(2\pi)^4}\,
  \widetilde A_\mu(-k)\,
  \widetilde D_{\mathrm{ax}}^{\mu\nu}(k)\,
  \widetilde A_\nu(k),
\]
where
\[
  \widetilde D_{\mathrm{ax}}^{\mu\nu}(k)
  =
  -\eta^{\mu\nu}k^2+k^\mu k^\nu,
  \qquad
  \mu,\nu=0,1,2.
\]
For \(k^2\ne0\) and \(k_3\ne0\), its inverse on this \(3\times3\) block is
\[
  \bigl(\widetilde D_{\mathrm{ax}}(k)^{-1}\bigr)_{\mu\nu}
  =
  -\frac{1}{k^2}
  \left(
    \eta_{\mu\nu}+\frac{k_\mu k_\nu}{k_3^2}
  \right),
  \qquad
  \mu,\nu=0,1,2.
\]
Indeed,
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\sum_{\rho=0}^2
  \bigl(-\eta^{\mu\rho}k^2+k^\mu k^\rho\bigr)
  \left[
    -\frac1{k^2}
    \left(\eta_{\rho\nu}+\frac{k_\rho k_\nu}{k_3^2}\right)
  \right]  \\
  &\qquad
  =
  \delta^\mu{}_\nu
  -
  \frac{k^\mu k_\nu}{k^2}
  +
  \frac{k^\mu k_\nu}{k_3^2}
  -
  \frac{k^\mu k_\nu}{k^2k_3^2}
  \sum_{\rho=0}^2 k^\rho k_\rho
  =
  \delta^\mu{}_\nu,
\end{aligned}
\]
because
\[
  \sum_{\rho=0}^2 k^\rho k_\rho=k^2-k_3^2 .
\]
The Lorentzian Feynman boundary value therefore gives, for
\(\mu,\nu=0,1,2\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\langle\Omega|
  \operatorname T\{\widehat A_\mu(x)\widehat A_\nu(y)\}
  |\Omega\rangle_{\mathrm{ax}}  \\
  &=
  \int\frac{\dd^4k}{(2\pi)^4}\,
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot(x-y)}
  \frac{-\ii}{k^2-\ii\epsilon}
  \left(
    \eta_{\mu\nu}+\frac{k_\mu k_\nu}{k_3^2}
  \right),
\end{aligned}
\]
with components involving \(A_3\) set to zero in this gauge. The displayed
kernel is singular at \(k_3=0\), precisely where the axial slice has residual
gauge directions.

The field-strength two-point function is obtained by antisymmetrizing
momentum factors. Away from coincident points, equivalently modulo local
contact terms fixed by the definition of time ordering, the \(k_\mu k_\nu\)
part of the axial propagator cancels:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\langle\Omega|
  \operatorname T\{
    \widehat F_{\mu\nu}(x)\widehat F_{\rho\sigma}(y)
  \}
  |\Omega\rangle  \\
  &=
  \int\frac{\dd^4k}{(2\pi)^4}\,
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot(x-y)}
  \frac{-\ii}{k^2-\ii\epsilon}
  \bigl(
    k_\mu k_\rho\eta_{\nu\sigma}
    -k_\nu k_\rho\eta_{\mu\sigma}
    -k_\mu k_\sigma\eta_{\nu\rho}
    +k_\nu k_\sigma\eta_{\mu\rho}
  \bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus the local gauge-invariant correlator has a Lorentz-covariant kernel
although the axial representative propagator does not.

\section{Covariant Gauges}

For perturbative calculations it is often useful to preserve manifest
Lorentz covariance in the representative propagator. The Lorenz gauge
condition is
\[
  \mathcal G_{\mathrm L}(A)=\partial_\mu A^\mu .
\]
Along a gauge orbit,
\[
  \partial_\mu(A^\mu+\partial^\mu\xi)
  =
  \partial_\mu A^\mu+\Box\xi,
\]
so the abelian Faddeev--Popov operator is \(M_{\mathrm L}=\Box\). In the
free abelian theory its determinant is field independent.

Instead of imposing \(\partial_\mu A^\mu=0\) sharply, introduce an auxiliary
function \(\varphi\) and average the condition
\[
  \partial_\mu A^\mu=\varphi
\]
with a Gaussian weight. In Euclidean signature the insertion is
\[
  \int D\varphi\,
  \exp\left[
    -\frac{1}{2\xi_{\mathrm g}}
    \int\dd^4x_E\,\varphi^2
  \right]
  \delta(\partial_\mu A^\mu-\varphi),
  \qquad
  \xi_{\mathrm g}>0.
\]
Equivalently, writing the delta-functional with a real Lagrange multiplier
\(\lambda\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\int D\varphi\,D\lambda\,
  \exp\left[
    -\frac{1}{2\xi_{\mathrm g}}\int\varphi^2
    +
    \ii\int\lambda(\partial_\mu A^\mu-\varphi)
  \right]  \\
  &\qquad
  \propto
  \exp\left[
    -\frac{1}{2\xi_{\mathrm g}}
    \int(\partial_\mu A^\mu)^2
  \right].
\end{aligned}
\]
The Euclidean gauge-fixing term is therefore
\[
  \Delta S_E
  =
  \frac{1}{2\xi_{\mathrm g}}
  \int\dd^4x_E\,(\partial_\mu A^\mu)^2 .
\]
The corresponding Lorentzian gauge-fixed action is
\[
  S_{\xi_{\mathrm g}}[A]
  =
  \int\dd^4x\,
  \left[
    -\frac14F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}
    -
    \frac{1}{2\xi_{\mathrm g}}(\partial_\mu A^\mu)^2
  \right].
\]
After integration by parts,
\[
  S_{\xi_{\mathrm g}}[A]
  =
  \frac12\int\dd^4x\,
  A_\mu
  \left[
    \eta^{\mu\nu}\Box
    -
    \left(1-\frac1{\xi_{\mathrm g}}\right)
    \partial^\mu\partial^\nu
  \right]
  A_\nu .
\]

In momentum space,
\[
  \widetilde D_{\xi_{\mathrm g}}^{\mu\nu}(k)
  =
  -\eta^{\mu\nu}k^2
  +
  \left(1-\frac1{\xi_{\mathrm g}}\right)k^\mu k^\nu .
\]
For \(\xi_{\mathrm g}\ne0\), its inverse is
\[
  \bigl(\widetilde D_{\xi_{\mathrm g}}^{-1}(k)\bigr)_{\mu\nu}
  =
  -\frac{\eta_{\mu\nu}}{k^2}
  +
  (1-\xi_{\mathrm g})\frac{k_\mu k_\nu}{(k^2)^2}.
\]
The verification is
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\left[
    -\eta^{\mu\rho}k^2
    +
    \left(1-\frac1{\xi_{\mathrm g}}\right)k^\mu k^\rho
  \right]
  \left[
    -\frac{\eta_{\rho\nu}}{k^2}
    +
    (1-\xi_{\mathrm g})\frac{k_\rho k_\nu}{(k^2)^2}
  \right]  \\
  &\qquad
  =
  \delta^\mu{}_\nu .
\end{aligned}
\]
The Feynman two-point function of the representative field is
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\langle\Omega|
  \operatorname T\{\widehat A_\mu(x)\widehat A_\nu(y)\}
  |\Omega\rangle_{\xi_{\mathrm g}}  \\
  &=
  \int\frac{\dd^4k}{(2\pi)^4}
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot(x-y)}
  \frac{-\ii}{k^2-\ii\epsilon}
  \left[
    \eta_{\mu\nu}
    -
    (1-\xi_{\mathrm g})
    \frac{k_\mu k_\nu}{k^2-\ii\epsilon}
  \right].
\end{aligned}
\]
The value \(\xi_{\mathrm g}=1\) is Feynman gauge:
\[
  \begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=-0.5ex,scale=0.75]
    \draw[thick,decorate,decoration={snake,amplitude=1.2pt,segment length=5pt}]
      (0,0)--(2.1,0);
    \node[blue!70!black] at (0.15,0.28) {\(\mu\)};
    \node[blue!70!black] at (1.95,0.28) {\(\nu\)};
    \draw[->,blue!70!black] (1.55,0.35)--(0.75,0.35);
    \node[blue!70!black] at (1.1,0.62) {\(k\)};
  \end{tikzpicture}
  \quad
  =
  \frac{-\ii\eta_{\mu\nu}}{k^2-\ii\epsilon}.
\]
The value \(\xi_{\mathrm g}\to0\) is Landau gauge.

\section{Field Strength and One-Photon States}

The gauge-fixed path integral defines Green functions of the representative
variable \(A_\mu\). In a positive-metric physical Hilbert space the local
observable obtained from the free Maxwell field is the field strength
\(\widehat F_{\mu\nu}\). The field-strength two-point function is independent
of \(\xi_{\mathrm g}\), because the gauge-parameter term in the representative
propagator is proportional to \(k_\mu k_\nu\) and is killed by
antisymmetrization.

The Wightman two-point function has the one-photon spectral form
\[
\begin{aligned}
  &\langle\Omega|
  \widehat F_{\mu\nu}(x)\widehat F_{\rho\sigma}(y)
  |\Omega\rangle  \\
  &=
  \sum_{h=\pm1}
  \int
  \frac{\dd^3\vec k}{(2\pi)^3\,2|\vec k|}
  \langle\Omega|\widehat F_{\mu\nu}(x)|k,h\rangle
  \langle k,h|\widehat F_{\rho\sigma}(y)|\Omega\rangle ,
\end{aligned}
\]
where the sharp states are generalized one-particle vectors in the sense of
Section~\ref{sec:rigged-hilbert-continuous-spectrum}. With the helicity
representatives of
Chapter~\ref{ch:massless-particles-helicity-gauge-redundancy},
\[
  \langle k,h|\widehat F_{\mu\nu}(x)|\Omega\rangle
  =
  Z_A^{1/2}\ee^{-\ii k\cdot x}
  \left(
    -\ii k_\mu\epsilon_\nu^{h*}(k)
    +\ii k_\nu\epsilon_\mu^{h*}(k)
  \right).
\]
The representative shift
\[
  \epsilon_\mu^h(k)\longmapsto \epsilon_\mu^h(k)+c(k)k_\mu
\]
does not change this matrix element.

For a fixed null momentum \(k\), choose a null covector \(C_\mu(k)\) satisfying
\[
  C^2=0,
  \qquad
  k^\mu C_\mu=-1.
\]
The two transverse helicity representatives may be normalized so that
\[
  \epsilon_\mu^h(k)\epsilon^{h'*\mu}(k)=\delta_{hh'},
  \qquad
  k^\mu\epsilon_\mu^h(k)=0,
\]
and obey the completeness relation
\[
  \sum_{h=\pm1}
  \epsilon_\mu^h(k)\epsilon_\nu^{h*}(k)
  =
  \eta_{\mu\nu}+k_\mu C_\nu+k_\nu C_\mu .
\]
For example, when \(k^\mu=(|\vec k|,\vec k)\), one may take
\[
  C^\mu(k)
  =
  \frac{1}{2|\vec k|^2}\bigl(|\vec k|,-\vec k\bigr).
\]
In the field-strength spectral sum all terms involving \(C_\mu\) cancel by
antisymmetrization. Comparison with the explicit Wightman boundary value of
the free field-strength correlator fixes the free normalization
\[
  Z_A=1.
\]

The next chapter couples this representative gauge field to a charged spinor
field. The perturbative Green functions will be written using gauge-fixed
representatives, while local gauge-invariant operators and external photon
states are read through the field strength and helicity data constructed
here.
