\chapter{Cross Sections, Phase Space, and Unitarity}
\label{ch:cross-sections-unitarity}

\section{Transition Probabilities}

The scattering operator \(S\) is unitary on the asymptotic Hilbert space in a
sector where incoming and outgoing scattering states span the same subspace.
Write
\[
  S=\mathbf 1+\ii T .
\]
For a normalized incoming wave packet \(\Psi_{\mathrm{in}}\) and an outgoing
set of states \(\mathcal D\), let \(\Pi_{\mathcal D}^{\mathrm{out}}\) be the
orthogonal projection onto the corresponding outgoing subspace. The transition
probability into \(\mathcal D\) is
\[
  P_{\Psi\to\mathcal D}
  =
  \left\|
    \Pi_{\mathcal D}^{\mathrm{out}}S\Psi_{\mathrm{in}}
  \right\|^2 .
\]
This is the Hilbert-space definition. Formulas with sharp momenta are
distributional kernels of this expression.

For positive-energy momenta
\[
  p_i=(E_i,\vec p_i),
  \qquad
  q_a=(E_a,\vec q_a),
\]
with
\[
  p_i^2=-m_i^2,\qquad q_a^2=-M_a^2,
\]
define
\[
  P_{\mathrm{in}}=\sum_i p_i,
  \qquad
  P_{\mathrm{out}}=\sum_a q_a .
\]
The invariant amplitude convention from the LSZ chapter is
\[
  \langle q_1,\ldots,q_m|T|p_1,\ldots,p_n\rangle
  =
  (2\pi)^D\delta^D(P_{\mathrm{out}}-P_{\mathrm{in}})
  \mathcal M(q_1,\ldots,q_m|p_1,\ldots,p_n).
\]
Here the bra and ket are basis vectors in the asymptotic Fock space on which
\(T\) acts. Equivalently, the same distribution is obtained from the connected
part of the Haag--Ruelle kernel
\({}_{\mathrm{out}}\langle q_1,\ldots,q_m|p_1,\ldots,p_n
\rangle_{\mathrm{in}}\) after subtracting the identity contribution.
The full connected contribution to \(S\) is therefore
\[
  \ii(2\pi)^D\delta^D(P_{\mathrm{out}}-P_{\mathrm{in}})\mathcal M .
\]

\section{Wave-Packet Origin of Cross Sections}

The sharp-momentum formulas below are densities obtained from wave-packet
probabilities.  Let \(\psi_1,\psi_2\in\Hilb_1\) be normalized incoming
one-particle packets whose momentum supports are narrow and whose group
velocities are distinct.  In the center-of-mass frame, translate the second
packet by a vector \(b\) in the transverse impact-parameter plane and denote
the resulting incoming two-particle state by
\[
  \Psi_b=\psi_1\odot \tau_b\psi_2 .
\]
Here \(\odot\) is the symmetric tensor product and \(\tau_b\) is the spatial
translation operator on the one-particle Hilbert space.
For an outgoing channel \(\mathcal D\) that does not contain the identity
no-scattering component, define the wave-packet cross section by
\[
  \sigma_{\psi_1,\psi_2}(\mathcal D)
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R^{D-2}}\dd^{D-2}b\,
  \left\|
    \Pi_{\mathcal D}^{\mathrm{out}}S\Psi_b
  \right\|^2 .
\]
The dimension is length to the power \(D-2\), as required for a transverse
area in \(D\) spacetime dimensions.

When the packets are narrowed around momenta \(p_1,p_2\), this expression has
a distributional limit.  The oscillatory integral over \(b\) enforces
transverse momentum conservation, the large-time separation of the packets
enforces energy conservation, and the remaining density is precisely the
plane-wave kernel with its single overall factor
\[
  (2\pi)^D\delta^D(P_{\mathrm{out}}-P_{\mathrm{in}}).
\]
The incoming current through the transverse impact-parameter plane is the
relative speed \(v_{\mathrm{rel}}\), while the one-particle normalization
contributes the factors \(2E_1\) and \(2E_2\).  Thus the invariant
denominator in the sharp-momentum formula is
\[
  \mathcal F(p_1,p_2)=4E_1E_2v_{\mathrm{rel}}.
\]
The phase-space formula in the next section is the narrow-packet limit of
this Hilbert-space definition.

\section{Invariant Phase Space and Flux}

For one on-shell final particle of mass \(M\), the Lorentz-invariant
positive-energy measure is
\[
  \dd\mu_M(\vec q)
  =
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec q}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2E_{\vec q}},
  \qquad
  E_{\vec q}=\sqrt{\vec q^{\,2}+M^2}.
\]
For \(m\) final particles with total incoming momentum \(P\), define the
\(m\)-body phase-space measure
\[
  \dd\Phi_m(P;q_1,\ldots,q_m)
  =
  (2\pi)^D\delta^D\!\left(P-\sum_{a=1}^m q_a\right)
  \prod_{a=1}^m
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec q_a}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2E_a}.
\]
If some final particles are identical, the phase-space integral is divided by
the factorial of the number of identical particles of each species.

For a two-particle initial state, the invariant flux factor is
\[
  \mathcal F(p_1,p_2)
  =
  4\sqrt{(p_1\cdot p_2)^2-m_1^2m_2^2}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
  \mathcal F=4E_1E_2\,v_{\mathrm{rel}},
\]
where the invariant relative speed is
\[
  v_{\mathrm{rel}}
  =
  \frac{\sqrt{(p_1\cdot p_2)^2-m_1^2m_2^2}}{E_1E_2}.
\]
In the center-of-mass frame,
\[
  p_1=(E_1,\vec p_*),
  \qquad
  p_2=(E_2,-\vec p_*),
\]
and
\[
  \mathcal F=4(E_1+E_2)|\vec p_*| .
\]

The differential cross section for
\[
  p_1+p_2\longrightarrow q_1+\cdots+q_m
\]
is
\[
  \dd\sigma
  =
  \frac{1}{\mathcal F(p_1,p_2)}
  \left|\mathcal M(q_1,\ldots,q_m|p_1,p_2)\right|^2
  \dd\Phi_m(P_{\mathrm{in}};q_1,\ldots,q_m).
\]
All factors in this formula have now been fixed: the state normalization,
the amplitude convention, the final-state measure, and the incoming flux.

\section{Two-Body Final States in Four Dimensions}

Set \(D=4\). For a two-body final state, define
\[
  s=-(p_1+p_2)^2=P_{\mathrm{in}}^0{}^2-\vec P_{\mathrm{in}}^{\,2}.
\]
In the center-of-mass frame, \(P_{\mathrm{in}}=(\sqrt s,\vec0)\). Let
\[
  |\vec p_*|
\]
be the magnitude of either incoming three-momentum in that frame and
\[
  |\vec q_*|
\]
the magnitude of either final three-momentum. Performing the two-body
phase-space integral except for the solid angle gives
\[
  \dd\Phi_2
  =
  \frac{1}{16\pi^2}
  \frac{|\vec q_*|}{\sqrt s}\,\dd\Omega .
\]
The flux is
\[
  \mathcal F=4\sqrt s\,|\vec p_*|.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \frac{\dd\sigma}{\dd\Omega}
  =
  \frac{1}{64\pi^2s}
  \frac{|\vec q_*|}{|\vec p_*|}
  |\mathcal M(s,\Omega)|^2 .
\]
For identical final particles, multiply the right hand side by \(1/2\).

For elastic scattering of identical scalar particles of mass \(m\),
\[
  |\vec q_*|=|\vec p_*|,
  \qquad
  \sqrt s=E_{\mathrm{cm}},
\]
and a constant tree-level \(\phi^4\) amplitude
\[
  \mathcal M=-g+O(g^2)
\]
gives
\[
  \sigma_{2\to2}
  =
  \frac{g^2}{32\pi s}+O(g^3)
\]
for identical final particles.

\section{Unitarity and the Optical Theorem}

The equation \(S^\dagger S=\mathbf 1\), with \(S=\mathbf 1+\ii T\), is
\[
  -\ii(T-T^\dagger)=T^\dagger T .
\]
Insert complete outgoing asymptotic states between \(T^\dagger\) and \(T\).
For sharp momentum kernels this gives
\[
  -\ii\left(T_{fi}-T_{if}^*\right)
  =
  \sum_X
  \int\dd\Phi_X\,
  T_{Xf}^*T_{Xi},
\]
where \(X\) runs over all allowed multiparticle channels and includes all
discrete quantum numbers. In amplitude notation,
\[
\begin{aligned}
&-\ii\left[
  \mathcal M(f|i)-\mathcal M(i|f)^*
  \right]
\\
&\quad =
  \sum_X
  \int\dd\Phi_X\,
  \mathcal M(X|f)^*\mathcal M(X|i),
\end{aligned}
\]
with the common momentum-conservation delta function removed.

For \(f=i\), the optical theorem is
\[
  2\,\operatorname{Im}\mathcal M(i|i)
  =
  \sum_X
  \int\dd\Phi_X\,|\mathcal M(X|i)|^2 .
\]
For two incoming particles this is equivalently
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{tot}}
  =
  \frac{2\,\operatorname{Im}\mathcal M(i|i)}
  {\mathcal F(p_1,p_2)} .
\]
The forward amplitude on the left is evaluated with the same incoming and
outgoing momenta.

\section{Partial Waves in Four Dimensions}

For scalar \(2\to2\) scattering in the center-of-mass frame, rotational
invariance makes the amplitude a function of \(s\) and
\[
  \cos\theta=\widehat p_*\cdot\widehat q_* .
\]
Use the partial-wave expansion
\[
  \mathcal M(s,\cos\theta)
  =
  16\pi
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}
  (2\ell+1)a_\ell(s)P_\ell(\cos\theta),
\]
where \(P_\ell\) is the Legendre polynomial and \(a_\ell(s)\) is the
partial-wave amplitude. The inverse formula is
\[
  a_\ell(s)
  =
  \frac{1}{32\pi}
  \int_{-1}^1\dd(\cos\theta)\,
  P_\ell(\cos\theta)\,\mathcal M(s,\cos\theta).
\]

For equal incoming masses in the elastic region, define
\[
  \rho(s)=\frac{2|\vec p_*|}{\sqrt s}.
\]
The partial-wave \(S\)-matrix eigenvalue is
\[
  S_\ell(s)=1+2\ii\,\rho(s)\,a_\ell(s).
\]
Elastic unitarity is
\[
  |S_\ell(s)|=1.
\]
Equivalently, there is a real phase shift \(\delta_\ell(s)\) such that
\[
  S_\ell(s)=\ee^{2\ii\delta_\ell(s)},
\]
and
\[
  a_\ell(s)
  =
  \frac{\ee^{2\ii\delta_\ell(s)}-1}{2\ii\rho(s)}
  =
  \frac{\ee^{\ii\delta_\ell(s)}\sin\delta_\ell(s)}{\rho(s)} .
\]
The same condition can be written
\[
  \operatorname{Im}a_\ell(s)=\rho(s)|a_\ell(s)|^2 .
\]
In the presence of inelastic channels,
\[
  |S_\ell(s)|\le 1,
\]
with equality precisely in an elastic partial wave.

For distinguishable scalar particles the elastic total cross section is
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{el}}
  =
  \frac{\pi}{|\vec p_*|^2}
  \sum_{\ell=0}^\infty
  (2\ell+1)|S_\ell(s)-1|^2
  =
  \frac{4\pi}{|\vec p_*|^2}
  \sum_{\ell=0}^\infty
  (2\ell+1)\sin^2\delta_\ell(s).
\]
For identical bosons only even \(\ell\) occur, and the final-state symmetry
factor changes the total cross section to
\[
  \sigma_{\mathrm{el}}^{\mathrm{id}}
  =
  \frac{2\pi}{|\vec p_*|^2}
  \sum_{\substack{\ell=0\\ \ell\ \mathrm{even}}}^{\infty}
  (2\ell+1)|S_\ell(s)-1|^2 .
\]

\section{Partial-Wave Unitarity Geometry}

The partial-wave normalization above gives a useful geometric form of
unitarity.  Define
\[
  b_\ell(s)=\rho(s)a_\ell(s),
  \qquad
  S_\ell(s)=1+2\ii b_\ell(s).
\]
In an elastic partial wave, \(|S_\ell|=1\).  Writing
\[
  b_\ell=x_\ell+\ii y_\ell,
\]
this condition becomes
\[
  x_\ell^2+\left(y_\ell-\frac12\right)^2=\frac14 .
\]
Thus the elastic amplitude lies on the circle of radius \(1/2\) centered at
\(\ii/2\) in the complex \(b_\ell\)-plane.  Equivalently,
\[
  y_\ell=|b_\ell|^2 .
\]
The phase-shift form
\[
  b_\ell=\ee^{\ii\delta_\ell}\sin\delta_\ell
\]
parametrizes this circle.

When inelastic channels are open, the elastic partial wave is a matrix element
of a unitary matrix on a larger channel space.  It is convenient to write
\[
  S_\ell=\eta_\ell\ee^{2\ii\delta_\ell},
  \qquad
  0\le\eta_\ell\le1 .
\]
Then \(b_\ell=(S_\ell-1)/(2\ii)\) lies inside the same circle:
\[
  x_\ell^2+\left(y_\ell-\frac12\right)^2\le\frac14,
  \qquad
  y_\ell\ge |b_\ell|^2 .
\]
The difference
\[
  y_\ell-|b_\ell|^2
  =
  \frac{1-\eta_\ell^2}{4}
\]
is the contribution of inelastic final states in that angular-momentum
channel.  This is the partial-wave form of the optical theorem.

\section{Thresholds, Poles, and Resonances}

The same variables organize the analytic structure of scattering. A threshold
is the value of \(s\) at which a new on-shell intermediate channel becomes
kinematically available. Across such a threshold, unitarity gives the
discontinuity of amplitudes in terms of phase-space integrals over lower
amplitudes.

A stable bound state in a two-particle channel appears as a pole of the
partial-wave amplitude on the physical sheet below threshold. A resonance is
represented by a pole reached after analytic continuation through a unitarity
cut. The precise sheet and residue statements require the analytic
continuation machinery developed later, but the organizing data are already
present here: the \(S\)-operator, phase space, unitarity, and partial waves.
