\chapter{Hydrodynamic Fluctuations And Long-Time Tails}
\label{ch:thermal-hydrodynamic-fluctuations}

\ConventionsRealTime

Hydrodynamics describes the long-wavelength evolution of conserved
densities.  In a thermal state those densities also fluctuate.  The
fluctuations are fixed at the linear level by static susceptibilities and
the KMS fluctuation--dissipation relation; their nonlinear couplings then
produce nonanalytic low-frequency terms in real-time correlators.  These
terms are the hydrodynamic long-time tails.  They are universal within the
hydrodynamic effective theory because their nonanalytic part is determined by
the conserved-mode propagators and by the local nonlinear vertices.

Throughout this chapter spacetime is \(\mathbb R_t\times\mathbb R^d\), with
\(d\) spatial dimensions.  Fourier modes are
\(\exp(-\ii\omega t+\ii\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf x)\).  The diffusion
normalizations are those of
Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-sk-hydrodynamic-effective-actions}: the charge
susceptibility is \(\chi=\partial n/\partial\mu\), the conductivity is
\(\sigma\), and \(D=\sigma/\chi\).  Equal-time thermal fluctuations of a
coarse-grained density carry the covariance \(T\chi\).

\section{Static Fluctuation Data}

Let \(q_A(t,\mathbf x)\) be conserved densities in a homogeneous KMS state
at temperature \(T\) and chemical potentials \(\mu_A\).  Write
\[
  \delta q_A=q_A-\omega_\beta(q_A),
\]
where \(\omega_\beta\) denotes the thermal state.  The static susceptibility
matrix in the chemical-potential convention is
\[
  \chi_{AB}
  =
  \left(\frac{\partial q_A}{\partial\mu_B}\right)_T .
\]
For hydrodynamic variables averaged over a cell whose size is large compared
with microscopic correlation lengths, the equal-time connected covariance is
\begin{equation}
  \int d^dx\,
  \omega_\beta\!\left(
    \delta q_A(0,\mathbf x)\delta q_B(0,\mathbf0)
  \right)_{\rm conn}
  =
  T\chi_{AB}.
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-static-covariance}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, if one uses the dimensionless thermodynamic force
\(\alpha_A=\mu_A/T\), the covariance equals
\(\partial q_A/\partial\alpha_B\).  This convention removes a common source
of factor-of-\(T\) errors in stochastic hydrodynamics.

For one diffusive charge density \(n\), the Gaussian equilibrium
probability functional for long-wavelength configurations is
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm eq}[n]\propto
  \exp\!\left[
    -\frac{1}{2T\chi}\int d^dx\,(\delta n)^2
  \right],
  \qquad
  \delta n=n-\omega_\beta(n).
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-static-gaussian}
\end{equation}
This formula is a local central-limit statement inside the hydrodynamic
window.  The continuum delta function in
\eqref{eq:hydro-fluct-static-covariance} is an effective-theory notation for
a cell-averaged covariance; the cell scale is kept between the microscopic
correlation length and the external wavelength.

\section{Linear Stochastic Diffusion}

The first-order stochastic constitutive relation for one conserved charge is
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t n+\partial_i j^i=0,
  \qquad
  j^i=-D\partial_i n+\xi^i,
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-langevin-diffusion}
\end{equation}
with Gaussian white current noise
\begin{equation}
  \langle\xi^i(t,\mathbf x)\xi^j(t',\mathbf x')\rangle
  =
  2T\sigma\,\delta^{ij}
  \delta(t-t')\delta^{(d)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf x').
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-current-noise}
\end{equation}
The coefficient \(2T\sigma\) is the same coefficient derived from the
dynamical KMS symmetry of the diffusion action in
Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-sk-hydrodynamic-effective-actions}.  The stochastic
equation gives
\[
  n(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \frac{\ii k_i\xi^i(\omega,\mathbf k)}
       {-\ii\omega+Dk^2},
  \qquad
  k^2=\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf k .
\]
Hence the symmetrized density correlator is
\begin{equation}
  G^{\rm sym}_{nn}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \frac{2T\sigma k^2}
       {\omega^2+(Dk^2)^2}.
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-density-symmetric}
\end{equation}
Integrating over frequency gives
\[
  \int\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}
  G^{\rm sym}_{nn}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \frac{T\sigma}{D}
  =
  T\chi,
\]
which is exactly the static covariance
\eqref{eq:hydro-fluct-static-covariance}.  Thus the Einstein relation
\(D=\sigma/\chi\) is forced by compatibility of the deterministic diffusion
constant, the conductivity, and the equilibrium fluctuation measure.

The retarded density source-response kernel from the Schwinger--Keldysh
action is
\begin{equation}
  K^R_{nn}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \chi\,\frac{Dk^2}{Dk^2-\ii\omega}.
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-diffusive-retarded}
\end{equation}
Its imaginary part obeys the low-frequency fluctuation--dissipation relation
\[
  G^{\rm sym}_{nn}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \frac{2T}{\omega}\,
  \operatorname{Im}K^R_{nn}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  +O(\omega/T)
\]
inside the classical hydrodynamic regime.  The exact quantum formula is the
KMS relation of Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-spectral-kubo-transport}; the
displayed expression is its \(|\omega|\ll T\) limit.

\section{Local Schwinger--Keldysh Origin}

The stochastic equation \eqref{eq:hydro-fluct-langevin-diffusion} is the
Hubbard--Stratonovich form of the local diffusion action
\begin{equation}
  I_{\rm diff}
  =
  \int dt\,d^dx\,
  \left[
  \chi B_{a0}B_{r0}
  -\sigma B_{ai}\partial_tB_{ri}
  +\ii T\sigma B_{ai}B_{ai}
  \right].
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-diffusion-sk-action}
\end{equation}
Here \(B_{r\mu}=A_{r\mu}+\partial_\mu\varphi_r\) and
\(B_{a\mu}=A_{a\mu}+\partial_\mu\varphi_a\) are the gauge-invariant
hydrodynamic source combinations.  The \(a\)-phase variation imposes the
continuity equation, and the imaginary term has the Gaussian representation
\[
  \exp\!\left[
    -T\sigma\int B_{ai}B_{ai}
  \right]
  =
  \int D\xi\,
  \exp\!\left[
    -\int\frac{\xi_i\xi_i}{4T\sigma}
    +\ii\int B_{ai}\xi_i
  \right].
\]
The same coefficient therefore controls current response, current noise, and
diffusive relaxation.  This is the local effective-action statement of KMS:
one chooses the dissipative term and the noise term together.

\section{Hydrodynamic Nonlinearities}

Hydrodynamic variables are local coordinates on the manifold of equilibrium
states.  Expanding the currents in fluctuations around a homogeneous state
gives
\begin{equation}
  J^i_A
  =
  J^i_{A,\rm lin}
  +\frac12\,\lambda^i{}_{A,BC}\,\phi_B\phi_C
  +O(\phi^3,\phi\,\partial\phi,\partial^2\phi),
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-quadratic-current}
\end{equation}
where \(\phi_A=\delta q_A\) after diagonalizing the linear hydrodynamic
modes, and \(\lambda^i{}_{A,BC}\) is symmetric in \(B,C\).  The coefficient
\(\lambda\) is determined by the equation of state and by the chosen
hydrodynamic frame.  For example, the convective part of the stress tensor
contains \(\pi^i\pi^j/(\varepsilon+p)\), where \(\pi^i=T^{0i}\) is momentum
density and \(\varepsilon+p\) is the enthalpy density.

The quadratic term in \eqref{eq:hydro-fluct-quadratic-current} produces a
loop of hydrodynamic modes in current-current correlators.  The calculation
is an effective-theory calculation with an ultraviolet cutoff
\(\Lambda_{\rm hyd}\) below the inverse microscopic length.  Local analytic
terms depend on this cutoff and renormalize local transport coefficients.
The low-frequency nonanalytic part is independent of the detailed cutoff
profile.

\section{Time-Domain Tail From A Diffusive Pair}

Consider one scalar diffusive mode \(\phi\) with
\[
  \langle\phi(t,\mathbf k)\phi(0,-\mathbf k)\rangle
  =
  T\chi\,e^{-Dk^2t},
  \qquad t>0.
\]
Let the long-wavelength current contain the normal-ordered quadratic piece
\[
  J(t,\mathbf x)=\lambda:\!\phi(t,\mathbf x)^2\!: .
\]
At Gaussian order, Wick contraction gives
\begin{align}
  C_{JJ}(t)
  &=
  \int d^dx\,
  \langle J(t,\mathbf x)J(0,\mathbf0)\rangle_{\rm conn}
  \nonumber\\
  &=
  2\lambda^2(T\chi)^2
  \int\frac{d^dk}{(2\pi)^d}\,e^{-2Dk^2t}
  \nonumber\\
  &=
  2\lambda^2(T\chi)^2\,
  (8\pi Dt)^{-d/2},
  \qquad t>0 .
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-time-tail-single-diffusive-mode}
\end{align}
This is the basic long-time tail.  More general hydrodynamic systems give
sums of terms of the same form, with diffusion constants replaced by sums of
the damping rates of the two modes in the loop and with tensor projectors
from momentum or sound modes.  The exponent is fixed by the Gaussian
integration over small loop momentum.

\section{Frequency-Domain Nonanalyticity}

The same tail appears in the retarded correlator as a nonanalytic
low-frequency term.  The scalar integral that controls a pair of diffusive
modes is
\begin{equation}
  I_d(z;D)
  =
  \int\frac{d^dk}{(2\pi)^d}
  \frac{1}{z+2Dk^2},
  \qquad
  z=-\ii\omega+0^+ .
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-basic-loop-integral}
\end{equation}
The analytic terms are cutoff dependent.  The nonanalytic part is obtained
by subtracting local Taylor terms, or equivalently by dimensional
regularization of the infrared integral:
\begin{equation}
  I_d^{\rm nonan}(z;D)
  =
  \frac{\Gamma(1-d/2)}{(4\pi)^{d/2}(2D)^{d/2}}\,
  z^{d/2-1},
  \qquad d\notin 2\mathbb Z_{\ge1}.
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-loop-nonanalytic-general}
\end{equation}
In even spatial dimension the pole of \(\Gamma(1-d/2)\) is replaced, after
local subtraction, by \(z^{d/2-1}\log z\).  The first cases are
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
d & I_d^{\rm nonan}(z;D)\\ \hline
1 & \displaystyle \frac{1}{2(2D)^{1/2}}\,z^{-1/2}\\[2mm]
2 & \displaystyle -\frac{1}{8\pi D}\,\log z\\[2mm]
3 & \displaystyle -\frac{1}{4\pi(2D)^{3/2}}\,z^{1/2}
\end{array}
\]
up to scheme-dependent additive constants in \(d=2\).  The branch is fixed
by \(z=-\ii\omega+0^+\), so the retarded function is analytic in the upper
half \(\omega\)-plane.

For \(d=3\), an explicit cutoff computation displays the separation between
local and nonlocal terms:
\[
  I_3^\Lambda(z;D)
  =
  \frac{1}{2\pi^2(2D)}
  \left[
    \Lambda
    -
    \left(\frac{z}{2D}\right)^{1/2}
    \arctan\!\left(
      \frac{\Lambda}{(z/2D)^{1/2}}
    \right)
  \right].
\]
As \(z/\Lambda^2\to0\),
\[
  I_3^\Lambda(z;D)
  =
  \frac{\Lambda}{4\pi^2D}
  -
  \frac{z^{1/2}}{4\pi(2D)^{3/2}}
  +O(z/\Lambda).
\]
The first term renormalizes a local transport coefficient.  The square-root
term is the long-time tail written in frequency space.

\section{Transport Coefficients As Scale-Dependent Local Data}

Let \(\kappa\) be a transport coefficient multiplying a local term in the
hydrodynamic constitutive relation or Schwinger--Keldysh action.  Hydrodynamic
loops with momenta below \(\Lambda_{\rm hyd}\) shift the coefficient:
\[
  \kappa_{\rm phys}(\omega)
  =
  \kappa(\Lambda_{\rm hyd})
  +
  \Delta\kappa_{\rm loc}(\Lambda_{\rm hyd})
  +
  \Delta\kappa_{\rm nonan}(\omega).
\]
The sum of the first two terms is the local coefficient appropriate to the
chosen hydrodynamic cutoff.  The last term is fixed by hydrodynamic mode
propagation and is a genuinely nonlocal-in-time low-frequency contribution,
distinct from a finite local derivative coefficient.  This is the
finite-temperature analogue of ordinary effective-field theory
renormalization: local coefficients are scheme dependent, while nonanalytic
infrared dependence is physical within the regime of validity.

The order of limits is part of the definition.  One first takes the
thermodynamic limit of the KMS state, then probes frequencies and momenta
inside the hydrodynamic scaling regime, and only then studies the small
\(\omega,\mathbf k\) asymptotics.  Interchanging this order can erase the
hydrodynamic pole, mix static and dynamic susceptibilities, or replace
retarded functions by finite-volume quasiperiodic sums.

\section{Stress-Tensor Noise}

For a relativistic fluid in the local rest frame, write the stochastic
spatial stress as
\[
  T^{ij}
  =
  p\,\delta^{ij}
  -\eta
  \left(
    \partial^iv^j+\partial^jv^i
    -\frac{2}{d}\delta^{ij}\partial_kv^k
  \right)
  -\zeta\,\delta^{ij}\partial_kv^k
  +\Xi^{ij}
  +O(v^2,\partial^2).
\]
The white-noise covariance required by local KMS is
\begin{align}
  \langle\Xi^{ij}(x)\Xi^{kl}(x')\rangle
  &=
  2T\Bigl[
    \eta
    \left(
      \delta^{ik}\delta^{jl}
      +\delta^{il}\delta^{jk}
      -\frac{2}{d}\delta^{ij}\delta^{kl}
    \right)
    +\zeta\,\delta^{ij}\delta^{kl}
  \Bigr]
  \nonumber\\
  &\qquad\qquad\times
  \delta(t-t')\delta^{(d)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf x').
  \label{eq:hydro-fluct-stress-noise}
\end{align}
For any real symmetric test tensor \(s_{ij}\), decompose
\[
  s_{ij}=s^{\rm T}_{ij}+\frac{1}{d}\delta_{ij}s^k{}_k,
  \qquad
  \delta^{ij}s^{\rm T}_{ij}=0.
\]
Contracting the tensor in \eqref{eq:hydro-fluct-stress-noise} with
\(s_{ij}s_{kl}\) gives
\[
  4T\eta\,s^{\rm T}_{ij}s^{\rm T}_{ij}
  +
  2T\zeta\,(s^k{}_k)^2 .
\]
Thus positivity of the stochastic stress covariance is equivalent, at this
order, to \(\eta\ge0\) and \(\zeta\ge0\).  The same inequalities are obtained
from nonnegative entropy production in deterministic hydrodynamics.

\section{Momentum Tails}

Momentum density \(\pi^i=T^{0i}\) has static covariance
\[
  \langle\pi^i(\mathbf k)\pi^j(-\mathbf k)\rangle_{\rm eq}
  =
  T(\varepsilon+p)\delta^{ij}
\]
in the local rest frame.  Transverse momentum diffuses with kinematic
viscosity
\[
  \nu=\frac{\eta}{\varepsilon+p}.
\]
Because the stress tensor contains the convective term
\[
  T^{ij}_{\rm conv}
  =
  \frac{\pi^i\pi^j}{\varepsilon+p},
\]
the shear-stress correlator contains a two-momentum hydrodynamic loop.  Its
late-time behavior is again of the form
\[
  \langle T^{ij}(t)T^{kl}(0)\rangle_{\rm conn}
  \sim
  T^2\,\mathcal P^{ij,kl}\,
  (8\pi\nu t)^{-d/2},
\]
with \(\mathcal P^{ij,kl}\) determined by the transverse projectors and by
angular averaging.  Sound modes add oscillatory pieces with damping
controlled by the sound attenuation constant.  The existence of the tail
therefore follows from the convective nonlinearity and the conserved
momentum pole; the exact tensor coefficient requires the full hydrodynamic
mode basis and frame convention.

\section{Microscopic Theorem Boundary}

A microscopic derivation from QFT must specify the KMS state, conserved
currents, thermodynamic susceptibilities, the thermodynamic limit, clustering
estimates, and the topology in which hydrodynamic correlators emerge from
real-time QFT correlators.  It must also identify the slow subspace of
conserved densities, prove local equilibration in the scaling regime being
used, and derive the Schwinger--Keldysh positivity and KMS constraints on
the effective action.  Hydrodynamic fluctuation theory then computes the
consequences of these inputs.

The long-time-tail calculation is rigorous as an internal statement of the
stochastic hydrodynamic effective theory with a stated cutoff and Gaussian
leading measure.  Promoting it to a theorem about a particular interacting
continuum QFT requires a proof that the QFT has the hydrodynamic scaling
limit assumed above.

\begin{openproblem}[Hydrodynamic limit]
\label{op:hydrodynamic-limit-qft}
Derive fluctuating hydrodynamics and long-time tails directly from an
interacting QFT with a KMS state, including the hydrodynamic scaling limit,
noise kernel, nonlinear couplings, transport-coefficient renormalization,
and control of the order of thermodynamic, real-time, and low-frequency
limits.
\end{openproblem}
