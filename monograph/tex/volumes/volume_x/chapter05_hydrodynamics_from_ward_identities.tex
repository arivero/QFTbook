\chapter{Hydrodynamics from Ward Identities}
\label{ch:thermal-hydrodynamics-from-ward-identities}

\ConventionsLorentzian

Hydrodynamics is the long-wavelength effective theory of a complete retained
slow spectral sector.  Exact conserved densities are always represented in
that sector, but they need not exhaust it: Goldstone phases, critical order
parameters, weakly-relaxed charges, kinetic moments, elastic variables, or
higher-form defect data can also remain slow in a declared scaling limit.  The
sector is an observable subspace paired with source directions, rather than a
list of preferred operator names.  This chapter develops the deterministic
first-order theory
for the ordinary normal-fluid case in which the retained slow sector is only
the subspace generated by energy, momentum, and ordinary charge densities.  It
fixes the sign and normalization conventions that
connect that restricted theory to the Kubo formulae of
Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-spectral-kubo-transport}, and then separates the
asymptotic low-frequency expansion from the stronger problem of causal,
well-posed time evolution.

Let \(D=d+1\), where \(d\) is the number of spatial dimensions.  The
Minkowski metric is mostly plus, \(\eta_{\mu\nu}=(-,+,\ldots,+)\).  Repeated
ordinary charge labels \(A,B\) are summed.  All formulas in this chapter are
written for parity-even fluids without anomalies; anomalous transport is
developed separately in Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-anomalous-topological-transport}.

\section{Background Sources and Ward Identities}

The conserved operators that enter hydrodynamics are defined by their
response to background sources.  Let \(g_{\mu\nu}\) be a Lorentzian metric
and let \(A^A_\mu\) be background gauge fields for ordinary Abelian internal
symmetries.  For the regulated Schwinger--Keldysh generating functional
\[
  W[g_+,A_+;g_-,A_-],
\]
the physical one-point functions are obtained by differentiating with
respect to the difference sources and then setting the two branches equal,
as in Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-schwinger-keldysh}.  On the diagonal source
\(g_+=g_-=g\), \(A_+=A_-=A\), we use the convention
\begin{equation}
  \delta W_{\rm phys}
  =
  \frac12\int d^Dx\,\sqrt{-g}\,
  \langle T^{\mu\nu}\rangle\,\delta g_{\mu\nu}
  +\int d^Dx\,\sqrt{-g}\,
  \langle J_A^\mu\rangle\,\delta A^A_\mu .
  \label{eq:hydro-source-variation}
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:hydro-source-variation} is a definition of the
stress-tensor and current normalization in this chapter.  Local contact
terms may change the local polynomial part of the response kernels, but they
do not change the operator Ward identities derived from gauge and
diffeomorphism invariance.

Gauge invariance under \(\delta_\lambda A^A_\mu=\partial_\mu\lambda^A\)
gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\mu J_A^\mu=0
  \label{eq:hydro-source-current-ward}
\end{equation}
when the corresponding symmetry is not anomalous.  Diffeomorphism invariance
under the infinitesimal vector field \(\xi^\mu\) gives
\[
  \delta_\xi g_{\mu\nu}=\nabla_\mu\xi_\nu+\nabla_\nu\xi_\mu,
  \qquad
  \delta_\xi A^A_\mu
  =
  \xi^\lambda F^A_{\lambda\mu}
  +\partial_\mu(\xi^\lambda A^A_\lambda),
\]
where \(F^A_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A^A_\nu-\partial_\nu A^A_\mu\).  Combining
this variation with \eqref{eq:hydro-source-current-ward} and integrating by
parts gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\mu T^\mu{}_\nu
  =
  F^A_{\nu\lambda}J_A^\lambda .
  \label{eq:hydro-source-stress-ward}
\end{equation}
Thus the flat, source-free conservation laws are the specialization of the
QFT Ward identities to
\(g_{\mu\nu}=\eta_{\mu\nu}\) and \(A^A_\mu=0\).

The same source formulation fixes the thermodynamic forces used below.  For
a fluid velocity \(u^\mu\), define the electric covector measured in the
local rest frame by
\[
  \mathcal E^A_\mu=F^A_{\mu\nu}u^\nu,
  \qquad
  u^\mu \mathcal E^A_\mu=0 .
\]
The source-free formulas in most of this chapter are recovered by setting
\(\mathcal E^A_\mu=0\).

\section{Hydrodynamic Scaling Families}

The hydrodynamic expansion is an asymptotic statement about a family of
states and sources whose variation scale is taken large compared with the
relaxation scales that are not retained.  The following definition records
the data that are being approximated.

\begin{definition}[Hydrodynamic scaling family]
\label{def:hydro-scaling-family}
A hydrodynamic scaling family near a homogeneous KMS state consists of
regulated real-time states \(\rho_\lambda\), background sources
\((g_{\lambda\mu\nu},A^A_{\lambda\mu})\), a regulated observable space
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal O_\lambda(\mathbf k)
  =
  \frac{
    \text{linear observable directions in the channel}
  }{
    \text{null vectors, Ward equivalences, and declared improvements}
  },
  \label{eq:hydro-observable-quotient}
\end{equation}
and a separate source space \(\mathcal J_\lambda(\mathbf k)\) paired with it
by the source insertion
\[
  \langle j,O\rangle_\lambda,
  \qquad
  j\in\mathcal J_\lambda,\quad O\in\mathcal O_\lambda .
\]
Local contact terms are not quotient vectors in
\(\mathcal O_\lambda\).  They are scheme data in the local polynomial part of
the source-response kernel, namely bilinear maps on
\(\mathcal J_\lambda\) after the source convention has been chosen.

The family also includes a susceptibility or Kubo--Mori pairing
\(\chi_\lambda:\mathcal O_\lambda\to\mathcal O_\lambda^*\) and a declared
thermodynamic-limit spectral datum.  This datum is either a Laplace-domain
inverse-response or memory pencil
\[
  \mathcal A_\lambda(z,\mathbf k):
  \mathcal O_\lambda(\mathbf k)\longrightarrow
  \mathcal J_\lambda(\mathbf k)^*,
  \qquad \operatorname{Re}z>0,
\]
after the order of limits has been specified, or an effective dissipative
generator constructed from such a pencil on a declared Banach or Hilbert
space.  The exact finite-regulator Hamiltonian Liouvillian
\(\mathcal L_{\rm mic}X=\ii[H_\lambda,X]\) may be used in the
Kubo--Mori/Mori--Zwanzig projection of
Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-spectral-kubo-transport} to choose candidate slow
observables, but its almost-periodic finite-volume spectrum is not by itself
the dissipative hydrodynamic relaxation spectrum.

If the chosen effective generator has an isolated slow spectral part, its
range defines the retained slow observable sector by
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal E_{\rm slow,\lambda}(\mathbf k)
  =
  \operatorname{Ran}P_{\rm slow,\lambda}(\mathbf k),
  \qquad
  P_{\rm slow,\lambda}
  =
  \frac{1}{2\pi\ii}
  \oint_{\Gamma_{\rm slow}}
  d\zeta\,
  \bigl(\zeta-\mathcal G_\lambda(\mathbf k)\bigr)^{-1}.
  \label{eq:hydro-retained-slow-sector}
\end{equation}
For a frequency-dependent pencil, the same role is played by a declared
linearization of \(\mathcal A_\lambda\) or by a spectral window and uniform
Schur-complement criterion for the pencil itself.  The same pencil or
effective generator must be used when defining the slow window and when
testing the complement.

Under a nonsingular observable basis change \(O'=MO\), sources transform
contragrediently, \(j'=M^{-\mathsf T}j\), so that the insertion
\(\langle j,O\rangle\) is invariant.  Matrix representatives transform as
\(\mathcal G'=M\mathcal G M^{-1}\), \(P'_{\rm slow}=MP_{\rm slow}M^{-1}\),
and \(\chi'=M\chi M^{\mathsf T}\) when \(\chi\) is written as a bilinear
Kubo--Mori pairing on observable basis vectors.  The subspace
\(\mathcal E_{\rm slow,\lambda}\) is the invariant object.  Diagonal entries
of a response, memory, or relaxation matrix do not define invariant
relaxation rates.

Local hydrodynamic fields \(\Psi^a_\lambda\), indexed by \(\lambda>0\), are
coordinates on the tangent space of the equilibrium-state manifold
\(\mathcal M_{\rm eq}\), not literally coordinates on
\(\mathcal O_\lambda\).  Exact energy-momentum and charge density directions
belong to \(\mathcal E_{\rm slow,\lambda}\) because their relaxation is
constrained at zero momentum by Ward identities.  The ordinary normal-fluid
specialization used in the derivations below uses
\[
  \Psi^a_\lambda=(T_\lambda,\mu_{\lambda A},u^\mu_\lambda)
\]
on \(\mathcal M_{\rm eq}\), and maps tangent vectors to retained density
fluctuations by the thermodynamic susceptibility and frame constraints:
\[
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \delta\varepsilon\\
    \delta n_A\\
    \delta\pi_i
  \end{pmatrix}
  =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \partial_T\varepsilon & \partial_{\mu_B}\varepsilon & 0\\
    \partial_T n_A & \partial_{\mu_B}n_A & 0\\
    0 & 0 & w\,\delta_{ij}
  \end{pmatrix}_{\rm eq}
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \delta T\\
    \delta\mu_B\\
    \delta u^j
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  w=\varepsilon+p .
\]
This normal-fluid chapter assumes that the image is the full retained slow
sector, namely the subspace generated by \(\{T^{0\mu},J_A^0\}\), and that no
additional slow spectral directions are present.  In local inertial
coordinates,
\[
  \partial_{\alpha_1}\cdots\partial_{\alpha_m}
  \Psi^a_\lambda,\quad
  \partial_{\alpha_1}\cdots\partial_{\alpha_{m-1}}
  F^A_{\lambda\alpha_m\alpha_{m+1}}
  =
  O(\lambda^m)
  \qquad(m\ge1),
\]
while the zeroth-order fields remain in a fixed compact subset of the
state manifold with \(T>0\) and away from singular thermodynamic
coordinates.  A constitutive relation through order \(N\) is an asymptotic
expansion of the one-point functions of the retained observables, expressed
in the hydrodynamic state coordinates and external sources; in particular
\(\langle T^{\mu\nu}\rangle_{\rho_\lambda}\) and
\(\langle J_A^\mu\rangle_{\rho_\lambda}\).  The expansion error is
\(O(\lambda^{N+1})\) after the thermodynamic limit and after subtraction of
declared local contact terms.
\end{definition}

This definition separates the kinematic part of hydrodynamics from the
microscopic QFT assertion.  The kinematic part is the classification of all
local tensor structures compatible with the Ward identities, symmetries, and
chosen slow variables.  The microscopic assertion is not supplied by Ward
identities alone.  It is that the retained subspace
\(\mathcal E_{\rm slow}\) is complete for the chosen scaling limit and that
the complement is uniformly regular for the same inverse-response or memory
pencil \(\mathcal A\), or for the same constructed effective generator,
whichever object defined the slow window.  The
normal-fluid chapters impose the restrictive case
\(\mathcal E_{\rm slow}^{\rm normal}=\operatorname{span}\{T^{0\mu},J_A^0\}\):
no spontaneously broken continuous symmetry, no critical slow order
parameter, no parametrically weak symmetry breaking, no unresolved kinetic
moment window, and no additional elastic, orientational, or higher-form
hydrodynamic variables.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Slow-sector completeness and complement regularity]
\label{ca:hydro-slow-sector-completeness}
Let \(P_{\rm slow}\) be the projection on the observable space
\(\mathcal O\) associated to the low spectral part of the declared
thermodynamic pencil \(\mathcal A(z,\mathbf k)\), or to a constructed
effective generator \(\mathcal G(\mathbf k)\) obtained from that pencil.  This
projection is not the Riesz projection of the exact finite Hamiltonian
Liouvillian unless an additional limit theorem identifies the two.  In the
induced observable/dual-source decomposition
\[
  \mathcal O
  =
  \mathcal E_{\rm slow}\oplus \mathcal E_{\rm fast},
  \qquad
  \mathcal J^*
  =
  \mathcal E_{\rm slow}^*\oplus\mathcal E_{\rm fast}^*,
  \qquad
  \mathcal A
  =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    A_{ss} & A_{sf}\\
    A_{fs} & A_{ff}
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
integrating out the complement is the Schur-complement operation
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_{\rm eff}
  =
  A_{ss}-A_{sf}A_{ff}^{-1}A_{fs}.
  \label{eq:hydro-slow-sector-schur-complement}
\end{equation}
A local hydrodynamic truncation is controlled only if \(A_{ff}^{-1}\) and
the Schur-complement coefficients remain uniformly bounded and expandable in
\(\omega\) and \(\mathbf k\) throughout the scaling window, with a norm and
topology declared before the thermodynamic limit.  Regularity of a projected
retarded response block alone is weaker than this Schur-complement statement.
Exact Ward identities supply some zero directions and constrain their
couplings, but they do not prove complement regularity.  If a spectral subspace,
nonconserved relaxation eigenvalue, Jordan block, kinetic continuum edge,
elastic restoring force, or critical inverse correlation length also enters
the hydrodynamic spectral window of the declared pencil or its effective
linearization, that subspace must be moved into \(\mathcal E_{\rm slow}\)
before a local constitutive expansion is claimed.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{definition}[First-order hydrodynamic constitutive structure]
\label{def:first-order-hydrodynamic-datum}
A parity-even first-order normal-fluid constitutive structure for ordinary
Abelian charges is a
tuple
\[
  \mathfrak D_{\rm hydro}
  =
  \bigl(
    W[g_+,A_+;g_-,A_-],
    T^{\mu\nu},J_A^\mu,
    \mathcal M_{\rm eq},
    \mathcal E_{\rm slow}^{\rm normal},
    T,\mu_A,u^\mu,
    \mathsf f,
    \mathsf C^{(0)},\mathsf C^{(1)},
    \mathsf E
  \bigr)
\]
with the following entries.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item \(W[g_+,A_+;g_-,A_-]\) is the regulated Schwinger--Keldysh generating
  functional of the microscopic QFT with background metric \(g_{\mu\nu}\) and
  Abelian background gauge fields \(A^A_\mu\).  The operators
  \(T^{\mu\nu}\) and \(J_A^\mu\) use the source normalization of
  \eqref{eq:hydro-source-variation}.
\item The exact QFT input is the Ward-identity pair
  \[
    \nabla_\mu J_A^\mu=0,
    \qquad
    \nabla_\mu T^\mu{}_\nu=F^A_{\nu\lambda}J_A^\lambda ,
  \]
  with \(F^A_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A^A_\nu-\partial_\nu A^A_\mu\).
\item \(\mathcal M_{\rm eq}\) is the thermodynamic manifold of homogeneous
  KMS states in the thermodynamic limit.  Its local coordinates are
  temperature \(T>0\), chemical potentials \(\mu_A\), and a
  future-timelike unit vector \(u^\mu\) satisfying \(u^\mu u_\mu=-1\).
\item \(\mathcal E_{\rm slow}^{\rm normal}\) denotes the declared retained
  slow subspace for this normal-fluid truncation:
  \[
    \mathcal E_{\rm slow}^{\rm normal}
    =
    \operatorname{span}\{T^{00},T^{0i},J_A^0\}.
  \]
  This entry is part of the datum.  The displayed operators are a convenient
  basis for the retained observable directions; the subspace is invariant
  under nonsingular changes of hydrodynamic coordinates, while the conjugate
  sources transform in the dual way.  If another spectral direction or
  continuum window becomes slow in the same scaling family, the tuple above
  is not the correct hydrodynamic datum for that state.
\item \(\mathsf f\) is the hydrodynamic frame, namely the prescription for
  extending \((T,\mu_A,u^\mu)\) away from exact equilibrium.  This chapter
  uses Landau matching:
  \[
    u_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=-\varepsilon u^\nu,
    \qquad
    n_A=-u_\mu J_A^\mu .
  \]
\item \(\mathsf C^{(0)}\) is the ideal constitutive map
  \[
    T^{\mu\nu}_{(0)}=\varepsilon u^\mu u^\nu+p\Delta^{\mu\nu},
    \qquad
    J^\mu_{A(0)}=n_Au^\mu,
    \qquad
    \Delta^{\mu\nu}=\eta^{\mu\nu}+u^\mu u^\nu .
  \]
  The thermodynamic functions obey
  \(dp=s\,dT+n_A\,d\mu_A\) and
  \(\varepsilon+p=Ts+\mu_A n_A\).
\item \(\mathsf C^{(1)}\) is the first-gradient constitutive map in the
  declared frame.  In the source-free parity-even sector treated here its
  independent transport data are shear viscosity \(\eta\), bulk viscosity
  \(\zeta\), and conductivity matrix \(\Sigma_{AB}\), appearing as
  \[
    \pi^{\mu\nu}_{(1)}=-\eta\sigma^{\mu\nu}
    -\zeta\Delta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\lambda u^\lambda,
    \qquad
    \nu_A^\mu{}_{(1)}
    =
    -T\Sigma_{AB}\Delta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\nu(\mu_B/T).
  \]
\item \(\mathsf E\) is the entropy-current and positivity condition.  It consists
  of \(S^\mu=su^\mu+O(\partial)\) and the requirement that the quadratic
  entropy-production form be nonnegative for all first gradients.  In this
  sector the condition is
  \[
    \eta\ge0,\qquad \zeta\ge0,\qquad
    \Sigma_{(AB)}v^Av^B\ge0
    \quad\text{for all }v^A\in\R .
  \]
  Here \(\Sigma_{(AB)}=(\Sigma_{AB}+\Sigma_{BA})/2\).
\end{enumerate}
The structure \(\mathfrak D_{\rm hydro}\) is evaluated on a hydrodynamic scaling
family in the sense of Definition~\ref{def:hydro-scaling-family}.  The
sections below derive the ideal equations, frame transformations, first-order
entropy production, and hydrodynamic poles from this structure.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Common extensions of the retained sector.}
When the normal-fluid restriction fails, the slow-sector entry must be
enlarged before constitutive relations are written.  A broken ordinary
\(U(1)\) symmetry adds a phase \(\varphi\) and a Josephson relation of the
form
\[
  u^\mu(\partial_\mu\varphi+A_\mu)+\mu=\text{dissipative corrections},
\]
so the superfluid velocity is not determined by the conserved charge density
alone.  A dynamic critical point may add a nonconserved order parameter
\(\phi\) with relaxational equation
\[
  \partial_t\phi=-\Gamma_\phi\,\frac{\delta \mathcal F}{\delta\phi}
  +\cdots ,
\]
possibly coupled to energy or charge densities as in Model C or to momentum
as in Model H.  A weakly broken charge obeys
\[
  \partial_\mu J^\mu=-\Gamma n+\cdots,
  \qquad
  \omega=-\ii\Gamma-\ii Dk^2+\cdots,
\]
and remains quasihydrodynamic when \(\Gamma\) scales with the external
frequency.  In a kinetic regime the slow variables can instead be angular or
momentum moments of a distribution function; ordinary hydrodynamics follows
only after the nonconserved collision eigenvalues are uniformly separated
from the conserved ones.  Elastic, orientational, and higher-form ordered
media similarly require displacement, orientation, or defect-density fields.
Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-kinetic-theory-controlled-limit} treats kinetic
moments, and Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-nonequilibrium-steady-states-open-systems}
discusses Model A/B/C/H-type nonequilibrium data.

\section{Conserved Densities and Local State Variables}

Let \(T^{\mu\nu}\) be the stress tensor and let \(J^\mu_A\) be conserved
currents for ordinary internal symmetries.  In flat space without external
sources,
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0,
  \qquad
  \partial_\mu J^\mu_A=0 .
  \label{eq:hydro-flat-ward-identities}
\end{equation}
A local equilibrium state is parametrized by a future-timelike velocity field
\(u^\mu(x)\), temperature \(T(x)>0\), and chemical potentials
\(\mu_A(x)\), with
\[
  u^\mu u_\mu=-1.
\]
The spatial projector in the local rest frame is
\[
  \Delta^{\mu\nu}=\eta^{\mu\nu}+u^\mu u^\nu,
  \qquad
  \Delta^{\mu\nu}u_\nu=0.
\]

The ideal constitutive relations are
\begin{equation}
  T^{\mu\nu}_{(0)}
  =
  \varepsilon u^\mu u^\nu
  +p\,\Delta^{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  J^\mu_{A(0)}=n_Au^\mu .
  \label{eq:hydro-ideal-constitutive-relations}
\end{equation}
Here \(\varepsilon(T,\mu)\), \(p(T,\mu)\), and \(n_A(T,\mu)\) are local
thermodynamic functions of the underlying QFT.  The frame used below is the
Landau frame:
\begin{equation}
  u_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=-\varepsilon u^\nu,
  \label{eq:hydro-landau-frame}
\end{equation}
so the nonideal stress tensor carries no energy flux in the local rest
frame.  Charge diffusion is allowed to have components orthogonal to
\(u^\mu\).

\section{Hydrodynamic Frames}

Hydrodynamic variables are local coordinates on the manifold of equilibrium
states and its derivative expansion.  They are not uniquely defined away
from strict equilibrium.  For any chosen \(u^\mu\), decompose the one-point
functions as
\begin{align}
  T^{\mu\nu}
  &=
  \varepsilon u^\mu u^\nu
  +p\Delta^{\mu\nu}
  +u^\mu q^\nu+u^\nu q^\mu
  +\pi^{\mu\nu},
  \label{eq:hydro-general-frame-stress}\\
  J_A^\mu&=n_Au^\mu+\nu_A^\mu ,
  \label{eq:hydro-general-frame-current}
\end{align}
with
\[
  u_\mu q^\mu=u_\mu\nu_A^\mu=0,
  \qquad
  u_\mu\pi^{\mu\nu}=0 .
\]
The scalar quantities \(\varepsilon,p,n_A\), the velocity \(u^\mu\), and the
transverse tensors \(q^\mu,\nu_A^\mu,\pi^{\mu\nu}\) depend on the chosen
frame.  Under a first-derivative redefinition
\[
  u^\mu\mapsto u^\mu+\delta u^\mu,\qquad
  T\mapsto T+\delta T,\qquad
  \mu_A\mapsto\mu_A+\delta\mu_A,\qquad
  u_\mu\delta u^\mu=0,
\]
while the physical one-point functions are held fixed, the transverse
vectors change at first derivative order as
\begin{equation}
  q^\mu\mapsto q^\mu-(\varepsilon+p)\delta u^\mu,
  \qquad
  \nu_A^\mu\mapsto\nu_A^\mu-n_A\delta u^\mu .
  \label{eq:hydro-frame-transformation}
\end{equation}
The scalar redefinitions change the separation between ideal scalar data and
first-order scalar corrections.  Landau matching is the choice
\[
  \varepsilon=u_\mu u_\nu T^{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  n_A=-u_\mu J_A^\mu ,
\]
so that no first-order correction is hidden inside the definitions of
\(\varepsilon\) and \(n_A\).  To derive
\eqref{eq:hydro-frame-transformation}, keep \(T^{\mu\nu}\) and \(J_A^\mu\)
fixed and vary only the ideal pieces.  Since
\(\delta\Delta^{\mu\nu}=u^\mu\delta u^\nu+\delta u^\mu u^\nu\), the ideal
stress changes by
\[
  \delta T^{\mu\nu}_{(0)}
  =
  (\varepsilon+p)(u^\mu\delta u^\nu+\delta u^\mu u^\nu)
  +\text{scalar variations}.
\]
Its transverse energy-flux part is therefore
\((\varepsilon+p)\delta u^\mu\).  The ideal current changes by
\(\delta J^\mu_{A(0)}=n_A\delta u^\mu\) in the transverse sector.  The
nonideal vectors must shift by the negatives of these transverse changes.
The Landau frame condition \eqref{eq:hydro-landau-frame} is the choice
\(q^\mu=0\).  If \(\varepsilon+p\ne0\), it is reached by
\(\delta u^\mu=q^\mu/(\varepsilon+p)\) to the order considered here.  This
coordinate choice changes the displayed form of charge diffusion but cannot
change pole locations, Kubo coefficients, or source-response functions.

\section{Thermodynamic Closure}

The equation of state supplies a function \(p=p(T,\mu_A)\).  In a finite
spatial volume, before the thermodynamic limit, it is obtained from the
grand-canonical partition function
\[
  Z_{\beta,\mu}
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}\exp[-\beta(H-\mu_AQ_A)],
  \qquad
  p_{\beta,\mu,V}
  =
  \frac{T}{V}\log Z_{\beta,\mu}.
\]
The pressure \(p(T,\mu)\) used in hydrodynamics is the thermodynamic limit
of \(p_{\beta,\mu,V}\), assuming this limit exists and is differentiable in
the state under consideration.  Differentiating the finite-volume expression
gives
\[
  \left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial\mu_A}\right)_T
  =
  \frac{\langle Q_A\rangle}{V}
  \equiv n_A,
\]
and
\[
  \left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right)_{\mu}
  =
  \frac{p+\varepsilon-\mu_A n_A}{T}
  \equiv s,
\]
where \(\varepsilon=\langle H\rangle/V\).  Thus
\[
  p=p(T,\mu_A),\qquad
  s=\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right)_\mu,\qquad
  n_A=\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial\mu_A}\right)_T
\]
are not independent definitions: they are the differentiability data of the
equilibrium KMS state.  Consequently,
\[
  s=\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial T}\right)_{\mu},
  \qquad
  n_A=\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial\mu_A}\right)_{T,\mu_{B\ne A}},
\]
and
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon+p=Ts+\mu_A n_A.
  \label{eq:hydro-gibbs-duhem-energy}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
  dp=s\,dT+n_A\,d\mu_A,
  \qquad
  d\varepsilon=T\,ds+\mu_A\,dn_A .
  \label{eq:hydro-thermodynamic-relations}
\end{equation}
In the rest frame of a homogeneous equilibrium state, spatial rotations give
\[
  \langle T^{00}\rangle=\varepsilon,\qquad
  \langle T^{0i}\rangle=0,\qquad
  \langle T^{ij}\rangle=p\,\delta^{ij},
  \qquad
  \langle J_A^0\rangle=n_A,\qquad
  \langle J_A^i\rangle=0.
\]
Lorentz covariance then gives the ideal tensors
\eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-constitutive-relations} in a state whose local rest
frame velocity is \(u^\mu\).  This derivation is local in the sense of the
gradient expansion: it assumes that, on each hydrodynamic cell, the state is
approximated by a homogeneous KMS state with the displayed values of
\(T,\mu_A,u^\mu\).
These identities are the local thermodynamic closure of the ideal Ward
system.  They are imposed pointwise on the slowly varying local state
variables; the microscopic QFT input is the equilibrium equation of state and
the assumption that the hydrodynamic scaling limit probes only this local
state manifold.

\section{Ideal Equations and Entropy Conservation}

\begin{remark}[Ideal hydrodynamic equations]
\label{rem:ideal-hydro-equations}
Substituting \eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-constitutive-relations} into the Ward
identities gives
\begin{align}
  u^\mu\partial_\mu\varepsilon
  +(\varepsilon+p)\partial_\mu u^\mu&=0,
  \label{eq:hydro-ideal-energy-equation}\\
  (\varepsilon+p)u^\mu\partial_\mu u^\alpha
  +\Delta^{\alpha\mu}\partial_\mu p&=0,
  \label{eq:hydro-ideal-euler-equation}\\
  u^\mu\partial_\mu n_A+n_A\partial_\mu u^\mu&=0 .
  \label{eq:hydro-ideal-charge-equation}
\end{align}

Contract \(\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}_{(0)}=0\) with \(u_\nu\).  Since
\[
  u_\nu\partial_\mu(\varepsilon u^\mu u^\nu)
  =
  -\partial_\mu(\varepsilon u^\mu),
  \qquad
  u_\nu\partial_\mu(p\Delta^{\mu\nu})
  =
  -p\,\partial_\mu u^\mu,
\]
using \(u_\nu\partial_\mu u^\nu=\frac12\partial_\mu(u_\nu u^\nu)=0\), one
obtains \eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-energy-equation}.  Projecting the same Ward
identity with \(\Delta^\alpha{}_\nu\) gives
\[
  \Delta^\alpha{}_\nu\partial_\mu(\varepsilon u^\mu u^\nu)
  =
  \varepsilon u^\mu\partial_\mu u^\alpha,
  \qquad
  \Delta^\alpha{}_\nu\partial_\mu(p\Delta^{\mu\nu})
  =
  p\,u^\mu\partial_\mu u^\alpha+\Delta^{\alpha\mu}\partial_\mu p,
\]
which is \eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-euler-equation}.  Current conservation gives
\eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-charge-equation} directly.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Ideal entropy current]
\label{rem:ideal-entropy-conservation}
The ideal entropy current
\[
  S^\mu_{(0)}=s u^\mu
\]
is conserved on ideal hydrodynamic solutions:
\[
  \partial_\mu S^\mu_{(0)}=0.
\]

Using the first law,
\[
  T u^\mu\partial_\mu s
  =
  u^\mu\partial_\mu\varepsilon
  -\mu_A u^\mu\partial_\mu n_A .
\]
Substitute the ideal energy equation
\eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-energy-equation} and the ideal charge equations
\eqref{eq:hydro-ideal-charge-equation}.  This gives
\[
  T u^\mu\partial_\mu s
  =
  -(\varepsilon+p)\partial_\mu u^\mu
  +\mu_A n_A\partial_\mu u^\mu
  =
  -Ts\,\partial_\mu u^\mu,
\]
where \(\varepsilon+p=Ts+\mu_A n_A\) was used.  Therefore
\[
  \partial_\mu(su^\mu)
  =
  u^\mu\partial_\mu s+s\partial_\mu u^\mu=0.
\]
\end{remark}

\section{First-Order Constitutive Relations}

The first-order derivative data must be defined before the transport
coefficients are introduced.  Define the expansion, acceleration, and shear
tensor by
\[
  \vartheta=\partial_\mu u^\mu,
  \qquad
  a^\mu=u^\nu\partial_\nu u^\mu,
\]
and
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{\mu\nu}
  =
  2\Delta^{\mu\alpha}\Delta^{\nu\beta}
  \left(
  \partial_{(\alpha}u_{\beta)}
  -\frac{1}{d}\Delta_{\alpha\beta}\vartheta
  \right).
  \label{eq:hydro-shear-tensor-definition}
\end{equation}
Then \(\sigma^{\mu\nu}u_\nu=0\), \(\sigma^\mu{}_\mu=0\), and
\(\sigma^{\mu\nu}=\sigma^{\nu\mu}\).
The antisymmetric transverse derivative is the vorticity
\[
  \omega^{\mu\nu}
  =
  \Delta^{\mu\alpha}\Delta^{\nu\beta}
  \partial_{[\alpha}u_{\beta]},
  \qquad
  \omega^{\mu\nu}=-\omega^{\nu\mu}.
\]
It does not contribute to the parity-even dissipative stress tensor at
first order because the stress tensor is symmetric and the entropy
production is quadratic in the symmetric thermodynamic forces.

With sources, the ideal Euler equation is obtained by projecting
\eqref{eq:hydro-source-stress-ward} orthogonally to \(u^\mu\):
\begin{equation}
  (\varepsilon+p)a^\mu+\Delta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\nu p
  =
  n_A\mathcal E^{A\mu}.
  \label{eq:hydro-sourceful-ideal-euler}
\end{equation}
Using \(dp=s\,dT+n_A\,d\mu_A\) and
\(\varepsilon+p=Ts+\mu_A n_A\), this equation is equivalently
\begin{equation}
  a^\mu+\Delta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\nu\log T
  =
  \frac{T n_A}{\varepsilon+p}\,
  V_A^\mu,
  \qquad
  V_A^\mu
  =
  \Delta^{\mu\nu}
  \left[
    \frac{\mathcal E^A_\nu}{T}
    -\partial_\nu\!\left(\frac{\mu_A}{T}\right)
  \right].
  \label{eq:hydro-acceleration-force-reduction}
\end{equation}
Thus acceleration and the transverse temperature gradient are not independent
first-order data once the ideal equations are used.  The independent
parity-even dissipative structures in Landau frame are therefore the
symmetric traceless tensor \(\sigma^{\mu\nu}\), the scalar
\(\vartheta\), and the transverse charge forces \(V_A^\mu\).  This is the
classification statement behind the constitutive relations below; it is not
an additional dynamical assumption.

In Landau frame, the parity-even first-order constitutive relations are
\begin{align}
  T^{\mu\nu}
  &=
  \varepsilon u^\mu u^\nu
  +p\Delta^{\mu\nu}
  -\eta\,\sigma^{\mu\nu}
  -\zeta\,\Delta^{\mu\nu}\vartheta
  +O(\partial^2),
  \label{eq:hydro-first-order-stress}\\
  J_A^\mu
  &=
  n_Au^\mu
  +\nu_A^\mu
  +O(\partial^2).
  \label{eq:hydro-first-order-current}
\end{align}
In the absence of external electric fields,
\begin{equation}
  \nu_A^\mu
  =
  -T\,\Sigma_{AB}\Delta^{\mu\nu}
  \partial_\nu\!\left(\frac{\mu_B}{T}\right).
  \label{eq:hydro-first-order-current-sourcefree}
\end{equation}
With background gauge fields, the same convention is
\begin{equation}
  \nu_A^\mu
  =
  T\,\Sigma_{AB}\Delta^{\mu\nu}
  \left[
    \frac{\mathcal E^B_\nu}{T}
    -\partial_\nu\!\left(\frac{\mu_B}{T}\right)
  \right].
  \label{eq:hydro-first-order-current-sourceful}
\end{equation}
The matrix \(\Sigma_{AB}\) is the conductivity matrix in the normalization
where, at constant \(T\) and zero background electric field, Fick diffusion
has diffusion matrix
\[
  D_{AB}=\Sigma_{AC}(\chi^{-1})_{CB},
  \qquad
  \chi_{AB}=\left(\frac{\partial n_A}{\partial\mu_B}\right)_T .
\]
The entropy analysis below constrains the symmetric part of \(\Sigma_{AB}\),
which is the hydrodynamic version of the Hermitian dissipative conductivity
sector isolated in Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-spectral-kubo-transport}.
In a parity-even state with no time-reversal-breaking background, the KMS
condition together with microscopic time reversal gives the Onsager
reciprocity \(\Sigma_{AB}=\Sigma_{BA}\).  If time reversal or parity is
broken, an antisymmetric part may appear; it is nondissipative because
\(\Sigma_{[AB]}X_A X_B=0\) for any real thermodynamic force \(X_A\), and it
belongs with Hall-type transport rather than with the dissipative
parity-even sector treated in this chapter.

\section{Entropy Production at First Order}

The first-order entropy current in Landau frame is
\begin{equation}
  S^\mu
  =
  s u^\mu-\frac{\mu_A}{T}\nu_A^\mu+O(\partial^2),
  \qquad
  \nu_A^\mu
  =
  -T\Sigma_{AB}\Delta^{\mu\nu}
  \partial_\nu\!\left(\frac{\mu_B}{T}\right)
  \quad(A^A_\mu=0).
  \label{eq:hydro-first-order-entropy-current}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Entropy production fixes positivity.}
Using \eqref{eq:hydro-first-order-stress} and
\eqref{eq:hydro-first-order-current}, the entropy production through second
order in gradients is
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu S^\mu
  =
  \frac{\eta}{2T}\sigma^{\mu\nu}\sigma_{\mu\nu}
  +\frac{\zeta}{T}\vartheta^2
  +T\,\Sigma_{AB}
  \Delta^{\mu\nu}
  \partial_\mu\!\left(\frac{\mu_A}{T}\right)
  \partial_\nu\!\left(\frac{\mu_B}{T}\right)
  +O(\partial^3).
  \label{eq:hydro-entropy-production}
\end{equation}
Hence local entropy production is nonnegative for arbitrary first gradients
if
\[
  \eta\ge0,\qquad \zeta\ge0,\qquad
  \Sigma_{AB}\ \text{is positive semidefinite}.
\]
Write the dissipative stress tensor as
\[
  \tau^{\mu\nu}
  =
  -\eta\sigma^{\mu\nu}-\zeta\Delta^{\mu\nu}\vartheta,
  \qquad
  T^{\mu\nu}=T_{(0)}^{\mu\nu}+\tau^{\mu\nu}.
\]
Using the Ward identities, the thermodynamic identities, and
\(u_\mu\tau^{\mu\nu}=u_\mu\nu_A^\mu=0\), the divergence of
\eqref{eq:hydro-first-order-entropy-current} through second order reduces to
\[
  \partial_\mu S^\mu
  =
  -\frac{1}{T}\tau^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu u_\nu
  -\nu_A^\mu\partial_\mu\!\left(\frac{\mu_A}{T}\right)
  +O(\partial^3).
\]
With background gauge fields, the second term is replaced by
\[
  \nu_A^\mu
  \left[
    \frac{\mathcal E^A_\mu}{T}
    -\partial_\mu\!\left(\frac{\mu_A}{T}\right)
  \right],
\]
which follows from the force term
\eqref{eq:hydro-source-stress-ward} in the energy equation.  The
source-free formula used in \eqref{eq:hydro-entropy-production} is the
special case \(\mathcal E^A_\mu=0\).
The decomposition of \(\partial_\mu u_\nu\) into shear, trace, vorticity,
and acceleration gives
\[
  \sigma^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu u_\nu
  =
  \frac12\sigma^{\mu\nu}\sigma_{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  \Delta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu u_\nu=\vartheta.
\]
Substituting \(\tau^{\mu\nu}\) and \(\nu_A^\mu\) gives
\eqref{eq:hydro-entropy-production}.

\section{Charge Diffusion and Susceptibility Geometry}

Consider a state with \(u^\mu=(1,\mathbf0)\), constant \(T\), and vanishing
background flow.  Linearize first a single conserved charge around
equilibrium.  The constitutive relation gives
\[
  J^0=n_0+\delta n,\qquad
  J^i=-\Sigma\,\partial_i\delta\mu,
  \qquad
  \delta n=\chi\,\delta\mu .
\]
Charge conservation gives
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t\delta n-D\nabla^2\delta n=0,
  \qquad
  D=\frac{\Sigma}{\chi}.
  \label{eq:hydro-charge-diffusion-equation}
\end{equation}
For a plane wave \(\delta n\propto e^{-\ii\omega t+\ii\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf x}\),
the pole is
\begin{equation}
  \omega=-\ii D k^2+O(k^4).
  \label{eq:hydro-charge-diffusion-pole}
\end{equation}
The Einstein relation \(D=\Sigma/\chi\) is the hydrodynamic form of the same
coefficient matching that appears in the Kubo conductivity formula.

For several charges, the susceptibility matrix is the Hessian of the
equilibrium pressure with respect to chemical potentials,
\[
  \chi_{AB}
  =
  \left(\frac{\partial n_A}{\partial\mu_B}\right)_T
  =
  \frac{\partial^2p}{\partial\mu_A\partial\mu_B}.
\]
Thermodynamic stability of the homogeneous KMS state means that \(\chi\) is
positive definite on the subspace of charges under consideration.  In a
parity-even time-reversal-invariant fluid, \(\Sigma\) is symmetric positive
semidefinite.  The linearized equations at constant temperature are
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t\delta n_A
  -D_{AB}\nabla^2\delta n_B=0,
  \qquad
  D=\Sigma\chi^{-1}.
  \label{eq:hydro-multicharge-diffusion}
\end{equation}
Although \(D\) need not be symmetric in the ordinary Euclidean inner product,
its spectrum is nonnegative.  Indeed \(D\) is similar to
\[
  \chi^{-1/2}\Sigma\chi^{-1/2},
\]
because
\[
  D
  =
  \chi^{1/2}
  \left(\chi^{-1/2}\Sigma\chi^{-1/2}\right)
  \chi^{-1/2}.
\]
The middle matrix is symmetric positive semidefinite.  Hence the diffusive
normal modes have
\[
  \omega_i(\mathbf k)=-\ii \lambda_i k^2+O(k^4),
  \qquad
  \lambda_i\ge0,
\]
where \(\lambda_i\) are the eigenvalues of \(D\).  Strictly positive
\(\lambda_i\) require that no linear combination of the charges has zero
conductivity.

The density source-response matrix also fixes the order of matrix
multiplication in diffusion formulae.  Couple a source \(h_A\) to the
density with the Hamiltonian convention \(H=H_0-\int h_A n_A\), so that the
electrochemical force is proportional to \(\nabla(\delta\mu_A-h_A)\).  In
Fourier space the linearized equation is
\[
  \left(Dk^2-\ii\omega\,\mathbf 1\right)_{AB}\delta n_B
  =
  k^2\Sigma_{AB}h_B .
\]
Therefore the nonlocal hydrodynamic part of the retarded density
source-response kernel is
\begin{equation}
  K^R_{n_An_B}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  k^2
  \left(Dk^2-\ii\omega\,\mathbf 1\right)^{-1}_{AC}
  \Sigma_{CB}
  +\text{analytic contact terms}.
  \label{eq:hydro-multicharge-density-response}
\end{equation}
At \(\omega=0\) and then \(k\to0\), the nonlocal term gives
\[
  D^{-1}\Sigma=\chi,
\]
assuming \(\Sigma\) is invertible on the diffusive subspace.  This is the
matrix version of the static susceptibility matching in
\eqref{eq:hydro-density-retarded-diffusion}.

\section{Neutral Linearized Modes}

Linearize a neutral fluid around a homogeneous rest state:
\[
  u^\mu=(1,\mathbf v)+O(\mathbf v^2),
  \qquad
  \varepsilon=\varepsilon_0+\delta\varepsilon,
  \qquad
  p=p_0+\delta p,
  \qquad
  \delta p=c_s^2\delta\varepsilon ,
\]
where
\[
  c_s^2=\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial\varepsilon}\right)_{\rm eq},
  \qquad
  w=\varepsilon_0+p_0 .
\]
The stress tensor components to first order in fluctuations and first order
in gradients are
\begin{align}
  T^{00}&=\varepsilon_0+\delta\varepsilon,
  \label{eq:hydro-linear-t00}\\
  T^{0i}&=wv^i,
  \label{eq:hydro-linear-t0i}\\
  T^{ij}
  &=
  p_0\delta^{ij}
  +c_s^2\delta\varepsilon\,\delta^{ij}
  -\eta\left(\partial_i v_j+\partial_j v_i
  -\frac{2}{d}\delta_{ij}\partial_\ell v^\ell\right)
  -\zeta\delta_{ij}\partial_\ell v^\ell .
  \label{eq:hydro-linear-tij}
\end{align}
The Ward identities become
\begin{align}
  \partial_t\delta\varepsilon+w\partial_i v^i&=0,
  \label{eq:hydro-linear-energy}\\
  w\partial_t v_i+\partial_i\delta p
  -\eta\nabla^2v_i
  -\left(\zeta+\eta\left(1-\frac{2}{d}\right)\right)
  \partial_i\partial_jv^j&=0 .
  \label{eq:hydro-linear-momentum}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Shear and sound poles.}
For fluctuations proportional to
\(e^{-\ii\omega t+\ii\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf x}\), the transverse velocity
mode has
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{\rm shear}
  =
  -\ii\,\frac{\eta}{w}k^2+O(k^4).
  \label{eq:hydro-shear-pole}
\end{equation}
The longitudinal energy-momentum modes obey
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{\rm sound,\pm}
  =
  \pm c_s k
  -\frac{\ii}{2}\Gamma_s k^2
  +O(k^3),
  \qquad
  \Gamma_s
  =
  \frac{\zeta+2\eta\frac{d-1}{d}}{w}.
  \label{eq:hydro-sound-poles}
\end{equation}

For a transverse mode, \(k_i v^i=0\) and
\eqref{eq:hydro-linear-momentum} becomes
\[
  -\ii\omega w v_i+\eta k^2v_i=0,
\]
which gives \eqref{eq:hydro-shear-pole}.

For a longitudinal mode take \(\mathbf k=k\hat x\) and \(v_i=v_L\hat x_i\).
Equations \eqref{eq:hydro-linear-energy} and
\eqref{eq:hydro-linear-momentum} give
\[
  -\ii\omega\delta\varepsilon+\ii wk v_L=0,
\]
and
\[
  -\ii\omega wv_L+\ii k c_s^2\delta\varepsilon
  +\left(\zeta+2\eta\frac{d-1}{d}\right)k^2v_L=0 .
\]
Eliminating \(v_L\) gives
\[
  \omega^2-c_s^2k^2
  +\ii\frac{\zeta+2\eta(d-1)/d}{w}\,\omega k^2=0.
\]
Solving perturbatively at small \(k\) gives
\eqref{eq:hydro-sound-poles}.

\section{First-Order Hydrodynamics Is Not a Causal Evolution Law}
\label{sec:hydro-first-order-acausality}

The poles above are hydrodynamic correlator data in a small
\((\omega,\mathbf k)\) region.  They are not, by themselves, a fundamental
initial-value problem at arbitrary wave number.  The transverse channel makes
the distinction explicit.  For a shear perturbation
\(v_x(t,y)\) with all other fluctuations set to zero,
\eqref{eq:hydro-linear-momentum} gives
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t v_x-D_\eta\,\partial_y^2v_x=0,
  \qquad
  D_\eta=\frac{\eta}{w}.
  \label{eq:hydro-first-order-shear-diffusion-pde}
\end{equation}
The retarded Green function of this equation on the line is
\begin{equation}
  G_{\rm diff}(t,y)
  =
  \theta(t)\frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi D_\eta t}}\,
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{y^2}{4D_\eta t}\right],
  \qquad D_\eta>0.
  \label{eq:hydro-shear-heat-kernel}
\end{equation}
For every \(t>0\), this kernel is nonzero at arbitrarily large \(|y|\).  If
\eqref{eq:hydro-first-order-shear-diffusion-pde} is treated as an exact
relativistic PDE, it therefore has instantaneous spatial support and cannot
respect the microscopic light cone.  This conclusion does not contradict
microscopic causality: the exact retarded QFT correlator is causal, while the
diffusion pole is only the first term in a hydrodynamic scaling expansion.
Analytic contact terms, nonhydrodynamic poles, branch cuts, and ultimately the
ultraviolet completion are invisible if the pole is extrapolated outside
\(\omega\tau_{\rm micro}\ll1\), \(|\mathbf k|\ell_{\rm micro}\ll1\).

The same failure appears as a boosted-frame pathology.  Suppose one takes the
rest-frame diffusion pole
\(\omega'=-\ii D_\eta k'^2\) and rewrites it in a frame moving with speed
\(u\), \(0<|u|<1\), along the wave vector:
\[
  \omega'=\gamma(\omega-uk),
  \qquad
  k'=\gamma(k-u\omega),
  \qquad
  \gamma=(1-u^2)^{-1/2}.
\]
The boosted dispersion relation is
\begin{equation}
  \omega-uk+\ii D_\eta\gamma(k-u\omega)^2=0 .
  \label{eq:hydro-boosted-diffusion-dispersion}
\end{equation}
It is now quadratic in \(\omega\).  At sufficiently large real \(k\) one
branch has \(\operatorname{Im}\omega>0\).  This growing branch is not a new QFT
instability; it is the high-wave-number artifact of boosting a parabolic
truncation beyond the scaling window in which it was derived.  Hydrodynamic
frame choices matter here.  A frame is a choice of variables away from
equilibrium, and different choices move time derivatives among the
constitutive relations.  Landau-frame first-order hydrodynamics is a valid
low-frequency correlator expansion but is not a causal global evolution
system.  Some first-order frames can be arranged to be causal and stable under extra
coefficient inequalities, but those inequalities are additional dynamical
input, not consequences of the first-order entropy argument alone.

\section{A Causal Relaxation Completion in the Shear Channel}
\label{sec:hydro-causal-shear-relaxation}

A minimal way to see what causal completion adds is to keep the shear stress
as a transient variable instead of replacing it instantly by
\(-\eta\sigma^{\mu\nu}\).  In the same transverse channel write
\(\Pi(t,y)=\delta T^{xy}\).  The linear Muller--Israel--Stewart shear sector is
\begin{align}
  w\,\partial_t v_x+\partial_y\Pi&=0,
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-shear-momentum}\\
  \tau_\pi\,\partial_t\Pi+\Pi&=-\eta\,\partial_yv_x .
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-shear-relaxation}
\end{align}
The new datum \(\tau_\pi\) is a relaxation time for the nonhydrodynamic shear
stress.  Eliminating \(\Pi\) gives the telegrapher equation
\begin{equation}
  \tau_\pi\,\partial_t^2v_x+\partial_tv_x
  -D_\eta\,\partial_y^2v_x=0,
  \qquad D_\eta=\frac{\eta}{w}.
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-shear-telegrapher}
\end{equation}
For \(v_x\propto e^{-\ii\omega t+\ii ky}\), the dispersion relation is
\begin{equation}
  \tau_\pi\omega^2+\ii\omega-D_\eta k^2=0.
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-shear-dispersion}
\end{equation}
At small \(k\) it has two poles:
\begin{align}
  \omega_{\rm hydro}
  &=
  -\ii D_\eta k^2+O(k^4),
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-hydrodynamic-shear-pole}\\
  \omega_{\rm transient}
  &=
  -\frac{\ii}{\tau_\pi}
  +\ii D_\eta k^2+O(k^4).
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-transient-shear-pole}
\end{align}
The first pole reproduces first-order shear diffusion inside the hydrodynamic
regime.  The second pole is a gapped, nonhydrodynamic relaxation mode.  At
large \(k\),
\[
  \omega_\pm
  =
  \pm v_T k-\frac{\ii}{2\tau_\pi}+O(k^{-1}),
  \qquad
  v_T^2=\frac{D_\eta}{\tau_\pi}
  =
  \frac{\eta}{w\tau_\pi}.
\]
Thus the shear sector is linearly stable and causal in this simplified sense
if
\begin{equation}
  w>0,\qquad
  \eta\ge0,\qquad
  \tau_\pi>0,\qquad
  v_T^2\le1 .
  \label{eq:hydro-mis-shear-stability-conditions}
\end{equation}
The first two inequalities follow from thermodynamic stability and entropy or
Kubo positivity.  Microscopic retardedness requires exact retarded kernels to
be holomorphic for \(\operatorname{Im}\omega>0\) and to have support only
after the source insertion; in local relativistic QFT the commutator support
is further restricted by the light cone.  It does \emph{not} require every
exact singularity to sit strictly below the real axis.  In finite volume the
Lehmann representation gives real-axis boundary poles or delta-function
spectral lines with the retarded \(+\,\ii0\) prescription.  In the
thermodynamic limit those lines may accumulate into real-axis cuts.  Poles in
the lower half-plane describe damped transients, resonances on an appropriate
continued sheet, or effective hydrodynamic/kinetic degrees of freedom after
coarse graining.  The exact stability statement is absence of singularities in
the open upper half-plane.  It does not, by itself, prove that a finite
Muller--Israel--Stewart or BRSSS truncation is hyperbolic, nor does it fix
\(\tau_\pi\) or the full set of second-order coefficients.  Those are
additional matching data and coefficient inequalities of the chosen
transient-hydrodynamic model.

For the sound channel and for nonlinear flows, the theorem boundary is
stronger.  One must choose state variables and frame, include the appropriate
bulk and shear relaxation sectors, and impose coefficient inequalities that
make the resulting PDE hyperbolic with a causal characteristic cone.  Linear
well-posedness around homogeneous equilibrium is known in selected
MIS/BRSSS-type and causal first-order formulations under such hypotheses.
Nonlinear well-posedness is also formulation- and regime-dependent, usually
proved only for states close to equilibrium and coefficients satisfying
specified bounds.  Therefore this chapter uses causal relaxation theory as a
controlled completion example, not as a general theorem that every
second-order hydrodynamic truncation of every QFT is causal.

Matching back to first order is a low-frequency operation.  Solving
\eqref{eq:hydro-mis-shear-relaxation} in Fourier space gives
\[
  \Pi(\omega,k)
  =
  -\frac{\eta}{1-\ii\omega\tau_\pi}\,\ii k\,v_x(\omega,k)
  =
  -\eta\,\ii k\,v_x
  +O(\omega\tau_\pi\,\partial v).
\]
Only when \(|\omega|\tau_\pi\ll1\) and \(|k|\ell_{\rm micro}\ll1\) may the
transient stress be integrated out and the first-order constitutive relation
used.  The causal completion and the first-order expansion therefore agree on
the shear pole through \(O(k^2)\), while disagreeing precisely where the
parabolic equation had been extrapolated beyond its domain.

\section{Retarded Source-Response Pole Matching}

Hydrodynamic poles are singularities of retarded source-response kernels
after the thermodynamic limit and the hydrodynamic scaling limit have been
taken in the specified order.  For the source convention
\(H=H_0-\int h\,n\), the density source-response kernel is the negative of
the commutator-retarded function of
Chapter~\ref{ch:thermal-spectral-kubo-transport}.  For a single diffusive
charge with susceptibility \(\chi\), it has the universal long-wavelength
form
\begin{equation}
  K^R_{nn}(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \chi\,
  \frac{Dk^2}{Dk^2-\ii\omega}
  +\text{terms analytic at }\omega,k=0 .
  \label{eq:hydro-density-retarded-diffusion}
\end{equation}
so that \(K^R_{nn}(0,\mathbf k\to0)=\chi\).  The pole
is \(\omega=-\ii Dk^2\).  Taking \(\mathbf k\to0\) before
\(\omega\to0\) removes the diffusive denominator; taking the hydrodynamic
scaling limit \(\omega\sim k^2\) keeps it.  This is the same order-of-limits
issue that appears in dc conductivity and static susceptibility.

In the shear channel, the momentum-density correlator has a pole at
\eqref{eq:hydro-shear-pole}.  In the sound channel, the stress-tensor and
energy-density correlators have the pair of poles
\eqref{eq:hydro-sound-poles}.  The residues are fixed by thermodynamic
susceptibilities and Ward identities, while the imaginary parts of the pole
locations are fixed by the Kubo coefficients \(\eta\), \(\zeta\), and
\(\Sigma_{AB}\).

\section{Omitted Slow Modes and Memory Kernels}
\label{sec:hydro-omitted-slow-mode-memory}

The normal-fluid truncation is local only when omitted variables relax on a
scale that remains microscopic in the hydrodynamic limit.  A single
relaxational order parameter is enough to see the issue.  Let \(X\) denote a
hydrodynamic force or density combination retained in the normal-fluid sector,
and let a nonconserved scalar \(\phi\) couple linearly to it.  Linearizing the
relaxational equation gives
\begin{equation}
  \left(\Gamma_\phi+\kappa_\phi k^2-\ii\omega\right)\phi(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  \lambda X(\omega,\mathbf k)+h_\phi(\omega,\mathbf k),
  \label{eq:hydro-relaxing-order-parameter-equation}
\end{equation}
where \(\Gamma_\phi>0\) is the homogeneous relaxation rate.  If \(\phi\) is
not retained, substituting its solution back into the \(X\)-sector produces
the memory correction
\begin{equation}
  \Delta K_X(\omega,\mathbf k)
  =
  -\frac{\lambda^2}
  {\Gamma_\phi+\kappa_\phi k^2-\ii\omega}.
  \label{eq:hydro-omitted-order-parameter-memory}
\end{equation}
For fixed \(\Gamma_\phi\) this is a local gradient expansion:
\[
  \Delta K_X
  =
  -\frac{\lambda^2}{\Gamma_\phi}
  +\frac{\lambda^2}{\Gamma_\phi^2}
  (\kappa_\phi k^2-\ii\omega)
  +\cdots .
\]
But near a dynamic critical point, or in any scaling family with
\(\Gamma_\phi=O(\omega,k^2)\), the denominator in
\eqref{eq:hydro-omitted-order-parameter-memory} is of the same order as the
hydrodynamic denominator.  The correction is then nonlocal in time in the
normal-fluid variables, and the local coefficients obtained by expanding at
fixed \(\Gamma_\phi\) diverge as the tuning parameter is sent to the slow
limit.  The correct effective theory retains \(\phi\) with its own equation,
noise, and KMS data.

In a multi-field problem the scalar denominator above is replaced by the
Schur complement of the same declared inverse-response or memory pencil used
to define the slow window.  A change of observable basis can move a small
number out of a diagonal entry without moving the slow observable subspace;
what is retained is \(\operatorname{Ran}P_{\rm slow}\), rather than the
coordinate whose diagonal entry looks slow in one basis.  In a kinetic or
critical continuum the obstruction may be a branch edge rather than an
isolated pole of a finite matrix.  For example, an omitted
family of relaxation rates \(a\in[a_0,1]\) with constant spectral density
contributes the finite memory kernel
\[
  \int_{a_0}^{1}\frac{da}{a-\ii\omega}.
\]
When \(a_0\) stays microscopic this is an analytic local coefficient after
expansion at small \(\omega\).  When the lower edge \(a_0\) scales into the
hydrodynamic window, the integral develops a nonlocal low-frequency
dependence and no finite list of named operators captures the whole slow
sector.  This is the finite-dimensional mechanism behind the Model A/C/H and
kinetic warnings: the failure is not a mathematical subtlety of the gradient
expansion but a physical omission of a slow spectral channel.

\section{Hydrodynamic Limit as a QFT Claim}

The deterministic equations above are consequences of a proposed
hydrodynamic scaling limit of the microscopic QFT.  The limit has the
following structure.  First take the thermodynamic limit of the KMS state.
Then consider sources and correlators with
\[
  \omega\tau_{\rm micro}\ll1,
  \qquad
  |\mathbf k|\ell_{\rm micro}\ll1,
\]
while keeping the ratios appropriate to the mode under study:
\(\omega\sim k^2\) for diffusion and shear, and \(\omega\sim k\) for sound.
For the normal-fluid theory of this chapter, the QFT assertion is that the
declared subspace \(\operatorname{span}\{T^{0\mu},J_A^0\}\) is complete in
this scaling family and that the complement satisfies
Controlled Approximation~\ref{ca:hydro-slow-sector-completeness}.  Then the
nonanalyticities in the retained source-response kernels are the
conservation-law poles derived above, up to fluctuation corrections and
long-time tails that belong to the next chapter.  In a different state or
tuning limit, the assertion changes: if a Goldstone phase, critical order
parameter, weakly relaxed charge, kinetic moment continuum, or elastic
variable enters the hydrodynamic spectral window of the declared pencil, or
of an explicitly declared linearization of that pencil, it must be part of the
hydrodynamic datum before the complement is tested for regularity.  Proving
the assertion requires
clustering, real-time analyticity, local equilibration, and a uniform
resolvent or memory-kernel gap for all omitted channels.

\begin{openproblem}[Hydrodynamic emergence theorem]
\label{op:hydrodynamic-emergence-theorem}
Find physically useful QFT hypotheses under which local thermalization,
clustering, analyticity of retarded functions, positivity of entropy
production, completeness of the retained slow sector, regularity of its
complement, and the hydrodynamic pole expansion can be proved from the
microscopic theory.
\end{openproblem}
