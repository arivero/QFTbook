\chapter{Two-Dimensional Supersymmetric Models}
\label{ch:susy-two-dimensional-lg_sigma_glsm}

\ConventionsMixed

This chapter begins the lower-dimensional supersymmetric examples.  The main
objects are \(2D\) \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) Landau--Ginzburg models, sigma
models, and gauged linear sigma models.  Each is treated as a QFT presentation with
declared fields, symmetries, operator coordinates, and infrared questions.
Flavor and \(R\)-symmetry data in this chapter are therefore not silently
interpreted as ordinary local continuous order parameters.  In
\(1+1\)-dimensional unitary local QFT the Coleman obstruction of
Section~\ref{sec:low-dimensional-continuous-ssb-no-go} is the relevant
default; protected chiral rings, indices, anomalies, twists, and topological
sectors are different infrared data.

\section{The N=(2,2) Algebra}

In Lorentzian signature let \(P_\pm=P_0\pm P_1\).  The supercharges
\[
  Q_\pm,\quad \bar Q_\pm
\]
obey
\[
  \{Q_+,\bar Q_+\}=2P_+,\qquad
  \{Q_-,\bar Q_-\}=2P_-,
\]
with all other anticommutators vanishing in the absence of central charges.
An \(R\)-symmetry assignment consists of vector and axial charges
\[
  F_V,\qquad F_A,
\]
whose action on supercharges is part of the model specification.  A BPS state with
central charge \(Z\) obeys an inequality obtained by positivity of
\((Q+\ee^{\ii\alpha}\bar Q)^\dagger(Q+\ee^{\ii\alpha}\bar Q)\); the saturated
states are kernels of a corresponding linear combination of supercharges.

\section{Landau--Ginzburg Presentations}

\begin{definition}[Two-dimensional Landau--Ginzburg presentation]
\label{def:two-dimensional-lg-presentation}
A polynomial \(2D\) \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) Landau--Ginzburg presentation in this
chapter is specified by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a Euclidean two-manifold \(\Sigma\), initially a smooth oriented
surface with a chosen spin structure when fermions are kept explicitly;
\item chiral multiplet coordinates
\[
  \Phi^i=(\phi^i,\psi^i_+,\psi^i_-,F^i),
  \qquad i=1,\ldots,n,
\]
where \(\phi^i:\Sigma\to\C\) are scalar field variables, \(\psi^i_\pm\) are
fermionic field variables of right- and left-moving chirality, and \(F^i\)
are auxiliary even field variables;
\item a K\"ahler potential \(K\) whose Hessian
\[
  g_{i\bar j}(\phi,\bar\phi)=\partial_i\partial_{\bar j}K(\phi,\bar\phi)
\]
is positive on the field chart used in the perturbative construction;
\item a holomorphic polynomial superpotential
\[
  W\in\C[\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n].
\]
\end{enumerate}
This is a field-variable presentation.  A continuum QFT requires a
regulator, a supersymmetric Ward identity, and a Hilbert-space or Euclidean
reconstruction; none of these is inferred from the polynomial \(W\) alone.
\end{definition}

Eliminating the auxiliary fields is a finite-dimensional calculation at each
point of \(\Sigma\).  In the normalization where the auxiliary part of the
Euclidean Lagrangian is
\[
  g_{i\bar j}F^i\bar F^{\bar j}
  +F^i\partial_iW+\bar F^{\bar j}\partial_{\bar j}\overline W ,
\]
the algebraic equation of motion is
\[
  g_{i\bar j}\bar F^{\bar j}+\partial_iW=0 .
\]
Substituting this equation gives the bosonic potential
\[
  V(\phi)=g^{i\bar j}\partial_iW(\phi)\,
  \overline{\partial_jW(\phi)}.
\]
Thus supersymmetric classical vacua in the affine field chart solve
\[
  \partial_iW(\phi)=0,\qquad i=1,\ldots,n.
\]

\begin{definition}[Quasihomogeneous isolated Landau--Ginzburg polynomial]
\label{def:vii-quasihomogeneous-lg-polynomial}
Let \(q_i\in\mathbb Q_{>0}\).  A polynomial
\[
  W\in\C[\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n]
\]
is quasihomogeneous of degree \(1\) with weights \(q_i\) when
\begin{equation}
  W(\lambda^{q_1}\phi^1,\ldots,\lambda^{q_n}\phi^n)
  =
  \lambda W(\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n)
  \qquad
  \lambda\in\C^\times .
  \label{eq:vii-lg-quasihomogeneous-condition}
\end{equation}
It is isolated when the common zero locus of
\[
  \partial_1W,\ldots,\partial_nW
\]
in \(\C^n\) is the single point \(0\).  The algebraic chiral coordinate ring
attached to the polynomial is the Jacobi algebra
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Jac}(W)
  =
  \C[\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n]/
  (\partial_1W,\ldots,\partial_nW).
  \label{eq:vii-lg-jacobi-ring}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The term ``algebraic chiral coordinate ring'' is deliberately narrower than
``the infrared chiral ring of the continuum QFT''.  It is the finite
algebraic object forced by the \(F\)-term equations and by the protected
cohomological argument below.  The statement that a regulated Landau--Ginzburg
model flows to a compact \(\mathcal N=2\) superconformal field theory with
this ring as its chiral-primary ring is an additional infrared construction
problem.

\paragraph{Jacobian quotient from the \(B\)-type differential.}
Let \(A=\C[\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n]\) and let \(I_W\subset A\) be the ideal
generated by \(\partial_iW\).  Assume that the regulated \(B\)-type local
operator complex contains odd local variables \(\eta_i\), \(i=1,\ldots,n\),
and a scalar odd differential \(Q_B\) satisfying
\begin{equation}
  Q_B\phi^i=0,\qquad
  Q_B\eta_i=\partial_iW(\phi),
  \qquad
  Q_B^2=0
  \label{eq:lg-btype-local-differential}
\end{equation}
on polynomial local coordinates.  Then the image of \(Q_B\) inside the
polynomial subalgebra \(A\) is exactly \(I_W\).  Hence the polynomial
\(Q_B\)-cohomology in degree zero is canonically
\[
  H^0_{Q_B}\bigl(A\otimes\Lambda(\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_n)\bigr)
  \cong
  \operatorname{Jac}(W).
\]

Every polynomial \(f(\phi)\in A\) is \(Q_B\)-closed because
\(Q_B\phi^i=0\) and \(Q_B\) is a derivation.  Conversely, the \(Q_B\)-exact
polynomials are generated by applying \(Q_B\) to degree-one expressions
\[
  \sum_i h^i(\phi)\eta_i,\qquad h^i\in A.
\]
Using \eqref{eq:lg-btype-local-differential},
\[
  Q_B\left(\sum_i h^i(\phi)\eta_i\right)
  =
  \sum_i h^i(\phi)\partial_iW(\phi),
\]
because \(Q_Bh^i(\phi)=0\).  Thus
\(\operatorname{im}Q_B\cap A=I_W\), and quotienting the closed polynomial
operators by exact polynomial operators gives \eqref{eq:vii-lg-jacobi-ring}.

\paragraph{Quasihomogeneous charge and central-charge test.}
Let \(W\) be a quasihomogeneous isolated polynomial in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:vii-quasihomogeneous-lg-polynomial}.  Assign the monomial
\[
  \phi^a=(\phi^1)^{a_1}\cdots(\phi^n)^{a_n}
\]
the charge
\begin{equation}
  Q_R(\phi^a)=\sum_{i=1}^n a_iq_i .
  \label{eq:vii-lg-monomial-charge}
\end{equation}
Then every monomial appearing in \(W\) has charge \(1\), while every monomial
appearing in \(\partial_iW\) has charge \(1-q_i\).  If, in addition,
\begin{equation}
  W=\sum_{i=1}^n(\phi^i)^{d_i},
  \qquad d_i\geq2,
  \label{eq:fermat-lg-polynomial}
\end{equation}
then \(q_i=1/d_i\), the Jacobi algebra has basis
\begin{equation}
  \prod_{i=1}^n(\phi^i)^{a_i},
  \qquad
  0\leq a_i\leq d_i-2,
  \label{eq:fermat-lg-jacobi-basis}
\end{equation}
and dimension
\begin{equation}
  \dim_\C\operatorname{Jac}(W)=\prod_{i=1}^n(d_i-1).
  \label{eq:fermat-lg-jacobi-dimension}
\end{equation}
The protected \(U(1)_R\)-anomaly prediction for a compact unitary infrared
\(\mathcal N=2\) superconformal fixed point is
\begin{equation}
  c_{\rm LG}=3\sum_{i=1}^n(1-2q_i).
  \label{eq:vii-lg-central-charge-prediction}
\end{equation}
In this chapter \eqref{eq:vii-lg-central-charge-prediction} is used as a
necessary protected-data test for any proposed compact unitary infrared fixed
point with these chiral fields and this \(U(1)_R\) current.

Equation~\eqref{eq:vii-lg-quasihomogeneous-condition} says precisely that
every monomial in \(W\) has total charge \(1\).  Differentiating a monomial
with respect to \(\phi^i\) lowers its charge by \(q_i\), hence the terms in
\(\partial_iW\) have charge \(1-q_i\).

For the Fermat polynomial \eqref{eq:fermat-lg-polynomial},
quasihomogeneity gives \(d_iq_i=1\), so \(q_i=1/d_i\).  The Jacobi ideal is
\[
  ((\phi^1)^{d_1-1},\ldots,(\phi^n)^{d_n-1}),
\]
up to multiplication by the nonzero constants \(d_i\).  Reduction modulo this
monomial ideal removes every factor with exponent \(a_i\geq d_i-1\), and the
remaining monomials \eqref{eq:fermat-lg-jacobi-basis} are linearly
independent in the quotient.  Counting the allowed exponents gives
\eqref{eq:fermat-lg-jacobi-dimension}.

The final formula is the protected anomaly calculation used by the
\(\mathcal N=2\) superconformal-algebra interface in
Chapter~\ref{ch:two-dimensional-superconformal-algebras}.  The present
paragraph records its algebraic inputs and status: once a continuum
infrared fixed point with these chiral fields and \(U(1)_R\) current has
been constructed, the anomaly supplies \eqref{eq:vii-lg-central-charge-prediction}.

This is therefore an algebraic and anomaly-matching datum.  The existence of
the infrared fixed point and the convergence of a Landau--Ginzburg regulator
to it are separate construction and RG questions.

\paragraph{Wilsonian superpotential coordinate for Fermat LG models.}
The polynomial \(W\) is treated here as part of the Wilsonian \(F\)-term
coordinate of a declared regulated theory.  The following
finite argument is the two-dimensional version of the holomorphic-coordinate
logic of Chapter~\ref{ch:susy-nonrenormalization-holomorphy}.

Fix
\[
  W_{\rm UV}(\Phi;g)=\sum_{i=1}^n g_i(\Phi^i)^{d_i},
  \qquad d_i\geq2,
\]
and assume a Wilsonian scheme at scale \(\Lambda\) with the following
properties:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the chiral superspace coordinate is preserved, so corrections to the
  superpotential are holomorphic functions of the chiral fields and of the
  background chiral couplings \(g_i\);
\item the patch is regular at \(g_i=0\), so the perturbative Wilsonian
  expansion contains nonnegative powers of the \(g_i\);
\item the scheme preserves the \(n\) flavor spurion symmetries
  \[
    \Phi^i\mapsto \lambda_i\Phi^i,\qquad
    g_i\mapsto \lambda_i^{-d_i}g_i,
    \qquad \lambda_i\in\C^\times,
  \]
  together with the quasihomogeneous \(R\)-charge convention
  \[
    R(\Phi^i)=\frac1{d_i},\qquad R(g_i)=0,\qquad R(W)=1 .
  \]
\end{enumerate}
Then every regular monomial allowed in the Wilsonian superpotential is one of
the original monomials \(g_i(\Phi^i)^{d_i}\), up to its coefficient.  Indeed,
write a candidate monomial as
\[
  \prod_{i=1}^n g_i^{m_i}(\Phi^i)^{a_i},
  \qquad m_i,a_i\in\mathbb Z_{\geq0}.
\]
The \(i\)-th spurion symmetry requires
\begin{equation}
  a_i-d_i m_i=0
  \qquad\text{for every }i,
  \label{eq:two-d-lg-spurion-neutrality}
\end{equation}
and the chiral \(R\)-charge condition requires
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i=1}^n\frac{a_i}{d_i}=1 .
  \label{eq:two-d-lg-r-charge-selection}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:two-d-lg-spurion-neutrality} into
\eqref{eq:two-d-lg-r-charge-selection} gives
\[
  \sum_{i=1}^n m_i=1.
\]
Since the \(m_i\) are nonnegative integers, exactly one \(m_j\) is \(1\) and
all other \(m_i\) vanish.  Then \(a_j=d_j\) and \(a_i=0\) for \(i\neq j\).
Thus the holomorphic selection rule alone permits only
\[
  g_j(\Phi^j)^{d_j},
  \qquad j=1,\ldots,n.
\]

The scope of the argument is the Wilsonian coordinate at the declared scale:
after a regulator with the stated chiral and spurion Ward identities has
been supplied, perturbative local corrections preserve the Fermat
superpotential coordinate up to finite redefinition of the displayed
couplings.  Existence of the continuum infrared fixed point, convergence of
the RG flow, and control of any allowed nonperturbative sectors are separate
analytic inputs.  The K\"ahler potential and other \(D\)-term coordinates
may still flow.

The same protected-data interface controls the finite \(\chi_y\) shadow of
the elliptic genus in
Section~\ref{sec:elliptic-genus-spectral-flow-covariance}.  If the infrared
fixed point exists and the Jacobi algebra classes become NS chiral primaries,
then spectral flow by \(\eta=-1/2\) sends a monomial of charge \(Q_R\) to a
left Ramond ground-state contribution of charge
\begin{equation}
  Q_R-\frac{c_{\rm LG}}6 .
  \label{eq:vii-lg-ramond-charge-shift}
\end{equation}
Thus the finite polynomial \(\sum_{[O]\in\operatorname{Jac}(W)}
y^{Q_R(O)-c_{\rm LG}/6}\) is a necessary protected check on the proposed
SCFT.  The full elliptic genus requires the compact Ramond trace and modular
sewing hypotheses stated in the CFT volume.

\begin{example}[\(A\)-series and quintic polynomial checks]
\label{ex:vii-lg-a-series-quintic-checks}
For \(W=X^{k+2}\), \(k\geq0\), one has \(q=1/(k+2)\),
\[
  \operatorname{Jac}(W)=\C[X]/(X^{k+1}),
  \qquad
  c_{\rm LG}=3\left(1-\frac{2}{k+2}\right)=\frac{3k}{k+2}.
\]
For
\[
  W=X_1^5+\cdots+X_5^5
\]
the weights are \(q_i=1/5\), the algebraic Jacobi dimension is
\[
  4^5=1024,
\]
and the protected central-charge test gives
\[
  c_{\rm LG}=3\cdot5\left(1-\frac25\right)=9.
\]
This is an intrinsic Landau--Ginzburg charge and Jacobi-algebra calculation.
\end{example}

\section{Sigma-Model Datum}

A \(2D\) \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) nonlinear sigma model is specified by a K\"ahler
target \(X\), metric \(g_{i\bar j}\), \(B\)-field \(B\), and possibly a
superpotential \(W\).  The bosonic Euclidean action is
\[
  S_{\mathrm{bos}}
  =
  \frac1{4\pi\alpha'}
  \int_\Sigma
  \dd^2x\,
  g_{i\bar j}(\phi)\partial_\mu\phi^i\partial_\mu\bar\phi^{\bar j}
  +
  \frac{\ii}{2\pi\alpha'}
  \int_\Sigma \phi^*B .
\]
The large-volume perturbative beta tensor begins with the Ricci form:
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd g_{i\bar j}}{\dd\mu}
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}R_{i\bar j}+\cdots .
\]
Thus Ricci-flatness is the first condition for a conformal infrared candidate
in a large-volume chart.  The full QFT claim includes instanton corrections,
operator renormalization, and a continuum limit; these are additional data,
separate from the local tensor equation.

\section{A And B Twisting Data}

The two cohomological twists of a \(2D\) \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) theory are
intrinsic operations on the two-dimensional QFT.  They require nonanomalous
\(R\)-symmetries and a regulator preserving the selected scalar supercharge.
The definition uses the two-dimensional rotation symmetry, the selected
\(R\)-current, and the supersymmetry algebra.

\begin{definition}[Two-dimensional twist convention]
\label{def:two-dimensional-ab-twist-convention}
Let \(s\) denote the Lorentz spin for \(U(1)_{\rm rot}\).  In this chapter
the supercharges have charges
\begin{equation}
\begin{array}{c|rrrr}
  & Q_+ & \bar Q_+ & Q_- & \bar Q_-\\ \hline
  s & \frac12 & \frac12 & -\frac12 & -\frac12\\
  F_V & 1 & -1 & 1 & -1\\
  F_A & 1 & -1 & -1 & 1 .
\end{array}
\label{eq:two-dimensional-supercharge-charge-table}
\end{equation}
The A twist uses
\begin{equation}
  s_A=s+\frac12F_V ,
  \label{eq:a-twist-spin-shift}
\end{equation}
and the B twist uses
\begin{equation}
  s_B=s+\frac12F_A .
  \label{eq:b-twist-spin-shift}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Twist bookkeeping: scalar supercharges]
\label{rem:two-dimensional-ab-twist-scalar-supercharges}
Assume the charge convention of
Definition~\ref{def:two-dimensional-ab-twist-convention} and assume that
there are no central charges in the local-sector supersymmetry algebra.
Then:
\begin{equation}
  Q_A=\bar Q_+ + Q_-
  \label{eq:a-twist-scalar-supercharge}
\end{equation}
is scalar for the A-twisted rotation action and satisfies \(Q_A^2=0\) on
gauge-invariant local observables, while
\begin{equation}
  Q_B=\bar Q_+ + \bar Q_-
  \label{eq:b-twist-scalar-supercharge}
\end{equation}
is scalar for the B-twisted rotation action and satisfies \(Q_B^2=0\) on
gauge-invariant local observables.

Using \eqref{eq:a-twist-spin-shift}, the A-twisted spins are
\[
\begin{array}{c|rrrr}
  & Q_+ & \bar Q_+ & Q_- & \bar Q_-\\ \hline
  s_A & 1 & 0 & 0 & -1 .
\end{array}
\]
Thus \(\bar Q_+\) and \(Q_-\) are scalars, while the other two supercharges
are not.  Using \eqref{eq:b-twist-spin-shift}, the B-twisted spins are
\[
\begin{array}{c|rrrr}
  & Q_+ & \bar Q_+ & Q_- & \bar Q_-\\ \hline
  s_B & 1 & 0 & -1 & 0 .
\end{array}
\]
Thus \(\bar Q_+\) and \(\bar Q_-\) are scalars for the B twist.  The
untwisted local algebra has only
\[
  \{Q_+,\bar Q_+\}=2P_+,\qquad
  \{Q_-,\bar Q_-\}=2P_-
\]
among these generators.  Therefore
\[
  Q_A^2=\frac12\{\bar Q_+ + Q_-,\bar Q_+ + Q_-\}=0,
  \qquad
  Q_B^2=\frac12\{\bar Q_+ + \bar Q_-,\bar Q_+ + \bar Q_-\}=0
\]
in the central-charge-free local sector.  If gauge transformations appear in
an off-shell realization, the same statement is made after passing to
gauge-invariant observables.
\end{remark}

\section{Gauged Linear Sigma-Model Presentations}

\begin{definition}[Abelian \(2D\) \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) GLSM presentation]
\label{def:abelian-2d-glsm-presentation}
An abelian GLSM presentation in this chapter is specified by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item gauge group \(G=U(1)\);
\item chiral multiplets \(\Phi_i\), \(i=1,\ldots,m\), with integer charges
  \(Q_i\in\mathbb Z\);
\item a \(U(1)\)-invariant holomorphic superpotential
  \(W\in\C[\Phi_1,\ldots,\Phi_m]^{U(1)}\);
\item a complexified FI-theta coordinate
\[
  t=\frac{\theta}{2\pi}+\ii r,
  \qquad r\in\mathbb R,\quad \theta\in\mathbb R/2\pi\mathbb Z;
\]
\item a gauge coupling \(e>0\) and a specified ultraviolet regulator for the
  gauge theory.
\end{enumerate}
The classical scalar potential contains
\begin{equation}
  \frac{e^2}{2}\left(\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i|\phi_i|^2-r\right)^2
  +\sum_i|\partial_iW|^2
  +|\sigma|^2\sum_i Q_i^2|\phi_i|^2 ,
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-classical-potential}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma\) is the complex scalar in the vector multiplet.  Classical
Higgs vacua with at least one charged scalar nonzero therefore solve the
\(F\)-term equations, the \(D\)-term equation, and then are quotiented by
\(U(1)\).
\end{definition}

The axial \(R\)-symmetry anomaly coefficient of this abelian presentation is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A_A=\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i.
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-axial-anomaly}
\end{equation}
This coefficient is a finite charge sum: each charged chiral multiplet
contributes its charge to the axial rotation of the fermion measure.  When
\(\mathcal A_A=0\), the one-loop axial shift of the FI coordinate vanishes.
This is a necessary condition for an axial \(R\)-symmetry in an infrared
superconformal candidate.  A conformality claim additionally requires a
construction of the infrared continuum theory and its stress tensor.

The vector and axial \(R\)-symmetries have different anomaly requirements.
The A twist is available only when \(F_V\) is a genuine symmetry of the
regulated theory.  The B twist is available only when \(F_A\) is a genuine
symmetry.  In the abelian GLSM presentation of
Definition~\ref{def:abelian-2d-glsm-presentation}, the axial anomaly obstruction is
the charge sum \eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-axial-anomaly}.  Therefore the
hypersurface GLSM with charges \((1,\ldots,1,-d)\) has an axial B-twist
candidate precisely when \(N=d\).

\section{B-Twisted Landau--Ginzburg Sector}

The B-twisted Landau--Ginzburg model uses the scalar supercharge
\eqref{eq:b-twist-scalar-supercharge}.  The Jacobian-quotient calculation
above gives the first
protected observable: polynomial local operators map to
\(\operatorname{Jac}(W)\).  The next finite-dimensional check is the residue
pairing at nondegenerate critical points.

\paragraph{Morse residue pairing on the LG Jacobi algebra.}
Let \(W\in\C[\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n]\) have a finite critical set
\[
  \operatorname{Crit}(W)=\{p\in\C^n:\partial_iW(p)=0
  \text{ for all }i\}.
\]
Assume every critical point is nondegenerate:
\[
  H_W(p)=\det(\partial_i\partial_jW)(p)\neq0 .
\]
Define
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Res}_W(f)
  =
  \sum_{p\in\operatorname{Crit}(W)}
  \frac{f(p)}{H_W(p)},
  \qquad f\in\C[\phi^1,\ldots,\phi^n].
  \label{eq:lg-morse-residue-functional}
\end{equation}
Then \(\operatorname{Res}_W\) kills the ideal
\((\partial_1W,\ldots,\partial_nW)\), so it descends to
\(\operatorname{Jac}(W)\).  The bilinear form
\begin{equation}
  \langle [f],[g]\rangle_{\rm res}
  =
  \operatorname{Res}_W(fg)
  \label{eq:lg-morse-residue-pairing}
\end{equation}
on \(\operatorname{Jac}(W)\) is nondegenerate.

If \(f=\sum_i h_i\partial_iW\), then \(f(p)=0\) at every critical point
\(p\), so \(\operatorname{Res}_W(f)=0\).  Hence the functional descends to
the quotient.  Nondegeneracy of the Hessian means that each zero of the
gradient map
\[
  \nabla W:\C^n\longrightarrow\C^n,
  \qquad
  \phi\longmapsto(\partial_1W,\ldots,\partial_nW)
\]
is simple.  The local algebra of \(\nabla W\) at such a zero is therefore
\(\C\).  Since the critical set is finite, the Chinese-remainder
decomposition gives
\[
  \operatorname{Jac}(W)\cong
  \prod_{p\in\operatorname{Crit}(W)}\C,
\]
where a class \([f]\) is sent to its values \(f(p)\).  In these coordinates
\eqref{eq:lg-morse-residue-pairing} is the diagonal pairing with nonzero
diagonal entries \(H_W(p)^{-1}\), hence is nondegenerate.

For a compact Riemann surface of genus \(g\), the B-twisted
Landau--Ginzburg zero-mode formula used in this chapter is
\begin{equation}
  \langle f_1\cdots f_m\rangle_g
  =
  \sum_{p\in\operatorname{Crit}(W)}
  \frac{f_1(p)\cdots f_m(p)}
  {H_W(p)^{1-g}}.
  \label{eq:b-twisted-lg-zero-mode-correlator}
\end{equation}
In this monograph \eqref{eq:b-twisted-lg-zero-mode-correlator} has the
following status: it is the finite-dimensional zero-mode and determinant
calculation after the \(Q_B\)-preserving regulator, determinant-line
orientation, and absence of boundary terms have been supplied.  The
continuum QFT assertion is conditional on those analytic inputs.

\section{A-Twisted Sigma-Model Zero Modes}

Let \(X\) be a smooth K\"ahler target with real tangent bundle \(TX\).  The
A-twisted sigma model uses the scalar supercharge
\eqref{eq:a-twist-scalar-supercharge}.  The full model includes nonconstant
maps \(\Sigma\to X\), instanton sectors, compactification of moduli spaces,
and operator contact terms.  The constant-map sector, however, is a
finite-dimensional calculation.

In the constant-map truncation of an A-twisted sigma model with target \(X\),
let \(x^a\) be local real coordinates on \(X\) and let \(\chi^a\) be odd
variables transforming as \(dx^a\).  Suppose the scalar differential acts by
\begin{equation}
  Q_Ax^a=\chi^a,\qquad Q_A\chi^a=0.
  \label{eq:a-model-zero-mode-differential}
\end{equation}
Then the algebra of polynomial functions in the odd variables identifies
with differential forms on \(X\), and \(Q_A\) identifies with the de Rham
differential \(d_X\).  Thus the constant-map local \(Q_A\)-cohomology is
\[
  H^\bullet_{Q_A}\cong H^\bullet_{\rm dR}(X;\C).
\]
Functions of \(x^a\) and the odd variables \(\chi^a\) are local functions on
the parity-shifted tangent bundle \(\Pi TX\).  The coordinate identification
\[
  \chi^{a_1}\cdots\chi^{a_k}
  \longleftrightarrow
  dx^{a_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{a_k}
\]
identifies these functions with differential forms.  Under this
identification the derivation determined by
\eqref{eq:a-model-zero-mode-differential} is
\[
  Q_A=\chi^a\frac{\partial}{\partial x^a},
\]
which is precisely the de Rham differential in local coordinates.  Its
cohomology is therefore de Rham cohomology after gluing the coordinate
charts.

The nonconstant A-model sectors are classified, at the classical level, by
homology classes \(\beta=\phi_*[\Sigma]\in H_2(X,\mathbb Z)\).  For a
K\"ahler metric with form \(\omega\), the bosonic energy admits the
pointwise Bogomolny decomposition
\begin{equation}
  E(\phi)
  =
  \int_\Sigma |\bar\partial_J\phi|^2\,\mathrm{vol}_\Sigma
  +
  \int_\Sigma\phi^*\omega
  \label{eq:a-model-energy-decomposition}
\end{equation}
in the normalization of the sigma-model action.  Thus holomorphic maps
\(\bar\partial_J\phi=0\) minimize the energy in a fixed homology class.  A
mathematical A-model correlation function requires compactifying the
holomorphic-map moduli space, orienting its virtual determinant line, and
controlling collisions of marked points.  These are theorem-level inputs,
additional to the twist construction.

\section{Abelian Duality And Circle T-Duality}
\label{sec:two-dimensional-abelian-duality}

Abelian duality is used here only as a statement about two-dimensional QFT.
The local calculation is a first-order path integral for a compact scalar
and, in the
\(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) extension, the corresponding Legendre transform between
chiral and twisted-chiral field variables.  The global QFT statement requires
the compact path-integral measure, spin structures, contact terms, and
topological sectors to be specified.

\begin{hypothesis}[Compact scalar first-order duality setup]
\label{hyp:compact-scalar-first-order-duality}
Let \(\Sigma\) be a closed oriented Euclidean surface.  A compact scalar
\(\vartheta:\Sigma\to\mathbb R/2\pi\mathbb Z\) of radius \(R>0\) has action
\begin{equation}
  S_R(\vartheta)
  =
  \frac{R^2}{4\pi}
  \int_\Sigma d\vartheta\wedge *d\vartheta .
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-radius-action}
\end{equation}
The functional integral sums over all winding sectors
\([d\vartheta/2\pi]\in H^1(\Sigma;\mathbb Z)\).  The first-order presentation
uses a real one-form \(A\) with the same integral-period normalization and a
dual periodic scalar \(\widetilde\vartheta\sim
\widetilde\vartheta+2\pi\):
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm fo}(A,\widetilde\vartheta)
  =
  \frac{R^2}{4\pi}\int_\Sigma A\wedge *A
  +
  \frac{\ii}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma
  \widetilde\vartheta\, dA .
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-first-order-action}
\end{equation}
The measure is assumed to be chosen so that integrating over
\(\widetilde\vartheta\) imposes flatness with integral periods and integrating
over \(A\) is the corresponding Gaussian integral along the standard
Euclidean contour.  Field-independent determinant constants are absorbed in
the normalization of the partition function.
\end{hypothesis}

\paragraph{Compact scalar radius inversion.}
Under Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:compact-scalar-first-order-duality}, the local
first-order duality maps the radius \(R\) compact scalar to a compact scalar
of radius \(R^\vee=R^{-1}\):
\begin{equation}
  S_{R^\vee}(\widetilde\vartheta)
  =
  \frac{1}{4\pi R^2}
  \int_\Sigma d\widetilde\vartheta\wedge *d\widetilde\vartheta .
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-dual-radius-action}
\end{equation}
Applying the same local operation twice returns the original radius.

Integrating over \(\widetilde\vartheta\) in
\eqref{eq:compact-scalar-first-order-action} imposes \(dA=0\) together with
the integral-period condition.  Locally \(A=d\vartheta\), and globally the
integral periods reconstruct the winding sectors of
\eqref{eq:compact-scalar-radius-action}.

For the other elimination, integrate the second term by parts:
\[
  \frac{\ii}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma\widetilde\vartheta\,dA
  =
  -\frac{\ii}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma d\widetilde\vartheta\wedge A .
\]
The Gaussian equation for \(A\) is
\begin{equation}
  R^2 *A=\ii\, d\widetilde\vartheta ,
  \qquad
  A=\frac{\ii}{R^2}*d\widetilde\vartheta ,
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-gaussian-saddle}
\end{equation}
with the usual Euclidean contour understood.  Substituting the saddle and
absorbing the field-independent determinant gives
\eqref{eq:compact-scalar-dual-radius-action}.  Therefore the dual radius is
\(R^{-1}\), and the second duality sends \(R^{-1}\) back to \(R\).

The Hamiltonian spectrum supplies a regulator-independent arithmetic check of
the same radius inversion.  On a spatial circle, write the momentum and
winding labels as
\[
  (n,w)\in\mathbb Z^2 .
\]
In the convention of \eqref{eq:compact-scalar-radius-action}, the left and
right momenta are
\begin{equation}
  p_L(n,w;R)=\frac{n}{R}+wR,\qquad
  p_R(n,w;R)=\frac{n}{R}-wR .
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-left-right-momenta}
\end{equation}
The duality map
\begin{equation}
  R\longmapsto R^{-1},\qquad (n,w)\longmapsto(w,n)
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-momentum-winding-map}
\end{equation}
preserves \(p_L^2+p_R^2\) and flips the sign of \(p_R\):
\begin{equation}
  p_L(w,n;R^{-1})=p_L(n,w;R),\qquad
  p_R(w,n;R^{-1})=-p_R(n,w;R).
  \label{eq:compact-scalar-spectrum-invariance}
\end{equation}
Thus the finite oscillator spectrum and the zero-mode quadratic form are
compatible with the first-order path integral.  A proof of equality of all
correlation functions must realize this exact arithmetic inside a specified
compact path-integral measure and operator-insertion prescription.

\begin{definition}[Local \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) abelian Legendre duality]
\label{def:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-setup}
Let \(\mathcal B\) be a real \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) superfield coordinate for a
shift-isometry direction, and let \(Y\) be a twisted-chiral superfield whose
imaginary part is periodic.  Locally, suppressing normalization constants
fixed by the component convention, a first-order superspace functional has
the form
\begin{equation}
  \int d^2x\,d^4\theta\,
  \bigl(\mathcal B\,u-K(\mathcal B)\bigr),
  \qquad u=Y+\bar Y,
  \label{eq:n22-abelian-duality-first-order-kahler}
\end{equation}
where \(K''(\mathcal B)>0\).  Eliminating \(Y\) enforces the local constraint
\(\mathcal B=\Phi+\bar\Phi\) for a chiral field \(\Phi\).  Eliminating
\(\mathcal B\) instead uses
\begin{equation}
  u=K'(\mathcal B)
  \label{eq:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-equation}
\end{equation}
and gives the Legendre-dual K\"ahler potential
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde K(u)=\mathcal B(u)u-K(\mathcal B(u)).
  \label{eq:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-transform}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Hessian inversion in local abelian duality]
\label{rem:n22-abelian-duality-hessian-inversion}
Assume Definition~\ref{def:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-setup}.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde K''(u)=\frac{1}{K''(\mathcal B(u))}.
  \label{eq:n22-abelian-duality-hessian-inverse}
\end{equation}
In particular, the free quadratic potential
\[
  K(\mathcal B)=\frac{R^2}{2}\mathcal B^2
\]
is dual to
\[
  \widetilde K(u)=\frac{1}{2R^2}u^2,
\]
the supersymmetric version of the radius inversion
\(R^2\mapsto R^{-2}\).

Differentiate \eqref{eq:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-transform}.  Since
\(u=K'(\mathcal B(u))\),
\[
  \frac{d\widetilde K}{du}
  =
  \mathcal B(u)+u\mathcal B'(u)
  -K'(\mathcal B(u))\mathcal B'(u)
  =
  \mathcal B(u).
\]
Differentiating \eqref{eq:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-equation} gives
\[
  1=K''(\mathcal B(u))\mathcal B'(u),
\]
and hence
\(\widetilde K''(u)=\mathcal B'(u)=1/K''(\mathcal B(u))\).  For
\(K(\mathcal B)=R^2\mathcal B^2/2\), equation
\eqref{eq:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-equation} gives
\(\mathcal B=u/R^2\), and
\eqref{eq:n22-abelian-duality-legendre-transform} gives
\(\widetilde K(u)=u^2/(2R^2)\).
\end{remark}

For a charged chiral field in an abelian GLSM, the same local dualization
couples the dual twisted-chiral variable linearly to the abelian field
strength \(\Sigma\), with coefficient equal to the integer gauge charge.
The perturbative local calculation is therefore compatible with the GLSM charge
matrix.  Nonperturbative exponential terms in the dual twisted
superpotential, when present, require a separate vortex-instanton
calculation: compactness of the vortex moduli space, fermion zero-mode
saturation, determinant-line orientation, and boundary control must be
supplied before such terms are promoted from semiclassical input to a QFT
statement.

\section{Charged-Chiral Dual Variables and Abelian Mirror Presentations}
\label{sec:charged-chiral-dual-mirror-presentations}

The preceding Legendre transform is the local part of the abelian GLSM mirror
construction.  The full mirror statement is a statement about two continuum
quantum field theories, not only about a formal twisted superpotential.  We
therefore name it before using its protected consequences.

\begin{conjecture}[Abelian GLSM/Hori--Vafa mirror QFT equivalence]
\label{conj:abelian-glsm-hori-vafa-mirror-qft}
Fix a two-dimensional \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) abelian GLSM on a spin worldsheet
with gauge group \(G=U(1)^s/\Gamma\), integral charge matrix \(Q_i{}^a\),
ordinary chiral multiplets \(\Phi_i\), a declared gauge-invariant chiral
superpotential if present, complexified FI-theta coordinates \(t_a\), flavor
and \(R\)-symmetry backgrounds, allowed local counterterms, topological
gauge-bundle sectors, a supersymmetric regulator, and a phase chamber avoiding
the singular FI locus, runaways, and unresolved mixed Coulomb--Higgs branches.
Let the proposed mirror be the periodic twisted-chiral Landau--Ginzburg
presentation with variables
\[
  Y_i\sim Y_i+2\pi\ii,\qquad \Sigma_a,
\]
global identifications induced by \(G\), vortex-normalization constants
\(c_i\), and twisted superpotential of the form
\eqref{eq:glsm-hv-dual-twisted-superpotential}, with the same spin structures
and background fields.

The conjecture asserts an equivalence of the continuum theories in that
chamber after compatible regulator limits: their Hilbert spaces on circles
and Hamiltonians agree, their local operator algebras and OPEs are identified,
their A- and B-twisted sectors, pairings, partition functions, and correlation
functions agree including background-field contact terms, and the equivalence
respects line defects, boundary conditions, topological sectors, theta
periods, RG endpoints, and global gauge data.  Equalities of twisted chiral
rings, Coulomb-branch critical equations, mirror residues, and protected
A-twisted correlators are consequences or evidence for this statement only
after their regulator, operator-map, determinant, and compactification data
have been supplied.
\end{conjecture}

The detailed comparison below is an observable comparison map for
Conjecture~\ref{conj:abelian-glsm-hori-vafa-mirror-qft}.  The physical question
is whether a protected GLSM correlator computed with a regulated vortex measure
agrees with the proposed mirror residue in the same FI, determinant-line, and
operator coordinates.  The object being compared is therefore not the formal
twisted superpotential alone, but the following finite-data package:
\[
  \begin{array}{rcl}
    D_{\rm loc} &:& Y_i\sim Y_i+2\pi\ii,\quad -Q_i{}^a\Sigma_aY_i,\\
    D_{\rm pert} &:& \hbox{Coulomb logarithm and finite FI coordinate},\\
    D_{\rm vort} &:& c_i\hbox{ from determinant, zero modes, orientation, boundary},\\
    D_{\rm obs} &:& \hbox{residue and stable-map comparison.}
  \end{array}
\]
The first two layers are local or protected algebraic checks.  The third layer
is the physical instanton calculation: the vortex collective coordinates are
only the zero-mode part of the problem, while the nonzero-mode determinant,
Berezin saturation, determinant-line orientation, and compactification or
continuum remainder determine whether the coefficient is present and in which
scheme.  The fourth layer compares that coefficient with A-twisted observables.
In this chapter a Hori--Vafa expression is used only after this comparison map
has supplied the direct vortex and observable data it needs.  The remaining
full-QFT proof debts are separate:
\[
  \begin{array}{rcl}
    E_{\rm cont} &:& \hbox{shared continuum construction and Hilbert spaces},\\
    E_{\rm op} &:& \hbox{local operators, OPEs, and source normalizations},\\
    E_{\rm top} &:& \hbox{defects, boundaries, twists, and topological sectors},\\
    E_{\rm bg} &:& \hbox{background fields, contact terms, and theta periods},\\
    E_{\rm sing} &:& \hbox{phase chamber, singular-locus, and RG-endpoint control}.
  \end{array}
\]
A protected-sector equality is recorded with its comparison label rather than
promoted silently to the full conjectural QFT equivalence.

Consider an abelian GLSM with gauge group \(U(1)^s\), twisted chiral field
strengths \(\Sigma_a\), complexified FI-theta coordinates \(t_a\), and
chiral multiplets \(\Phi_i\), \(i=1,\ldots,m\), of integer charges
\[
  Q_i{}^a\in\mathbb Z,\qquad a=1,\ldots,s .
\]
For each charged chiral multiplet introduce a periodic twisted chiral
variable
\[
  Y_i\sim Y_i+2\pi\ii .
\]
Repeated abelian gauge indices are summed in this section.

The local first-order functional for one charged chiral variable is the
superspace analogue of the compact scalar construction.  Let \(B_i\) be a
real superfield and set
\begin{equation}
  U_i=Y_i+\overline Y_i .
  \label{eq:glsm-mirror-real-dual-coordinate}
\end{equation}
The \(D\)-term
\begin{equation}
  \int d^2x\,d^4\theta\,
  \left[
    e^{B_i+2Q_i{}^aV_a}
    -
    \frac12 U_iB_i
  \right]
  \label{eq:glsm-charged-chiral-first-order-dterm}
\end{equation}
is invariant under
\[
  V_a\mapsto V_a+\ii(\Lambda_a-\overline\Lambda_a),
  \qquad
  B_i\mapsto
  B_i-2\ii Q_i{}^a(\Lambda_a-\overline\Lambda_a),
  \qquad
  Y_i\mapsto Y_i .
\]
Integrating over \(Y_i\) imposes the twisted linear constraint on \(B_i\).
In components of superspace this is the pair of constraints
\[
  \overline D_+D_-B_i=0,\qquad D_+\overline D_-B_i=0 .
\]
Locally this says
\[
  B_i=\Psi_i+\overline\Psi_i
\]
with \(\Psi_i\) a chiral superfield.  Then
\[
  e^{B_i+2Q_i{}^aV_a}
  =
  \overline\Phi_i
  \exp(2Q_i{}^aV_a)\Phi_i,
  \qquad
  \Phi_i=e^{\Psi_i},
\]
which recovers the original charged chiral kinetic term in that field chart.

Eliminating \(B_i\) instead gives the algebraic equation
\begin{equation}
  e^{B_i+2Q_i{}^aV_a}=\frac12 U_i ,
  \qquad
  B_i=\log\frac{U_i}{2}-2Q_i{}^aV_a .
  \label{eq:glsm-charged-chiral-b-equation}
\end{equation}
Substitution gives a dual \(D\)-term of Legendre form, together with the
linear \(V_aU_i\) term
\[
  Q_i{}^a\int d^2x\,d^4\theta\,V_a(Y_i+\overline Y_i).
\]
The superspace integration-by-parts identity in the present convention is
\begin{equation}
  \int d^4\theta\,V_a(Y_i+\overline Y_i)
  =
  -\int d^2\widetilde\theta\,\Sigma_aY_i
  +{\rm c.c.},
  \label{eq:glsm-dual-integration-by-parts}
\end{equation}
where \(Y_i\) is twisted chiral and
\(\Sigma_a=\overline D_+D_-V_a\).  Thus the local dualization produces the
twisted \(F\)-term
\[
  -\int d^2\widetilde\theta\,
  Q_i{}^a\Sigma_aY_i
  +{\rm c.c.}
\]
before any vortex effect is invoked.  This derivation fixes the charge
matrix in the dual variables: the integer \(Q_i{}^a\) that couples
\(\Phi_i\) to \(V_a\) is the same integer multiplying \(\Sigma_aY_i\).

The convention in the rest of this section is chosen so that eliminating the
\(Y_i\) matches the one-loop Coulomb superpotential
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-twisted-superpotential}.  Put
\begin{equation}
  M_i(\Sigma)=\sum_{a=1}^s Q_i{}^a\Sigma_a .
  \label{eq:glsm-mirror-mass-linear-form}
\end{equation}
The dual twisted-superpotential ansatz is
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde W_{\rm dual}(\Sigma,Y)
  =
  -\sum_{a=1}^s t_a\Sigma_a
  -
  \sum_{i=1}^m M_i(\Sigma)Y_i
  -
  \mu\sum_{i=1}^m c_i e^{-Y_i},
  \qquad c_i\in\C^\times .
  \label{eq:glsm-hv-dual-twisted-superpotential}
\end{equation}
The constants \(c_i\) depend on the normalization of the dual variables and
on the vortex determinant convention; rescaling \(e^{-Y_i}\) shifts the
corresponding logarithmic branch and changes \(t_a\) by a finite affine
scheme transformation.  The intrinsic data are the charge matrix, the
periodicity of the \(Y_i\), and the fact that the exponential monomial is
nonzero with the charge dictated by the dualized chiral field.

The local dualization has not derived the exponential term.  It has derived
the periodic variable \(Y_i\), the linear coupling \(-M_iY_i\), and the charge
normalization.  The remaining input is a protected connected amplitude on the
asymptotic end of the dual cylinder.  Because \(Y_i\) is periodic with
imaginary period \(2\pi\ii\), holomorphic local functions on that end are
Laurent series in
\[
  X_i=e^{-Y_i}.
\]
The large positive \(\operatorname{Re}Y_i\) region is the weak-coupling end
of the dualized chiral variable, so regularity there permits nonnegative
powers of \(X_i\).  Suppose, for one dual field and on a simply connected
branch where \(M_i\neq0\), that the connected protected correction is an
ordinary holomorphic germ \(h_i(X_i)\) with \(h_i(0)=0\), so that the relevant
part of the twisted superpotential is
\begin{equation}
  -M_iY_i-h_i(X_i)
  =
  M_i\log X_i-h_i(X_i),
  \qquad X_i=e^{-Y_i}.
  \label{eq:glsm-mirror-general-holomorphic-correction}
\end{equation}
The critical equation is
\begin{equation}
  M_i=X_i h_i'(X_i).
  \label{eq:glsm-mirror-general-critical-equation}
\end{equation}
At a critical point the derivative of the eliminated superpotential is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \widetilde W_{\rm elim}}{\partial M_i}
  =
  \log X_i(M_i).
  \label{eq:glsm-mirror-eliminated-branch-derivative}
\end{equation}
Indeed, the coefficient of \(\partial X_i/\partial M_i\) is
\(M_i/X_i-h_i'(X_i)\), which vanishes by
\eqref{eq:glsm-mirror-general-critical-equation}.  Exact agreement with the
Coulomb one-loop logarithm
\[
  \frac{\partial}{\partial M_i}
  \left[
    M_i\left(\log\frac{M_i}{\mu c_i}-1\right)
  \right]
  =
  \log\frac{M_i}{\mu c_i}
\]
therefore forces \(X_i(M_i)=M_i/(\mu c_i)\) on the branch.  Substituting into
\eqref{eq:glsm-mirror-general-critical-equation} gives
\(h_i'(X_i)=\mu c_i\), hence
\[
  h_i(X_i)=\mu c_iX_i
\]
up to an irrelevant additive constant.  Thus the primitive monomial
\(-\mu c_i e^{-Y_i}\) is singled out by the combination of periodicity,
regularity at the weak-coupling end, and exact Coulomb-branch matching.  This
argument is not a construction of the amplitude.  It reduces the
nonperturbative theorem boundary to the following sharp questions: construct
the single-vortex sector in the chosen regulator, prove that its fermion zero
modes leave precisely the twisted \(F\)-term measure, prove
\(c_i\neq0\), and justify that possible higher connected harmonics do not
contribute in the protected coordinate being matched.

The next two propositions and the retained-window bound supply the
\(D_{\rm vort}\) part of the observable comparison map for
Conjecture~\ref{conj:abelian-glsm-hori-vafa-mirror-qft}.  They analyze the
coefficient of the primitive mirror monomial as a regulated instanton
amplitude; they supply neither the full continuum operator map nor the
Hilbert-space equivalence by themselves.

\begin{proposition}[Vortex zero-mode filter for twisted \(F\)-terms]
\label{prop:glsm-vortex-zero-mode-filter}
In the finite-regulator charge-one sector used below, let
\(\mathcal D_{F,i,\Lambda}(z)\) be the fermion Hessian at a vortex
configuration \(z\).  Assume its kernel has been split as
\[
  \ker \mathcal D_{F,i,\Lambda}(z)
  =
  \Pi_{\rm univ}(z)\oplus K^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z),
  \qquad
  \dim \Pi_{\rm univ}=2,
\]
where \(\Pi_{\rm univ}\) is the universal pair identified with the
\(d^2\widetilde\theta\) measure.  Let
\[
  \nu^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)=\dim K^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z).
\]
After the universal pair has been removed, the residual fermion-zero-mode
coefficient has the Berezin form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal Z^0_{i,F,\Lambda}(z;P)
  =
  \int
  \prod_{\alpha=1}^{\nu^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)} \dd\eta_\alpha\,
  P_{\rm ins}(z,\eta),
  \label{eq:glsm-vortex-berezin-zero-mode-filter}
\end{equation}
where \(P_{\rm ins}\) denotes insertions or regulator-allowed zero-mode
liftings.  Hence the charge-one sector can contribute to the uninserted
twisted superpotential only on the locus
\[
  \nu^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)=0 .
\]
If \(\nu^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)>0\) and no insertion or lifting supplies the
missing residual Grassmann degree, then
\(\mathcal Z^0_{i,F,\Lambda}(z;1)=0\).  The nonzero-mode determinant entering
the coefficient is therefore the determinant on the complement of the full
kernel of \(\mathcal D_{F,i,\Lambda}(z)\), not the raw determinant before
zero-mode removal.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The two universal zero modes are coordinates on the superspace measure of the
twisted \(F\)-term, so they are not integrated into the scalar coefficient.
All remaining fermion zero modes are ordinary Grassmann variables in the
finite stationary-phase integral.  Berezin integration extracts the coefficient
of the top exterior monomial
\(\eta_1\cdots\eta_{\nu^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)}\).  If the insertion
polynomial has smaller residual Grassmann degree, that coefficient is absent
and the integral vanishes.  With no insertion or lifting, the polynomial is
constant, so the only nonzero case is
\(\nu^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)=0\).  Removing the full kernel before forming
the determinant is the same collective-coordinate separation on the fermionic
side as the bosonic zero-mode quotient in the vortex measure.
\end{proof}

This is the amplitude question hidden behind the compact mirror formula.  The
integer vortex number fixes the exponential \(e^{-Y_i}\), and Coulomb matching
fixes the primitive harmonic once a nonzero coefficient is present.  Neither
step proves that the regulated vortex sector has exactly the two universal
fermion zero modes or that its determinant line has been oriented
consistently.

\begin{proposition}[Finite-regulator vortex fluctuation complex]
\label{prop:glsm-vortex-fluctuation-complex}
At a charge-one vortex saddle \(z\), the coefficient \(c_i\) is controlled by
a gauge-fixed fluctuation complex, not by the vortex collective coordinates
alone.  In a finite supersymmetric regulator, write
\[
  0\longrightarrow
  \mathfrak g_{\Lambda,z}
  \xrightarrow{\ G_z\ }
  \mathcal B_{\Lambda,z}
  \xrightarrow{\ L_z\ }
  \mathcal E_{\Lambda,z}
  \longrightarrow 0,
  \qquad L_zG_z=0,
\]
where \(\mathfrak g_{\Lambda,z}\) is the infinitesimal gauge-orbit space,
\(\mathcal B_{\Lambda,z}\) is the bosonic fluctuation space
\((\delta A,\delta\phi)\), and \(L_z\) is the linearized vortex equation plus
gauge-slice operator.  Let \(\mathcal M_z=G_z^\dagger G_z\) be the
Faddeev--Popov operator on the orbit directions, let \(H_{B,z}\) be the
bosonic Hessian on the orthogonal slice with the vortex collective zero modes
removed, and let \(\mathcal D_{F,z}\) be the fermion Hessian with
\(\Pi_{\rm univ}(z)\oplus K^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)\) removed from its
kernel.  Then the nonzero-mode factor entering
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral} is a determinant-line
element of the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal W_{i,1,\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)
  =
  \frac{
    \det{}' \mathcal D_{F,z}\,
    \det{}'\mathcal M_z
  }{
    \bigl(\det{}'H_{B,z}\bigr)^{1/2}
  }
  \quad
  \hbox{after pairing with the chosen zero-mode orientations.}
  \label{eq:glsm-vortex-nonzero-mode-determinant-complex}
\end{equation}
A raw determinant over \(\mathcal B_{\Lambda,z}\) or over the full fermion
space vanishes because it includes zero modes.  Omitting
\(\det{}'\mathcal M_z\) changes the gauge-slice density.  Omitting the
orientation of \(\Pi_{\rm univ}\) and \(K^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}\) leaves a
line element rather than a scalar coefficient.  Thus the primitive
Hori--Vafa monomial can be used as a protected term only after the
finite-regulator complex, zero-mode removal, determinant-line orientation,
and residual zero-mode saturation have been fixed.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In finite dimensions the stationary-phase expansion first quotients the gauge
orbit and separates the tangent directions to the vortex moduli.  Gauge
fixing replaces the orbit volume by the Faddeev--Popov determinant
\(\det{}'\mathcal M_z\).  The Gaussian integral over nonzero bosonic normal
directions gives \((\det{}'H_{B,z})^{-1/2}\).  The fermion Gaussian gives
\(\det{}'\mathcal D_{F,z}\) only after its zero modes have been removed from
the determinant and treated as Grassmann collective coordinates.  The two
universal fermion zero modes are identified with the
\(d^2\widetilde\theta\) measure; any residual zero modes are paired with the
Berezin coefficient in
\eqref{eq:glsm-vortex-berezin-zero-mode-filter}.  Multiplying these finite
factors gives
\eqref{eq:glsm-vortex-nonzero-mode-determinant-complex}.  Each prime is
load-bearing: if the bosonic collective modes or fermion zero modes are left
inside the determinant, the determinant is zero rather than a vortex
coefficient.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Single-vortex coefficient as a protected amplitude]
\label{prop:glsm-single-vortex-exponential-amplitude}
Fix one charged chiral multiplet \(\Phi_i\) in the abelian GLSM and choose a
supersymmetric finite regulator preserving the integer vortex number and the
twisted chiral field strength.  Let \(\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}\) be a smooth
finite-dimensional chart for the charge-one vortex sector attached to
\(\Phi_i\), after quotienting by gauge transformations and after choosing
collective-coordinate coordinates \(z\).  Write
\(\dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)\) for the bosonic zero-mode density,
\(\mathcal W_{i,1,\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)\) for the nonzero-mode determinant
ratio in that vortex background, and
\(\mathcal Z_{i,F,\Lambda}^{0}(z)\) for the Berezin coefficient left after
the two universal fermion zero modes have been identified with
\(d^2\widetilde\theta\), as filtered by
Proposition~\ref{prop:glsm-vortex-zero-mode-filter}.  Let
\(q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}\) be the numerical FI-theta weight of this
charge-one sector in the original GLSM regulator.  Finally let
\[
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(z)
  =
  \left\langle
    \exp\!\bigl[-V^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda,z}(\varphi,\psi)\bigr]
  \right\rangle'_{\Lambda,z}
\]
denote the primed normal-mode expectation of the cubic and higher interaction
vertices after the Gaussian determinant, zero modes, and ghosts have been
separated.  In a localization or protected large-FI window this factor may be
proved to be \(1\), or \(1\) plus a controlled remainder; without that extra
argument it is part of the coefficient.

The original GLSM calculation determines the numerical charge-one coefficient
\begin{equation}
  c^{\rm orig}_{i,\Lambda}
  =
  \mu^{-1}q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}
  \int_{\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}}
  e^{-S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)}\,
  \mathcal W_{i,1,\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)\,
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(z)\,
  \mathcal Z_{i,F,\Lambda}^{0}(z)\,
  \dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z).
  \label{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral}
\end{equation}
The dual Wilsonian statement is obtained only after the abelian dual
operator map sends the charge-one vortex event to the twisted-chiral monomial
\(e^{-Y_i}\).  In that dual frame the corresponding term is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\widetilde W_{i,\Lambda}^{(1)}(Y_i)
  =
  -\mu\,c_{i,\Lambda}\,e^{-Y_i},
  \label{eq:glsm-single-vortex-superpotential-term}
\end{equation}
where \(c_{i,\Lambda}\) is the image of
\(c^{\rm orig}_{i,\Lambda}\) in the same finite determinant, FI, and operator
normalization convention.  Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  c_{i,\Lambda}
  =
  Z^{\rm map}_{i,\Lambda}\,c^{\rm orig}_{i,\Lambda},
  \label{eq:glsm-single-vortex-coefficient-integral}
\end{equation}
with \(Z^{\rm map}_{i,\Lambda}=1\) only after the original and dual operator
frames have been normalized identically.  Here
\(S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}\) is the original-regulator action with the
numerical FI-theta sector weight \(q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}\) displayed
separately; \(e^{-Y_i}\) is not inserted into the original path integral.
If additional fermion zero modes remain unsaturated, then
\(\mathcal Z_{i,F,\Lambda}^{0}=0\) and this vortex sector supplies no
twisted-superpotential term.  If the continuum limit of
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral} exists and is
nonzero, and if the dual operator map has a matching continuum limit, then
the resulting \(c_{i,\Lambda}\) has a limit \(c_i\) in
\eqref{eq:glsm-hv-dual-twisted-superpotential}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At fixed regulator the semiclassical expansion around the charge-one vortex
is a finite stationary-phase calculation, but stationary phase is not a
Gaussian identity.  After the collective coordinates, gauge ghosts, and
fermion zero modes are separated, the normal coordinates still carry the
interaction \(V^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda,z}\).  The Gaussian part supplies
\(\mathcal W^{\rm nz}_{i,1,\Lambda}\), while the remaining normal-mode
expectation supplies \(\mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}\) or a separately
bounded remainder.  Two universal fermion zero modes are precisely the
superspace measure of a twisted \(F\)-term; every extra zero mode must be
saturated by an insertion or lifted by an allowed deformation.  This gives
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral}.  Abelian
dualization is then a second step: the dual periodic variable records vortex
number, so the charge-one event maps to \(e^{-Y_i}\), and the period
\(Y_i\sim Y_i+2\pi\ii\) fixes this monomial.  This produces
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-superpotential-term} only after the finite
operator-map factor in
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-coefficient-integral} has been fixed.
Multiplying \(e^{-Y_i}\) by a nonzero finite constant rescales the dual
coefficient, which is the same finite normalization freedom recorded above as
an affine shift of the FI-theta coordinate.
\end{proof}

Formula
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral} isolates the data
hidden in the coefficient \(c_i\).  The original GLSM supplies a numerical
sector weight and a normal-mode expectation; the dual frame supplies the
operator \(e^{-Y_i}\).  The determinant ratio, the interaction cumulants or
their localization proof, the orientation of the two universal fermion zero
modes, the saturation of possible additional zero modes, and the boundary
behavior of \(\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}\) decide the value and possible
vanishing of the coefficient.  Agreement with the Coulomb logarithm is then a
protected consistency test: it fixes the primitive harmonic and exposes any
finite FI-coordinate shift caused by determinant or operator-map
normalization.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[One-vortex source functional and
\(F\)-term extraction]
\label{ca:glsm-one-vortex-source-functional-extraction}
The coefficient
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral} should be read as an
amplitude extraction from a source functional, not as a volume of the vortex
moduli chart.  In the same finite regulator, introduce bookkeeping
Grassmann sources \(\lambda_\pm\) for the two universal fermion zero modes
\(\xi_\pm\), residual Grassmann coordinates \(\eta_\alpha\) for
\(K^{\rm res}_{i,\Lambda}(z)\), and local sources \(J_A\) for the component
operators whose instanton contribution is to be differentiated.  On a
retained charge-one chart the source polynomial is written in finite stage form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal Z^{(1)}_{i,\Lambda}[\lambda,J]
  =
  q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}
  \int_{\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}}
  e^{-S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)}
  \mathcal W^{\rm nz}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(z)
  \dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)
  \int d\xi_+\,d\xi_-\,d\eta\,
  \mathcal P_{\Lambda}(z;\xi,\eta,\lambda,J).
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-functional}
\end{equation}
The polynomial \(\mathcal P_\Lambda\) contains the zero-mode wavefunctions
and the primed nonzero-mode propagators in the chosen source coordinates.
The Wilsonian twisted \(F\)-term projection is the coefficient of
\(\xi_+\xi_-\) and of the top residual \(\eta\)-degree, with the
\(\xi_\pm\) line normalized as the \(d^2\widetilde\theta\) measure:
\begin{equation}
  c_{i,\Lambda}
  =
  Z^{\rm map}_{i,\Lambda}\mu^{-1}q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}
  \int_{\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}}
  e^{-S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)}
  \mathcal W^{\rm nz}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(z)
  \Bigl[
    \mathcal P_\Lambda(z;\xi,\eta,0,0)
  \Bigr]_{\xi_+\xi_-\,\eta_{\rm top}}\,
  \dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z).
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-fterm-projection}
\end{equation}
The ordinary component functional at \(J=0\) is different: if the universal
zero modes are not extracted into \(d^2\widetilde\theta\), the
\(d\xi_+d\xi_-\) integral kills the uninserted component contribution.
Conversely, a two-source component amplitude is obtained by differentiating
\eqref{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-functional} before setting sources to zero:
\begin{equation}
  \left.
  \frac{\partial^2\mathcal Z^{(1)}_{i,\Lambda}}
       {\partial\lambda_+\partial\lambda_-}
  \right|_{\lambda=J=0}
  =
  q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}
  \int_{\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}}
  e^{-S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)}
  \mathcal W^{\rm nz}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(z)
  U_{+,\Lambda}(z)U_{-,\Lambda}(z)
  \mathcal Z^{0,{\rm res}}_{i,F,\Lambda}(z)
  \dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z),
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-derivative}
\end{equation}
after residual zero modes are saturated.  The functions \(U_{\pm,\Lambda}\)
are source-to-zero-mode overlaps.  They are part of the physical component
amplitude and cannot be replaced by one unless the source normalization has
been transported to the same determinant-line convention as
\eqref{eq:glsm-one-vortex-fterm-projection}.

Thus a direct instanton/source comparison has the residual form
\begin{equation}
  A^{(1)}_{i,\Lambda}(\mathcal O)
  -
  c^{\rm orig}_{i,\Lambda}I^{0}_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}
  =
  R_{\rm src}+R_{\rm prop}+R_{\rm det}
  +R_{\rm int}+R_{\rm zm}+R_{\rm cpt}+R_{\rm cont},
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-amplitude-telescope}
\end{equation}
where \(I^{0}_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}\) is the retained zero-mode/source
overlap in the normalized convention, \(R_{\rm src}\) records source
wavefunction and operator-coordinate transport, \(R_{\rm prop}\) records
primed nonzero-mode propagator contractions beyond the determinant factor,
\(R_{\rm int}\) records the interacting normal-mode cumulants or their
localization remainder, and the remaining terms have the same determinant, zero-mode,
compactification, and continuum meanings as above.  If the residuals are
bounded by \(B_\alpha\), then
\begin{equation}
  \left|
  A^{(1)}_{i,\Lambda}(\mathcal O)
  -
  c^{\rm orig}_{i,\Lambda}I^{0}_{\mathcal O,\Lambda}
  \right|
  \le
  B_{\rm src}+B_{\rm prop}+B_{\rm det}
  +B_{\rm int}+B_{\rm zm}+B_{\rm cpt}+B_{\rm cont}.
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-amplitude-bound}
\end{equation}
After the dual operator map, the corresponding dual-frame comparison replaces
the numerical sector weight by the monomial \(e^{-Y_i}\) and transports
\(c^{\rm orig}_{i,\Lambda}\) to \(c_{i,\Lambda}\).  Before that map has been
fixed, inserting \(e^{-Y_i}\) into the original source functional is a frame
substitution.  The collective-coordinate chart supplies only the bosonic
integration variables.
The source-differentiated amplitude additionally requires the primed
fluctuation determinant, the ghost density, the zero-mode wavefunctions, the
interacting normal-mode cumulants, the source/operator normalization, and the
residual Berezin gate.  This is why a moduli-space count, a Coulomb logarithm,
or a formal Hori--Vafa exponential cannot by itself replace the regulated
instanton amplitude.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Finite cell component amplitude from a
one-vortex sector]
\label{ca:glsm-one-vortex-component-amplitude-cell}
The previous source-functional formula becomes a concrete component amplitude
only after the operator sources are projected onto the universal fermion
zero-mode line.  Let a retained finite regulator replace
\(\mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}\) by cells \(p\in P\) with positive weights
\[
  K_p
  =
  e^{-S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}(p)}
  \mathcal W^{\rm nz}_{i,1,\Lambda}(p)
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(p)
  \mathcal Z^{0,{\rm res}}_{i,F,\Lambda}(p)
  \nu_p .
\]
For two local component sources \(A,B\), write
\[
  u_A(p)=(u_{A,+}(p),u_{A,-}(p)),\qquad
  u_B(p)=(u_{B,+}(p),u_{B,-}(p))
\]
for their overlaps with the two universal zero modes.  The Berezin extraction
of \(d\xi_+d\xi_-\) gives the oriented minor
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{AB}(p)
  =
  u_{A,+}(p)u_{B,-}(p)
  -
  u_{A,-}(p)u_{B,+}(p),
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-component-source-minor}
\end{equation}
not the product of norms and not the protected coefficient \(c_i\).  At this
finite regulator the retained component amplitude is
\begin{equation}
  A^{(1)}_{AB,\Lambda}
  =
  q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}
  \sum_{p\in P}
  K_p\,
  \bigl(\Delta_{AB}(p)+C_{AB}^{\prime}(p)\bigr)
  +
  R_{AB,\Lambda},
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-component-cell-amplitude}
\end{equation}
where \(C'_{AB}\) is the primed nonzero-mode contact correction in the same
determinant-line convention.  Its dual-frame image is obtained only after
the charge-one event and local component sources are transported by the
operator map; in that frame the common monomial is \(e^{-Y_i}\).  The absolute
residual is bounded by
\begin{equation}
  |R_{AB,\Lambda}|
  \le
  B_{\rm src}+B_{\rm prop}+B_{\rm det}+B_{\rm zm}
  +B_{\rm cpt}+B_{\rm op}+B_{\rm cont}.
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-component-cell-bound}
\end{equation}
Several shortcuts are now visibly wrong.  If \(u_A(p)\) and \(u_B(p)\) are
parallel on every retained cell and \(C'_{AB}=0\), the component amplitude
vanishes even when the protected vortex coefficient is nonzero.  If
\(C'_{AB}\) is nonzero, it is a source-specific primed-propagator contribution
and cannot be absorbed into the scalar coefficient \(c_i\).  Finally, changing
the normalization of \(u_A,u_B\), the orientation of the zero-mode line, or
the operator coordinate changes \(\Delta_{AB}\); the mirror monomial records
the dual image of the sector but does not supply these original
component-source data.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{remark}[Source-frame calibration of a one-vortex
component amplitude]
\label{rem:glsm-one-vortex-source-frame-calibration-map}
The finite component cell also gives a useful calibration test.  Define the
retained protected coefficient and the retained component source integrals by
\[
  C_\Lambda=\sum_{p\in P}K_p,\qquad
  J_{AB,\Lambda}
  =
  \sum_{p\in P}K_p
  \bigl(\Delta_{AB}(p)+C'_{AB}(p)\bigr).
\]
The original retained component amplitude carries the numerical vortex-sector
weight and the source integral:
\[
  A^{(1),{\rm ret}}_{AB,\Lambda}
  =
  q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}J_{AB,\Lambda}
  =
  q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}C_\Lambda\,s_{AB,\Lambda},
  \qquad
  s_{AB,\Lambda}:=\frac{J_{AB,\Lambda}}{C_\Lambda}
\]
when \(C_\Lambda\neq0\).  The number \(s_{AB,\Lambda}\) is a source-frame
quantity: it is made from zero-mode overlaps and primed nonzero-mode contact
terms in the same regulator.  It is not determined by the mirror fugacity.
After the original-to-dual map is fixed, the same common sector factor is
represented in the dual Wilsonian functional by \(e^{-Y_i}\); before that
matching, replacing \(q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}\) by \(e^{-Y_i}\) is a frame
substitution rather than a direct amplitude calculation.

Suppose a reference source pair \(C,D\) has
\(J_{CD,\Lambda}\neq0\).  At the retained level the target/source ratio is
\begin{equation}
  A^{(1),{\rm ret}}_{AB,\Lambda}
  =
  \frac{J_{AB,\Lambda}}{J_{CD,\Lambda}}\,
  A^{(1),{\rm ret}}_{CD,\Lambda}.
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-frame-ratio}
\end{equation}
Thus a reference component measurement can calibrate the common vortex
fugacity only after the direct source-frame ratio
\(J_{AB,\Lambda}/J_{CD,\Lambda}\) has been computed.  If
\(J_{CD,\Lambda}\) is small, the same noncancellation discipline as in
Controlled Approximation~\ref{ca:glsm-vortex-coefficient-noncancellation} is
needed for the reference channel.  The retained ratio identity becomes a
physical amplitude estimate only after component and source-frame transport
errors have been estimated in the same regulator convention.  If those
separate estimates are supplied as
\(|R_{AB,\Lambda}|\le B_{AB}\), \(|R_{CD,\Lambda}|\le B_{CD}\), and a
source-frame transport error \(B_{\rm frame}\), the calibrated difference
obeys
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    A^{(1)}_{AB,\Lambda}
    -
    \frac{J_{AB,\Lambda}}{J_{CD,\Lambda}}
    A^{(1)}_{CD,\Lambda}
  \right|
  \le
  B_{AB}
  +
  \left|\frac{J_{AB,\Lambda}}{J_{CD,\Lambda}}\right|B_{CD}
  +
  B_{\rm frame}.
  \label{eq:glsm-one-vortex-source-frame-calibration-bound}
\end{equation}
This equation is the two-dimensional analogue of the four-dimensional
instanton lesson: the topological saddle and its collective-coordinate chart
do not by themselves determine a physical amplitude.  A parallel source pair
has \(J_{AB,\Lambda}=0\) when the primed contact term vanishes, even if
\(C_\Lambda\) and the dual primitive monomial are nonzero.  Conversely, a
nonzero \(C'_{AB}\) changes the component ratio without changing the protected
twisted-superpotential coefficient.  Those data have to be calculated in the
direct vortex fluctuation problem before the mirror expression is used as an
amplitude statement.
\end{remark}

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Single-vortex coefficient noncancellation bound]
\label{ca:glsm-vortex-coefficient-noncancellation}
Fix a compact retained window
\(\mathcal U_R\Subset \mathcal U_{i,1,\Lambda}\) in the regulated
charge-one vortex chart and write the scalar integrand in
\eqref{eq:glsm-single-vortex-original-coefficient-integral} as
\[
  K_{i,\Lambda}(z)
  =
  q^{\rm vort}_{i,\Lambda}
  e^{-S^{\rm red}_{i,1,\Lambda}(z)}
  \mathcal W_{i,1,\Lambda}^{\rm nz}(z)
  \mathcal N^{\rm int}_{i,\Lambda}(z)
  \mathcal Z_{i,F,\Lambda}^{0}(z).
\]
Let
\[
  C_R
  =
  \mu^{-1}\int_{\mathcal U_R}K_{i,\Lambda}(z)\,\dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z),
  \qquad
  M_R
  =
  \mu^{-1}\int_{\mathcal U_R}|K_{i,\Lambda}(z)|\,\dd\nu_{i,1,\Lambda}(z),
\]
and suppose \(M_R>0\) and
\[
  |C_R|\ge \kappa_R M_R,\qquad 0<\kappa_R\le 1 .
\]
The number \(\kappa_R\) is the retained-window noncancellation margin: it
measures how much of the absolute one-vortex mass survives the determinant
phase, the orientation of the universal fermion-zero-mode line, and the
residual Berezin coefficient.  Assume further that the omitted part of the
vortex chart, the determinant comparison, the zero-mode/orientation
identification, the compactification boundary, and the continuum comparison
are bounded by
\[
  T_R,\quad
  \epsilon_{\rm det},\quad
  \epsilon_{\rm zm},\quad
  \epsilon_{\rm cpt},\quad
  \epsilon_{\rm cont}.
\]
Set \(c_i^\star=c_{i,\Lambda}\) for a fixed-regulator bound, with
\(\epsilon_{\rm cont}=0\), and set \(c_i^\star=c_i\) when the continuum
coefficient exists and the last comparison error is included.  Then
\begin{equation}
  |c_i^\star-C_R|
  \le
  T_R+\epsilon_{\rm det}+\epsilon_{\rm zm}
  +\epsilon_{\rm cpt}+\epsilon_{\rm cont}.
  \label{eq:glsm-vortex-coefficient-noncancellation-bound}
\end{equation}
If the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:glsm-vortex-coefficient-noncancellation-bound} is strictly smaller
than \(|C_R|\), then \(c_i^\star\neq0\); more quantitatively,
\[
  \frac{|c_i^\star-C_R|}{|C_R|}
  \le
  \frac{
    T_R+\epsilon_{\rm det}+\epsilon_{\rm zm}
    +\epsilon_{\rm cpt}+\epsilon_{\rm cont}
  }{\kappa_R M_R}.
\]
This bound is stronger than charge matching, holomorphy, or the
Coulomb logarithm alone.  Those inputs identify the allowed primitive
monomial and its FI-coordinate normalization.  The bound is the
amplitude-side statement that the regulated determinant line, zero-mode
saturation, and boundary tail have not made the one-vortex coefficient
cancel to zero.
\end{controlledapproximation}

We now move from the \(D_{\rm vort}\) amplitude input to the \(D_{\rm pert}\)
and protected twisted-sector consequences of
Conjecture~\ref{conj:abelian-glsm-hori-vafa-mirror-qft}.  The following
critical-equation and FI-coordinate identities are algebraic consequences of
the mirror presentation once the vortex coefficients and branches have been
supplied.

The \(Y_i\)-critical equation is
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{Y_i}\widetilde W_{\rm dual}=0
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  e^{-Y_i}=\frac{M_i(\Sigma)}{\mu c_i}.
  \label{eq:glsm-hv-y-critical-equation}
\end{equation}
On a branch where \(M_i(\Sigma)\neq0\), substituting
\eqref{eq:glsm-hv-y-critical-equation} gives
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde W_{\rm dual}\big|_{\partial_Y\widetilde W=0}
  =
  -\sum_{a=1}^s t_a\Sigma_a
  +
  \sum_{i=1}^m
  M_i(\Sigma)
  \left(
    \log\frac{M_i(\Sigma)}{\mu c_i}-1
  \right).
  \label{eq:glsm-hv-eliminated-one-loop-superpotential}
\end{equation}
For \(s=1\), \(M_i=Q_i\Sigma\), and \(c_i=1\), this is precisely the
charge-resolved form of
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-twisted-superpotential}, with the constants
\(c_i\) and logarithm branches absorbed into the finite definition of the
FI coordinate.  Thus the charged-chiral dual variables give a nontrivial
consistency check: after the exponential terms have been supplied, their
elimination reproduces the protected Coulomb-branch determinant.

The finite shift of the FI coordinate is explicit.  In the rank-one case,
\eqref{eq:glsm-hv-eliminated-one-loop-superpotential} is
\[
  -t\Sigma
  +
  \sum_iQ_i\Sigma
  \left(
    \log\frac{Q_i\Sigma}{\mu}-1
  \right)
  -
  \Sigma\sum_iQ_i\log c_i .
\]
The last term is affine in \(\Sigma\), hence is the same as replacing
\[
  t\longmapsto t+\sum_iQ_i\log c_i .
\]
This is why the constants \(c_i\) are convention data for the protected
Coulomb matching unless one separately fixes a normalization of the vortex
measure.

\begin{proposition}[Vortex normalization and all-rank FI coordinates]
\label{prop:glsm-vortex-normalization-fi-coordinate}
For an abelian gauge group \(U(1)^s\), fix nonzero vortex coefficients
\(c_i\) and choices of \(\log c_i\).  Set
\[
  \widehat X_i=c_i e^{-Y_i}.
\]
Then the mirror superpotential has normalized primitive terms
\[
  -\mu\sum_i\widehat X_i ,
\]
while the logarithmic-torus constraints are written in the shifted FI
coordinates
\begin{equation}
  \prod_{i=1}^m \widehat X_i^{\,Q_i{}^a}
  =
  \exp\left(t_a+\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i{}^a\log c_i\right),
  \qquad a=1,\ldots,s .
  \label{eq:glsm-all-rank-vortex-normalized-constraints}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, in the Coulomb-eliminated expression the constants \(c_i\)
replace
\begin{equation}
  t_a\longmapsto
  t_a^{\rm phys}
  =
  t_a+\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i{}^a\log c_i .
  \label{eq:glsm-all-rank-vortex-fi-shift}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The constraint \(\sum_i Q_i{}^aY_i=-t_a\) is equivalent, on the logarithmic
torus \(X_i=e^{-Y_i}\), to
\[
  \prod_iX_i^{Q_i{}^a}=e^{t_a}.
\]
Multiplying \(X_i\) by \(c_i\) gives
\[
  \prod_i\widehat X_i^{Q_i{}^a}
  =
  \left(\prod_iX_i^{Q_i{}^a}\right)
  \left(\prod_i c_i^{Q_i{}^a}\right),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:glsm-all-rank-vortex-normalized-constraints}.  The same
shift appears before imposing the low-energy constraint.  Since
\[
  \sum_i M_i(\Sigma)\log c_i
  =
  \sum_i\sum_a Q_i{}^a\Sigma_a\log c_i
  =
  \sum_a\Sigma_a\sum_iQ_i{}^a\log c_i ,
\]
the \(c_i\)-dependent part of
\eqref{eq:glsm-hv-eliminated-one-loop-superpotential} is
\(-\sum_a\Sigma_a\sum_iQ_i{}^a\log c_i\), precisely the affine FI-theta
shift in \eqref{eq:glsm-all-rank-vortex-fi-shift}.
\end{proof}

Thus the Hori--Vafa expression is not a normalization-free statement of a
bare path integral.  Its invariant protected input is the charge lattice, the
primitive nonzero vortex monomial for each allowed charge-one sector, and the
FI-theta coordinate after the finite determinant normalization has been
chosen.  Changing the regulator normalization rescales the mirror torus
coordinates and moves the same finite data into \(t_a^{\rm phys}\); it does
not by itself prove the existence or nonvanishing of the vortex coefficient.
Changing the branch by \(\log c_i\mapsto\log c_i+2\pi\ii n_i\) changes
\(t_a^{\rm phys}\) by
\[
  2\pi\ii\sum_iQ_i{}^a n_i ,
  \qquad n_i\in\mathbb Z ,
\]

\begin{proposition}[\(\mathbb P^{N-1}\) mirror residue and the quantum-product test]
\label{prop:cpn-hv-residue-quantum-product}

variables of Proposition~\ref{prop:glsm-vortex-normalization-fi-coordinate}
and write
\begin{equation}
  q_{\rm phys}
  =
  \exp\left(t+\sum_{i=1}^N\log c_i\right)
  \neq0 .
  \label{eq:cpn-hv-physical-q}
\end{equation}
On the logarithmic torus
\[
  \prod_{i=1}^N\widehat X_i=q_{\rm phys},
  \qquad
  \widetilde W=-\mu\sum_{i=1}^N\widehat X_i ,
\]

mirror Jacobi quotient by \([\widehat X_1]\).  With the determinant-line
normalization that strips the universal factor \((-\mu)^{N-1}\) from the
logarithmic Hessian, the mirror residue trace is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal R_{\mathbb P^{N-1}}(H^k)
  =
  \sum_{x^N=q_{\rm phys}}
  \frac{x^k}{N x^{N-1}}
  =
  \begin{cases}
    q_{\rm phys}^{\,d}, & k=N-1+dN,\quad d\ge0,\\
    0, & k\not\equiv N-1\pmod{N} .
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:cpn-hv-residue-selection-rule}
\end{equation}
Consequently the protected Frobenius algebra seen by this trace has
\[
  H^N=q_{\rm phys},
  \qquad
  \mathcal R_{\mathbb P^{N-1}}(H^{N-1})=1 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Use logarithmic coordinates
\[
  u_a=\log\widehat X_a,\qquad a=1,\ldots,N-1,
\]
and eliminate
\[
  \widehat X_N=q_{\rm phys}\exp\left(-\sum_{a=1}^{N-1}u_a\right).
\]
For the reduced function
\[
  S(u)=\sum_{a=1}^{N-1}e^{u_a}
  +
  q_{\rm phys}\exp\left(-\sum_{a=1}^{N-1}u_a\right),
\]
the critical equations are
\[
  e^{u_a}=\widehat X_N,\qquad a=1,\ldots,N-1.
\]
Thus every critical point has
\[
  \widehat X_1=\cdots=\widehat X_N=x,
  \qquad
  x^N=q_{\rm phys}.
\]
At such a point the logarithmic Hessian of \(S\) is
\[
  x(I_{N-1}+J_{N-1}),
\]
where \(J_{N-1}\) is the all-ones matrix.  Since \(I_{N-1}+J_{N-1}\) has
eigenvalues \(1,\ldots,1,N\), its determinant is \(N x^{N-1}\).  The factor
from \(\widetilde W=-\mu S\) is the universal \((-\mu)^{N-1}\) determinant-line
factor already fixed in the trace normalization, leaving the denominator in
\eqref{eq:cpn-hv-residue-selection-rule}.

It remains to evaluate the finite root sum.  Put
\[
  \ell=k-(N-1).
\]
Then
\[
  \sum_{x^N=q_{\rm phys}}\frac{x^k}{N x^{N-1}}
  =
  \frac1N\sum_{x^N=q_{\rm phys}}x^\ell .
\]
If \(\ell\) is not divisible by \(N\), the sum over the \(N\)-th roots vanishes.
If \(\ell=dN\) with \(d\ge0\), each critical point contributes \(x^{dN}
=q_{\rm phys}^{\,d}\), and the prefactor \(1/N\) cancels the number of roots.
The same critical-point equations identify \(H^N\) and \(q_{\rm phys}\) in the
Jacobi quotient; the nondegenerate residue pairing separates the quotient
classes.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Stable-map computation for the \(\mathbb P^{N-1}\) quantum product]
\label{prop:cpn-stable-map-quantum-product-gate}
Let \(N\ge2\).  Work in the A-twisted positive chamber of the
\(\mathbb P^{N-1}\) GLSM, or equivalently in the large-volume sigma-model
chart, and assume the compactified genus-zero stable-map moduli spaces
\[

\]

evaluation maps \({\rm ev}_j\), and the operator map identifying \(H\) with
the hyperplane class.  Then
\[
  {\rm vdim}_{\mathbb C}\,
  \overline{\mathcal M}_{0,3}(\mathbb P^{N-1},d)
  =
  (N-1)+Nd .
\]
For \(d=1\), the three-point invariant
\begin{equation}
  I_1(H,H^{N-1},H^{N-1})
  =
  \int_{[\overline{\mathcal M}_{0,3}(\mathbb P^{N-1},1)]^{\rm vir}}
  {\rm ev}_1^\ast H\,
  {\rm ev}_2^\ast H^{N-1}\,
  {\rm ev}_3^\ast H^{N-1}
  \label{eq:cpn-degree-one-stable-map-line-count}
\end{equation}
equals \(1\), with the complex orientation.  Consequently the degree-one
part of the A-model product gives
\[
  H\star H^{N-1}=q_{\rm phys}\,1,
  \qquad
  H^N=q_{\rm phys}
\]
provided the degree-one stable-map/vortex coefficient is the same
\(q_{\rm phys}\) used in the normalized mirror coordinate.  If the virtual
class, determinant orientation, compactification boundary terms, or operator
map are not fixed, this implication is only a target comparison, not a
derivation from the mirror residue.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The expected-dimension formula for genus-zero stable maps gives
\[
  {\rm vdim}_{\mathbb C}
  =
  \dim_{\mathbb C}\mathbb P^{N-1}
  +c_1(T\mathbb P^{N-1})\cdot d
  =
  (N-1)+Nd
\]
for three marked points.  At \(d=1\), the insertion in
\eqref{eq:cpn-degree-one-stable-map-line-count} has complex codimension
\[
  1+(N-1)+(N-1)=2N-1,
\]
which is exactly the virtual dimension.  Geometrically, the two
\(H^{N-1}\) insertions constrain the image line to pass through two generic
points of \(\mathbb P^{N-1}\).  There is a unique such line.  A generic
hyperplane is not that line and intersects it once, giving the first marked
point uniquely.  The complex orientation gives positive sign, so the count is
one.

For the quantum product,
\[
  \eta(\alpha\star\beta,\gamma)
  =
  \sum_{d\ge0}q_{\rm phys}^{\,d} I_d(\alpha,\beta,\gamma).
  \]
Taking \(\alpha=H\), \(\beta=H^{N-1}\), and
\(\gamma=H^a\), the dimension constraint is
\[
  1+(N-1)+a=N-1+Nd .
\]
With \(0\le a\le N-1\), it is satisfied only for \(d=1\) and
\(a=N-1\).  Thus \(H\star H^{N-1}\) pairs with the top class exactly as
\(q_{\rm phys}1\) does, and it pairs trivially with the other basis classes.
Nondegeneracy of the classical pairing gives the product relation.
\end{proof}

  +

\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{remark}[Degree-one stable-map incidence proof-obligation template]
\label{rem:cpn-degree-one-vortex-observable-proof-obligation-template}
The degree-one product coefficient is a physical A-twisted observable only after

\section{The Cigar--Liouville Mirror Chain as a QFT Duality Problem}
\label{sec:cigar-liouville-mirror-qft-problem}

The same dualization mechanism gives a useful intrinsic two-dimensional
description of the relation between the \(\mathcal N=2\) cigar coset and
\(\mathcal N=2\) Liouville theory.  This subsection records the construction
as a QFT comparison problem.  The names ``cigar'' and ``Liouville'' refer to
two-dimensional field theories and their semiclassical target descriptions.

\begin{conjecture}[Cigar--Liouville mirror QFT equivalence]
\label{conj:cigar-liouville-mirror-qft}
Fix a level \(k>0\) in the conventions used below, a spin structure, \(R\)-
and flavor-background fields, contact-term conventions, and a continuum
definition of the axial \(\mathcal N=2\) cigar coset
\(SL(2,\mathbb R)_k/U(1)\) or of the logarithmic-chiral GLSM flowing to it.
Fix also the \(\mathcal N=2\) Liouville theory with the corresponding
linear-dilaton/background-charge convention, periodic twisted chiral
coordinate, exponential twisted superpotential, and the same background data.

The conjecture asserts an equivalence of the two continuum QFTs: the
Hamiltonians and \(\mathcal N=2\) superconformal generators agree under a
unitary map of Hilbert spaces; spectral-flow sectors, spin structures, and
discrete and continuous representations are matched; normalizable states,
nonnormalizable source operators, reflection amplitudes, local OPEs,
partition functions, and correlation functions are identified with their
contact terms; and the equivalence respects topological twists, defects,
branes, and boundary states.  The semiclassical quotient metric, the
asymptotic dualization, the single exponential from the ordinary charged
chiral multiplet, and the absence of a smooth logarithmic-chiral vortex
exponential are evidence for this statement.  They leave separate continuum
obligations: the continuous-spectrum measure, operator completeness,
strong-coupling control at the cigar tip or Liouville wall, and the
defect/boundary matching.
\end{conjecture}

For Conjecture~\ref{conj:cigar-liouville-mirror-qft}, the checks in this
section form the following protected-data and proof-debt ledger:
\[
  \begin{array}{rcl}
    C_{\rm cl} &:& \hbox{classical quotient metric and asymptotic cylinder},\\
    C_{\rm dual} &:& \hbox{periodic dual variables and vector constraint},\\
    C_{\rm vort} &:& \hbox{ordinary vortex exponential and logarithmic-vortex obstruction},\\
    C_{\rm spec} &:& \hbox{spectral flow, continuous measure, and reflection data},\\
    C_{\rm bdry} &:& \hbox{defect, brane, boundary-state, and contact-term matching}.
  \end{array}
\]
Only the first three entries are calculated in this chapter.

Start with a \(U(1)\) vector multiplet \(V\), an ordinary chiral multiplet
\(\Phi\) of charge \(1\), and a logarithmic chiral multiplet
\[
  P\sim P+2\pi\ii
\]
whose imaginary part shifts under the gauge symmetry so that
\[
  P+\overline P+V
\]
is gauge invariant.  With \(k>0\), the bosonic low-derivative part of the
GLSM contains
\begin{equation}
  |(\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu)\phi|^2
  +
  \frac{k}{2}(\partial_\mu\operatorname{Re}p)^2
  +
  \frac{k}{2}
  (\partial_\mu\operatorname{Im}p-A_\mu)^2
  \label{eq:cigar-glsm-bosonic-kinetic}
\end{equation}
together with the auxiliary constraint
\[
  |\phi|^2+\frac{k}{2}(p+\overline p)=0
\]
in the low-energy quotient.  Write
\[
  \rho=\sqrt2\,|\phi|,\qquad \vartheta=\arg\phi,\qquad
  p=u+\ii\chi .
\]
Then
\[
  |(\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu)\phi|^2
  =
  \frac12(\partial_\mu\rho)^2
  +
  \frac{\rho^2}{2}(\partial_\mu\vartheta-A_\mu)^2 .
\]
The auxiliary constraint gives \(u=-\rho^2/(2k)\), hence
\[
  \frac{k}{2}(\partial_\mu u)^2
  =
  \frac{\rho^2}{2k}(\partial_\mu\rho)^2 .
\]
Fix the gauge \(\chi=0\).  The \(A_\mu\)-dependent angular part is
\[
  \frac12\rho^2(\partial_\mu\vartheta-A_\mu)^2
  +
  \frac{k}{2}A_\mu A^\mu .
\]
Its algebraic equation is
\begin{equation}
  (\rho^2+k)A_\mu=\rho^2\partial_\mu\vartheta .
  \label{eq:cigar-glsm-gauge-field-saddle}
\end{equation}
Substituting
\[
  A_\mu=\frac{\rho^2}{\rho^2+k}\partial_\mu\vartheta
\]
gives the angular coefficient
\[
  \frac12
  \frac{k\rho^2}{\rho^2+k}
  (\partial_\mu\vartheta)^2
  =
  \frac12
  \frac{\rho^2}{1+\rho^2/k}
  (\partial_\mu\vartheta)^2 .
\]
Combining the radial and angular terms, the classical two-real-dimensional
quotient metric is
\begin{equation}
  ds^2
  =
  \left(1+\frac{\rho^2}{k}\right)d\rho^2
  +
  \frac{\rho^2}{1+\rho^2/k}\,d\vartheta^2 .
  \label{eq:cigar-glsm-classical-quotient-metric}
\end{equation}
This metric is the classical quotient metric.  The conformal cigar coset is
an infrared fixed-point statement whose definition requires the corresponding
quantum current algebra or continuum fixed-point construction, rather than
the low-derivative quotient metric alone.

Dualize both logarithmic directions.  Introduce twisted chiral variables
\[
  Y,\qquad Y_P,
  \qquad
  Y\sim Y+2\pi\ii,\quad Y_P\sim Y_P+2\pi\ii .
\]
The ordinary charged chiral multiplet supplies the exponential term
\(\mu e^{-Y}\).  The logarithmic chiral multiplet contributes a quadratic
dual Kahler term rather than a second exponential term.  The absence of a
\(P\)-vortex exponential has a finite topological reason in the smooth
finite-action sector.  The periodic field \(e^{\ii\chi}\) is a unit-norm
charged field for the same \(U(1)\) connection \(A\):
\[
  D(e^{\ii\chi})
  =
  \ii e^{\ii\chi}(d\chi-A).
\]
Thus a smooth \(P\)-configuration on a Euclidean surface \(\Sigma_E\)
supplies a nowhere-vanishing section of the associated unit circle bundle.
If the gauge bundle has vortex number
\[
  n=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\Sigma_E}F\in\mathbb Z ,
\]
such a nowhere-vanishing section trivializes the line bundle and forces
\(n=0\).  Equivalently, on a disk, a boundary winding of \(\chi\) extends
smoothly through the center only when the winding is zero; unlike the
ordinary charged scalar \(\phi=\rho e^{\ii\vartheta}/\sqrt2\), the
logarithmic multiplet has no radial modulus whose zero can absorb the
winding.  Allowing singular \(P\)-fields would be a different defect
insertion, not the smooth finite-action vortex sector used to generate
twisted \(F\)-terms.

In the convention compatible with
\eqref{eq:glsm-hv-dual-twisted-superpotential}, the
low-energy twisted superpotential has the form
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde W_{\rm cig}(\Sigma,Y,Y_P)
  =
  -\Sigma(Y+Y_P)
  -
  \mu e^{-Y},
  \label{eq:cigar-liouville-dual-superpotential-before-constraint}
\end{equation}
up to affine redefinitions of \(Y_P\) and \(\Sigma\).  The vector multiplet
imposes
\begin{equation}
  Y+Y_P=0 .
  \label{eq:cigar-liouville-linear-constraint}
\end{equation}
Substituting the constraint leaves one twisted chiral variable \(Y\) with
twisted superpotential
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde W_{\rm Liouville}(Y)
  =
  -\mu e^{-Y}.
  \label{eq:n2-liouville-twisted-superpotential}
\end{equation}
The dual Kahler potential has the asymptotic large-\(\operatorname{Re}Y\)
behavior
\begin{equation}
  K(Y,\overline Y)
  =
  \frac{1}{8\pi k}\,\overline Y Y
  +
  O(e^{-\operatorname{Re}Y}),
  \qquad
  \operatorname{Re}Y\to+\infty,
  \label{eq:n2-liouville-asymptotic-kahler}
\end{equation}
with the sign convention appropriate to twisted chiral superspace.  This is
the \(\mathcal N=2\) Liouville presentation: an asymptotic cylinder
coordinate \(Y\) together with the exponential twisted superpotential
\eqref{eq:n2-liouville-twisted-superpotential}.

The construction supplies a precise protected-data bridge:
\[
  \begin{aligned}
  &\text{charged chiral dualization}
  +\text{nonzero vortex exponential}
  +\text{IR vector constraint}
  \\
  &\hspace{3em}\Longrightarrow
  \mathcal N=2\ \text{Liouville data}.
  \end{aligned}
\]
This is the \(C_{\rm cl}\), \(C_{\rm dual}\), and \(C_{\rm vort}\) evidence
lane for Conjecture~\ref{conj:cigar-liouville-mirror-qft}.  It identifies
the asymptotic variables and the protected Liouville superpotential, while
the equality of continuum Hilbert spaces, local algebras,
\(\mathcal N=2\) generators, defect categories, spectral-flow sectors, and
continuous-spectrum measures remains in the \(C_{\rm spec}\) and
\(C_{\rm bdry}\) obligations.  The semiclassical quotient metric
\eqref{eq:cigar-glsm-classical-quotient-metric} is a coordinate on the
asymptotic target geometry rather than a construction of the full cigar QFT.

\section{Abelian GLSM Coulomb-Branch One-Loop Calculation}
\label{sec:abelian-glsm-coulomb-one-loop-calculation}

The GLSM has a second protected local calculation beyond the classical Higgs
quotient.  Move to a Coulomb-branch region where
\(\sigma\neq0\), so charged chiral multiplets have masses
\[
  m_i(\sigma)=Q_i\sigma .
\]
The calculation below is a Wilsonian twisted \(F\)-term calculation whose
scope is the protected Coulomb coordinate chart.  The full infrared QFT
requires a separate continuum construction.

\begin{hypothesis}[Abelian Coulomb one-loop determinant]
\label{hyp:abelian-glsm-coulomb-one-loop-determinant}
Fix an abelian GLSM presentation as in
Definition~\ref{def:abelian-2d-glsm-presentation}, assume all charges \(Q_i\) are
nonzero, and restrict to a simply connected branch of the region
\[
  Q_i\sigma\neq0,\qquad i=1,\ldots,m.
\]
Assume a supersymmetric Wilsonian regulator preserving the twisted chiral
field strength \(\Sigma\), the gauge symmetry, and the integer charge
normalization.  The finite part of the massive chiral determinant is assumed
to be represented by the local twisted superpotential
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde W_{\rm eff}(\Sigma)
  =
  -t\Sigma
  +
  \sum_{i=1}^m
  Q_i\Sigma\left(
    \log\frac{Q_i\Sigma}{\mu}-1
  \right)
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-twisted-superpotential}
\end{equation}
up to an additive constant and an affine function of \(\Sigma\).  The
renormalization scale \(\mu>0\) and the logarithm branches are part of the
scheme choice.  Boundary contributions from \(\sigma=0\), vortex sectors,
and singular loci are not included in this local determinant hypothesis.
\end{hypothesis}

\paragraph{Local determinant derivation.}
The part of Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:abelian-glsm-coulomb-one-loop-determinant}
that is a perturbative determinant calculation can be made explicit.  Work in
a background in which the twisted chiral field strength is slowly varying and
has lowest component \(\sigma\).  For a chiral multiplet of charge \(Q\), the
Coulomb-region complex mass is
\[
  M=Q\sigma .
\]
The local Wilsonian determinant is obtained by expanding the massive
Gaussian path integral to first order in the top component of \(\Sigma\), or
equivalently to first order in the background \(D+\ii F_{12}\) that couples
to the FI-theta coordinate.  The bosonic and fermionic determinants cancel at
zero background \(D+\ii F_{12}\); the uncancelled linear term is the massive
propagator at coincident points.  With a proper-time subtraction at the
reference mass \(\mu\),
\begin{equation}
  4\pi
  \int_0^\infty
  \frac{\dd s}{4\pi s}\,
  \bigl(e^{-s|M|^2}-e^{-s\mu^2}\bigr)
  =
  \log\frac{\mu^2}{|M|^2}.
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-proper-time-log}
\end{equation}
The equality follows by differentiating both sides with respect to
\(|M|^2\) and fixing the constant at \(|M|=\mu\):
\[
  \frac{\partial}{\partial |M|^2}
  \int_0^\infty\frac{\dd s}{s}
  \bigl(e^{-s|M|^2}-e^{-s\mu^2}\bigr)
  =
  -\int_0^\infty \dd s\,e^{-s|M|^2}
  =
  -\frac1{|M|^2}.
\]
Equation~\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-proper-time-log} determines the real
logarithm in the finite FI shift.  Its imaginary partner is fixed by the
two-dimensional axial anomaly: changing the phase of \(M\) by
\(M\mapsto e^{\ii\alpha}M\) is a chiral rotation of a charge-\(Q\) massive
multiplet and shifts the theta coordinate by \(Q\alpha\) in the same
normalization in which the flux of a unit-charge \(U(1)\) bundle is integral.
Thus the real subtraction and the anomaly combine into the holomorphic
branch
\[
  Q\log\frac{M}{\mu}
  =
  Q\log\frac{Q\Sigma}{\mu}
\]
for the derivative of the twisted superpotential.  For \(m\) massive chiral
multiplets this gives, on the chosen simply connected Coulomb branch,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \widetilde W_{\rm eff}}{\partial\Sigma}
  =
  -t+\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i\log\frac{Q_i\Sigma}{\mu}.
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-derivative}
\end{equation}
Integrating \eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-derivative} in the holomorphic
coordinate \(\Sigma\) gives
\[
  -t\Sigma+\sum_iQ_i\Sigma
  \left(\log\frac{Q_i\Sigma}{\mu}-1\right),
\]
up to an additive constant and an affine function of \(\Sigma\).  This is
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-twisted-superpotential}.  The calculation is
local on the Coulomb chart: it uses \(Q_i\sigma\neq0\), chooses logarithm
branches, and says nothing by itself about vortex sectors, singular loci, or
the existence of the infrared continuum QFT.

\paragraph{Coulomb critical equation.}
Under Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:abelian-glsm-coulomb-one-loop-determinant}, the
local Coulomb critical equation is
\begin{equation}
  -t+\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i\log\frac{Q_i\sigma}{\mu}=0 .
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-log-critical-equation}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, on the chosen logarithm branch,
\begin{equation}
  \prod_{i=1}^m
  \left(\frac{Q_i\sigma}{\mu}\right)^{Q_i}
  =
  \ee^t .
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-exponential-critical-equation}
\end{equation}
If all \(Q_i>0\), then this equation has
\begin{equation}
  Q_{\rm tot}=\sum_{i=1}^m Q_i
  \label{eq:abelian-glsm-positive-charge-vacuum-count}
\end{equation}
simple solutions for \(\sigma\) whenever \(\ee^t\neq0\).  For \(m=N\) fields
of charge \(1\), the count is \(N\).

Differentiate \eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-one-loop-twisted-superpotential} with
respect to \(\Sigma\).  For a single summand,
\[
  \frac{d}{d\Sigma}
  \left[
    Q_i\Sigma\left(\log\frac{Q_i\Sigma}{\mu}-1\right)
  \right]
  =
  Q_i\log\frac{Q_i\Sigma}{\mu}.
\]
The critical equation is therefore
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-log-critical-equation}.  Exponentiating gives
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-exponential-critical-equation}.

When all charges are positive, write
\[
  C_Q=\prod_{i=1}^m Q_i^{Q_i}.
\]
Then \eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-exponential-critical-equation} becomes
\[
  C_Q\,\mu^{-Q_{\rm tot}}\sigma^{Q_{\rm tot}}=\ee^t .
\]
Since \(Q_{\rm tot}>0\) and the right side is nonzero, this polynomial
equation has \(Q_{\rm tot}\) nonzero roots.  Its derivative is proportional
to \(\sigma^{Q_{\rm tot}-1}\), so every nonzero root is simple.  For \(N\)
charge-one fields, \(Q_{\rm tot}=N\).

\begin{example}[\(\mathbb P^{N-1}\) GLSM quantum vacua]
\label{ex:cpn-glsm-coulomb-vacua}
Take \(m=N\), \(Q_i=1\), and \(W=0\).  The local equation is
\[
  \left(\frac{\sigma}{\mu}\right)^N=\ee^t .
\]
Thus the Coulomb-branch one-loop calculation predicts \(N\) massive twisted
chiral vacua in the chamber where this effective description is valid.  This
is the protected arithmetic behind the usual \(\mathbb P^{N-1}\) vacuum
count.  The statement that these vacua exhaust the continuum theory requires
the same regulator, compactness, and singular-locus controls stated in
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:abelian-glsm-coulomb-one-loop-determinant}.
\end{example}

For the hypersurface charge vector \((1,\ldots,1,-d)\), the exponent of
\(\sigma\) in
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-exponential-critical-equation} is \(N-d\),
the same integer as the axial anomaly coefficient
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-axial-anomaly}.  In the anomaly-free case \(N=d\),
the Coulomb equation becomes a condition on \(t\) and the logarithm branch
rather than an equation with finitely many isolated \(\sigma\)-solutions.
This is a protected signal of a singular locus in the FI-theta parameter
space.  A full infrared phase-transition statement requires control of the
continuum theory at the singular locus.

\paragraph{Classical chambers for the hypersurface GLSM.}
Fix integers \(N\geq2\) and \(d\geq2\).  Let the GLSM have chiral fields
\[
  X_1,\ldots,X_N,\quad P
\]
with charges
\begin{equation}
  Q(X_i)=1,\qquad Q(P)=-d,
  \label{eq:hypersurface-glsm-charges}
\end{equation}
and superpotential
\begin{equation}
  W=P\,G_d(X_1,\ldots,X_N),
  \label{eq:hypersurface-glsm-superpotential}
\end{equation}
where \(G_d\) is homogeneous of degree \(d\).  Assume that the projective
hypersurface
\begin{equation}
  Y_G=\{[X]\in\mathbb P^{N-1}:G_d(X)=0\}
  \label{eq:projective-hypersurface-phase-target}
\end{equation}
is smooth, equivalently the equations
\[
  G_d(X)=\partial_1G_d(X)=\cdots=\partial_NG_d(X)=0
\]
have no solution with \(X\neq0\).  Then the classical Higgs-vacuum quotient
has the following chamber description.
\begin{enumerate}
\item If \(r>0\), then \(X\neq0\), \(P=0\), and the quotient is
  \(Y_G\subset\mathbb P^{N-1}\).
\item If \(r<0\), then \(P\neq0\), \(X=0\) on the vacuum locus, and the
  unbroken finite gauge group is
  \[
    \mu_d=\{\zeta\in U(1):\zeta^d=1\}.
  \]
  Fluctuations of the \(X_i\) are therefore organized as a Landau--Ginzburg
  model with polynomial \(G_d(X)\), together with the residual finite
  \(\mu_d\) gauge quotient.
\item At \(r=0\), the point \(X=P=0\) obeys the \(D\)-term equation and the
  gauge quotient is singular.  A definition of the infrared QFT at the phase
  boundary requires additional continuum data beyond the chamber
  calculation.
\end{enumerate}

The \(F\)-term equations of
\eqref{eq:hypersurface-glsm-superpotential} are
\begin{equation}
  G_d(X)=0,\qquad P\,\partial_iG_d(X)=0,\quad i=1,\ldots,N.
  \label{eq:hypersurface-glsm-f-terms}
\end{equation}
The \(D\)-term equation is
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i=1}^N|X_i|^2-d|P|^2=r.
  \label{eq:hypersurface-glsm-d-term}
\end{equation}

If \(r>0\), equation~\eqref{eq:hypersurface-glsm-d-term} forces
\(X\neq0\).  On the locus \(G_d(X)=0\), smoothness of \(Y_G\) implies that
the gradient \((\partial_iG_d(X))\) is not the zero vector.  Hence
\eqref{eq:hypersurface-glsm-f-terms} forces \(P=0\).  Quotienting nonzero
\(X\) by the charge-one \(U(1)\) action gives \([X]\in\mathbb P^{N-1}\), and
the remaining equation is \(G_d(X)=0\).

If \(r<0\), equation~\eqref{eq:hypersurface-glsm-d-term} forces \(P\neq0\).
Then \eqref{eq:hypersurface-glsm-f-terms} gives
\(\partial_iG_d(X)=0\) for all \(i\).  Homogeneity gives Euler's identity
\[
  d\,G_d(X)=\sum_{i=1}^N X_i\partial_iG_d(X),
\]
so \(G_d(X)=0\) follows as well.  By the smoothness assumption, the common
zero of \(G_d\) and all \(\partial_iG_d\) is \(X=0\).  A gauge
transformation \(\zeta\in U(1)\) preserves a nonzero value of \(P\) exactly
when \(\zeta^{-d}=1\), equivalently \(\zeta\in\mu_d\).  The transverse
coordinates are the \(X_i\), and the residual superpotential is \(G_d(X)\).

At \(r=0\), substituting \(X=0\) and \(P=0\) into
\eqref{eq:hypersurface-glsm-d-term} gives \(0=0\), and the \(F\)-term
equations are also satisfied.  The whole \(U(1)\) gauge group fixes this
point, so the quotient contains a positive-dimensional stabilizer stratum.
That stabilizer stratum is the singular locus of the classical quotient at
the phase boundary.

\paragraph{Adjunction, central charge, and the residual finite gauge theory.}
The smooth degree-\(d\) hypersurface
\[
  Y_G\subset\mathbb P^{N-1}
\]
has complex dimension \(N-2\).  Let
\[
  H=c_1(\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^{N-1}}(1)).
\]
The normal bundle of \(Y_G\) in \(\mathbb P^{N-1}\) is
\[
  N_{Y_G/\mathbb P^{N-1}}\cong \mathcal O(d)|_{Y_G}.
\]
The exact sequence
\[
  0\longrightarrow TY_G
  \longrightarrow T\mathbb P^{N-1}|_{Y_G}
  \longrightarrow \mathcal O(d)|_{Y_G}
  \longrightarrow0
\]
gives
\begin{equation}
  c_1(TY_G)=(N-d)H|_{Y_G},
  \qquad
  K_{Y_G}\cong \mathcal O(d-N)|_{Y_G}.
  \label{eq:hypersurface-adjunction-c1}
\end{equation}
Thus the topological Calabi--Yau condition in this hypersurface family is
\[
  d=N .
\]
In that case the large-volume sigma-model central-charge target is
\begin{equation}
  c_{\rm sigma}=3\dim_{\mathbb C}Y_G=3(N-2),
  \label{eq:hypersurface-sigma-central-charge-target}
\end{equation}
provided the infrared fixed point exists and realizes the standard
\(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) superconformal stress tensor.

On the Landau--Ginzburg side, the Fermat reference polynomial
\[
  G_d(X)=X_1^d+\cdots+X_N^d
\]
has quasihomogeneous weights \(q_i=1/d\), hence the protected
Landau--Ginzburg central-charge calculation gives
\begin{equation}
  c_{\rm LG}
  =
  3\sum_{i=1}^N\left(1-\frac{2}{d}\right)
  =
  3N\left(1-\frac{2}{d}\right).
  \label{eq:hypersurface-lg-central-charge}
\end{equation}
The equality
\[
  c_{\rm LG}=3(N-2)
\]
holds precisely when \(d=N\).  This is a protected numerical consistency
condition linking the two chambers.  The equality of central charges is one
piece of data in an infrared comparison; the equivalence of continuum QFTs
also requires the operator algebra, stress tensor, state space, and extended
sectors to be constructed and matched.

The finite group in the \(r<0\) chamber is an actual residual gauge group.
Choose a gauge in which the nonzero field \(P\) is fixed to a positive real
constant.  A gauge transformation \(\zeta\in U(1)\) preserves this gauge
choice exactly when
\[
  \zeta^{-d}=1.
\]
Therefore \(\zeta\in\mu_d\), and the remaining chiral fields transform as
\[
  X_i\longmapsto \zeta X_i .
\]
Homogeneity gives
\[
  G_d(\zeta X)=\zeta^dG_d(X)=G_d(X),
\]
so the Landau--Ginzburg superpotential descends to the finite gauge
quotient.  The finite-gauge QFT contains the invariant untwisted local
operators together with twisted sectors determined by the finite gauge
theory.  The invariant part of \(\operatorname{Jac}(G_d)\) is therefore only
one protected component of the orbifold theory; a full state-space
comparison must include the sectors created by the \(\mu_d\) defect lines.
For the Fermat Jacobi basis
\[
  X_1^{a_1}\cdots X_N^{a_N},
  \qquad 0\leq a_i\leq d-2,
\]
the untwisted invariant condition is
\[
  a_1+\cdots+a_N\equiv0\pmod d .
\]

\paragraph{Coulomb coordinate for the phase-boundary signal.}
For the charge vector \((1,\ldots,1,-d)\), the one-loop Coulomb equation
\eqref{eq:abelian-glsm-coulomb-exponential-critical-equation} becomes
\begin{equation}
  \left(\frac{\sigma}{\mu}\right)^N
  \left(\frac{-d\sigma}{\mu}\right)^{-d}_{\rm branch}
  =
  \ee^t .
  \label{eq:hypersurface-coulomb-charge-vector-equation}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  \left(\frac{\sigma}{\mu}\right)^{N-d}
  (-d)^{-d}_{\rm branch}
  =
  \ee^t .
  \label{eq:hypersurface-coulomb-power-equation}
\end{equation}
The subscript records the chosen logarithm branch for the negatively charged
field.  In the anomaly-free case \(d=N\), the \(\sigma\)-dependence cancels
and the local Coulomb chart selects the branch-dependent condition
\begin{equation}
  \ee^t=C_{\rm branch},
  \qquad
  C_{\rm branch}=(-N)^{-N}_{\rm branch}.
  \label{eq:hypersurface-coulomb-singular-coordinate}
\end{equation}
In the additive local FI coordinate
\[
  T=t-\log C_{\rm branch},
\]
the condition is \(T=0\).  At this value the one-loop large-\(\sigma\)
twisted superpotential has a critical direction rather than isolated local
Coulomb vacua.  This is the protected Coulomb-coordinate signal associated
with the chamber boundary.  The continuum behavior at that coordinate is a
separate infrared construction problem.

\begin{example}[The quintic GLSM as intrinsic two-dimensional QFT data]

Set \(N=d=5\).  The charges are

  (1,1,1,1,1,-5),
\]
so the axial anomaly coefficient

  \quad\text{inside}\quad

For \(r<0\), the residual finite gauge group is \(\mu_5\), and the local

statements about chambers of a two-dimensional supersymmetric gauge theory.
The associated infrared equivalence claim requires a two-dimensional
continuum construction and a comparison of its protected and unprotected
operator data.
\end{example}

\section{Infrared Comparison Problem}

For each two-dimensional supersymmetric model one must distinguish:
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{UV regulator},\qquad
  \text{supersymmetric Wilsonian flow},\\
  \text{protected operator algebra},\qquad
  \text{full infrared QFT}.
\end{gathered}
\]
Protected rings and indices can match before the full Hilbert space,
correlators, and stress tensor are identified.  A complete infrared
equivalence therefore requires a map of local algebras, states, stress
tensors, supercharges, and extended operators, with convergence or scaling
limits stated.

\begin{openproblem}[Two-dimensional supersymmetric RG construction]
\label{op:two-dimensional-susy-rg-construction}
Construct \(2D\) \(\mathcal N=(2,2)\) GLSM flows to Landau--Ginzburg and
hypersurface sigma-model fixed points with a regulator, supersymmetric
Wilsonian maps, protected-ring comparison, stress-tensor convergence, and
Hilbert-space reconstruction in one declared framework.  The target-space
terminology here refers only to an intrinsic two-dimensional QFT comparison
between continuum limits.
\end{openproblem}
