\chapter{Quantum Spectral Curve and Hexagon Form Factors}
\label{ch:planar-n4-quantum-spectral-curve-hexagon}

\ConventionsLorentzian

The mirror TBA is exact but highly redundant.  The quantum spectral curve
compresses the same spectral information into a finite system of analytic
functions.  This chapter states the \(P\mu\)-system, explains its weak-coupling
reduction to Baxter equations, and records the separate hexagon framework for
three-point functions.

\section{Analytic T-Hook Gauge}

Before introducing the \(P\mu\)-system it is useful to isolate the analytic
T-hook step that makes the later two-row Wronskians plausible.  The input is
not the full QSC; it is the analytic Y-system data of
Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-mirror-tba-y-system} together with a gauge choice for
Hirota \(T\)-functions.

\begin{assumption}[One-row analytic T-gauge]
\label{ass:qsc-one-row-t-gauge}
Use the Hirota equation
\[
  T_{a,s}^+T_{a,s}^-
  =
  T_{a+1,s}T_{a-1,s}+T_{a,s+1}T_{a,s-1},
  \qquad f^\pm(u)=f(u\pm\ii/2).
\]
In a horizontal wing of the planar \(\mathcal N=4\) T-hook, choose a gauge
with
\[
  \mathcal T_{0,m}=1,\qquad
  \mathcal T_{1,m}\in\mathcal A_m,\qquad
  \mathcal T_{1,m}(u)\to m\quad (u\to\infty),
  \qquad m\geq1,
\]
where \(\mathcal A_m=\{|\operatorname{Im}u|<m/2\}\) with the prescribed
Zhukovsky cuts removed.  Assume that the discontinuity data of the two
fermionic nodes can be represented by a real density \(\rho\) through Cauchy
transforms
\[
  G(u)=-\int\frac{\dd v}{2\pi\ii}\frac{\rho(v)}{u-v},
  \qquad \operatorname{Im}u>0,
  \qquad
  \overline G(u)=+\int\frac{\dd v}{2\pi\ii}\frac{\rho(v)}{u-v},
  \qquad \operatorname{Im}u<0.
\]
The gauge is normalized so that
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal T_{1,m}
  =
  m+G^{[m]}+\overline G^{[-m]},
  \qquad m\geq1.
  \label{eq:qsc-t-gauge-one-row}
\end{equation}
\end{assumption}

This assumption is deliberately stated as an analytic reconstruction datum.  It
summarizes the information normally hidden in the phrase ``choose the
\(\mathcal T\)-gauge'': asymptotics, analyticity strips, and the branch on which
the Cauchy transforms are evaluated.

\begin{proposition}[Hirota factorization in the one-row T-gauge]
\label{prop:qsc-t-gauge-hirota-factorization}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-one-row-t-gauge}, the Hirota equation at
\((1,m)\), \(m\geq2\), gives
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal T_{2,m}
  =
  \left(1+G^{[m+1]}-G^{[m-1]}\right)
  \left(1+\overline G^{[-m-1]}-\overline G^{[-m+1]}\right).
  \label{eq:qsc-t-gauge-two-row-factorization}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(\mathcal T_{0,m}=1\), Hirota gives
\[
  \mathcal T_{2,m}
  =
  \mathcal T_{1,m}^+\mathcal T_{1,m}^-
  -
  \mathcal T_{1,m+1}\mathcal T_{1,m-1}.
\]
Put
\[
  a=G^{[m+1]},\quad b=G^{[m-1]},\quad
  c=\overline G^{[-m-1]},\quad d=\overline G^{[-m+1]}.
\]
Then the right-hand side is
\[
  (m+a+d)(m+b+c)-(m+1+a+c)(m-1+b+d).
\]
Expanding and collecting terms gives
\[
  1+a-b+c-d+(a-b)(c-d)=(1+a-b)(1+c-d),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:qsc-t-gauge-two-row-factorization}.
\end{proof}

The analytic Y-system condition
\(Y_{2,\pm2}(u+\ii0)=1/Y_{1,\pm1}(u-\ii0)\) across the mirror cut forces the
upper and lower Cauchy transforms to be opposite boundary values on that cut:
\[
  G(u+\ii0)=-\overline G(u-\ii0).
\]
Consequently the density is supported on the short cut in the magic-sheet
presentation.  Let \(\widehat G\) denote the single-cut continuation which
equals \(G\) in the upper half-plane and \(-\overline G\) in the lower
half-plane.  Then
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\mathcal T}_{1,m}
  =
  m+\widehat G^{[m]}-\widehat G^{[-m]},
  \qquad
  \widehat{\mathcal T}_{2,m}
  =
  \widehat{\mathcal T}_{1,1}^{[m]}
  \widehat{\mathcal T}_{1,1}^{[-m]},
  \qquad m\geq2.
  \label{eq:qsc-magic-sheet-t-gauge}
\end{equation}
The hats emphasize that these are not new functions on the same sheet but
analytic continuations through the Zhukovsky cut.  In the strip
\(|\operatorname{Im}u|<m/2\), \(\widehat{\mathcal T}_{1,m}\) agrees with the
original \(\mathcal T_{1,m}\).  Outside that strip, the equality is a statement
about a chosen continuation path.  The \(P\mu\)-system below is a finite
Riemann--Hilbert compression of this analytically continued T-hook data.

\section{Pmu System}

Let \(u\in\C\) be the spectral parameter.  The quantum spectral curve uses four
functions \(P_a(u)\), \(a=1,\ldots,4\), and an antisymmetric matrix
\(\mu_{ab}(u)=-\mu_{ba}(u)\).  The functions \(P_a\) have one short branch
cut, conventionally on \([-2g,2g]\), on their principal sheet.  Analytic
continuation through this cut is denoted by a tilde.  Introduce
\[
  P^a=\chi^{ab}P_b,
\]
where \(\chi^{ab}\) is the fixed antisymmetric tensor with nonzero entries
chosen so that \(P_aP^a=0\).

The \(P\mu\)-system is
\begin{align}
  \widetilde P_a(u)&=\mu_{ab}(u)P^b(u),
  \label{eq:planar-n4-pmu-one}\\
  \widetilde\mu_{ab}(u)-\mu_{ab}(u)
  &=
  P_a(u)\widetilde P_b(u)-P_b(u)\widetilde P_a(u),
  \label{eq:planar-n4-pmu-two}\\
  \mu_{ab}(u+\ii)&=\widetilde\mu_{ab}(u).
  \label{eq:planar-n4-pmu-period}
\end{align}
The normalization is completed by the Pfaffian condition
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Pf}\mu
  =
  \mu_{12}\mu_{34}-\mu_{13}\mu_{24}+\mu_{14}\mu_{23}
  =
  1,
  \label{eq:planar-n4-pmu-pfaffian}
\end{equation}
up to the standard \(P\)-basis gauge transformations.  The tilde operation is
analytic continuation through the short cut of the corresponding function;
it is not complex conjugation.  On real states one imposes an additional
reality or gluing condition relating the two sheets.

\begin{definition}[QSC Riemann--Hilbert datum]
\label{def:planar-n4-qsc-rh-datum}
Within the planar integrability framework, a \(P\mu\) Riemann--Hilbert datum
consists of the spectral plane with parameter \(u\in\mathbb C\), four
single-short-cut functions \(P_a\), an antisymmetric matrix
\(\mu_{ab}\), the fixed antisymmetric tensor \(\chi^{ab}\), and the
continuation operation \(\widetilde{\phantom P}\) through the short cut
\([-2g,2g]\).  The datum is required to satisfy
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-one}--\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-period},
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-pfaffian}, regularity on the defining sheet, and
large-\(u\) asymptotics specifying the global charges.  A physical solution
adds state-dependent gluing conditions and cyclic single-trace constraints.
\end{definition}

This is a Riemann--Hilbert problem, not a finite collection of ordinary
functions on one copy of the plane.  The data above tell the reader both what
is evaluated on the defining sheet and how the functions are continued to
the neighboring sheets.

\begin{assumption}[Two-row T-hook reconstruction datum]
\label{ass:qsc-two-row-t-hook-reconstruction}
Use the shift notation
\[
  f^{[n]}(u)=f(u+n\ii/2).
\]
In the physical or magic T-hook gauge, assume that the two horizontal rows
adjacent to the central node are reconstructed from two single-short-cut
functions \(P_1,P_2\) by
\[
  \mathbb T_{1,m}
  =
  P_1^{[m]}P_2^{[-m]}-P_2^{[m]}P_1^{[-m]},
  \qquad m\geq1,
  \label{eq:qsc-two-row-wronskian}
\]
and that Hirota continuation extends this expression to the central nodes.
Let
\[
  \mathbb T_{0,1}=\mu_{12}^2,\qquad
  \mathbb T_{3,2}=\mathbb T_{2,3}\mu_{12}.
\]
The functions \(\mathbb T_{2,1}\) and \(\mathbb T_{1,0}\) are assumed to be
regular across the central short cut in the strip where the following
calculation is made.  Finally, the Wronskian
\[
  D=P_1^{[+2]}P_2^{[-2]}-P_2^{[+2]}P_1^{[-2]}
\]
is not identically zero; any isolated zeros are handled by analytic
continuation of the resulting identities.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Local T-hook algebra behind the \(P\mu\) bridge]
\label{prop:qsc-local-t-hook-pmu-bridge}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-two-row-t-hook-reconstruction}, regularity of
the T-hook functions across the central short cut forces
\[
  \widetilde\mu_{12}-\mu_{12}
  =
  P_1\widetilde P_2-P_2\widetilde P_1.
  \label{eq:qsc-local-mu12-discontinuity}
\]
Moreover
\[
  \mathbb T_{1,0}=\mu_{12}\widetilde\mu_{12},
  \qquad
  Y_{1,1}Y_{2,2}
  =
  \frac{\widetilde\mu_{12}}{\mu_{12}}
  =
  1+
  \frac{P_1\widetilde P_2-P_2\widetilde P_1}{\mu_{12}}.
  \label{eq:qsc-local-y11-y22-bridge}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write \(P_a^+=P_a^{[+2]}\), \(P_a^-=P_a^{[-2]}\), and
\[
  D=P_1^+P_2^- - P_2^+P_1^- .
\]
The Hirota equation at the \((2,2)\) square, expressed through the
Wronskians in \eqref{eq:qsc-two-row-wronskian}, gives in the upper
half-plane sector
\[
  \mathbb T_{2,1}
  =
  (\widetilde P_1P_2^- - \widetilde P_2P_1^-)
  (P_1^+P_2-P_2^+P_1)
  -\mu_{12}D .
\]
Continuing across the central cut replaces \(P_1,P_2,\mu_{12}\) in the
unshifted slot by their tilded values, so the same node is
\[
  \widetilde{\mathbb T}_{2,1}
  =
  (P_1P_2^- - P_2P_1^-)
  (P_1^+\widetilde P_2-P_2^+\widetilde P_1)
  -\widetilde\mu_{12}D .
\]
Since \(\mathbb T_{2,1}\) is regular in this strip, these two expressions
are equal.  Moving the \(\mu\)-terms to one side gives
\[
  (\mu_{12}-\widetilde\mu_{12})D
  =
  A-B,
\]
where
\[
\begin{aligned}
  A&=(\widetilde P_1P_2^- - \widetilde P_2P_1^-)
    (P_1^+P_2-P_2^+P_1),\\
  B&=(P_1P_2^- - P_2P_1^-)
    (P_1^+\widetilde P_2-P_2^+\widetilde P_1).
\end{aligned}
\]
Expanding both products and canceling pairwise gives the Pluecker identity
\[
  A-B=(\widetilde P_1P_2-\widetilde P_2P_1)D.
\]
Because \(D\) is not identically zero, division by \(D\) on the open set
where it is nonzero gives
\[
  \mu_{12}-\widetilde\mu_{12}
  =
  \widetilde P_1P_2-\widetilde P_2P_1,
\]
and analyticity extends the identity through isolated zeros of \(D\).  This
is \eqref{eq:qsc-local-mu12-discontinuity}.

The \((1,1)\) Hirota square gives the central-node relation
\[
  \frac{\mathbb T_{1,0}}{\mu_{12}}
  =
  \mu_{12}+\widetilde P_2P_1-\widetilde P_1P_2 .
\]
Using the discontinuity identity just proved, the right side is
\(\widetilde\mu_{12}\), hence
\(\mathbb T_{1,0}=\mu_{12}\widetilde\mu_{12}\).  In this gauge
\[
  Y_{1,1}Y_{2,2}
  =
  \frac{\mathbb T_{1,0}}{\mathbb T_{0,1}}
  =
  \frac{\mu_{12}\widetilde\mu_{12}}{\mu_{12}^2},
\]
which proves the first equality in
\eqref{eq:qsc-local-y11-y22-bridge}.  The second equality is the same
statement after substituting
\eqref{eq:qsc-local-mu12-discontinuity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}[Fermionic-product energy normalization]
\label{ass:qsc-t-hook-charge-bridge}
Work in the analytic T-hook gauge of
Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-mirror-tba-y-system}, and consider an \(SL(2)\)
single-trace state of twist \(J\), Lorentz spin \(S\), and total physical
momentum zero.  Let \(Y_{1,1}\) and \(Y_{2,2}\) denote the two fermionic
T-hook nodes adjacent to the central strip in this gauge.  The local
reconstruction algebra in
Proposition~\ref{prop:qsc-local-t-hook-pmu-bridge} supplies the \(P\mu\)
expression for \(Y_{1,1}Y_{2,2}\).  The remaining framework input is the
energy normalization inherited from the excited-state mirror TBA:
\[
  \log\bigl(Y_{1,1}(u)Y_{2,2}(u)\bigr)
  =
  \frac{\ii(\Delta-J)}{u}+O(u^{-2}),
  \qquad u\to\infty
\]
in the upper-half-plane sector used for the \(SL(2)\) large-\(u\)
asymptotics.
\end{assumption}

\begin{lemma}[Dimension exponent of \(\mu_{12}\)]
\label{lem:qsc-mu12-dimension-exponent}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-t-hook-charge-bridge} and the \(P\mu\)
equations,
\[
  Y_{1,1}(u)Y_{2,2}(u)
  =
  \frac{\mu_{12}(u+\ii)}{\mu_{12}(u)}.
  \label{eq:qsc-y11-y22-mu-ratio}
\]
If \(\mu_{12}\) has power-law large-\(u\) behavior on this sheet, then
\[
  \mu_{12}(u)\sim u^{\Delta-J}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The \((1,2)\) component of the \(P\mu\) discontinuity equation gives
\[
  \widetilde\mu_{12}(u)-\mu_{12}(u)
  =
  P_1(u)\widetilde P_2(u)-P_2(u)\widetilde P_1(u).
\]
Dividing by \(\mu_{12}(u)\) and using the bridge formula
\eqref{eq:qsc-local-y11-y22-bridge} gives
\[
  Y_{1,1}(u)Y_{2,2}(u)
  =
  \frac{\widetilde\mu_{12}(u)}{\mu_{12}(u)}.
\]
Pseudo-periodicity \eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-period} then gives
\eqref{eq:qsc-y11-y22-mu-ratio}.  Now suppose
\(\mu_{12}(u)=C u^\alpha(1+O(u^{-1}))\) with \(C\neq0\).  Then
\[
  \log\frac{\mu_{12}(u+\ii)}{\mu_{12}(u)}
  =
  \alpha\log\left(1+\frac{\ii}{u}\right)+O(u^{-2})
  =
  \frac{\ii\alpha}{u}+O(u^{-2}).
\]
Comparison with the large-\(u\) energy normalization in
Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-t-hook-charge-bridge} gives
\(\alpha=\Delta-J\).
\end{proof}

State quantum numbers also enter through large-\(u\) asymptotics of \(P_a\)
and the remaining \(\mu_{ab}\).  In the \(SL(2)\) sector with twist \(J\),
spin \(S\), and dimension \(\Delta\), these asymptotics encode the charges
\[
  (J,S,\Delta)
\]
through powers and leading coefficients constrained by the algebraic
relations of the QSC.
With the \(SL(2)\) conventions used in the previous chapter, the large-\(u\)
powers of the \(P\)-functions are
\[
  (P_1,P_2,P_3,P_4)
  \sim
  (A_1u^{-J/2},A_2u^{-J/2-1},A_3u^{J/2},A_4u^{J/2-1}).
\]
Together with Lemma~\ref{lem:qsc-mu12-dimension-exponent}, the leading
terms in
\[
  \widetilde P_a=\mu_{ab}P^b,\qquad
  (P^1,P^2,P^3,P^4)=(-P_4,P_3,-P_2,P_1)
\]
then force the remaining \(\mu\)-powers
\[
  (\mu_{12},\mu_{13},\mu_{14},\mu_{24},\mu_{34})
  \sim
  (u^{\Delta-J},u^{\Delta+1},u^\Delta,u^{\Delta-1},u^{\Delta+J}),
\]
provided the leading terms do not undergo accidental cancellations.  For
example, the three contributions
\[
  \mu_{14}P_1,\qquad \mu_{13}P_2,\qquad \mu_{12}P_3
\]
to \(\widetilde P_1\) all scale as \(u^{\Delta-J/2}\); this fixes the
relative powers of \(\mu_{13}\) and \(\mu_{14}\) once the powers of \(P_a\)
and \(\mu_{12}\) are known.  The same balance applied to
\(\widetilde P_2,\widetilde P_3,\widetilde P_4\) gives the displayed list.

\begin{assumption}[Large-\(u\) QSC characteristic roots]
\label{ass:qsc-large-u-characteristic-roots}
Let
\[
  X=A_1A_4,\qquad Y=A_2A_3 .
\]
After inserting the \(SL(2)\) large-\(u\) powers into the one-step
monodromy recursion for \(\mu\), the leading system for the five independent
\(\mu_{ab}\) components has the characteristic equation
\[
  \Phi(\alpha)
  =
  (J+1)^2\alpha^2
  -4\ii J(J+1)Y
  -
  \Bigl(
    \alpha^2+J-\ii(J+1)Y+\ii(J-1)X
  \Bigr)^2
  =
  0 .
  \label{eq:qsc-large-u-characteristic-polynomial}
\]
The physical dimension root is \(\alpha=\Delta\).  The representation-theory
input fixing the second nonzero root is
\(\alpha=S-1\), where \(S\) is the Lorentz spin of the \(SL(2)\) state.
\end{assumption}

This assumption is not a path-integral theorem.  It is the large-\(u\)
characteristic form of the QSC gluing/asymptotic data in this gauge.  The
local algebra below is included because it is a common source of sign errors:
the products \(A_1A_4\) and \(A_2A_3\) carry the sign convention for
\(\chi\), for \(f^{[\pm1]}(u)=f(u\pm\ii/2)\), and for the short-cut sheet.

\begin{proposition}[Large-\(u\) coefficient products]
\label{prop:qsc-large-u-coefficient-products}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-large-u-characteristic-roots}, the leading
coefficient products are
\[
\begin{aligned}
  A_1A_4
  &=
  \ii\,
  \frac{((J+S-2)^2-\Delta^2)((J-S)^2-\Delta^2)}
       {16J(J-1)},\\
  A_2A_3
  &=
  \ii\,
  \frac{((J-S+2)^2-\Delta^2)((J+S)^2-\Delta^2)}
       {16J(J+1)}.
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Put \(\beta=S-1\).  Since \(\Phi(\Delta)=\Phi(\beta)=0\), subtracting the
two equations cancels the term proportional to \(Y\) and gives
\[
  \Delta^2+\beta^2+2J+2U=(J+1)^2,
  \qquad
  U=-\ii(J+1)Y+\ii(J-1)X,
\]
as long as \(\Delta^2\neq\beta^2\); the degenerate cases are obtained by
continuity.  Thus
\[
  U=\frac{J^2+1-\Delta^2-\beta^2}{2}.
\]
Substituting this value in \(\Phi(\Delta)=0\) gives
\[
  4\ii J(J+1)Y
  =
  (J+1)^2\Delta^2
  -
  \frac14\left(\Delta^2+(J+1)^2-\beta^2\right)^2 .
\]
Now
\[
  (J+1)^2-\beta^2=(J-S+2)(J+S),
\]
and the elementary factorization
\[
  4(J+1)^2\Delta^2
  -
  \left(\Delta^2+(J-S+2)(J+S)\right)^2
  =
  -\bigl(\Delta^2-(J-S+2)^2\bigr)
   \bigl(\Delta^2-(J+S)^2\bigr)
\]
gives the displayed formula for \(A_2A_3=Y\).  The formula for
\(A_1A_4=X\) follows from
\[
  \ii(J-1)X=U+\ii(J+1)Y
\]
and the same factorization with
\((J-S+2,J+S)\) replaced by \((J-S,J+S-2)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Consistency of the \(P\mu\) discontinuity]
\label{lem:planar-n4-pmu-pfaffian-consistency}
The discontinuity equation
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-two} preserves the Pfaffian normalization
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-pfaffian} if the \(P\)-functions satisfy
\[
  P_aP^a=0 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Use the rank-two Pfaffian identity
\[
  \operatorname{Pf}(M+v\wedge w)
  =
  \operatorname{Pf}(M)\bigl(1+w^TM^{-1}v\bigr)
\]
for an invertible antisymmetric \(4\times4\) matrix \(M\).  Here
\(M=\mu\), \(v=P\), and \(w=\widetilde P=\mu P^\sharp\), where
\((P^\sharp)^a=P^a=\chi^{ab}P_b\).  Hence
\[
  w^T\mu^{-1}v
  =
  (\mu P^\sharp)^T\mu^{-1}P
  =
  -(P^\sharp)^TP
  =
  -P^aP_a
  =
  0,
\]
because \(\chi^{ab}\) is antisymmetric.  Therefore crossing the cut does not
change \(\operatorname{Pf}\mu\), so the normalization can be imposed on one
sheet and transported consistently.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Monodromy recursion for \(\mu\)]
\label{lem:planar-n4-pmu-monodromy-recursion}
The \(P\mu\) equations imply the one-step continuation formula
\[
  \mu_{ab}(u+\ii)
  =
  \mu_{ab}(u)
  +P_a(u)\mu_{bc}(u)P^c(u)
  -P_b(u)\mu_{ac}(u)P^c(u).
  \label{eq:planar-n4-pmu-monodromy-recursion}
\]
Consequently, once the functions in one strip and the continued \(P\)-sheet
are known, the shifted \(\mu\)-sheets are generated recursively by algebraic
substitution.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Use the pseudo-periodicity equation
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-period} and the discontinuity equation
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-two}:
\[
  \mu_{ab}(u+\ii)-\mu_{ab}(u)
  =
  P_a(u)\widetilde P_b(u)-P_b(u)\widetilde P_a(u).
\]
Substituting \(\widetilde P_b=\mu_{bc}P^c\) from
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-one} gives
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-monodromy-recursion}.  Applying the same identity at
successively shifted arguments gives the recursion through the tower of
short cuts.  The lemma is algebraic; it does not by itself prove that the
regularity, gluing, and large-\(u\) conditions select a unique physical
solution.
\end{proof}

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Quantum spectral curve spectral problem]
\label{qthm:planar-n4-qsc-status}
Within the planar \(\mathcal N=4\) integrability framework, a physical
single-trace state is specified by QSC analyticity, gluing, asymptotic, and
regularity data.  Solving the resulting \(P\mu\)-system determines the exact
planar scaling dimension of the state as a function of \(\lambda\).
\end{quotedtheorem}

The theorem is quoted with its framework stated.  The QSC is a powerful exact
solution of the planar integrability system; it is not presently a
constructive definition of four-dimensional \(\mathcal N=4\) SYM.  In this
chapter, the algebraic consequences of the \(P\mu\) equations are derived
when feasible: Pfaffian preservation, monodromy recursion, the weak-coupling
Baxter degeneration, and the one-loop dimension extraction below.  The
existence of the single-short-cut \(P\)-functions, the normalization of the
discontinuity coefficient in \eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-two}, the physical
gluing conditions, and the equivalence to the exact planar gauge-theory
spectrum remain QSC framework inputs.

\section{Q-Omega System and Gauge Choices}

The \(P\mu\)-system is one gauge for the QSC.  A dual description uses four
functions \(Q_i(u)\), \(i=1,\ldots,4\), and an antisymmetric matrix
\(\omega_{ij}(u)\).  In the standard convention the \(Q_i\) have long cuts,
while the \(P_a\) have one short cut on the principal sheet.  The equations
mirror the \(P\mu\) system:
\[
  \widetilde Q_i=\omega_{ij}Q^j,\qquad
  \widetilde\omega_{ij}-\omega_{ij}
  =
  Q_i\widetilde Q_j-Q_j\widetilde Q_i,
  \qquad
  \omega_{ij}(u+\ii)=\widetilde\omega_{ij}(u).
\]
The two descriptions are related by the full \(Q_{A|I}\) system of
finite-difference relations.  The useful point for this monograph is not the
formal symmetry itself but the bookkeeping: choosing short cuts for \(P_a\)
and long cuts for \(Q_i\) is an analytic gauge.  Physical charges are read
from large-\(u\) asymptotics only after this gauge and the gluing conditions
are fixed.

For real left-right symmetric states, the gluing conditions may be written,
after a basis choice, as relations between \(\widetilde P_a(u)\) and complex
conjugates of \(P_a(u)\) on the real axis outside the cut.  These conditions
select the physical solution among many formal solutions of the same
finite-difference equations.  They play the same role as contour data in the
mirror TBA.

\section{Weak-Coupling Baxter Limit}

At weak coupling the short cut collapses to the origin in the rescaled
spectral plane.  In the \(SL(2)\) sector, the QSC reduces to a finite-difference
Baxter equation for a polynomial \(Q(u)\) whose zeros are one-loop Bethe roots.
The equation has the form
\begin{equation}
  (u+\ii/2)^JQ(u+\ii)
  +(u-\ii/2)^JQ(u-\ii)
  =
  T(u)Q(u),
  \label{eq:planar-n4-qsc-baxter-limit}
\end{equation}
where \(T(u)\) is a degree-\(J\) transfer polynomial fixed by charges and
regularity.  If
\[
  Q(u)=\prod_{k=1}^S(u-u_k),
\]
then evaluating \eqref{eq:planar-n4-qsc-baxter-limit} at \(u=u_j\) gives
\[
  \left(\frac{u_j+\ii/2}{u_j-\ii/2}\right)^J
  =
  -\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\neq j}}^S
  \frac{u_j-u_k-\ii}{u_j-u_k+\ii},
\]
which is the one-loop \(SL(2)\) Bethe equation in this convention.  Thus the
QSC contains the one-loop spin chain as its weak-coupling degeneration.

\begin{assumption}[Weak-coupling regularity of the \(SL(2)\) QSC]
\label{ass:qsc-weak-coupling-regularity}
Fix an \(SL(2)\) state of twist \(J\in\mathbb Z_{>0}\), Lorentz spin
\(S\in\mathbb Z_{\geq0}\), and dimension
\(\Delta=J+S+O(g^2)\).  In the weak-coupling degeneration of the
\(P\mu\)-system assume the following analytic data.
\[
  P_2P_3=O(g^2),\qquad
  P_1(u)=A_1u^{-J/2}+O(g^2),
\]
and \(P_4/P_1\) tends to a polynomial of degree \(J-1\).  Moreover
\[
  \mu_{12}(u+\ii/2)=Q(u)+g^2R(u)+O(g^4),
\]
where \(Q(u)\) is a degree-\(S\) polynomial and the singular part of \(R\) is
produced only by the short cuts \([-2g,2g]+\ii n\) collapsing to the points
\(\ii n\).  The defining sheet has no additional poles.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Baxter equation from QSC degeneration]
\label{prop:qsc-baxter-degeneration}
Assume that in the weak-coupling limit the QSC functions in the \(SL(2)\)
sector reduce so that the ratio of two independent \(P\)-functions is a
degree-\(S\) polynomial \(Q(u)\), and that \(\mu\)-periodicity becomes a
finite-difference relation with shifts \(\pm\ii\).  Then regularity at the
zeros of \(Q\) gives the one-loop \(SL(2)\) Bethe equations.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under the stated degeneration, the finite-difference relation is
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-qsc-baxter-limit}.  The right side is regular at every
zero \(u_j\) of \(Q\), so the left side must vanish there:
\[
  (u_j+\ii/2)^JQ(u_j+\ii)
  +(u_j-\ii/2)^JQ(u_j-\ii)=0.
\]
Dividing by \((u_j-\ii/2)^JQ(u_j-\ii)\) and using
\[
  \frac{Q(u_j+\ii)}{Q(u_j-\ii)}
  =
  -\prod_{\substack{k=1\\k\neq j}}^S
  \frac{u_j-u_k+\ii}{u_j-u_k-\ii}
\]
gives the displayed Bethe equation.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Half-integer digamma primitive]
\label{lem:qsc-half-integer-digamma-primitive}
Let
\[
  \Phi(u)=
  \psi\!\left(\frac12-\ii u\right)
  +\psi\!\left(\frac12+\ii u\right),
  \qquad \psi=\Gamma'/\Gamma .
\]
As a meromorphic function of \(u\), \(\Phi\) has only simple poles at
\[
  u=\pm\ii\left(n+\frac12\right),\qquad n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0}.
\]
It satisfies the one-step shift identities
\[
  \Phi(u+\ii)-\Phi(u)=\frac{2\ii}{u+\ii/2},
  \qquad
  \Phi(u)-\Phi(u-\ii)=\frac{2\ii}{u-\ii/2},
  \label{eq:qsc-digamma-primitive-shift-defects}
\]
and hence the central second-difference identity
\[
  \Phi(u+\ii)-2\Phi(u)+\Phi(u-\ii)
  =
  \frac{2}{u^2+1/4}.
  \label{eq:qsc-digamma-primitive-second-difference}
\]
Moreover, if \(G\) is another meromorphic function with the same two
one-step defects in \eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-primitive-shift-defects}, then
\(G-\Phi\) is \(\ii\)-periodic wherever both functions are holomorphic.  If
this difference extends to an entire function bounded on a vertical
fundamental strip \(0\leq \operatorname{Im}u\leq1\), then \(G-\Phi\) is
constant.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The pole statement follows from the poles of \(\psi\) at
\(0,-1,-2,\ldots\).  The recurrence relation
\[
  \psi(z+1)=\psi(z)+\frac1z
\]
gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \Phi(u+\ii)
  &=
  \psi\!\left(\frac32-\ii u\right)
  +\psi\!\left(-\frac12+\ii u\right)  \\
  &=
  \psi\!\left(\frac12-\ii u\right)
  +\frac{1}{1/2-\ii u}
  +\psi\!\left(\frac12+\ii u\right)
  -\frac{1}{-1/2+\ii u}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Since \(1/2-\ii u=-\ii(u+\ii/2)\), this is the first identity in
\eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-primitive-shift-defects}.  The second identity is the
same calculation with \(u\) replaced by \(u-\ii\), or equivalently with the
two digamma arguments interchanged.  Subtracting the two one-step identities
gives \eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-primitive-second-difference}.

If \(G\) has the same one-step defects, then
\[
  (G-\Phi)(u+\ii)=(G-\Phi)(u)
\]
on the common holomorphy domain.  If this difference is entire and bounded
on a vertical fundamental strip, periodicity bounds it on all of
\(\mathbb C\); Liouville's theorem makes it constant.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Collapsed-cut digamma package]
\label{lem:qsc-collapsed-cut-digamma-package}
Let \(Q(u)\) be a polynomial with \(Q(\ii/2)\neq0\), and set
\[
  r_0=Q'(\ii/2)-Q'(-\ii/2).
\]
Define
\[
  R_{\rm sing}(u)
  =
  \ii r_0\,\frac{Q(u)}{Q(\ii/2)}
  \left(
    \psi\!\left(\frac12-\ii u\right)
    +\psi\!\left(\frac12+\ii u\right)
  \right),
  \qquad \psi=\Gamma'/\Gamma .
  \label{eq:qsc-digamma-singular-package}
\]
Then \(R_{\rm sing}\) has only simple poles at
\[
  u=\pm\ii\left(n+\frac12\right),\qquad n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0},
\]
and their residues are
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \operatorname*{Res}_{u=\ii(n+1/2)}R_{\rm sing}
  &=
  -r_0\,\frac{Q(\ii(n+1/2))}{Q(\ii/2)},\\
  \operatorname*{Res}_{u=-\ii(n+1/2)}R_{\rm sing}
  &=
  r_0\,\frac{Q(-\ii(n+1/2))}{Q(\ii/2)} .
\end{aligned}
\]
In particular, if \(Q(\ii/2)=Q(-\ii/2)\), the residues at
\(\ii/2\) and \(-\ii/2\) are \(-r_0\) and \(r_0\).  In the large-\(u\)
sector used for the \(SL(2)\) asymptotics,
\[
  \frac{R_{\rm sing}(u)}{Q(u)}
  =
  2\ii\,\frac{r_0}{Q(\ii/2)}\log u+O(u^{-1}).
\]
Finally, for the meromorphic quotient \(F_{\rm sing}(u)=R_{\rm sing}(u)/Q(u)\),
\[
  F_{\rm sing}(u+\ii)-F_{\rm sing}(u)
  =
  -\frac{2r_0}{Q(\ii/2)(u+\ii/2)},
  \qquad
  F_{\rm sing}(u)-F_{\rm sing}(u-\ii)
  =
  -\frac{2r_0}{Q(\ii/2)(u-\ii/2)} .
  \label{eq:qsc-digamma-singular-shift-defects}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The digamma function has simple poles at \(0,-1,-2,\ldots\), each with
residue \(-1\).  The factor
\(\psi(1/2+\ii u)\) therefore has a pole at
\(u=\ii(n+1/2)\); since
\[
  \frac12+\ii u+n=\ii\bigl(u-\ii(n+1/2)\bigr),
\]
its residue as a function of \(u\) is \(-1/\ii=\ii\).  Multiplication by
the prefactor \(\ii r_0Q(u)/Q(\ii/2)\) gives the first residue formula.
The factor \(\psi(1/2-\ii u)\) gives the second formula in the same way,
because
\[
  \frac12-\ii u+n=-\ii\bigl(u+\ii(n+1/2)\bigr)
\]
and hence the residue in \(u\) is \(-1/(-\ii)=-\ii\).

Finally, Stirling's expansion gives
\[
  \psi(z)=\log z+O(z^{-1})
\]
in sectors avoiding the negative real axis.  Applying this to
\(z=1/2\pm\ii u\) in the chosen large-\(u\) sector gives
\[
  \psi\!\left(\frac12-\ii u\right)
  +\psi\!\left(\frac12+\ii u\right)
  =
  2\log u+O(u^{-1}),
\]
with the branch inherited from the QSC large-\(u\) sheet.  This proves the
logarithmic coefficient.  The quotient shift identities
\eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-singular-shift-defects} follow by multiplying
\eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-primitive-shift-defects} by \(\ii r_0/Q(\ii/2)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[One-loop dimension from the weak QSC]
\label{prop:qsc-one-loop-dimension}
Assume the regular weak-coupling degeneration of
Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-weak-coupling-regularity}, and impose the cyclic
single-trace condition
\[
  \frac{Q(\ii/2)}{Q(-\ii/2)}=1 .
\]
Then the first anomalous-dimension coefficient extracted from the QSC is
\[
  \Delta
  =
  J+S
  +2\ii g^2
  \left.
    \partial_u\log\frac{Q(u+\ii/2)}{Q(u-\ii/2)}
  \right|_{u=0}
  +O(g^4).
\]
Equivalently, if \(Q(u)=\prod_{k=1}^S(u-u_k)\), then
\[
  \Delta
  =
  J+S
  +2g^2\sum_{k=1}^S\frac1{u_k^2+1/4}
  +O(g^4).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The leading \(P_2P_3=O(g^2)\) scaling decouples the
\((\mu_{12},\mu_{24})\) equations of the \(P\mu\)-system.  Eliminating
\(\mu_{24}\), and using the regularity of \(P_1\) and \(P_4/P_1\) in
Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-weak-coupling-regularity}, gives the Baxter equation
of Proposition~\ref{prop:qsc-baxter-degeneration} for
\(Q(u)=\mu_{12}(u+\ii/2)+O(g^2)\).  The same \(i\)-pseudoperiodicity of
\(\mu_{12}\), evaluated at the collapsed branch point, gives the cyclicity
condition \(Q(\ii/2)=Q(-\ii/2)\).

It remains to extract the coefficient of \(g^2\) in \(\Delta\).  Since
\(\mu_{12}(u)\sim u^{\Delta-J}\) and \(Q(u-\ii/2)\sim u^S\), the ratio
\(\mu_{12}(u)/Q(u-\ii/2)\) has the large-\(u\) expansion
\[
  1+(\Delta-J-S)\log u+O(g^4)+O(u^{-1}).
\]
The collapsed square-root cuts force the singular part of the \(g^2\)
correction to \(\mu_{12}(u+\ii/2)\) to be
\eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-singular-package}.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:qsc-collapsed-cut-digamma-package}, this expression has the
prescribed simple poles at the collapsed cuts, with residues fixed by the
branch-point relation and cyclic endpoint condition.  More precisely, after
division by the leading Baxter polynomial, the singular quotient has the
one-step defects \eqref{eq:qsc-digamma-singular-shift-defects}.  These
defects are the collapsed-cut remnant of the square-root decomposition of
\(\mu_{12}\); by Lemma~\ref{lem:qsc-half-integer-digamma-primitive}, they fix
the non-regular meromorphic primitive up to an entire \(\ii\)-periodic
ambiguity.  In the weak-coupling regularity class of
Assumption~\ref{ass:qsc-weak-coupling-regularity}, that ambiguity belongs to
the regular polynomial/asymptotic-power part of \(R\), and it carries no
\(\log u\) term.  Comparing the logarithmic coefficient in
Lemma~\ref{lem:qsc-collapsed-cut-digamma-package} with the large-\(u\)
expansion of \(\mu_{12}/Q\) gives
\[
  \Delta-J-S
  =
  2\ii g^2\,\frac{Q'(\ii/2)-Q'(-\ii/2)}{Q(\ii/2)}+O(g^4).
\]
Using \(Q(\ii/2)=Q(-\ii/2)\) rewrites this as the derivative formula.  Finally
\[
  \ii\left(
  \frac{Q'(\ii/2)}{Q(\ii/2)}
  -
  \frac{Q'(-\ii/2)}{Q(-\ii/2)}
  \right)
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^S\frac1{u_k^2+1/4},
\]
which proves the root-sum form.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Konishi Baxter polynomial]
\label{ex:planar-n4-konishi-baxter-polynomial}
For the twist-two spin-two representative of Konishi, take
\[
  J=2,\qquad S=2,\qquad
  Q(u)=u^2-\frac1{12}.
\]
Then
\[
  (u+\ii/2)^2Q(u+\ii)+(u-\ii/2)^2Q(u-\ii)
  =
  \left(2u^2-\frac{13}{2}\right)Q(u).
\]
Thus the transfer polynomial is \(T(u)=2u^2-13/2\), and the zeros
\[
  u=\pm\frac{1}{2\sqrt3}
\]
are precisely the one-loop Konishi Bethe roots used in
Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-spectral-problem-spin-chains}.  This example is a
finite calculation showing how the QSC weak-coupling limit remembers the
spin-chain normalization.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Twist-two Baxter family]
\label{prop:planar-n4-twist-two-baxter-family}
For twist \(J=2\) and spin \(S\), define the terminating polynomial
\[
  Q_S(u)
  =
  {}_3F_2\!\left(
    \begin{matrix}
      -S,\ S+1,\ \frac12-\ii u\\
      1,\ 1
    \end{matrix}
    ;1
  \right),
\]
where \((a)_k\) is the rising Pochhammer symbol in the terminating
hypergeometric series.  It obeys
\[
  (u+\ii/2)^2Q_S(u+\ii)
  +(u-\ii/2)^2Q_S(u-\ii)
  =
  \left(2u^2-S(S+1)-\frac12\right)Q_S(u).
\]
Thus \(T_S(u)=2u^2-S(S+1)-1/2\).  The endpoint values and logarithmic
derivative are the finite identities
\[
  Q_S(-\ii/2)=1,\qquad
  Q_S(\ii/2)=(-1)^S,\qquad
  \ii\left(
  \frac{Q'_S(\ii/2)}{Q_S(\ii/2)}
  -
  \frac{Q'_S(-\ii/2)}{Q_S(-\ii/2)}
  \right)
  =
  4H_S,
\]
with \(H_S=\sum_{m=1}^S m^{-1}\).  Hence the physical even-spin twist-two
single-trace states have
\[
  \Delta_{J=2,S}
  =
  2+S+8g^2H_S+O(g^4).
\]
For \(S=2\), \(Q_2(u)=1/4-3u^2\), which is the same root set as the monic
Konishi polynomial \(u^2-1/12\), and the formula gives
\(\Delta_{\rm K}=4+12g^2+O(g^4)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Put
\[
  z=\frac12-\ii u,\qquad Q_S(u)=\widehat Q_S(z),
\]
so that \(u+\ii/2=\ii z\), \(u-\ii/2=\ii(z-1)\), and the shifts
\(u\mapsto u\pm\ii\) become \(z\mapsto z\pm1\).  The Baxter equation is
equivalent to
\[
  z^2\widehat Q_S(z+1)+(z-1)^2\widehat Q_S(z-1)
  =
  \bigl(2z^2-2z+S(S+1)+1\bigr)\widehat Q_S(z).
\]
Write the \(k\)-th summand of \(\widehat Q_S\) as
\[
  \tau_k(z)=
  \frac{(-S)_k(S+1)_k(z)_k}{(k!)^3},
  \qquad 0\leq k\leq S,
\]
and set \(\tau_k=0\) outside this range.  For \(k\geq1\),
\[
  (z+1)_k=(z)_k\frac{z+k}{z},
  \qquad
  (z-1)_k=(z)_k\frac{z-1}{z+k-1}.
\]
A direct substitution gives the summand identity
\[
\begin{aligned}
&z^2\tau_k(z+1)+(z-1)^2\tau_k(z-1)
  -\bigl(2z^2-2z+S(S+1)+1\bigr)\tau_k(z)  \\
&\hspace{2cm}
  =
  \frac{(k+1)^3}{z+k}\tau_{k+1}(z)
  -\frac{k^3}{z+k-1}\tau_k(z).
\end{aligned}
\]
The identity is first read away from the apparent poles \(z=1-k\) and then
extended across the removable singularities by polynomial continuation.  It is
a finite telescoping certificate.  The right side sums to zero because the
lower boundary has the factor \(k^3\) and the upper boundary has
\(\tau_{S+1}=0\).  Hence the \(z\)-form of the Baxter equation holds, and the
displayed Baxter equation follows after returning to \(u\).

The endpoint \(u=-\ii/2\) corresponds to \(z=0\), so
\(\widehat Q_S(0)=1\).  The endpoint \(u=\ii/2\) corresponds to \(z=1\), and
Chu--Vandermonde gives the finite sum
\[
  \widehat Q_S(1)
  =
  {}_2F_1(-S,S+1;1;1)
  =
  \frac{(-S)_S}{S!}=(-1)^S.
\]
It remains to prove the logarithmic-derivative identity.  Since
\[
  \frac{d}{du}Q_S(u)=-\ii\,\widehat Q'_S(z),
\]
we must compute the endpoint logarithmic derivatives of \(\widehat Q_S\).
First,
\[
  \widehat Q'_S(0)
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{S}
  \frac{(-S)_k(S+1)_k}{(k!)^2\,k}.
  \label{eq:qsc-twist-two-derivative-zero-sum}
\]
Indeed \(d(z)_k/dz|_{z=0}=(k-1)!\).  The same sum may be written as
\[
  \widehat Q'_S(0)
  =
  \int_0^1
  \frac{{}_2F_1(-S,S+1;1;t)-1}{t}\,dt .
\]
Using \({}_2F_1(-S,S+1;1;t)=\mathsf P_S(1-2t)\), where
\(\mathsf P_S\) is the Legendre polynomial, and setting \(x=1-2t\), this is
\[
  I_S=\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{\mathsf P_S(x)-1}{1-x}\,dx .
\]
The finite Christoffel--Darboux identity at one endpoint,
\[
  n\,\frac{\mathsf P_n(x)-\mathsf P_{n-1}(x)}{x-1}
  =
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{n-1}(2\ell+1)\mathsf P_\ell(x),
\]
is the telescoping form of the Legendre three-term recurrence.  It gives
\(I_n-I_{n-1}=-2/n\) after integration, because only the \(\ell=0\) Legendre
polynomial has nonzero integral over \([-1,1]\).  Since \(I_0=0\),
\[
  \widehat Q'_S(0)=-2H_S.
\]
At \(z=1\),
\[
  \widehat Q'_S(1)
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{S}
  \frac{(-S)_k(S+1)_k}{(k!)^2}H_k .
\]
This is obtained from \(d(1)_k/dz=k!H_k\).  Differentiate
Chu--Vandermonde in the denominator parameter:
\[
  F(a)={}_2F_1(-S,S+1;a;1)
  =
  \frac{(a-S-1)_S}{(a)_S}.
\]
At \(a=1\),
\[
  \frac{F'(1)}{F(1)}
  =
  \sum_{m=0}^{S-1}\frac{1}{1-S-1+m}
  -
  \sum_{m=0}^{S-1}\frac{1}{1+m}
  =
  -2H_S .
\]
On the other hand differentiating the series in \(a\) gives
\[
  F'(1)=-\widehat Q'_S(1).
\]
Since \(F(1)=(-1)^S\), we obtain
\[
  \frac{\widehat Q'_S(1)}{\widehat Q_S(1)}=2H_S.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \ii\left(
  \frac{Q'_S(\ii/2)}{Q_S(\ii/2)}
  -
  \frac{Q'_S(-\ii/2)}{Q_S(-\ii/2)}
  \right)
  =
  \frac{\widehat Q'_S(1)}{\widehat Q_S(1)}
  -\frac{\widehat Q'_S(0)}{\widehat Q_S(0)}
  =
  4H_S.
\]
The dimension formula is now the specialization of
Proposition~\ref{prop:qsc-one-loop-dimension}.
\end{proof}

\section{Cusp Anomalous Dimension and Konishi}

Two benchmark spectral quantities organize many tests of the framework.  The
first is the cusp anomalous dimension \(f(\lambda)\), extracted from large
spin twist operators:
\[
  \Delta-S
  =
  f(\lambda)\log S+O(S^0),
  \qquad S\to\infty.
\]
The QSC reproduces the same function obtained from the BES equation.  At weak
coupling it has a perturbative series in \(g^2\); at strong coupling the QSC
asymptotics begin with
\[
  f(\lambda)=\frac{\sqrt\lambda}{\pi}+O(\lambda^0)
\]
in the convention where \(\Delta-S=f(\lambda)\log S+O(S^0)\).  Comparisons
with semiclassical string theory are historically important but are not used
as inputs to the QFT-side spectral problem in this chapter.

The second is the Konishi dimension.  The QSC treats it as an ordinary finite
state with \(J=2\), \(S=2\), and zero total momentum.  At weak coupling it
matches the asymptotic Bethe ansatz until wrapping corrections enter; at
strong coupling it gives the QSC strong-coupling expansion
\[
  \Delta_{\rm K}(\lambda)
  =
  2\lambda^{1/4}
  +O(\lambda^0).
\]
The numerical and analytic solution of the QSC is a calculation problem
inside the finite functional system \eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-one}--\eqref{eq:planar-n4-pmu-period}.

\section{Small Spin Expansion}

The QSC gives a controlled analytic expansion near \(S=0\), where the state
approaches the protected vacuum.  At leading order the \(\mu\)-matrix may be
chosen, up to QSC gauge transformations, so that
\[
  \mu_{12}=-1,\quad \mu_{13}=0,\quad
  \mu_{14}=\mu_{23}=1,\quad
  \mu_{24}=-\sinh(2\pi u),\quad \mu_{34}=0 .
\]
Writing
\[
  x(u)=\frac{u}{2g}+\sqrt{\frac{u^2}{4g^2}-1},
\]
with the short-cut branch, the leading solution has
\[
  P_1=\epsilon x^{-J/2},
  \qquad
  P_3=\epsilon(x^{-J/2}-x^{J/2}),
\]
and \(P_2,P_4\) determined by the Laurent expansion of \(\sinh(2\pi u)\) in
powers of \(x\).  Comparing the large-\(u\) coefficients with the algebraic
relations above gives
\[
  \Delta-J-S
  =
  \frac{4\pi g\,I_{J+1}(4\pi g)}{J\,I_J(4\pi g)}\,S+O(S^2),
\]
where \(I_J\) is the modified Bessel function.  Continuing the resulting
strong-coupling expansion to \(J=2,S=2\) gives the Konishi estimate
\[
  \Delta_{\rm K}
  =
  2\lambda^{1/4}+2\lambda^{-1/4}+O(\lambda^{-3/4}).
\]
The analytic continuation from small spin to integer spin is a QSC input, not
a theorem proved from the gauge-theory path integral.

\section{Hexagon Form Factors}

The spectral problem determines scaling dimensions.  Three-point functions
require additional data.  In the planar limit, a single-trace three-point
function can be represented by a pair of pants worldsheet.  The hexagon
program cuts this pair of pants into two hexagons and assigns a form factor to
each half.

For three single-trace operators with spin-chain lengths \(L_1,L_2,L_3\), the
bridge lengths are
\[
  \ell_{12}=\frac{L_1+L_2-L_3}{2},\qquad
  \ell_{23}=\frac{L_2+L_3-L_1}{2},\qquad
  \ell_{31}=\frac{L_3+L_1-L_2}{2}.
\]
Asymptotically, the structure constant is a sum over partitions of magnons
between the two hexagons:
\[
  C_{123}^{\rm asym}
  =
  \sum_{\alpha\cup\bar\alpha=\{u\}}
  (-1)^{|\bar\alpha|}
  \ee^{\ii p(\bar\alpha)\ell}
  H(\alpha)H(\bar\alpha)\,
  \mathcal N^{-1/2}.
\]
Here \(H\) is the hexagon form factor, \(p(\bar\alpha)\) is the total momentum
of the magnons moved across the bridge, and \(\mathcal N\) is the Gaudin norm
factor of the Bethe state.  Mirror particles on the bridges add finite-size
corrections, analogous in spirit to wrapping corrections in the spectral
problem.

For scalar magnons the elementary two-particle scalar factor entering the
hexagon can be written, in the same \(x^\pm\) and \(\sigma\) conventions as
Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-asymptotic-bethe-ansatz}, as
\begin{equation}
  h(u,v)
  =
  \frac{x_u^- -x_v^-}{x_u^- -x_v^+}
  \frac{1-1/(x_u^+x_v^-)}{1-1/(x_u^+x_v^+)}
  \frac{1}{\sigma(u,v)} .
  \label{eq:planar-n4-hexagon-scalar-factor}
\end{equation}
The matrix part is the same centrally extended symmetry intertwiner that
appears in the magnon S-matrix, after the crossing moves needed to put
excitations on a chosen hexagon edge.  Thus the hexagon construction imports
the same analytic-continuation obligations: specifying \(h(u,v)\) without the
crossing path of the moved excitation is incomplete.

\begin{openproblem}[Derivation of the hexagon expansion]
\label{op:planar-n4-hexagon-derivation}
Give a derivation of the hexagon expansion from a nonperturbatively defined
planar gauge-theory path integral, including the emergence of the hexagon
form factor, the measure for mirror corrections, and the treatment of
operator mixing at finite length.  Existing evidence is strong inside the
integrability framework.  A derivation at the level of a theorem in
four-dimensional QFT remains open.
\end{openproblem}

\section{Maldacena--Wilson Line, Cusp, and Bremsstrahlung}

Let \(C\) be an oriented smooth curve with parameter \(s\), tangent
\(\dot x^\mu(s)\), and scalar-coupling unit vector
\(\theta^I(s)\in S^5\).  The Maldacena--Wilson line in the fundamental
representation is
\[
  W_{\rm MW}(C,\theta)
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}\,P\exp\int_C
  \bigl(\ii A_\mu(x(s))\dot x^\mu(s)
  +\Phi_I(x(s))\theta^I(s)|\dot x(s)|\bigr)\,\dd s .
\]
It is a gauge-invariant line operator after taking the trace and imposing the
usual endpoint prescription for closed curves or external heavy sources.  A
straight line with constant \(\theta\) is half-BPS and has vanishing cusp
anomalous dimension.

For two rays meeting with Euclidean deflection angle \(\varphi\), define
\(\Gamma_{\rm cusp}(\varphi,\theta)\) by the logarithmic divergence of the
renormalized cusped line.  At small geometric angle and fixed scalar coupling,
\[
  \Gamma_{\rm cusp}(\varphi)
  =
  -B(\lambda)\varphi^2+O(\varphi^4).
\]
The coefficient \(B(\lambda)\) is the Bremsstrahlung function.  On the
straight-line defect, the displacement operator \(\mathbb D(t)\) has
\[
  \langle \mathbb D(t)\mathbb D(0)\rangle
  =
  \frac{C_D}{(t-\ii0)^4},
  \qquad C_D=12B(\lambda),
\]
and the radiated power of a slowly accelerating heavy probe is
\[
  \frac{\dd E_{\rm rad}}{\dd t}=2\pi B(\lambda)\,\dot v^2 .
\]
For planar \(\mathcal N=4\) SYM, supersymmetric localization of the circular
half-BPS Wilson loop gives the exact structural formula
\[
  B(\lambda)
  =
  \frac{\sqrt\lambda}{4\pi^2}
  \frac{I_2(\sqrt\lambda)}{I_1(\sqrt\lambda)}
  \qquad (N=\infty).
\]
This relation is a quoted exact input from the localization framework.  The
QSC and mirror-TBA descriptions of the cusped line reproduce the same
function with cusp-specific asymptotic data, providing a stringent
normalization check on \(g=\sqrt\lambda/(4\pi)\).

\section{Proof Boundary}

Planar integrability gives a highly constrained exact spectral structure for
four-dimensional interacting QFT.  Symmetry fixes matrix scattering, crossing
fixes the scalar factor, Bethe equations organize asymptotic spectra, mirror
TBA incorporates finite size, and the QSC compresses the exact spectral
problem into analytic functional equations.  The remaining mathematical task
is to derive this structure from a precise nonperturbative formulation of
planar \(\mathcal N=4\) SYM, including the topology in which local operators,
large-\(N\) limits, and analytic continuation in \(\lambda\) are controlled.
