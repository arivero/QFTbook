\chapter{All-Loop Asymptotic Bethe Ansatz}
\label{ch:planar-n4-asymptotic-bethe-ansatz}

\ConventionsLorentzian

The one-loop spin chain of
Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-spectral-problem-spin-chains} is only the first
term in the planar dilatation operator.  The all-loop asymptotic Bethe ansatz
is a proposal for the spectrum of long single-trace operators before wrapping
effects become important.  Its inputs are the residual centrally extended
\(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)\oplus\mathfrak{su}(2|2)\) symmetry of the BMN vacuum,
factorized magnon scattering, crossing, and analyticity.

\section{Convention Ledger}

The integrability literature uses several mutually inverse scalar-factor and
Zhukovsky conventions.  This chapter follows the convention used in the
stringbook integrability chapters, with the notational replacement
\[
  h_{\rm stringbook}=g_{\rm monograph}=\frac{\sqrt\lambda}{4\pi}.
\]
Thus
\[
  x^\pm+\frac1{x^\pm}
  =
  \frac{u\pm\ii/2}{g},
  \qquad
  \ee^{\ii p}=\frac{x^+}{x^-},
\]
and the physical weak-coupling branch has
\[
  x^\pm
  =
  \frac{\ee^{\pm\ii p/2}}{2g\sin(p/2)}+O(g).
\]
The one-magnon energy is written in the stringbook-compatible form
\[
  H
  =
  -1-2\ii g(x^+-x^-)
  =
  1+2\ii g\left(\frac1{x^+}-\frac1{x^-}\right).
\]
The equality of the two expressions is not an additional assumption: it is
obtained by multiplying the defining \(x^\pm\) relation by \(2\ii g\).

The scalar factor convention is also fixed here.  We write
\(\sigma=\ee^{\ii\theta}\) and use the crossing equation in the same
orientation as the stringbook:
\[
  \sigma_{12}\sigma_{\bar1 2}
  =
  \frac{x_2^+}{x_2^-}
  \frac{x_2^+ -x_1^+}{x_2^+ -x_1^-}
  \frac{x_2^- -1/x_1^+}{x_2^- -1/x_1^-}.
\]
Authors who put the rational scalar factor into the opposite part of the
S-matrix, or define the two-body operator as \(S_{21}\) rather than \(S_{12}\),
write the reciprocal of this expression.  The companion check script enforces
the \(x^\pm\)-energy and sheet conventions used here; crossing itself remains
a monodromy input rather than a finite algebraic identity.

\section{BMN Vacuum and Central Extension}

The vacuum spin-chain state is
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(Z^J).
\]
It is protected and carries large \(R\)-charge \(J\).  A magnon is an
insertion into this long chain.  The residual symmetry preserving the vacuum
contains two centrally extended \(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)\) algebras.  For one
copy, write the bosonic states as \(\ket{\phi^a}\), \(a=1,2\), and the
fermionic states as \(\ket{\psi^\alpha}\), \(\alpha=1,2\).  The supercharges
act in the form
\[
\begin{aligned}
  Q^\alpha{}_a\ket{\phi^b}
  &=a\,\delta_a^b\ket{\psi^\alpha},
&
  Q^\alpha{}_a\ket{\psi^\beta}
  &=b\,\varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\varepsilon_{ab}\ket{\phi^b Z^+},
\\
  S^a{}_\alpha\ket{\phi^b}
  &=c\,\varepsilon^{ab}\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}\ket{\psi^\beta Z^-},
&
  S^a{}_\alpha\ket{\psi^\beta}
  &=d\,\delta_\alpha^\beta\ket{\phi^a}.
\end{aligned}
\]
The symbols \(Z^+\) and \(Z^-\) record insertion or removal of one vacuum
letter.  On a momentum eigenstate,
\[
  Z^+\mapsto \ee^{\ii p}-1,
  \qquad
  Z^-\mapsto \ee^{-\ii p}-1
\]
up to the chosen side convention.  The representation constraint is
\[
  ad-bc=1.
\]
The central charges are
\[
  P=ab,\qquad K=cd,\qquad H=ad+bc .
\]
The shortening condition of the centrally extended algebra is
\[
  H^2-4PK=1 .
\]
The central extension is compatible with trace cyclicity.  A single magnon is
an excitation on an open segment of the BMN vacuum, so \(Z^\pm\) records
length data at the edge of that segment.  On a closed trace the product of
all such phases is \(\ee^{\ii P_{\rm tot}}-1\), where \(P_{\rm tot}\) is the
total spin-chain momentum.  The physical single-trace projection sets
\(P_{\rm tot}=0\), but individual magnons may still transform in centrally
extended short representations before that projection is imposed.
With
\[
  P=g(1-\ee^{\ii p}),\qquad K=g(1-\ee^{-\ii p}),
\]
this gives the exact single-magnon dispersion relation
\begin{equation}
  H(p)=\sqrt{1+16g^2\sin^2\frac p2}.
  \label{eq:planar-n4-magnon-dispersion}
\end{equation}
The anomalous contribution of a magnon is \(H(p)-1\).

\begin{controlledapproximation}[BMN scaling check]
\label{ca:planar-n4-bmn-scaling-check}
Let \(J\to\infty\) with \(\lambda'=\lambda/J^2\) fixed and take
\[
  p=\frac{2\pi n}{J}+O(J^{-2}),\qquad n\in\mathbb Z .
\]
Then
\[
  H(p)=\sqrt{1+\lambda' n^2}+O(J^{-1}).
\]
This reproduces the pp-wave oscillator normalization.  Here it is a
convention check on the gauge-theory spectral problem, not a proof of the
all-coupling Bethe ansatz from string theory.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{proposition}[Dispersion from shortening]
\label{prop:planar-n4-dispersion-shortening}
Assuming the centrally extended residual symmetry acts on a one-magnon
representation with central charges \(P=g(1-\ee^{\ii p})\) and
\(K=g(1-\ee^{-\ii p})\), the shortening condition fixes the dispersion
relation to \eqref{eq:planar-n4-magnon-dispersion}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Compute
\[
  PK
  =
  g^2(1-\ee^{\ii p})(1-\ee^{-\ii p})
  =
  4g^2\sin^2\frac p2 .
\]
Substitution into \(H^2-4PK=1\) gives
\[
  H^2=1+16g^2\sin^2\frac p2 .
\]
The positive square root is selected because \(H=1\) at \(g=0\) for an
elementary magnon.
\end{proof}

\section{Zhukovsky Variables}

Introduce the Zhukovsky map \(x(u)\) by
\begin{equation}
  x(u)+\frac1{x(u)}=\frac ug .
  \label{eq:planar-n4-zhukovsky-map}
\end{equation}
The physical branch is the solution satisfying \(x(u)\sim u/g\) as
\(|u|\to\infty\).  Equivalently,
\[
  x_{\rm ph}(u)
  =
  \frac12\left(
    \frac ug+\sqrt{\frac ug-2}\sqrt{\frac ug+2}
  \right),
\]
with the square-root cuts chosen so that the only short cut on the physical
sheet is \([-2g,2g]\).  Crossing this cut replaces \(x\) by \(1/x\).  More
precisely, the two boundary values on the cut satisfy
\[
  x_{\rm above}(u)x_{\rm below}(u)=1,
  \qquad -2g<u<2g .
\]
The large-\(u\) expansion on the physical sheet is
\[
  x_{\rm ph}(u)
  =
  \frac ug-\frac gu-\frac{g^3}{u^3}
  -\frac{2g^5}{u^5}+O(u^{-7}).
\]
For a one-magnon state set
\[
  x^\pm=x(u\pm\ii/2).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \ee^{\ii p}=\frac{x^+}{x^-},
  \qquad
  x^++\frac1{x^+}-x^- -\frac1{x^-}=\frac{\ii}{g}.
  \label{eq:planar-n4-xpm-definition}
\end{equation}
The physical region for real magnons is chosen so that
\((x^+)^*=x^-\) and \(|x^\pm|>1\) at weak coupling.  The magnon energy is
the sheet-dependent but single-valued physical-branch expression
\begin{equation}
  H
  =
  -1-2\ii g(x^+-x^-)
  =
  1+2\ii g\left(
    \frac1{x^+}-\frac1{x^-}
  \right),
  \label{eq:planar-n4-energy-xpm}
\end{equation}
with the same branch as \eqref{eq:planar-n4-magnon-dispersion}.  Conserved
charges used in the dressing phase are
\[
  q_r(x^\pm)
  =
  \frac{\ii}{r-1}
  \left[
    \frac1{(x^+)^{r-1}}-\frac1{(x^-)^{r-1}}
  \right],
  \qquad r\geq2 .
\]

\begin{lemma}[Zhukovsky form of the dispersion]
\label{lem:planar-n4-zhukovsky-dispersion}
Let \(x^\pm\) obey \eqref{eq:planar-n4-xpm-definition} on the physical sheet.
Then \eqref{eq:planar-n4-energy-xpm} is equal to
\[
  \sqrt{1+16g^2\sin^2\frac p2}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For a physical real magnon write
\[
  x^\pm
  =
  \ee^{\pm\ii p/2}
  \frac{1+\sqrt{1+16g^2\sin^2(p/2)}}
       {4g\sin(p/2)} .
\]
This parametrization gives \(x^+/x^-=\ee^{\ii p}\).  Substituting it into the
difference \(x^++1/x^+-x^- -1/x^-\) gives \(\ii/g\), so it is the physical
solution of \eqref{eq:planar-n4-xpm-definition}.  The inverse difference is
\[
  \frac1{x^+}-\frac1{x^-}
  =
  \frac{\sqrt{1+16g^2\sin^2(p/2)}-1}{2\ii g}.
\]
Equation~\eqref{eq:planar-n4-energy-xpm} therefore reproduces
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-magnon-dispersion}.  The equality extends to the
physical sheet by analytic continuation away from the branch points.
\end{proof}

\begin{warning}[Sheet data in crossing]
\label{warn:planar-n4-crossing-sheet-data}
The formal replacement \(x^\pm\mapsto1/x^\pm\) is not a harmless algebraic
substitution.  It records a path on the Zhukovsky surface.  Poles crossed by
that path alter the scalar factor through the crossing equation, and the
answer depends on whether the continuation crosses the short cut of \(x^+\),
the short cut of \(x^-\), or the composed path used in the mirror theory.
In the \(u\)-plane, the cuts of \(x^\pm=x(u\pm\ii/2)\) have endpoints
\[
  u=\pm2g-\ii/2,\qquad u=\pm2g+\ii/2 .
\]
The physical crossing path for one magnon winds around the appropriate pair
of these branch points so that both \(x^+\) and \(x^-\) reach the crossed
sheet.  Applying the path twice generally lands on a different sheet of the
scalar factor even though the projected \(x^\pm\)-values have returned.
\end{warning}

\section{Two-Body S-Matrix}

The magnon two-body \(S\)-matrix is fixed by symmetry up to a scalar factor.
Let \(S^{\rm mat}_{12}\) denote the matrix part determined by the two
centrally extended \(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)\) copies.  The full two-magnon
operator is
\[
  S_{12}=S_0(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)\,S^{\rm mat}_{12}.
\]
For one \(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)\) copy, the matrix part is the graded
intertwiner
\[
  S^{\rm mat}_{12}\Delta_{12}(J)
  =
  \Delta_{21}(J)S^{\rm mat}_{12},
  \qquad J\in\mathfrak{su}(2|2)_{\rm c},
\]
where the coproduct includes the braiding phase carried by the magnon
momentum.  Its independent amplitudes are fixed by
\[
\begin{aligned}
  S\ket{\phi_1^a\phi_2^b}
  &=
  A_{12}\ket{\phi_2^{\{a}\phi_1^{b\}}}
  +B_{12}\ket{\phi_2^{[a}\phi_1^{b]}}
  +C_{12}\varepsilon^{ab}\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}
    \ket{\psi_2^\alpha\psi_1^\beta},\\
  S\ket{\psi_1^\alpha\psi_2^\beta}
  &=
  D_{12}\ket{\psi_2^{\{\alpha}\psi_1^{\beta\}}}
  +E_{12}\ket{\psi_2^{[\alpha}\psi_1^{\beta]}}
  +F_{12}\varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\varepsilon_{ab}
    \ket{\phi_2^a\phi_1^b},\\
  S\ket{\phi_1^a\psi_2^\beta}
  &=
  G_{12}\ket{\psi_2^\beta\phi_1^a}
  +H_{12}\ket{\phi_2^a\psi_1^\beta},
\end{aligned}
\]
with analogous \(K_{12},L_{12}\) amplitudes on
\(\ket{\psi_1^\alpha\phi_2^b}\).  Solving the intertwining equations fixes
these ten amplitudes as rational functions of \(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm\) and of the
representation coefficients \(a,b,c,d\), up to one common scalar factor.
The scalar factor is written as
\[
  S_0(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)
  =
  S_{\rm rat}(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)\,
  \sigma(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)^2,
  \qquad
  \sigma=\ee^{\ii\theta}.
\]
The rational part is fixed by the same representation data that determine
the matrix part.  The dressing phase has the antisymmetric charge expansion
\[
  \theta(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)
  =
  -
  \sum_{r=2}^\infty\sum_{\substack{s>r\\ r+s\ {\rm odd}}}
  c_{r,s}(g)\,
  \bigl(q_r(x_1^\pm)q_s(x_2^\pm)-q_s(x_1^\pm)q_r(x_2^\pm)\bigr).
\]
Equivalently, the BES phase may be packaged as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \theta(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)
  &=
  \chi(x_1^+,x_2^+)-\chi(x_1^+,x_2^-)
  -\chi(x_1^-,x_2^+)+\chi(x_1^-,x_2^-),\\
  \chi(x,y)
  &=
  -\ii
  \oint\frac{\dd z}{2\pi\ii}
  \oint\frac{\dd w}{2\pi\ii}
  \frac{1}{x-z}\frac{1}{y-w}
  \log
  \frac{\Gamma\!\left(1+\ii g(z+z^{-1}-w-w^{-1})\right)}
       {\Gamma\!\left(1-\ii g(z+z^{-1}-w-w^{-1})\right)} .
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:planar-n4-bes-chi-integral}
\end{equation}
The contours encircle the unit circle singularities of \(z\) and \(w\) in the
domain where \(|x|>1\), \(|y|>1\).  Formula
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-bes-chi-integral} is therefore not a global formula on
all sheets by itself; outside that domain it is continued with the crossing
prescription.

Inside this domain the weak-coupling coefficients can be extracted without
any sheet continuation.  Expand
\begin{equation}
  \chi(x,y)
  =
  \sum_{r=2}^{\infty}
  \sum_{\substack{s>r\\ r+s\ {\rm odd}}}
  \frac{c_{r,s}(g)}{(r-1)(s-1)}
  \left(
    \frac{1}{x^{r-1}y^{s-1}}
    -
    \frac{1}{x^{s-1}y^{r-1}}
  \right).
  \label{eq:planar-n4-chi-coefficient-expansion}
\end{equation}
The charge-expansion convention above uses the same \(c_{r,s}\); the two
forms agree because \(q_r=\ii A_r/(r-1)\), where
\(A_r=(x^+)^{1-r}-(x^-)^{1-r}\).

\begin{proposition}[Weak coefficients from the DHM contour]
\label{prop:planar-n4-dhm-weak-coefficients}
For \(N=2m+1\geq3\), let \(R^{(N)}_{r,s}\) be the Laurent coefficient
\[
  R^{(N)}_{r,s}
  =
  [z^{-(r-1)}w^{-(s-1)}]
  \bigl(z+z^{-1}-w-w^{-1}\bigr)^N .
\]
Then the contribution of order \(g^N\) to the coefficient \(c_{r,s}(g)\) in
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-chi-coefficient-expansion} is
\begin{equation}
  c_{r,s}(g)\big|_{g^N}
  =
  \frac{2(-1)^{m+1}}{N}\,
  (r-1)(s-1)R^{(N)}_{r,s}\,
  \zeta(N)g^N .
  \label{eq:planar-n4-dhm-residue-coefficient}
\end{equation}
In particular,
\[
  c_{2,3}(g)=4\zeta(3)g^3-40\zeta(5)g^5+O(g^7).
\]
The first dressing contribution to a weak-coupling anomalous dimension is
nevertheless order \(g^6\), since \(q_2=O(g)\) and \(q_3=O(g^2)\) on the
physical weak-coupling branch.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(|x|,|y|>1\),
\[
  \frac{1}{x-z}\frac{1}{y-w}
  =
  \sum_{a,b\geq0}\frac{z^aw^b}{x^{a+1}y^{b+1}} .
\]
The logarithm in \eqref{eq:planar-n4-bes-chi-integral} has the Taylor
expansion at small \(g\)
\[
  \log\frac{\Gamma(1+\eta)}{\Gamma(1-\eta)}
  =
  -2\gamma\eta
  -
  2\sum_{m=1}^{\infty}
  \frac{\zeta(2m+1)}{2m+1}\eta^{2m+1},
  \qquad
  \eta=\ii g(z+z^{-1}-w-w^{-1}).
\]
The linear Euler-constant term is symmetric data that drops out of the
antisymmetric coefficient family \(s>r\).  For \(N=2m+1\geq3\), multiplying by
the outside factor \(-\ii\) gives
\[
  \frac{2(-1)^{m+1}\zeta(N)}{N}
  g^N
  \bigl(z+z^{-1}-w-w^{-1}\bigr)^N .
\]
The two contour integrals pick the coefficient of \(z^{-1}w^{-1}\).  Thus the
coefficient of \(x^{1-r}y^{1-s}\) is the displayed constant times
\(R^{(N)}_{r,s}\).  Comparing with
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-chi-coefficient-expansion} proves
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-dhm-residue-coefficient}.  For \(r=2,s=3\),
\[
  R^{(3)}_{2,3}=3,\qquad R^{(5)}_{2,3}=50,
\]
which gives \(4\zeta(3)g^3-40\zeta(5)g^5\).
\end{proof}

A saddle expansion of the same contour representation gives the AFS and
Hernandez--Lopez strong-coupling terms.
Crossing relates \(\sigma(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)\) to the phase obtained by
analytically continuing the first magnon through the crossed sheet
\(x_1^\pm\mapsto1/x_1^\pm\).  It supplies an additional analyticity condition
on the scalar factor beyond factorized Yang--Baxter compatibility.
In the normalization where the rational matrix part is separated as above,
Janik crossing has the convention-fixed form
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(\bar x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)\sigma(x_1^\pm,x_2^\pm)
  =
  \frac{x_2^+}{x_2^-}
  \frac{x_2^+ -x_1^+}{x_2^+ -x_1^-}
  \frac{x_2^- -1/x_1^+}{x_2^- -1/x_1^-} ,
  \label{eq:planar-n4-crossing-scalar-factor}
\end{equation}
where \(\bar x_1^\pm\) denotes the crossed-sheet continuation of the first
magnon, not merely a new independent pair of complex numbers.  The equation
is best read as a monodromy statement for the scalar factor on the
Zhukovsky rapidity surface.  The BES solution selects the minimal solution
compatible with \eqref{eq:planar-n4-crossing-scalar-factor}, unitarity
\(\sigma_{12}\sigma_{21}=1\), and the prescribed weak/strong asymptotics.

\begin{definition}[Dressing-phase data and status]
\label{def:planar-n4-dressing-phase-data}
The scalar dressing phase used in the asymptotic equations is specified by
the antisymmetric charge expansion above, the AFS leading strong-coupling
term \(c_{r,r+1}(g)=g+O(g^0)\), the Hernandez--Lopez semiclassical
correction, Janik crossing on the Zhukovsky surface, and the BES integral
representation selecting the minimal crossing-symmetric solution.  Its weak
series begins with
\[
  c_{2,3}(g)=4\zeta(3)g^3+O(g^5),
\]
and, because the weak charges contribute \(q_2q_3-q_3q_2=O(g^3)\), the first
dressing contribution to Konishi enters at four loops.  This is an exact
datum of the planar-integrability framework and a highly tested claim about
gauge theory, not a theorem here derived from a constructive definition of
four-dimensional \(\mathcal N=4\) SYM.
\end{definition}

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Asymptotic planar magnon S-matrix]
\label{qthm:planar-n4-asymptotic-smatrix-status}
Assume planar integrability of the long \(\mathcal N=4\) SYM spin chain, the
centrally extended residual symmetry, physical unitarity, Yang--Baxter
factorization, crossing, and the minimal analyticity condition used in the
BES construction.  Then the asymptotic two-body magnon S-matrix is the
\(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)^2\)-invariant matrix part multiplied by the scalar
dressing factor whose phase has the charge expansion above with the BES
coefficients \(c_{r,s}(g)\).
\end{quotedtheorem}

This is stated as a quoted theorem because the full claim is not a theorem of
four-dimensional QFT derived from a nonperturbative definition of the gauge
theory.  It is an integrability theorem inside a framework whose physical
identification with the planar gauge-theory dilatation operator has extensive
checks and strong structural constraints.

\section{Asymptotic Bethe Equations}

\begin{assumption}[Asymptotic Bethe--Yang regime]
\label{ass:planar-n4-aba-regime}
The all-loop asymptotic Bethe ansatz is used as a long-chain
Bethe--Yang quantization rule for planar single traces.  More precisely, for
a sequence of operators of length \(L\), with either fixed magnon number or a
controlled large-\(L\) distribution of Bethe roots, one assumes:
\[
  L\to\infty,\qquad
  \text{virtual propagation around the trace is suppressed,}
\]
and the many-magnon quantization is obtained by factorizing scattering into
the two-body S-matrix of the preceding section.  This intended domain includes
high-density configurations of roots in the \(L\to\infty\) limit.  The
additional assumption is not thermodynamic Bethe ansatz; it is the
asymptotic finite-volume quantization of the physical spin-chain problem.
At finite \(L\), or at a loop order where the long-range dilatation operator
can wrap around the trace, the omitted mirror-winding effects are no longer
parametrically absent.
\end{assumption}

The word ``asymptotic'' in this assumption refers to the spatial length of the
spin chain, not to a sparse-gas approximation.  The following elementary
counting statement is the precise large-\(L\) bridge used whenever an ABA
solution is replaced by a macroscopic root distribution.

\begin{proposition}[Finite-density ABA counting equation]
\label{prop:planar-n4-aba-finite-density-counting}
Consider a diagonal component of a long-chain Bethe--Yang problem with rapidity
variables \(u_j\), \(j=1,\ldots,M_L\), and logarithmic equations
\begin{equation}
  Lp(u_j)+\sum_{\substack{k=1\\k\neq j}}^{M_L}\Theta(u_j,u_k)
  =
  2\pi I_j,
  \qquad I_j\in\mathbb Z .
  \label{eq:planar-n4-aba-log-counting-form}
\end{equation}
Assume a branch of the two-body phase \(\Theta\) has been chosen on the
rapidity interval under consideration; possible diagonal jumps have either
been removed by this branch choice or are treated as explicit source terms.
Assume also that \(M_L/L\) has a finite limit and that the empirical measures
\[
  \nu_L=\frac1L\sum_{j=1}^{M_L}\delta_{u_j}
\]
converge weakly to \(\rho(v)\dd v\) on compact subintervals where
\(p\) and \(\Theta\) are \(C^1\).  Define
\[
  Z_L(u)=p(u)+\frac1L\sum_{j=1}^{M_L}\Theta(u,u_j),
  \qquad
  Z(u)=p(u)+\int\Theta(u,v)\rho(v)\,\dd v .
\]
Then \(Z_L\to Z\) locally uniformly after differentiating under the stated
regularity hypotheses, and the density of available Bethe levels is
\begin{equation}
  \rho(u)+\rho_{\rm h}(u)
  =
  \frac{1}{2\pi}Z'(u)
  =
  \frac{p'(u)}{2\pi}
  +
  \int K(u,v)\rho(v)\,\dd v,
  \qquad
  K(u,v)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\partial_u\Theta(u,v).
  \label{eq:planar-n4-aba-finite-density-counting-equation}
\end{equation}
Here \(\rho_{\rm h}\) is the macroscopic density of unoccupied integer levels
in the chosen branch.  In a completely filled interval \(\rho_{\rm h}=0\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Weak convergence gives
\[
  \frac1L\sum_{j=1}^{M_L}\Theta(u,u_j)
  \longrightarrow
  \int\Theta(u,v)\rho(v)\,\dd v
\]
uniformly on compact subsets on which the phase is continuous and the root
cloud stays away from unaccounted singularities.  The same statement applied
to \(\partial_u\Theta\) gives the derivative formula for \(Z'(u)\).
Equation~\eqref{eq:planar-n4-aba-log-counting-form} says that the integer
level attached to rapidity \(u\) is, to leading order, \(LZ(u)/(2\pi)\).  Hence
the number of available integer levels in a small interval \([u,u+\dd u]\) is
\[
  \frac{L}{2\pi}Z'(u)\,\dd u+o(L\,\dd u).
\]
Dividing by \(L\,\dd u\) gives the density of occupied plus unoccupied
available levels, namely \(\rho+\rho_{\rm h}\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-aba-finite-density-counting-equation}.
\end{proof}

This is a large-\(L\) continuum limit of the same physical ABA equations.  It
is not mirror TBA: no mirror energy, entropy functional, pseudoenergy, or
thermal variational problem has been introduced.  The additional dynamical
assumption is exactly the one stated above, namely that the long-chain
two-body magnon S-matrix continues to quantize the physical chain when
\(M_L/L=O(1)\), as long as mirror winding remains asymptotically suppressed.

In the \(SU(2)\) scalar subsector, the all-loop asymptotic Bethe equations can
be written
\begin{equation}
  \left(\frac{x_j^+}{x_j^-}\right)^L
  =
  \prod_{\substack{k=1\\ k\neq j}}^M
  \frac{x_j^- -x_k^+}{x_j^+ -x_k^-}
  \frac{1-1/(x_j^+x_k^-)}{1-1/(x_j^-x_k^+)}
  \sigma(x_j^\pm,x_k^\pm)^2 ,
  \label{eq:planar-n4-su2-aba}
\end{equation}
with cyclicity
\[
  \prod_{j=1}^M\frac{x_j^+}{x_j^-}=1.
\]
The scaling dimension is
\[
  \Delta
  =
  L+\sum_{j=1}^M\left(
    \sqrt{1+16g^2\sin^2\frac{p_j}{2}}-1
  \right),
\]
up to wrapping corrections.  The full
\(\mathfrak{psu}(2,2|4)\) asymptotic Bethe equations require nested roots
for the residual symmetry algebra.  Their algebraic role is the same as in
Chapter~\ref{ch:integrable-nested-bethe-ansatz-matrix-bethe-yang}; their
physical meaning is different because these auxiliary roots organize
polarizations of magnons on a gauge-theory trace.

The first nesting step can be derived without the full
\(\mathfrak{psu}(2,2|4)\) bookkeeping.  Start with \(M\) level-I magnons
\(\phi_1(p_k)\).  A single level-II fermionic excitation with spectral
parameter \(y\) is a wave packet
\[
  \ket{\Psi_{\rm II}(y)}
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^M
  C_k(y;\{p_j\})\,
  \ket{\phi_1(p_1)\cdots\psi(p_k)\cdots\phi_1(p_M)} ,
\]
where the omitted flavour index of \(\psi\) is a spectator.  Write
\(x_i^\pm=x^\pm(p_i)\) and let \(\mathsf a_i=\mathsf a(p_i)\) be the
nonzero representation coefficient which converts a bosonic vacuum magnon
to the chosen fermionic excitation in the spin-chain basis.  Normalize the
two-body matrix part by the boson-boson amplitude \(A_{12}\).  In the
stringbook orientation of the \(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)_{\rm c}\) intertwiner,
the four mixed amplitudes needed for one fermionic defect obey
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \frac{G_{12}}{A_{12}}
  &=
  \frac{x_2^+-x_1^+}{x_2^+-x_1^-},
  &
  \frac{K_{12}}{A_{12}}
  &=
  \frac{\mathsf a_2}{\mathsf a_1}
  \frac{x_1^+-x_1^-}{x_2^+-x_1^-},\\
  \frac{L_{12}}{A_{12}}
  &=
  \frac{x_2^--x_1^-}{x_2^+-x_1^-},
  &
  \frac{H_{12}}{A_{12}}
  &=
  \frac{\mathsf a_1}{\mathsf a_2}
  \frac{x_2^+-x_2^-}{x_2^+-x_1^-}.
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:planar-n4-mixed-amplitude-ratios-for-nesting}
\end{equation}
These are ratios of matrix amplitudes, so the scalar dressing factor and
reciprocal scalar-factor conventions cancel.

\begin{proposition}[Single level-II nesting step]
\label{prop:planar-n4-single-level-ii-nesting-step}
Assume \(x_i^\pm\), \(\mathsf a_i\), and the denominators in
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-mixed-amplitude-ratios-for-nesting} are nonzero.  The
one-defect ansatz
\[
  C_k(y;\{p_j\})
  =
  f(y,p_k)\prod_{\ell<k}S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}(y,p_\ell)
\]
is compatible with every adjacent transposition of level-I magnons if
\begin{equation}
  f(y,p)=\frac{\mathsf a(p)}{y-x^+(p)},
  \qquad
  S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}(y,p)
  =
  \frac{y-x^-(p)}{y-x^+(p)} .
  \label{eq:planar-n4-single-level-ii-data}
\end{equation}
The normalization of \(f\) may be multiplied by a function of \(y\) alone;
this rescales the one-defect state and has no Bethe-equation content.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
It is enough to check a two-site exchange, because the many-site statement is
then obtained by multiplying the same local move along the chain.  Let
\[
  f_i=f(y,p_i),
  \qquad
  S_i=S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}(y,p_i).
\]
Before exchanging magnons \(1\) and \(2\), the defect can sit on the first
or second site with coefficients \(f_1\) and \(f_2S_1\).  After applying the
matrix S-matrix to this two-site block, equality with the ansatz in the
exchanged order gives the two coefficient equations
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  f_1L_{12}+f_2S_1H_{12}
  &=
  S_2f_1A_{12},\\
  f_1K_{12}+f_2S_1G_{12}
  &=
  f_2A_{12}.
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:planar-n4-single-level-ii-local-equations}
\end{equation}
These equations are the algebraic content of moving a single auxiliary
fermion through two neighbouring physical magnons.

Substitute \eqref{eq:planar-n4-mixed-amplitude-ratios-for-nesting} and
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-single-level-ii-data}.  The second equation in
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-single-level-ii-local-equations} reduces, after
clearing the common factor
\(A_{12}\mathsf a_2/((y-x_1^+)(x_2^+-x_1^-))\), to
\[
  (x_1^+-x_1^-)(y-x_2^+)
  +(x_2^+-x_1^+)(y-x_1^-)
  =
  (x_2^+-x_1^-)(y-x_1^+).
\]
The first equation reduces in the same way to
\[
  (x_2^--x_1^-)(y-x_2^+)
  +(x_2^+-x_2^-)(y-x_1^-)
  =
  (x_2^+-x_1^-)(y-x_2^-).
\]
Both are polynomial identities in \(y\).  Therefore the local exchange is
consistent, and repeated adjacent exchanges give the displayed coefficient
formula for an arbitrary ordering of the level-I magnons.
\end{proof}

The square-root braiding factors of the string basis multiply
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-single-level-ii-data} by convention-dependent one-body
factors; they cancel from the local identities above after the corresponding
basis change is applied to the mixed amplitudes.

With two level-II fermions there is a genuine contact term: when the two
auxiliary fermions occupy the same level-I site, the local state can turn
into the boson \(\phi_2\) accompanied by the length-changing marker \(Z^+\).
Let
\[
  v(y)=y+y^{-1}.
\]
In the spin-chain basis, the local two-defect calculation fixes the
\(SU(2)\)-invariant level-II matrix
\begin{equation}
  (S^{{\rm II}})_{\alpha\beta}^{\delta\gamma}(y_1,y_2)
  =
  \delta_\alpha^\delta\delta_\beta^\gamma\,M(y_1,y_2)
  +
  \delta_\alpha^\gamma\delta_\beta^\delta\,N(y_1,y_2),
  \label{eq:planar-n4-level-ii-mn-definition}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
  M+N=-1,
  \qquad
  M-N=
  \frac{v(y_1)-v(y_2)-\ii/g}{v(y_1)-v(y_2)+\ii/g}.
  \label{eq:planar-n4-level-ii-mn-eigenvalues}
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\[
  M(y_1,y_2)=
  -\,\frac{\ii/g}{v(y_1)-v(y_2)+\ii/g},
  \qquad
  N(y_1,y_2)=
  -\,\frac{v(y_1)-v(y_2)}{v(y_1)-v(y_2)+\ii/g}.
\]
The accompanying same-site coefficient is
\begin{equation}
  \mathsf f^{(2)}(y_1,y_2;p)
  =
  \frac{g}{\mathsf a(p)^2}
  \left(\frac{x^-(p)}{x^+(p)}-1\right)
  \frac{y_1y_2-x^+(p)x^-(p)}{y_1y_2}
  \frac{y_1-y_2}{v(y_1)-v(y_2)+\ii/g}.
  \label{eq:planar-n4-level-ii-contact-coefficient}
\end{equation}
The sign \(M+N=-1\) is not a scalar scattering phase to be inserted into
the level-III vacuum Bethe equation.  It is the ungraded matrix eigenvalue
on the state with two identical level-II fermions; after the fermionic
exchange sign is accounted for, the level-III vacuum scattering is trivial.
This is the local sign convention behind the sentence that the
\(\psi_1\)-vacuum has \(S^{{\rm II},{\rm II}}=1\).

\begin{proposition}[Single level-III nesting step]
\label{prop:planar-n4-single-level-iii-nesting-step}
Assume \(g\neq0\), \(v(y_1)-v(y_2)+\ii/g\neq0\), and the denominators below
are nonzero.  In the graded convention just described, a single level-III
excitation with spectral parameter \(w\) has one-body coefficient
\[
  \gamma(w,y)=
  \frac{w+\ii/(2g)}{w-v(y)+\ii/(2g)}
\]
and level-III/level-II scattering factor
\begin{equation}
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w,y)
  =
  \frac{w-v(y)-\ii/(2g)}
       {w-v(y)+\ii/(2g)}.
  \label{eq:planar-n4-level-iii-level-ii-factor}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Put \(\eta=\ii/(2g)\), \(v_i=v(y_i)\), \(D=v_1-v_2\), and
\(a_i=w-v_i\).  Then \(a_2=a_1+D\), and
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-level-ii-mn-eigenvalues} is equivalent to
\[
  M=-\frac{2\eta}{D+2\eta},
  \qquad
  N=-\frac{D}{D+2\eta}.
\]
For an adjacent exchange of two level-II sites, the two independent
coefficients must obey
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \gamma(w,y_1)M
  +\gamma(w,y_2)S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w,y_1)N
  &=
  -\gamma(w,y_2),\\
  \gamma(w,y_1)N
  +\gamma(w,y_2)S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w,y_1)M
  &=
  -\gamma(w,y_1)S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w,y_2).
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:planar-n4-level-iii-local-equations}
\end{equation}
The minus signs are precisely the fermionic exchange signs which convert the
ungraded \(M+N=-1\) vacuum eigenvalue into trivial graded vacuum scattering.

Substituting \(\gamma(w,y_i)=(w+\eta)/(a_i+\eta)\) and
\(S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w,y_i)=(a_i-\eta)/(a_i+\eta)\), the first equation
in \eqref{eq:planar-n4-level-iii-local-equations} reduces to
\[
  -2\eta(a_2+\eta)-D(a_1-\eta)
  =
  -(D+2\eta)(a_1+\eta).
\]
The second reduces to
\[
  -D(a_2+\eta)-2\eta(a_1-\eta)
  =
  -(D+2\eta)(a_2-\eta).
\]
Both identities follow immediately from \(a_2=a_1+D\).  Therefore the
one-level-III ansatz is compatible with every adjacent level-II exchange.
\end{proof}

A pair of level-III roots scatters by
\[
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm III}}(w_1,w_2)
  =
  \frac{w_1-w_2+\ii/g}{w_1-w_2-\ii/g}.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Single-copy nested Bethe--Yang equations]
\label{prop:planar-n4-single-copy-nested-bethe-yang}
In the spin-chain basis for one \(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)_{\rm c}\) copy, let
\(K^{\rm I}\), \(K^{\rm II}\), and \(K^{\rm III}\) be the numbers of
level-I momenta \(p_k\), level-II roots \(y_a\), and level-III roots \(w_b\).
Assume the asymptotic long-chain regime and the absence of singular
coincidences among the displayed factors.  The closed-chain periodicity
conditions are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \ee^{\ii p_kL}
  &=
  \prod_{\substack{j=1\\j\neq k}}^{K^{\rm I}}
  S^{{\rm I},{\rm I}}(p_j,p_k)
  \prod_{a=1}^{K^{\rm II}}
  S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}(y_a,p_k),
  \qquad
  k=1,\ldots,K^{\rm I},\\
  1
  &=
  \prod_{j=1}^{K^{\rm I}}
  S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}(y_a,p_j)^{-1}
  \prod_{b=1}^{K^{\rm III}}
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w_b,y_a),
  \qquad
  a=1,\ldots,K^{\rm II},\\
  1
  &=
  \prod_{a=1}^{K^{\rm II}}
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w_b,y_a)^{-1}
  \prod_{\substack{c=1\\c\neq b}}^{K^{\rm III}}
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm III}}(w_c,w_b),
  \qquad
  b=1,\ldots,K^{\rm III}.
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:planar-n4-single-copy-nested-bethe-yang}
\end{equation}
For a state with \(N_1,N_2,N_3,N_4\) excitations of types
\(\phi_1,\phi_2,\psi_1,\psi_2\), the nesting numbers are
\[
  K^{\rm I}=N_1+N_2+N_3+N_4,\qquad
  K^{\rm II}=2N_2+N_3+N_4,\qquad
  K^{\rm III}=N_2+N_4.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first equation is obtained by transporting the \(k\)-th level-I magnon
once around the trace.  It crosses every other physical magnon and every
level-II defect, hence the two products in the first line.  The second
equation transports the \(a\)-th level-II defect around the level-II chain.
Crossing a physical magnon in the reverse direction contributes
\(S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}(y_a,p_j)^{-1}\), while crossing a level-III defect
contributes \(S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w_b,y_a)\).  The third equation is the
same argument one nesting level higher.  The three equations are therefore
the Bethe--Yang periodicity conditions for the three Zamolodchikov--Faddeev
chains, not thermodynamic Bethe ansatz.  The formulas for
\(K^{\rm I},K^{\rm II},K^{\rm III}\) follow from the nesting content of each
letter: \(\phi_1\) contributes only level I, \(\psi_1\) contributes one
level-II root, \(\psi_2\) contributes one level-II and one level-III root,
and \(\phi_2\) is the two-level-II contact state carrying one level-III root.
\end{proof}

The dynamic spin-chain basis contains explicit \(Z^\pm\) markers.  In the
string basis one absorbs half of this marker into each boson:
\[
  \ket{\phi_A(p)}_{\rm str}=\ket{\phi_A(p)(Z^+)^{1/2}},
  \qquad
  \ket{\psi_\alpha(p)}_{\rm str}=\ket{\psi_\alpha(p)} .
\]
The scattering factors which enter
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-single-copy-nested-bethe-yang} are then replaced by
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  S^{{\rm I},{\rm I}}_{\rm str}(p_1,p_2)
  &=
  S^{{\rm I},{\rm I}}(p_1,p_2)
  \sqrt{\frac{x_1^+x_2^-}{x_1^-x_2^+}},\\
  S^{{\rm II},{\rm I}}_{\rm str}(y,p)
  &=
  \frac{y-x^-(p)}{y-x^+(p)}
  \sqrt{\frac{x^+(p)}{x^-(p)}},\\
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}_{\rm str}(w,y)
  &=
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm II}}(w,y),
  \qquad
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm III}}_{\rm str}(w_1,w_2)
  =
  S^{{\rm III},{\rm III}}(w_1,w_2).
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:planar-n4-string-basis-nesting-factors}
\end{equation}
For the full \(\mathfrak{su}(2|2)_{\rm c}\otimes
\mathfrak{su}(2|2)_{\rm c}\) magnon, the left and right copies have
independent roots \(y_a^{(\varsigma)},w_b^{(\varsigma)}\),
\(\varsigma=L,R\).  The physical level-I Bethe equation is obtained by
multiplying the left and right auxiliary factors and by using the chosen
physical vacuum scalar factor.  In the \(SU(2)\)-vacuum nesting, the
auxiliary insertion on the \(k\)-th physical magnon is
\[
  \prod_{\varsigma=L,R}\prod_{a=1}^{K^{\rm II}_{(\varsigma)}}
  \frac{y_a^{(\varsigma)}-x_k^-}{y_a^{(\varsigma)}-x_k^+}
  \sqrt{\frac{x_k^+}{x_k^-}}.
\]
In the alternative \(SL(2)\)-vacuum nesting this factor is inverted:
\[
  \prod_{\varsigma=L,R}\prod_{a=1}^{K^{\rm II}_{(\varsigma)}}
  \frac{y_a^{(\varsigma)}-x_k^+}{y_a^{(\varsigma)}-x_k^-}
  \sqrt{\frac{x_k^-}{x_k^+}}.
\]
This reciprocal relation is a nesting-frame statement, not a change of the
BES dressing phase.  Thus the nested Bethe equations are the
diagonalization of a matrix-valued factorized scattering problem, not
additional dynamical assumptions.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Asymptotic long-chain regime]
\label{ca:planar-n4-asymptotic-regime}
Equation~\eqref{eq:planar-n4-su2-aba} is an instance of
Assumption~\ref{ass:planar-n4-aba-regime}.  At weak coupling, wrapping
effects begin at the loop order at which planar interactions can close around
the trace.  At finite coupling, the distinction is between the asymptotic
Bethe--Yang problem and the finite-size problem solved by mirror TBA or the
quantum spectral curve.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{Weak-Coupling Reduction}

At small \(g\), \(x^\pm\) reduce to the one-loop rapidity variable:
\[
  x^\pm=\frac{1}{g}\left(u\pm\frac{\ii}{2}\right)+O(g).
\]
Then
\[
  \frac{x_j^+}{x_j^-}
  =
  \frac{u_j+\ii/2}{u_j-\ii/2}+O(g^2),
\]
and the rational part of \eqref{eq:planar-n4-su2-aba} reduces to
\[
  \frac{u_j-u_k+\ii}{u_j-u_k-\ii}+O(g^2).
\]
The dressing phase starts at higher weak-coupling order, so the leading
equation is the one-loop Bethe equation
\eqref{eq:planar-n4-one-loop-su2-bethe}.  The dispersion expands as
\[
  H(p)-1
  =
  8g^2\sin^2\frac p2
  -32g^4\sin^4\frac p2
  +256g^6\sin^6\frac p2
  +O(g^8).
\]
Using \(g^2=\lambda/(16\pi^2)\), the leading term agrees with the one-loop
normalization of Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-spectral-problem-spin-chains}.

\section{Bound States and the Large-Spin Cusp Limit}

A pole of the \(SL(2)\) scalar factor at \(x_1^-=x_2^+\) gives a Bethe
string.  A \(Q\)-magnon bound state is described by
\[
  u_j=u+\frac{\ii}{2}(Q-2j+1),
  \qquad j=1,\ldots,Q,
\]
and has
\[
  \ee^{\ii p}=\frac{x_1^+}{x_Q^-},
  \qquad
  E_Q(p)=\sqrt{Q^2+16g^2\sin^2\frac p2}.
\]
Its scattering with another bound state is obtained by fusing the elementary
S-matrices over all constituent rapidities and projecting to the short
\(Q\)-particle representation.  This fusion step is one of the inputs that
later reappears in mirror TBA kernels.

In the \(SL(2)\) sector, twist-\(L\) spin-\(S\) operators are
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(D_+^{s_1}Z\cdots D_+^{s_L}Z),
  \qquad \sum_{j=1}^Ls_j=S .
\]
The one-loop Bethe equations in this noncompact sector are
\begin{equation}
  \left(\frac{u_k+\ii/2}{u_k-\ii/2}\right)^L
  =
  \prod_{\substack{j=1\\j\neq k}}^S
  \frac{u_k-u_j-\ii}{u_k-u_j+\ii},
  \qquad k=1,\ldots,S,
  \label{eq:planar-n4-sl2-one-loop-bethe}
\end{equation}
with cyclicity selecting zero total spin-chain momentum.  For the minimal
large-spin trajectory the roots are real, symmetric under \(u\mapsto -u\),
and have mode numbers \(\pm1\) in the logarithmic equation.  Write
\[
  u_k=S\bar u_k,
  \qquad
  \int_{-a}^a\bar\rho_0(\bar u)\,\dd\bar u=1,
\]
where \(\bar\rho_0\) is the continuum density of the scaled roots.  In the
limit \(S\gg L\), the derivative of the continuum logarithmic equation is
encoded by the resolvent
\[
  G_0(z)=\int_{-a}^a\frac{\bar\rho_0(v)}{z-v}\,\dd v,
  \qquad
  G_0(z)=\frac1z+O(z^{-2}).
\]
The discontinuity convention is
\[
  G_0(y+\ii0)-G_0(y-\ii0)=-2\pi\ii\,\bar\rho_0(y).
\]
After differentiating the continuum Bethe equation, the boundary values obey
\[
  G_0'(y+\ii0)+G_0'(y-\ii0)=4\pi\delta(y)
  \qquad (-a<y<a).
\]
The unique solution with one symmetric cut and the displayed normalization is
\begin{equation}
  G_0(z)
  =
  -\ii
  \log\frac{\sqrt{4z^2-1}+\ii}{\sqrt{4z^2-1}-\ii},
  \qquad a=\frac12,
  \label{eq:planar-n4-sl2-cusp-resolvent}
\end{equation}
where the square root is chosen so that \(\sqrt{4z^2-1}\sim2z\) at infinity.
Its density is
\begin{equation}
  \bar\rho_0(y)
  =
  \frac1\pi
  \log
  \frac{1+\sqrt{1-4y^2}}{1-\sqrt{1-4y^2}},
  \qquad |y|<\frac12 .
  \label{eq:planar-n4-sl2-cusp-density}
\end{equation}
The one-loop anomalous dimension is
\[
  \gamma^{(1)}
  =
  2g^2\sum_{k=1}^S\frac{1}{u_k^2+1/4}.
\]
The singular region near \(u=0\) is resolved by evaluating the resolvent at
\(z=\ii/(2S)\):
\[
  \gamma^{(1)}
  =
  -4g^2\,\operatorname{Im}G_0\!\left(\frac{\ii}{2S}\right)+O(S^0g^2).
\]
Using the continuation
\[
  G_0\!\left(\frac{\ii}{2S}\right)
  =
  -\ii
  \log
  \frac{\sqrt{1+S^{-2}}+1}{\sqrt{1+S^{-2}}-1},
\]
one obtains
\begin{equation}
  \Delta-S-L
  =
  \gamma^{(1)}
  =
  8g^2\log S+O(S^0g^2,g^4).
  \label{eq:planar-n4-sl2-one-loop-cusp-log}
\end{equation}
With the all-loop ABA and the BES dressing phase, the density fluctuation
satisfies the BES integral equation.  The resulting scaling function begins
\[
  \Delta-S-L=f(g)\log S+O(S^0),
  \qquad
  f(g)=8g^2-\frac{8\pi^2}{3}g^4+\frac{88\pi^4}{45}g^6+O(g^8).
\]
This \(f(g)\) is the lightlike cusp anomalous dimension in the convention of
Chapter~\ref{ch:planar-n4-quantum-spectral-curve-hexagon}.  The equality is
a structural matching between large-spin single-trace operators and the
Wilson-line cusp observable; the nonperturbative gauge-theory proof remains
outside the present monograph.
