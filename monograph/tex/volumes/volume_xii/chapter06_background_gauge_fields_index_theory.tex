\chapter{Background Gauge Fields and Index Theory}
\label{ch:curved-background-gauge-fields-index-theory}

\ConventionsEuclidean

Background gauge fields are not auxiliary decorations.  They are sources for
global currents, they specify the bundles in which charged fields take
values, and for chiral fermions they determine both zero-mode selection
rules and anomaly lines.  This chapter develops the index-theoretic part of
that structure with explicit conventions.  The analytic input is the index
of a coupled Dirac operator; the QFT input is the Berezin integral over its
zero modes and the determinant line of its nonzero modes.

\section{Spinors, Bundles, and Curvature Conventions}

Let \(M^{2n}\) be a closed oriented Riemannian spin manifold.  The complex
spinor bundle splits into chiral pieces
\[
  S=S^+\oplus S^- .
\]
Let \(E\to M\) be a Hermitian vector bundle with unitary connection
\(\nabla^E\).  In the characteristic-class formulas of this chapter the
curvature \(F_E=(\nabla^E)^2\) is skew-Hermitian, so
\[
  \operatorname{ch}(E)
  =
  \operatorname{tr}_E\exp\!\left(\frac{\ii F_E}{2\pi}\right)
\]
is a real even cohomology class.  If a physics convention uses Hermitian
generators and Hermitian curvature \(F_{\rm phys}\), then
\[
  F_E=-\ii F_{\rm phys}
\]
in this formula.  All traces in \(\operatorname{ch}(E)\) are traces in the
fermion representation, not necessarily the trace used in the Yang-Mills
kinetic term.

The coupled Dirac operator is
\[
  D_A:\Gamma(S\otimes E)\longrightarrow\Gamma(S\otimes E),
  \qquad
  D_A=\gamma^\mu\nabla_\mu^{S\otimes E}.
\]
With respect to chirality,
\[
  D_A=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  0 & D_A^-\\
  D_A^+ & 0
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  D_A^+:\Gamma(S^+\otimes E)\to\Gamma(S^-\otimes E).
\]
The analytic index is
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_A^+)
  =
  \dim\ker D_A^+-\dim\ker D_A^- .
  \label{eq:index-analytic-definition}
\end{equation}
Because \(M\) is compact and \(D_A^+\) is elliptic, this is a finite integer.

\section{McKean--Singer Supertrace}

Let \(\Gamma\) denote the chirality operator, acting by \(+1\) on \(S^+\) and
by \(-1\) on \(S^-\).  The heat operator \(e^{-tD_A^2}\) is trace class for
\(t>0\).

\begin{proposition}[McKean--Singer identity]
\label{prop:index-mckean-singer}
For every \(t>0\),
\[
  \operatorname{Str}\,e^{-tD_A^2}
  :=
  \operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\Gamma e^{-tD_A^2}\right)
  =
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_A^+).
  \label{eq:index-mckean-singer}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The nonzero spectrum of \(D_A^2\) on \(S^+\otimes E\) is paired with the
nonzero spectrum on \(S^-\otimes E\).  Indeed, if
\(\psi_+\in\Gamma(S^+\otimes E)\) satisfies
\[
  D_A^-D_A^+\psi_+=\lambda\psi_+,\qquad \lambda>0,
\]
then \(D_A^+\psi_+\neq0\) and
\[
  D_A^+D_A^-(D_A^+\psi_+)=\lambda(D_A^+\psi_+).
\]
The inverse pairing is \(\psi_-\mapsto D_A^-\psi_-\).  Hence the positive
eigenvalue contributions to
\[
  \operatorname{Tr}_{S^+}e^{-tD_A^-D_A^+}
  -
  \operatorname{Tr}_{S^-}e^{-tD_A^+D_A^-}
\]
cancel.  The zero eigenspaces are precisely \(\ker D_A^+\) and
\(\ker D_A^-\), so the supertrace is
\(\dim\ker D_A^+-\dim\ker D_A^-\).
\end{proof}

This proof is finite-dimensional after elliptic spectral theory supplies the
discrete spectrum and heat-kernel trace class property.  It is the analytic
bridge between zero-mode counting and local curvature densities.

\section{Local Index Theorem}

Let \(R\) denote the Riemannian curvature two-form of \(TM\).  The
\(\widehat A\)-class is
\[
  \widehat A(TM)
  =
  \det\nolimits^{1/2}
  \left(\frac{R/4\pi}{\sinh(R/4\pi)}\right)
  =
  1-\frac{p_1(TM)}{24}
  +\frac{7p_1(TM)^2-4p_2(TM)}{5760}+\cdots .
\]

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Atiyah--Singer local index theorem]
\label{thm:index-atiyah-singer-local}
For the chiral Dirac operator coupled to \(E\),
\[
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_A^+)
  =
  \int_M
  \left[\widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}(E)\right]_{2n}.
  \label{eq:index-atiyah-singer}
\]
Equivalently, the local heat-kernel density of the supertrace has
\[
  \lim_{t\to0^+}{\rm str}\,K_t(x,x)\,d{\rm vol}_g
  =
  \left[\widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}(E)\right]_{2n}.
\]
\end{quotedtheorem}

The theorem is a theorem of differential geometry, not an axiom of QFT.  This
monograph uses it as mathematical input while deriving the QFT consequences:
zero-mode selection rules, anomaly polynomials, determinant lines, and
inflow.

In four dimensions the formula becomes
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_A^+)
  =
  \int_M
  \left[
  \operatorname{ch}_2(E)
  -
  \frac{\operatorname{rank}(E)}{24}p_1(TM)
  \right].
  \label{eq:index-four-dimensional-formula}
\end{equation}
For an \(SU(N)\) bundle \(E_R\) associated to a representation \(R\),
\[
  c_1(E_R)=0,
  \qquad
  \int_M\operatorname{ch}_2(E_R)=T_\Delta(R)\,k .
  \label{eq:index-trace-delta-instanton-number}
\]
Here \(k\in\mathbb Z\) is the instanton number in the fundamental bundle and
\(T_\Delta(R)\) is the quadratic index in the monograph's trace-delta
normalization
\[
  \operatorname{tr}_R(t^a_Rt^b_R)=T_\Delta(R)\delta^{ab},
  \qquad
  T_\Delta(\square)=1.
\]
Thus \(T_\Delta({\rm adj})=2N\).  This is the same normalization compatible
with the Yang-Mills kinetic term
\[
  \frac{1}{4g^2}\int\operatorname{tr}_\Delta(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}).
\]

\section{Four-Dimensional Examples}

On a spin four-manifold with \(p_1(TM)=0\), a unit \(SU(N)\) instanton gives
\[
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_{\square}^+)=1,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_{\rm adj}^+)=2N .
\]
For \(SU(2)\), the fundamental Weyl fermion has one complex zero mode in the
orientation convention of \eqref{eq:index-trace-delta-instanton-number}, and
an adjoint Weyl fermion has four complex zero modes.  Reversing the
orientation of the gauge instanton reverses the sign of the index and swaps
the chirality of the zero modes.

For an Abelian line bundle \(L^q\) of charge \(q\),
\[
  \operatorname{ch}(L^q)=e^{q x},
  \qquad x=c_1(L).
\]
On \(T^4\) with \(p_1(TT^4)=0\), take integral two-forms \(a,b\) with
\[
  \int_{T^4}a\wedge b=1,
  \qquad a\wedge a=b\wedge b=0,
\]
and \(x=m_1a+m_2b\).  Then
\[
  \operatorname{Ind}(D_{L^q}^+)
  =
  \int_{T^4}\frac12q^2x^2
  =
  q^2m_1m_2 .
  \label{eq:index-u1-t4-example}
\]
This example is useful because it is completely Abelian: the index is the
intersection pairing of magnetic fluxes through complementary two-tori.

\section{Zero Modes and Berezin Selection Rules}

Let \(\psi\) be a left-handed Weyl fermion valued in \(E\).  Decompose it in
an orthonormal eigenbasis of the coupled Dirac operator.  If
\(\{\zeta_1,\ldots,\zeta_m\}\) is a basis of \(\ker D_A^+\), then the
Grassmann field has zero-mode coordinates
\[
  \psi_0(x)=\sum_{i=1}^m\eta_i\zeta_i(x),
  \qquad \eta_i\eta_j=-\eta_j\eta_i .
\]

\begin{proposition}[Finite-dimensional zero-mode rule]
\label{prop:index-berezin-zero-mode-rule}
After nonzero modes have been paired, the zero-mode part of a fermionic path
integral is
\[
  \int d\eta_m\cdots d\eta_1\,\mathcal O(\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_m).
\]
It vanishes unless the insertion \(\mathcal O\) contains the top monomial
\(\eta_1\cdots\eta_m\).  Therefore a background with \(m\) unpaired Weyl
zero modes requires \(m\) corresponding fermion insertions for a nonzero
correlator.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Berezin integration is the linear functional fixed by
\[
  \int d\eta\,1=0,
  \qquad
  \int d\eta\,\eta=1.
\]
For \(m\) generators it extracts the coefficient of
\(\eta_1\cdots\eta_m\) in the declared order.  All lower-degree monomials are
annihilated by at least one of the integrations.
\end{proof}

For a vectorlike Dirac fermion in representation \(R\), written in
two-component language as left-handed fields in \(R\) and \(\overline R\),
a charge-\(k\) instanton produces \(T_\Delta(R)k\) zero modes in each of the
two conjugate chiral variables, with chirality determined by the instanton
orientation.  The resulting finite-dimensional Berezin integral is the
precise origin of the fermion-number selection rule usually encoded by the
't Hooft vertex.

\section{Anomaly Polynomial and Descent}

For a chiral fermion in \(2n\) dimensions, the anomaly polynomial is the
\((2n+2)\)-form
\begin{equation}
  I_{2n+2}
  =
  \left[\widehat A(TM)\operatorname{ch}(E)\right]_{2n+2}.
  \label{eq:index-anomaly-polynomial}
\end{equation}
Choose a Chern-Simons form \(I_{2n+1}\) satisfying
\[
  dI_{2n+1}=I_{2n+2}.
\]
For an infinitesimal background gauge transformation with parameter
\(\alpha\),
\[
  \delta_\alpha I_{2n+1}=dI_{2n}^{(1)}(\alpha).
\]
The local anomalous variation of the Euclidean effective action is
\[
  \delta_\alpha W[A,g]
  =
  2\pi\ii\int_M I_{2n}^{(1)}(\alpha),
  \label{eq:index-local-descent-variation}
\]
with an overall sign changed if the opposite chirality convention is chosen.
The sign is not independent data; it is fixed by the orientation of
\(D_A^+\), the definition of \(W=-\log Z\), and the chirality assignment of
the fermion.

In four-dimensional QFT, the relevant six-form is
\begin{equation}
  I_6
  =
  \operatorname{ch}_3(E)
  -
  \frac{p_1(TM)}{24}\operatorname{ch}_1(E).
  \label{eq:index-four-dimensional-anomaly-polynomial}
\end{equation}
The first term gives cubic gauge anomalies.  The second gives the mixed
gauge-gravitational anomaly for a \(U(1)\) background.  For a semisimple
group in a traceless representation, \(\operatorname{ch}_1(E)=0\), so the
mixed term is absent.  A background symmetry can be gauged only after the
total anomaly line has been trivialized; cancellation of the local polynomial
is necessary but does not by itself eliminate global anomalies.

\section{Determinant Line and Families}

As the metric and background connection vary, the chiral fermion determinant
is not naturally a complex number.  It is a section of the determinant line
\[
  \operatorname{Det}D
  =
  \bigwedge\nolimits^{\max}\ker D_A^+
  \otimes
  \left(\bigwedge\nolimits^{\max}\ker D_A^-\right)^\ast
\]
over background-field space.  The local anomaly is the curvature of this
line with its natural connection.  Global anomalies are its holonomies around
loops.  Thus a well-defined background-field partition function requires a
trivialization of the determinant line, not merely a vanishing local
curvature form.

The next chapter develops the eta-invariant formula for these holonomies.
The present chapter supplies the local and zero-mode input: the index counts
the imbalance of chiral zero modes, while the family of such indices
organizes the determinant line.

\begin{openproblem}[Index-theoretic anomaly line in interacting QFT]
\label{op:index-anomaly-line-interacting-qft}
Extend the determinant-line and index-theoretic description from free chiral
fermions coupled to backgrounds to interacting continuum QFTs with composite
currents, contact terms, and extended operators, while retaining an explicit
comparison with local anomaly descent and global anomaly holonomy.
\end{openproblem}
