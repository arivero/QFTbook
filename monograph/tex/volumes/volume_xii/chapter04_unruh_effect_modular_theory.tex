\chapter{The Unruh Effect and Wedge Modular Theory}
\label{ch:curved-unruh-effect-modular-theory}

\ConventionsLorentzian

The Unruh effect is the thermal character of the Minkowski vacuum when
observables are restricted to a Rindler wedge and time evolution is generated
by Lorentz boosts.  The precise local-QFT theorem is the
Bisognano--Wichmann theorem; the accelerated-detector calculation is a
concrete consequence in free-field examples.
The chapter uses wedge locality, spectrum positivity, tube analyticity, and
the KMS condition.  It is a fixed-background AQFT statement, not a
perturbative interacting curved-spacetime construction; the microlocal and
pAQFT machinery earlier in the volume supplies the state and composite-field
discipline used by later curved-background applications.

\section{Rindler Wedges and Boost Flow}

In \(\mathbb M^D\), define the right Rindler wedge
\[
  W_R=\{x\in\mathbb M^D:x^1>|x^0|\}.
\]
The boost preserving \(W_R\) is
\[
  x^0(\eta)=x^0\cosh\eta+x^1\sinh\eta,\qquad
  x^1(\eta)=x^1\cosh\eta+x^0\sinh\eta .
\]
Let \(K\) be the self-adjoint generator of this boost representation,
\[
  U(\eta)=\exp(\ii\eta K).
\]
For a uniformly accelerated observer with proper acceleration \(a\), the
Rindler time parameter is \(\eta=a\tau\), where \(\tau\) is proper time.

\section{KMS Statement}

Let \(\mathcal A(W_R)\) be the von Neumann algebra generated by observables
localized in \(W_R\).  The vacuum state restricted to \(\mathcal A(W_R)\) is
KMS at inverse temperature \(2\pi\) with respect to boost time if, for
\(A,B\in\mathcal A(W_R)\), the function
\[
  F_{A,B}(\eta)=\langle\Omega,A\,U(\eta)B\,U(\eta)^{-1}\Omega\rangle
\]
extends to the strip \(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi\) and satisfies the KMS
boundary relation
\[
  F_{A,B}(\eta+2\pi\ii)
  =
  \langle\Omega,U(\eta)B\,U(\eta)^{-1}A\Omega\rangle .
\]
In proper time, the temperature is therefore
\[
  T_{\rm Unruh}=\frac{a}{2\pi}.
\]

\section{Complex Boost Geometry}

The factor \(2\pi\) in the preceding definition is fixed by the complexified
boost geometry.  This subsection records the elementary geometry because it
is the part of the Unruh effect that is often hidden behind a detector
calculation.

Let \(\Lambda_\eta\) denote the boost in the \((x^0,x^1)\)-plane:
\[
  (\Lambda_\eta x)^0=x^0\cosh\eta+x^1\sinh\eta,\qquad
  (\Lambda_\eta x)^1=x^1\cosh\eta+x^0\sinh\eta,
\]
with transverse coordinates fixed.  For complex \(\eta=t+\ii s\), write
\(\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta x\) for the imaginary part of the complex
vector \(\Lambda_\eta x\).

\paragraph{Imaginary part of a complex boost.}
If \(y\in W_R\) and \(\eta=t+\ii s\), then
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \operatorname{Im}(\Lambda_\eta y)^0
  &=\sin s\,(\Lambda_t y)^1,\\
  \operatorname{Im}(\Lambda_\eta y)^1
  &=\sin s\,(\Lambda_t y)^0 .
\end{aligned}
\]
Consequently \(\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta y\) is future timelike for
\(0<s<\pi\), past timelike for \(\pi<s<2\pi\), and
\[
  \Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}y=-\Lambda_t y
\]
in the \((0,1)\)-plane.  In particular \(\Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}W_R\) is the
left wedge
\[
  W_L=\{x\in\mathbb M^D:x^1<-|x^0|\}.
\]
Every point of \(W_R\) is spacelike separated from every point of \(W_L\).
These are elementary hyperbolic-function identities, recorded here because
they fix the tube domain used in the KMS strip.  The formulas follow from
\[
  \cosh(t+\ii s)=\cosh t\cos s+\ii\sinh t\sin s,\qquad
  \sinh(t+\ii s)=\sinh t\cos s+\ii\cosh t\sin s .
\]
Since \(y\in W_R\), also \(\Lambda_t y\in W_R\), so
\((\Lambda_t y)^1>|(\Lambda_t y)^0|\).  The vector
\(((\Lambda_t y)^1,(\Lambda_t y)^0,0,\ldots,0)\) is therefore future
timelike in the mostly-plus convention.  Multiplication by \(\sin s\) gives
the future/past alternatives.

At \(s=\pi\), \(\cosh(t+\ii\pi)=-\cosh t\) and
\(\sinh(t+\ii\pi)=-\sinh t\), hence the stated left-wedge image.  If
\(x\in W_R\) and \(\ell\in W_L\), then
\[
  x^1-\ell^1>|x^0|+|\ell^0|\ge |x^0-\ell^0|.
\]
Thus \(x-\ell\) is spacelike.

\section{Bisognano--Wichmann Boundary and the Local Model}

\begin{remark}[Bisognano--Wichmann theorem boundary]
\label{rem:bisognano-wichmann-boundary}
The Bisognano--Wichmann theorem in wedge-modular form is a structural theorem
about the standard pair \((\mathcal A(W_R),\Omega)\), where
\(\mathcal A(W_R)\) is the right-wedge algebra and \(\Omega\) is the vacuum
vector.  Under the Wightman or Haag--Kastler hypotheses of positivity,
Poincare covariance, locality, spectrum condition, and wedge cyclicity and
separatingness of the vacuum, its conclusion is that the Tomita--Takesaki
modular automorphism group of \((\mathcal A(W_R),\Omega)\) is the Lorentz
boost flow preserving \(W_R\).  With the boost convention
\(U(\eta)=\exp(\ii\eta K)\), this is equivalently the KMS condition at inverse
boost temperature \(2\pi\).

The full theorem is kept here as an external AQFT theorem boundary.  Its proof
requires Tomita--Takesaki modular theory, the analytic continuation supplied
by the spectrum condition, covariance under complexified boosts, and wedge
locality at imaginary boost angle \(\ii\pi\).  The self-contained theorem in
this chapter is the free massive scalar model calculation below: it proves the
same strip analyticity and KMS boundary relation directly for the polynomial
wedge algebra, with every analytic domain and ordering step visible.
\end{remark}

\section{Free Scalar Wedge KMS}

We now prove the wedge KMS property for the free scalar polynomial algebra.
The result is a complete model calculation with all orderings and analytic
domains visible; the full Bisognano--Wichmann theorem requires the additional
net-theoretic hypotheses stated later in this chapter.

\begin{definition}[Free scalar two-point distribution]
\label{def:unruh-free-scalar-two-point}
For \(m>0\), the massive scalar vacuum two-point distribution on
\(\mathbb M^D\) is
\[
  \Delta_+^{(m)}(z)
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R^{D-1}}
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}{\bf p}}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2E_{\bf p}}\,
  e^{-\ii E_{\bf p}z^0+\ii{\bf p}\cdot{\bf z}},
  \qquad
  E_{\bf p}=\sqrt{|{\bf p}|^2+m^2}.
\]
For test functions \(f,g\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb M^D)\),
\[
  \langle\Omega,\phi(f)\phi(g)\Omega\rangle
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(x-y)\,\dd x\,\dd y .
\]
\end{definition}

The distribution \(\Delta_+^{(m)}(z)\) is the boundary value of a holomorphic
function in the past tube
\[
  \mathcal T_-=\{z\in\mathbb C^D:\operatorname{Im}z\in V_-\},
\]
where \(V_-\) is the open past timelike cone.  Indeed, if
\(\operatorname{Im}z=u\in V_-\), then
\[
  E_{\bf p}u^0-{\bf p}\cdot{\bf u}\le -c_u E_{\bf p}
\]
for some \(c_u>0\) on every compact subcone of \(V_-\), and the defining
mass-shell integral is absolutely convergent after the usual polynomial
bounds from differentiating in \(z\).

\begin{theorem}[Free scalar wedge KMS]
\label{thm:unruh-free-scalar-wedge-kms}
Let \(f,g\in C_c^\infty(W_R)\).  Let the boost automorphism act on smeared
fields by
\[
  \alpha_\eta(\phi(g))=\phi(g_\eta),
  \qquad
  g_\eta(x)=g(\Lambda_{-\eta}x).
\]
Then
\[
  F_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \langle\Omega,\phi(f)\alpha_\eta(\phi(g))\Omega\rangle
\]
is the lower boundary value of a function holomorphic in the strip
\[
  0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi ,
\]
continuous as a distributional boundary value on the two edges, and its
upper boundary satisfies
\begin{equation}
  F_{f,g}(\eta+2\pi\ii)
  =
  \langle\Omega,\alpha_\eta(\phi(g))\phi(f)\Omega\rangle .
  \label{eq:unruh-free-two-point-kms-boundary}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For real \(\eta\), change variables in \(g_\eta\) to write
\begin{equation}
  F_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(x-\Lambda_\eta y)\,\dd x\,\dd y .
  \label{eq:unruh-free-two-point-lower-boundary}
\end{equation}
For \(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<\pi\), the complex-boost geometry above implies
that
\[
  \operatorname{Im}(x-\Lambda_\eta y)
  =
  -\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta y
  \in V_-
\]
for all \(x,y\in W_R\).  Since the supports of \(f\) and \(g\) are compact
subsets of \(W_R\), the image stays in a compact subcone of the past tube on
compact sub-strips.  Thus
\[
  F^{(1)}_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(x-\Lambda_\eta y)\,\dd x\,\dd y
\]
is holomorphic for \(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<\pi\).

For \(\pi<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi\), the same geometry says that
\(\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta y\in V_-\).  Hence
\[
  F^{(2)}_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(\Lambda_\eta y-x)\,\dd x\,\dd y
\]
is holomorphic on the upper half of the strip.

It remains to glue the two holomorphic functions across
\(\operatorname{Im}\eta=\pi\).  At \(\eta=t+\ii\pi\), the point
\(\Lambda_\eta y\) lies in \(W_L\).  The complex-boost geometry above
therefore makes \(x\) and \(\Lambda_\eta y\) spacelike separated.  The free
scalar commutator is
\[
  [\phi(x),\phi(y)]=\ii\Delta_m(x-y)\mathbf 1,
\]
where \(\Delta_m\) is the difference of the retarded and advanced
fundamental solutions of \(\Box-m^2\).  Hyperbolic finite propagation gives
\(\Delta_m(z)=0\) for spacelike \(z\).  Therefore the two boundary values
\[
  \Delta_+^{(m)}(x-\Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}y)
  \quad\hbox{and}\quad
  \Delta_+^{(m)}(\Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}y-x)
\]
agree after smearing with \(f(x)g(y)\).  The edge-of-the-wedge gluing in this
one-complex-variable strip is the following elementary distributional fact:
if two holomorphic functions on adjacent strips have the same distributional
boundary value on the common real line, they define a holomorphic function on
the union.  Applying it here gives a holomorphic \(F_{f,g}\) on
\(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi\).

The lower boundary is
\eqref{eq:unruh-free-two-point-lower-boundary}.  The upper boundary is
obtained from \(F^{(2)}\) because
\(\Lambda_{\eta+2\pi\ii}=\Lambda_\eta\):
\[
  \lim_{s\uparrow2\pi}F^{(2)}_{f,g}(t+\ii s)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(\Lambda_t y-x)\,\dd x\,\dd y
  =
  \langle\Omega,\alpha_t(\phi(g))\phi(f)\Omega\rangle .
\]
This is \eqref{eq:unruh-free-two-point-kms-boundary}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Polynomial wedge algebra.}
Let \(\mathcal P(W_R)\) be the unital \(\ast\)-algebra generated by the
smeared free fields \(\phi(f)\), \(f\in C_c^\infty(W_R)\), modulo the free
field equation and canonical commutation relations.  The vacuum state
restricted to \(\mathcal P(W_R)\) is KMS at inverse boost temperature
\(2\pi\) with respect to \(\alpha_\eta\).  The analytic input remains
Theorem~\ref{thm:unruh-free-scalar-wedge-kms}; the passage from fields to
polynomials is Wick expansion:
every vacuum \(n\)-point function of the free scalar field is a finite sum of
products of two-point functions by Wick's theorem.  If a polynomial \(B\) is
boosted by complex \(\eta\), each two-point factor connecting a field in
\(A\) to a field in \(B\) is one of the strip-holomorphic functions proved in
Theorem~\ref{thm:unruh-free-scalar-wedge-kms}.  Pairings entirely inside
\(A\) or entirely inside \(B\) are independent of \(\eta\) or are invariant
under the common boost.  Products and finite sums preserve holomorphy in the
strip.  The boundary relation follows term by term from
\eqref{eq:unruh-free-two-point-kms-boundary}; the reordering at the upper
edge is legitimate because the free commutator has already supplied the
spacelike gluing at imaginary boost time \(\pi\).  This is exactly the KMS
condition for all polynomial elements.

\section{Worldline Specialization}

For a uniformly accelerated trajectory in \(D=2\),
\[
  x^0(\tau)=a^{-1}\sinh(a\tau),\qquad
  x^1(\tau)=a^{-1}\cosh(a\tau),
\]
the invariant separation between two points is
\[
  -(x(\tau)-x(0))^2
  =
  \frac{4}{a^2}\sinh^2\!\left(\frac{a(\tau-\ii0)}{2}\right).
\]
The Wightman function along the trajectory is therefore a function of
\(\sinh^2(a(\tau-\ii0)/2)\).  Since
\[
  \sinh\!\left(\frac{a(\tau+\ii 2\pi/a)}{2}\right)
  =
  -\sinh\!\left(\frac{a\tau}{2}\right),
\]
the two-point function has imaginary proper-time periodicity
\(\beta=2\pi/a\) with the KMS ordering prescription.  This verifies the
two-point form of the free-field wedge KMS theorem on a single orbit.

\section{Detector Response}

An Unruh--DeWitt detector with energy gap \(E>0\) coupled linearly to the
field has transition rate proportional to
\[
  \dot P(E)
  =
  \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}d\tau\,
  e^{-\ii E\tau}
  W(x(\tau),x(0)).
\]
The KMS condition implies detailed balance
\[
  \dot P(-E)=e^{\beta E}\dot P(E),
  \qquad \beta=\frac{2\pi}{a}.
\]
Thus the detector response is thermal at temperature \(a/(2\pi)\) when the
coupling is weak, the detector is operated for a long time, and switching
effects have been separated from the stationary rate.

\paragraph{Detailed balance from the KMS boundary relation.}
Let \(G(\tau)\) be a tempered distribution which is the stationary two-point
function sampled by a detector along a time-translation orbit, and suppose
that it has the KMS boundary relation at inverse temperature \(\beta\) in the
following convention:
\[
  G(\tau-\ii\beta)=G(-\tau)
\]
as boundary values of one holomorphic function in the strip
\(-\beta<\operatorname{Im}\tau<0\).  If the stationary transition rate
\[
  \dot P(E)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-\ii E\tau}G(\tau)\,\dd\tau
\]
exists as the Fourier transform of this tempered distribution at
\(\pm E\), \(E>0\), and if the contour shift is justified by inserting a
Schwartz switching function and then taking the stationary limit, then
\[
  \dot P(-E)=e^{\beta E}\dot P(E).
\]
With a Schwartz switching function, all integrals below are ordinary
absolutely convergent contour integrals; the stated distributional formula is
the limit after the switching function is removed.  Start from
\[
  \dot P(-E)
  =
  \int e^{\ii E\tau}G(\tau)\,\dd\tau
  =
  \int e^{-\ii E\tau}G(-\tau)\,\dd\tau .
\]
Use the KMS boundary relation \(G(-\tau)=G(\tau-\ii\beta)\):
\[
  \dot P(-E)
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R}e^{-\ii E\tau}G(\tau-\ii\beta)\,\dd\tau .
\]
Shift the contour to \(u=\tau-\ii\beta\), so
\(\tau=u+\ii\beta\).  The exponential becomes
\[
  e^{-\ii E(u+\ii\beta)}=e^{\beta E}e^{-\ii Eu}.
\]
Holomorphy in the strip gives
\[
  \dot P(-E)=e^{\beta E}\int_{\mathbb R}e^{-\ii Eu}G(u)\,\dd u
  =
  e^{\beta E}\dot P(E).
\]
