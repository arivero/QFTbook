\chapter{The Unruh Effect and Wedge Modular Theory}
\label{ch:curved-unruh-effect-modular-theory}

\ConventionsLorentzian

The Unruh effect is the thermal character of the Minkowski vacuum when
observables are restricted to a Rindler wedge and time evolution is generated
by Lorentz boosts.  The precise local-QFT theorem is the
Bisognano--Wichmann theorem; the accelerated-detector calculation is a
concrete consequence in free-field examples.
The chapter uses wedge locality, spectrum positivity, tube analyticity, and
the KMS condition.  It is a fixed-background AQFT statement, not a
perturbative interacting curved-spacetime construction; the microlocal and
pAQFT machinery earlier in the volume supplies the state and composite-field
discipline used by later curved-background applications.

\section{Rindler Wedges and Boost Flow}

In \(\mathbb M^D\), define the right Rindler wedge
\[
  W_R=\{x\in\mathbb M^D:x^1>|x^0|\}.
\]
The boost preserving \(W_R\) is
\[
  x^0(\eta)=x^0\cosh\eta+x^1\sinh\eta,\qquad
  x^1(\eta)=x^1\cosh\eta+x^0\sinh\eta .
\]
Let \(K\) be the self-adjoint generator of this boost representation,
\[
  U(\eta)=\exp(\ii\eta K).
\]
For a uniformly accelerated observer with proper acceleration \(a\), the
Rindler time parameter is \(\eta=a\tau\), where \(\tau\) is proper time.

\section{KMS Statement}

Let \(\mathcal A(W_R)\) be the von Neumann algebra generated by observables
localized in \(W_R\).  The vacuum state restricted to \(\mathcal A(W_R)\) is
KMS at inverse temperature \(2\pi\) with respect to boost time if, for
\(A,B\in\mathcal A(W_R)\), the function
\[
  F_{A,B}(\eta)=\langle\Omega,A\,U(\eta)B\,U(\eta)^{-1}\Omega\rangle
\]
extends to the strip \(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi\) and satisfies the KMS
boundary relation
\[
  F_{A,B}(\eta+2\pi\ii)
  =
  \langle\Omega,U(\eta)B\,U(\eta)^{-1}A\Omega\rangle .
\]
In proper time, the temperature is therefore
\[
  T_{\rm Unruh}=\frac{a}{2\pi}.
\]

\section{Complex Boost Geometry}

The factor \(2\pi\) in the preceding definition is fixed by the complexified
boost geometry.  This subsection records the elementary geometry because it
is the part of the Unruh effect that is often hidden behind a detector
calculation.

Let \(\Lambda_\eta\) denote the boost in the \((x^0,x^1)\)-plane:
\[
  (\Lambda_\eta x)^0=x^0\cosh\eta+x^1\sinh\eta,\qquad
  (\Lambda_\eta x)^1=x^1\cosh\eta+x^0\sinh\eta,
\]
with transverse coordinates fixed.  For complex \(\eta=t+\ii s\), write
\(\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta x\) for the imaginary part of the complex
vector \(\Lambda_\eta x\).

\paragraph{Imaginary part of a complex boost.}
If \(y\in W_R\) and \(\eta=t+\ii s\), then
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \operatorname{Im}(\Lambda_\eta y)^0
  &=\sin s\,(\Lambda_t y)^1,\\
  \operatorname{Im}(\Lambda_\eta y)^1
  &=\sin s\,(\Lambda_t y)^0 .
\end{aligned}
\]
Consequently \(\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta y\) is future timelike for
\(0<s<\pi\), past timelike for \(\pi<s<2\pi\), and
\[
  \Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}y=-\Lambda_t y
\]
in the \((0,1)\)-plane.  In particular \(\Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}W_R\) is the
left wedge
\[
  W_L=\{x\in\mathbb M^D:x^1<-|x^0|\}.
\]
Every point of \(W_R\) is spacelike separated from every point of \(W_L\).
These are elementary hyperbolic-function identities, recorded here because
they fix the tube domain used in the KMS strip.  The formulas follow from
\[
  \cosh(t+\ii s)=\cosh t\cos s+\ii\sinh t\sin s,\qquad
  \sinh(t+\ii s)=\sinh t\cos s+\ii\cosh t\sin s .
\]
Since \(y\in W_R\), also \(\Lambda_t y\in W_R\), so
\((\Lambda_t y)^1>|(\Lambda_t y)^0|\).  The vector
\(((\Lambda_t y)^1,(\Lambda_t y)^0,0,\ldots,0)\) is therefore future
timelike in the mostly-plus convention.  Multiplication by \(\sin s\) gives
the future/past alternatives.

At \(s=\pi\), \(\cosh(t+\ii\pi)=-\cosh t\) and
\(\sinh(t+\ii\pi)=-\sinh t\), hence the stated left-wedge image.  If
\(x\in W_R\) and \(\ell\in W_L\), then
\[
  x^1-\ell^1>|x^0|+|\ell^0|\ge |x^0-\ell^0|.
\]
Thus \(x-\ell\) is spacelike.

\section{Bisognano--Wichmann Boundary and the Local Model}

\begin{remark}[Bisognano--Wichmann theorem boundary]
\label{rem:bisognano-wichmann-boundary}
The Bisognano--Wichmann theorem in wedge-modular form is a structural theorem
about the standard pair \((\mathcal A(W_R),\Omega)\), where
\(\mathcal A(W_R)\) is the right-wedge algebra and \(\Omega\) is the vacuum
vector.  Under the Wightman or Haag--Kastler hypotheses of positivity,
Poincare covariance, locality, spectrum condition, and wedge cyclicity and
separatingness of the vacuum, its conclusion is that the Tomita--Takesaki
modular automorphism group of \((\mathcal A(W_R),\Omega)\) is the Lorentz
boost flow preserving \(W_R\).  With the boost convention
\(U(\eta)=\exp(\ii\eta K)\), this is equivalently the KMS condition at inverse
boost temperature \(2\pi\).

The full theorem is kept here as an external AQFT theorem boundary.  Its proof
requires Tomita--Takesaki modular theory, the analytic continuation supplied
by the spectrum condition, covariance under complexified boosts, and wedge
locality at imaginary boost angle \(\ii\pi\).  The self-contained theorem in
this chapter is the free massive scalar model calculation below: it proves the
same strip analyticity and KMS boundary relation directly for the polynomial
wedge algebra, with every analytic domain and ordering step visible.
\end{remark}

\section{Free Scalar Wedge KMS Core}

We now prove the distributional KMS core for the free scalar field.  The
result is a complete model calculation with all orderings and analytic
domains visible.  It is deliberately a statement about smeared unbounded
fields and their correlation distributions.  A genuine \(C^\ast\)- or von
Neumann-algebra KMS statement for bounded observables is stated separately
below.

\begin{definition}[Free scalar two-point distribution]
\label{def:unruh-free-scalar-two-point}
For \(m>0\), the massive scalar vacuum two-point distribution on
\(\mathbb M^D\) is
\[
  \Delta_+^{(m)}(z)
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R^{D-1}}
  \frac{\dd^{D-1}{\bf p}}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2E_{\bf p}}\,
  e^{-\ii E_{\bf p}z^0+\ii{\bf p}\cdot{\bf z}},
  \qquad
  E_{\bf p}=\sqrt{|{\bf p}|^2+m^2}.
\]
For test functions \(f,g\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb M^D)\),
\[
  \langle\Omega,\phi(f)\phi(g)\Omega\rangle
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(x-y)\,\dd x\,\dd y .
\]
\end{definition}

The distribution \(\Delta_+^{(m)}(z)\) is the boundary value of a holomorphic
function in the past tube
\[
  \mathcal T_-=\{z\in\mathbb C^D:\operatorname{Im}z\in V_-\},
\]
where \(V_-\) is the open past timelike cone.  Indeed, if
\(\operatorname{Im}z=u\in V_-\), then
\[
  E_{\bf p}u^0-{\bf p}\cdot{\bf u}\le -c_u E_{\bf p}
\]
for some \(c_u>0\) on every compact subcone of \(V_-\), and the defining
mass-shell integral is absolutely convergent after the usual polynomial
bounds from differentiating in \(z\).

\begin{theorem}[Free scalar two-point distributional wedge KMS]
\label{thm:unruh-free-scalar-wedge-kms}
Let \(f,g\in C_c^\infty(W_R)\).  Let the boost automorphism act on smeared
fields by
\[
  \alpha_\eta(\phi(g))=\phi(g_\eta),
  \qquad
  g_\eta(x)=g(\Lambda_{-\eta}x).
\]
Then
\[
  F_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \langle\Omega,\phi(f)\alpha_\eta(\phi(g))\Omega\rangle
\]
is the lower boundary value of a function holomorphic in the strip
\[
  0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi ,
\]
continuous as a distributional boundary value on the two edges, and its
upper boundary satisfies
\begin{equation}
  F_{f,g}(\eta+2\pi\ii)
  =
  \langle\Omega,\alpha_\eta(\phi(g))\phi(f)\Omega\rangle .
  \label{eq:unruh-free-two-point-kms-boundary}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For real \(\eta\), change variables in \(g_\eta\) to write
\begin{equation}
  F_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(x-\Lambda_\eta y)\,\dd x\,\dd y .
  \label{eq:unruh-free-two-point-lower-boundary}
\end{equation}
For \(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<\pi\), the complex-boost geometry above implies
that
\[
  \operatorname{Im}(x-\Lambda_\eta y)
  =
  -\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta y
  \in V_-
\]
for all \(x,y\in W_R\).  Since the supports of \(f\) and \(g\) are compact
subsets of \(W_R\), the image stays in a compact subcone of the past tube on
compact sub-strips.  Thus
\[
  F^{(1)}_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(x-\Lambda_\eta y)\,\dd x\,\dd y
\]
is holomorphic for \(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<\pi\).

For \(\pi<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi\), the same geometry says that
\(\operatorname{Im}\Lambda_\eta y\in V_-\).  Hence
\[
  F^{(2)}_{f,g}(\eta)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(\Lambda_\eta y-x)\,\dd x\,\dd y
\]
is holomorphic on the upper half of the strip.

It remains to glue the two holomorphic functions across
\(\operatorname{Im}\eta=\pi\).  At \(\eta=t+\ii\pi\), the point
\(\Lambda_\eta y\) lies in \(W_L\).  The complex-boost geometry above
therefore makes \(x\) and \(\Lambda_\eta y\) spacelike separated.  The free
scalar commutator is
\[
  [\phi(x),\phi(y)]=\ii\Delta_m(x-y)\mathbf 1,
\]
where \(\Delta_m\) is the difference of the retarded and advanced
fundamental solutions of \(\Box-m^2\).  Hyperbolic finite propagation gives
\(\Delta_m(z)=0\) for spacelike \(z\).  Therefore the two boundary values
\[
  \Delta_+^{(m)}(x-\Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}y)
  \quad\hbox{and}\quad
  \Delta_+^{(m)}(\Lambda_{t+\ii\pi}y-x)
\]
agree after smearing with \(f(x)g(y)\).  The edge-of-the-wedge gluing in this
one-complex-variable strip is the following elementary distributional fact:
if two holomorphic functions on adjacent strips have the same distributional
boundary value on the common real line, they define a holomorphic function on
the union.  Applying it here gives a holomorphic \(F_{f,g}\) on
\(0<\operatorname{Im}\eta<2\pi\).

The lower boundary is
\eqref{eq:unruh-free-two-point-lower-boundary}.  The upper boundary is
obtained from \(F^{(2)}\) because
\(\Lambda_{\eta+2\pi\ii}=\Lambda_\eta\):
\[
  \lim_{s\uparrow2\pi}F^{(2)}_{f,g}(t+\ii s)
  =
  \iint f(x)g(y)\Delta_+^{(m)}(\Lambda_t y-x)\,\dd x\,\dd y
  =
  \langle\Omega,\alpha_t(\phi(g))\phi(f)\Omega\rangle .
\]
This is \eqref{eq:unruh-free-two-point-kms-boundary}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Polynomial distributional core.}
Let \(\mathcal P(W_R)\) be the unital \(\ast\)-algebra generated by the
smeared free fields \(\phi(f)\), \(f\in C_c^\infty(W_R)\), modulo the free
field equation and canonical commutation relations.  The vacuum correlation
functions on \(\mathcal P(W_R)\) form a distributional KMS core at inverse
boost temperature \(2\pi\) with respect to \(\alpha_\eta\).  This means that
for polynomial words \(A,B\) in smeared fields, the vacuum distributions
\(\langle\Omega,A\,\alpha_\eta(B)\Omega\rangle\), after smearing all real
field variables, have the same strip analyticity and boundary reordering as
in Theorem~\ref{thm:unruh-free-scalar-wedge-kms}.  The analytic input remains
the two-point theorem; the passage from fields to polynomials is Wick
expansion:
every vacuum \(n\)-point function of the free scalar field is a finite sum of
products of two-point functions by Wick's theorem.  If a polynomial \(B\) is
boosted by complex \(\eta\), each two-point factor connecting a field in
\(A\) to a field in \(B\) is one of the strip-holomorphic functions proved in
Theorem~\ref{thm:unruh-free-scalar-wedge-kms}.  Pairings entirely inside
\(A\) or entirely inside \(B\) are independent of \(\eta\) or are invariant
under the common boost.  Products and finite sums preserve holomorphy in the
strip.  The boundary relation follows term by term from
\eqref{eq:unruh-free-two-point-kms-boundary}; the reordering at the upper
edge is legitimate because the free commutator has already supplied the
spacelike gluing at imaginary boost time \(\pi\).  This is exactly the KMS
boundary relation on the unbounded polynomial core.

The completed \(C^\ast\)-algebra KMS theorem lives at the bounded-observable
level, while the polynomial expressions above are an unbounded analytic core.
Upgrading this core requires either a common analytic domain with strip-growth
estimates or a passage to bounded observables.

\paragraph{Bounded wedge algebra.}
For the free scalar, the bounded Weyl operators
\[
  W(f)=\exp\bigl(\ii\phi(f)\bigr),
  \qquad f\in C_c^\infty(W_R;\mathbb R),
\]
generate the Weyl \(C^\ast\)-algebra and, in the vacuum representation, the
right-wedge von Neumann algebra.  On this bounded algebra the KMS assertion is
the usual algebraic one: for bounded \(A,B\) in the wedge algebra,
\[
  \eta\longmapsto\langle\Omega,A\,\alpha_\eta(B)\Omega\rangle
\]
is bounded and continuous on the closed strip, holomorphic inside, and obeys
the boundary relation in the definition of KMS.  The Bisognano--Wichmann
theorem supplies this statement for the von Neumann algebra.  In the free
quasifree model, the same conclusion is equivalently obtained from the
one-particle standard-subspace/Weyl-algebra construction: the two-point
strip relation above is the infinitesimal core, while boundedness and norm
continuity enter only after exponentiating to Weyl operators and completing
the algebra.

\section{Worldline Specialization}

For a uniformly accelerated trajectory in \(D=2\),
\[
  x^0(\tau)=a^{-1}\sinh(a\tau),\qquad
  x^1(\tau)=a^{-1}\cosh(a\tau),
\]
the invariant separation between two points is
\[
  -(x(\tau)-x(0))^2
  =
  \frac{4}{a^2}\sinh^2\!\left(\frac{a(\tau-\ii0)}{2}\right).
\]
The Wightman function along the trajectory is therefore a function of
\(\sinh^2(a(\tau-\ii0)/2)\).  Since
\[
  \sinh\!\left(\frac{a(\tau+\ii 2\pi/a)}{2}\right)
  =
  -\sinh\!\left(\frac{a\tau}{2}\right),
\]
the two-point function has imaginary proper-time periodicity
\(\beta=2\pi/a\) with the KMS ordering prescription.  This verifies the
two-point form of the free-field wedge KMS theorem on a single orbit.

\section{Detector Response}

An Unruh--DeWitt detector with energy gap \(E>0\), weak coupling \(\lambda\),
and switching function \(\chi\) has leading transition probability
\begin{equation}
  P_\chi(E)
  =
  \lambda^2
  \iint_{\mathbb R^2}
  \chi(\tau)\chi(\tau')e^{-\ii E(\tau-\tau')}
  W(x(\tau),x(\tau'))\,\dd\tau\,\dd\tau' .
  \label{eq:unruh-finite-switching-probability}
\end{equation}
This finite-time probability is the operational quantity.  Exact detailed
balance is a statement about the stationary transition rate obtained after a
controlled long-time limit; finite switching generally breaks exact
stationarity.

\paragraph{Stationary spectral detailed balance.}
Let \(G(u)=W(x(u),x(0))\) be the stationary two-point distribution along a
time-translation orbit, and let its Fourier transform be the positive
tempered spectral measure \(d\mu_G(\omega)\):
\[
  d\mu_G(\omega)
  =
  \widehat G(\omega)
\]
in distributional notation.  A pointwise stationary rate
\[
  \dot P(E)=\rho_G(E)
\]
is defined only at energies where \(d\mu_G\) has an absolutely continuous
density \(d\mu_G(\omega)=\rho_G(\omega)\,\dd\omega\) in a neighborhood of
\(E\).  The KMS boundary condition at inverse temperature \(\beta\) is
equivalently the spectral measure relation
\begin{equation}
  \mu_G(-B)
  =
  \int_B e^{\beta\omega}\,d\mu_G(\omega),
  \qquad B\subset(0,\infty)\ \text{Borel},
  \label{eq:unruh-spectral-detailed-balance}
\end{equation}
for the positive-type spectral measure of \(G\).  At energies where both
pointwise densities exist, this gives the stationary detector-rate relation
\[
  \dot P(-E)=e^{\beta E}\dot P(E).
\]
This derivation is the KMS spectral theorem for stationary distributions.  It
leaves finite switched real-time integrals to the spectral convolution formula
below.

\paragraph{Controlled switching family.}
Choose a real \(\chi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R)\) or Schwartz function, and set
\[
  \chi_T(\tau)=\chi(\tau/T),\qquad
  T_{\rm eff}(T)=\int_{\mathbb R}\chi_T(\tau)^2\,\dd\tau
  =
  T\|\chi\|_2^2 .
\]
For a stationary trajectory, \eqref{eq:unruh-finite-switching-probability}
can be written in spectral form as
\begin{equation}
  P_{\chi_T}(E)
  =
  \lambda^2
  \int_{\mathbb R}
  \bigl|\widehat{\chi_T}(E-\omega)\bigr|^2
  \frac{d\mu_G(\omega)}{2\pi},
  \label{eq:unruh-switched-response-spectral-convolution}
\end{equation}
whenever the right-hand side is defined by the positive spectral measure.
Since
\(\widehat{\chi_T}(\nu)=T\widehat\chi(T\nu)\), the normalized kernel
\[
  K_T(\nu)
  =
  \frac{|\widehat{\chi_T}(\nu)|^2}{2\pi T_{\rm eff}(T)}
\]
is an approximate identity:
\[
  \int_{\mathbb R}K_T(\nu)\,\dd\nu=1.
\]
The normalized switched response is therefore the measure convolution
\[
  R_T(E)
  :=
  \frac{P_{\chi_T}(E)}{\lambda^2T_{\rm eff}(T)}
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R}K_T(E-\omega)\,d\mu_G(\omega).
\]

\paragraph{Pointwise switching-rate error hypotheses.}
Fix \(E\in\mathbb R\).  The pointwise error estimate below assumes the
following data.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item There is \(r_E>0\) such that \(d\mu_G\) is absolutely continuous on
\((E-r_E,E+r_E)\):
\[
  d\mu_G(\omega)=\rho_G(\omega)\,\dd\omega ,
\]
and \(\rho_G\) is Lipschitz there with constant \(L_E\).
\item The spectral measure has polynomial global growth: for some
\(C_G,q\ge0\),
\[
  \mu_G([-R,R])\le C_G(1+R)^q,
  \qquad R\ge0 .
\]
\item The switch has the first approximate-identity moment
\[
  M_1(\chi)
  :=
  \frac{1}{2\pi\|\chi\|_2^2}
  \int_{\mathbb R}|s|\,|\widehat\chi(s)|^2\,\dd s
  <\infty ,
\]
and has enough Fourier decay for the spectral tail: for some
\(N>q+2\),
\[
  D_N(\chi)
  :=
  \sup_{s\in\mathbb R}(1+|s|)^N|\widehat\chi(s)|^2
  <\infty .
\]
\end{enumerate}
Schwartz switches, including compactly supported smooth switches, satisfy
the switch hypotheses for every \(N\).  The absolute continuity assumption
excludes an atom at the detector gap \(E\).  To use detailed balance at both
gaps, the same local density hypothesis must hold at \(E\) and at \(-E\).

\begin{proposition}[Switching approximate identity with spectral tails]
\label{prop:unruh-switching-rate-error-hypotheses}
Under the pointwise switching-rate error hypotheses,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{P_{\chi_T}(E)}{\lambda^2T_{\rm eff}(T)}
  =
  \rho_G(E)+O(T^{-1}).
  \label{eq:unruh-switching-stationary-rate-limit}
\end{equation}
The constant in the \(O(T^{-1})\) term depends on \(E,r_E,L_E,C_G,q\) and the
displayed switch bounds, but not on \(T\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write \(\nu=E-\omega\).  Plancherel gives the normalization of \(K_T\), and
the first moment scales as
\[
  \int_{\mathbb R}|\nu|K_T(\nu)\,\dd\nu
  =
  \frac{M_1(\chi)}{T}.
\]
Split the response into \(|\nu|<r_E\) and \(|\nu|\ge r_E\).  On the near
region the measure has density, so the difference from the pointwise rate is
bounded by
\[
  \int_{|\nu|<r_E}
  K_T(\nu)|\rho_G(E-\nu)-\rho_G(E)|\,\dd\nu
  \le
  \frac{L_E M_1(\chi)}{T}.
\]

Two tail terms remain: the missing Lebesgue mass of the approximate identity
and the spectral measure outside the local density window.  The first is
controlled directly by the Fourier decay of \(\widehat\chi\).  For the
second, the bound \(D_N(\chi)<\infty\) gives
\[
  K_T(E-\omega)
  \le
  C_{\chi,N}\,T(1+T|E-\omega|)^{-N}.
\]
Since \(|E-\omega|\ge r_E\) on the tail and
\(\mu_G([-R,R])\le C_G(1+R)^q\), a dyadic decomposition gives
\[
  \int_{|E-\omega|\ge r_E}
  (1+|E-\omega|)^{-N}\,d\mu_G(\omega)
  <\infty
\]
when \(N>q+2\).  Hence the spectral tail is \(O(T^{1-N})\), in particular
\(O(T^{-1})\).  Combining the near bound with the two tail estimates proves
\eqref{eq:unruh-switching-stationary-rate-limit}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Atoms, thresholds, and measure limits.}
Without the pointwise switching-rate hypotheses, the convolution formula is
still a measure statement, not a pointwise-rate theorem.  For test functions
\(h\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R)\),
\[
  \int h(E)R_T(E)\,\dd E
  =
  \int (h\ast K_T)(\omega)\,d\mu_G(\omega)
  \longrightarrow
  \int h(\omega)\,d\mu_G(\omega),
\]
so \(R_T(E)\,\dd E\) converges weakly to \(d\mu_G(E)\).  If
\(d\mu_G\) has an atom \(m\delta_{E_0}\), then at the atom
\[
  R_T(E_0)\supset
  m K_T(0)
  =
  m\,\frac{T|\widehat\chi(0)|^2}{2\pi\|\chi\|_2^2},
\]
which need not approach a finite pointwise rate.  Thresholds, singular
continuous pieces, and discontinuous densities likewise require a
distributional, averaged, or Lebesgue-point statement rather than the
Lipschitz \(O(T^{-1})\) estimate above.  The same pointwise theorem applied
at \(-E\) gives detailed balance only after the \(T\to\infty\) limit.

A contour proof of
\(\tau\mapsto\tau-\ii\beta\) requires the switching function to have a
holomorphic strip extension with uniform bounds; generic compactly supported
smooth functions and generic Schwartz functions supply no such strip
regulator.  Analytic regulators with uniform strip bounds may be used for
contour proofs, but
\eqref{eq:unruh-switched-response-spectral-convolution} is the regulator-free
statement needed for physical switching.

\paragraph{Four-dimensional massless scalar rate.}
For the trajectory
\[
  x^0(\tau)=a^{-1}\sinh(a\tau),\qquad
  x^1(\tau)=a^{-1}\cosh(a\tau),
\]
the four-dimensional massless scalar Wightman function restricts to
\begin{equation}
  G_a(\tau)
  =
  -\frac{a^2}{16\pi^2}
  \frac{1}{\sinh^2\!\left(\frac{a(\tau-\ii0)}{2}\right)} .
  \label{eq:unruh-4d-worldline-wightman}
\end{equation}
For \(E>0\), close the stationary Fourier transform in the lower half-plane,
with the \(i0\) prescription in \eqref{eq:unruh-4d-worldline-wightman}.  The
second-order poles are at
\[
  \tau_n=-\frac{2\pi\ii n}{a},\qquad n=1,2,\ldots .
\]
Near \(\tau_n\),
\[
  \sinh^2\!\left(\frac{a\tau}{2}\right)
  =
  \frac{a^2}{4}(\tau-\tau_n)^2+O((\tau-\tau_n)^4),
\]
so the residue of
\(-a^2(16\pi^2)^{-1}e^{-\ii E\tau}/\sinh^2(a\tau/2)\) is
\[
  \frac{\ii E}{4\pi^2}e^{-2\pi En/a}.
\]
The lower contour is clockwise, hence
\begin{equation}
  \dot P(E)
  =
  \frac{E}{2\pi}
  \sum_{n\ge1}e^{-2\pi En/a}
  =
  \frac{E}{2\pi}
  \frac{1}{e^{2\pi E/a}-1}.
  \label{eq:unruh-4d-planck-rate}
\end{equation}
The de-excitation rate follows from
\eqref{eq:unruh-spectral-detailed-balance}:
\[
  \dot P(-E)
  =
  e^{2\pi E/a}\dot P(E)
  =
  \frac{E}{2\pi}\frac{1}{1-e^{-2\pi E/a}} .
\]
Thus the normalized stationary excitation spectrum is Planckian at
\(\beta=2\pi/a\), while any finite switched probability is its spectral
average as in
\eqref{eq:unruh-switched-response-spectral-convolution}.

\begin{openproblem}[Interacting wedge thermality in examples]
\label{op:interacting-wedge-thermality-examples}
Develop explicit interacting examples where the wedge algebra, modular flow,
and detector response can be computed or bounded beyond formal perturbation
