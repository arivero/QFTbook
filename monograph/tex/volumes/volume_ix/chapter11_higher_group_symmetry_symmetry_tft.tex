\chapter{Higher-Group Symmetry And Symmetry TQFT}
\label{ch:global-higher-group-symmetry-tft}

\ConventionsMixed

Higher-form symmetries and ordinary symmetries may combine into a single
background-field structure.  The combined object is not merely a product of
groups; its gauge transformations and anomalies may be linked by Postnikov
classes.  This chapter defines higher-group backgrounds and then explains
the symmetry-TQFT organization of boundary conditions and gauging operations.

\section{Two-Group Backgrounds}

Let \(G_0\) be an ordinary finite internal symmetry and let \(A\) be an
abelian one-form symmetry.  A two-group is specified by an action of \(G_0\)
on \(A\) and a Postnikov class
\[
  \beta\in H^3(BG_0,A).
\]
On a triangulated spacetime \(M\), a background consists of a \(G_0\)-valued
one-cocycle \(g\) and an \(A\)-valued two-cochain \(B\) obeying
\[
  \delta_g B=\beta(g).
\]
Here \(\delta_g\) is the coboundary twisted by the \(G_0\)-action on \(A\).
Thus \(B\) is not closed when the ordinary background \(g\) has nontrivial
Postnikov obstruction.

\section{Gauge Transformations}

A \(G_0\) gauge transformation changes \(g\) by a coboundary.  The two-form
background \(B\) must transform at the same time so that
\(\delta_gB=\beta(g)\) remains valid.  A one-form gauge transformation with
\(A\)-valued one-cochain \(\lambda\) acts by
\[
  B\mapsto B+\delta_g\lambda .
\]
If \(\beta\ne0\), the ordinary and one-form transformations do not commute as
independent operations on the background fields.  The physical statement is
that charged line operators and local operators transform in a single
higher-group representation problem.

\section{Anomaly Functionals}

An anomaly of a two-group symmetry in \(D\) dimensions is an invertible
\((D+1)\)-dimensional action for \((g,B)\).  A basic term is
\[
  S_{\mathrm{anom}}[g,B]
  =
  2\pi i\int_X
  \langle B\smile \gamma(g)\rangle ,
\]
where \(\gamma(g)\) is a \(G_0\)-dependent cochain with values in the Pontryagin
dual of \(A\).  Gauge invariance on closed \(X\) imposes a cocycle condition
using \(\delta_gB=\beta(g)\).  If \(X\) has boundary, the boundary QFT
partition function is a section of the anomaly line over background fields.

\section{Symmetry TQFT}

For a \(D\)-dimensional QFT with finite symmetry, one can package background
fields, gauging, and duality defects in a \((D+1)\)-dimensional topological
theory.  A physical QFT is then represented by a boundary condition
\[
  \mathfrak B_Q
\]
for the symmetry TQFT.  Coupling to a background corresponds to ending the
topological theory on a background boundary.  Gauging corresponds to replacing
that boundary condition by a summing boundary condition.

For an ordinary finite group \(G\), the symmetry TQFT is finite \(G\)-gauge
theory in one higher dimension.  For higher groups, the bulk field space is
the groupoid of pairs \((g,B)\) satisfying the Postnikov constraint.  The
fusion of topological defects in the QFT is inherited from the fusion of
topological boundary-changing operators in the bulk.

\section{\texorpdfstring{\(\mathbb Z_N\)}{ZN} One-Form Symmetry TQFT}
\label{sec:zn-one-form-symtft}

We now write the finite cochain model that will be used as the local model for
one-form gauging and condensation defects.  Let \(X\) be an oriented
triangulated five-manifold.  Let
\[
  b,c\in C^2(X,\mathbb Z_N)
\]
be two \(\mathbb Z_N\)-valued two-cochains.  Their gauge transformations are
\[
  b\mapsto b+\delta\lambda,\qquad
  c\mapsto c+\delta\kappa,
  \qquad
  \lambda,\kappa\in C^1(X,\mathbb Z_N).
\]
The finite Heisenberg, or \(BF\), symmetry TQFT has exponentiated action
\begin{equation}
  Z_H(X;b,c)
  =
  \exp\left(\frac{2\pi i}{N}\int_X b\smile\delta c\right).
  \label{eq:zn-one-form-symtft-action}
\end{equation}
The integral means evaluation of the resulting \(\mathbb Z_N\)-valued
five-cochain on the fundamental class and then embedding
\(\mathbb Z_N\hookrightarrow \mathbb R/\mathbb Z\).  Equivalently one chooses
integer lifts and keeps the result modulo \(N\); changing the lift changes the
exponent by an integral multiple of \(2\pi i\).

\begin{proposition}[Closed-manifold gauge invariance]
\label{prop:zn-one-form-symtft-gauge-invariance}
If \(X\) is closed, \eqref{eq:zn-one-form-symtft-action} is invariant under
the gauge transformations above.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The transformation \(c\mapsto c+\delta\kappa\) does not change \(\delta c\),
because \(\delta^2=0\).  Under \(b\mapsto b+\delta\lambda\), the exponent
changes by
\[
  \frac{2\pi i}{N}\int_X \delta\lambda\smile\delta c .
\]
Since \(\deg\lambda=1\),
\[
  \delta(\lambda\smile\delta c)
  =
  \delta\lambda\smile\delta c
  -\lambda\smile\delta^2c
  =
  \delta\lambda\smile\delta c .
\]
Thus the change is the integral of a coboundary.  Evaluation of a coboundary on
the fundamental cycle of a closed triangulated manifold is zero modulo \(N\).
The exponent is therefore unchanged.
\end{proof}

If \(X\) has boundary \(M=\partial X\), the last computation leaves the
boundary term
\[
  \frac{2\pi i}{N}\int_M \lambda\smile\delta c .
\]
A boundary condition must therefore specify which combination of \(b\) and
\(c\) is fixed as a background and which is summed as a dynamical higher gauge
field.  A four-dimensional QFT \(\mathcal Q\) with a \(\mathbb Z_N\) one-form
symmetry is a boundary condition for this five-dimensional theory after the
choice of background boundary has been made.  Gauging the one-form symmetry is
the operation of replacing the boundary condition that fixes \(b\) by the
boundary condition that sums over \(b\) and fixes the dual background \(c\).

\begin{example}[Four-dimensional \(\mathbb Z_N\) line data]
\label{ex:zn-four-dimensional-line-data}
In a four-dimensional theory whose electric and magnetic one-form backgrounds
are both finite \(\mathbb Z_N\) backgrounds, a line charge may be labelled
abstractly by
\[
  (e,m)\in \mathbb Z_N\oplus\mathbb Z_N .
\]
The pairing controlling mutual locality is the finite Dirac pairing
\[
  \langle(e,m),(e',m')\rangle
  =
  em'-me'
  \quad\in\mathbb Z_N .
\]
A set of simultaneously genuine line operators is a subgroup on which this
pairing vanishes.  The five-dimensional action
\eqref{eq:zn-one-form-symtft-action} packages the two one-form backgrounds and
their finite symplectic pairing.  The wall that exchanges \(b\) and \(c\) acts
on line charges by
\[
  (e,m)\mapsto (m,-e),
\]
the finite analogue of the \(S\)-wall discussed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-duality-defects-gauging-orbifold-data}.  This statement
is about the line-charge lattice and its mutual-locality pairing; it is not an
assertion that arbitrary closed loops in four Euclidean dimensions have a
topological pairwise braiding invariant.
\end{example}

\section{Condensation And Gauging}

Let \(\mathcal C\) be the category or higher category of topological defects
describing a finite symmetry.  Gauging a sub-symmetry is condensation of a
commutative algebra object
\[
  A_{\mathrm{cond}}\in\mathcal C .
\]
The gauged theory has boundary condition obtained by allowing endpoints of
the condensed defects.  If the algebra object is obstructed by an anomaly,
the condensation exists only after coupling to an appropriate bulk or after
choosing a trivialization of the anomaly.

\begin{definition}[Triangulated higher-gauging defect]
\label{def:triangulated-higher-gauging-defect}
Let \(\mathcal Q\) be a four-dimensional QFT with an anomaly-free finite
\(\mathbb Z_N\) one-form symmetry.  Assume that for every closed oriented
surface \(\Sigma\) there are topological symmetry surfaces
\[
  U_n(\Sigma),\qquad n\in\mathbb Z_N,
\]
with junctions implementing \(U_nU_m\simeq U_{n+m}\), and assume these
surface networks are invariant under triangulation moves.  For a triangulated
oriented three-manifold \(Y\) supporting a codimension-one defect, define the
formal condensation insertion
\begin{equation}
  \mathfrak C(Y)
  =
  \frac{1}{|C^1(Y,\mathbb Z_N)|}
  \sum_{b\in Z^2(Y,\mathbb Z_N)}
  \mathsf U_b(Y).
  \label{eq:triangulated-one-form-condensation-defect}
\end{equation}
Here \(b\) is a closed two-cochain on \(Y\).  The symbol
\(\mathsf U_b(Y)\) denotes the topological wall network obtained from the
Poincare-dual one-cycle of \(b\): locally one thickens each dual edge in a
small normal collar of \(Y\) and inserts the corresponding one-form symmetry
surface segment.  The condition \(\delta b=0\) is the conservation law at the
vertices of the dual one-cycle, equivalently the absence of an uncancelled
endpoint for the wall network.  The normalization is a chosen cellular
normalization for the one-form gauge transformations acting on cochains;
changing the groupoid normalization changes the defect by an invertible
three-dimensional topological factor.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Fusion of the finite condensation defect]
\label{prop:finite-condensation-defect-fusion}
Under the hypotheses of Definition~\ref{def:triangulated-higher-gauging-defect},
the fusion of two parallel condensation defects is the original condensation
defect tensored with the finite three-dimensional \(\mathbb Z_N\) two-form
gauge theory state sum on \(Y\).  In particular the defect is noninvertible
whenever this three-dimensional topological factor is nontrivial.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Place two copies of the defect on nearby parallel copies of \(Y\).  Their
product is
\[
  \frac{1}{|C^1(Y,\mathbb Z_N)|^2}
  \sum_{b,b'\in Z^2(Y,\mathbb Z_N)}
  \mathsf U_b(Y)\,\mathsf U_{b'}(Y).
\]
Using the junction isomorphism \(U_nU_m\simeq U_{n+m}\), change variables to
\[
  s=b+b',\qquad t=b .
\]
For each closed \(s\), the sum over \(t\in Z^2(Y,\mathbb Z_N)\) with the same
cellular normalization is, by definition, the partition function of the finite
three-dimensional two-form gauge theory on \(Y\) in this normalization.  The
remaining sum over \(s\) is exactly the condensation insertion
\(\mathfrak C(Y)\), because
\(\mathsf U_b(Y)\mathsf U_{b'}(Y)\simeq\mathsf U_{b+b'}(Y)\).  Thus fusion has
the stated form.  If the additional three-dimensional factor is not the
trivial invertible theory, no inverse defect can cancel it to the invisible
defect.
\end{proof}

For Yang--Mills theories this construction is not a substitute for a
continuum definition of the surface operators.  It becomes a QFT statement only
after the \(\mathbb Z_N\) one-form symmetry surfaces, their junctions, their
correlation topology, and their anomaly trivialization have been constructed
inside the theory or by a regulator such as the lattice.  Once that input is
available, the cochain calculation above is the local algebraic mechanism
behind higher gauging and the resulting noninvertible condensation defects.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Finite higher gauging]
\label{qt:finite-higher-gauging-condensation-defects}
Let \(\mathcal Q\) be a four-dimensional QFT with a suitable anomaly-free
finite one-form symmetry.  Gauging that symmetry on a hypersurface produces a
topological condensation defect.  If \(\mathcal Q\) is equivalent to the theory
obtained by gauging, after all discrete theta and background choices are
fixed, the half-space gauging interface becomes a noninvertible duality
defect.  Its fusion coefficients are generally three-dimensional topological
theories, not only nonnegative integers.
\end{quotedtheorem}

This theorem is quoted as a finite topological and defect-network theorem in
the source lineage of higher gauging and noninvertible condensation defects.
The proof in this monograph is complete only for the finite cochain mechanism
in Proposition~\ref{prop:finite-condensation-defect-fusion}.  The remaining
continuum work is to construct the required surface operators and their
junctions in the particular interacting QFT under discussion.

\section{Chiral Defects In Abelian Gauge Theory}
\label{sec:chiral-defects-abelian-gauge-theory}

Let a four-dimensional abelian gauge theory contain massless fermions with a
classical chiral transformation \(R_\alpha\).  Suppose the ABJ anomaly changes
the action by
\[
  \Delta S_\alpha
  =
  \frac{i\alpha q}{8\pi^2}\int_M F\wedge F ,
\]
where \(F\) is the \(U(1)\) field strength and \(q\in\mathbb Z\) is the anomaly
coefficient in the chosen charge normalization.  The codimension-one defect
implementing \(R_\alpha\) is topological only if this phase is cancelled or
trivialized as the defect crosses insertions.  In the absence of dynamical
monopoles, the theory also has a magnetic one-form symmetry, and the anomaly
phase can be represented by coupling the defect worldvolume to a
three-dimensional topological theory whose anyons attach to magnetic
background flux sectors.  The resulting dressed defect is generally
noninvertible: fusing it with its orientation reverse leaves a sum or a
three-dimensional topological factor rather than the invisible defect alone.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Noninvertible chiral defects, source-lineage form]
\label{qt:noninvertible-chiral-defects-source-lineage}
In massless abelian gauge theories with no dynamical monopoles and with the
magnetic one-form symmetry kept as part of the QFT datum, certain rational
chiral rotations whose ABJ anomaly is not an ordinary symmetry can be dressed
by lower-dimensional topological degrees of freedom to define topological
noninvertible chiral defects.  If dynamical monopoles are added so that the
magnetic one-form symmetry is explicitly broken, the dressed defect is no
longer topological and the associated selection rule can be violated by
monopole operators.
\end{quotedtheorem}

We will not use this quoted theorem as a black-box axiom in later arguments.
For the standard of this monograph, the still-missing part is a
regulator-level construction that simultaneously defines the massless fermion
measure, the magnetic one-form symmetry backgrounds, the three-dimensional
defect theory, and reflection-positive defect Hilbert spaces.  The statement is
therefore recorded as a precise target for later development rather than as a
completed derivation.

\section{Higher Fusion Categories And Truncations}
\label{sec:higher-fusion-categories-truncations}

The categorical level depends on which defects are retained.  In a
four-dimensional QFT, the full wall-junction system includes walls, junction
surfaces, junction lines, and point junctions.  It is therefore at least a
three-categorical defect object.  A fusion two-category appears only after a
controlled truncation, for example by focusing on topological surfaces and
their line and point junctions, or by studying the three-dimensional theory
living on a wall.  A finite semisimple fusion two-category has objects,
one-morphisms, and two-morphisms, a monoidal product, duals, and finite
semisimple Hom categories.  Condensation is then formulated by separable
algebra objects and their module two-categories.

This distinction matters in four-dimensional generalized symmetry.  A
one-form symmetry generated by topological surfaces is not fully described by
a one-category of simple surfaces if its line junctions carry nontrivial
operator spaces.  The symmetry TQFT keeps this extra information because bulk
boundary-changing defects, their junctions, and their endpoint operators live
one categorical level higher than the ordinary group law.  When a chapter later
uses only a fusion ring of surfaces, the omission is a truncation and not a
definition of the full symmetry object.

\section{Example: Mixed Zero- And One-Form Data}

Consider a four-dimensional gauge theory with gauge group \(SU(N)\) and a
charge conjugation symmetry acting on the center one-form symmetry
\[
  A=\mathbb Z_N
\]
by inversion.  A background for the ordinary symmetry changes the local
system in which the \(A\)-valued two-form background lives.  If a theta-angle
or discrete theta term changes under charge conjugation, the anomaly is a
five-dimensional functional of the combined background.  The line-operator
lattice then transforms as a module over the two-group rather than as a
separate representation of each symmetry.

\section*{Comparison Ledger}

The symmetry-TQFT description is not a replacement for constructing the QFT.
It records:
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{allowed background fields},\qquad
  \text{anomaly lines},\\
  \text{gauging and condensation operations},\qquad
  \text{topological defect fusion}.
\end{gathered}
\]
Local correlators, Hilbert space, stress tensor, and dynamical phase
structure must still be supplied by the QFT itself.

\begin{openproblem}[Higher-group examples]
\label{op:higher-group-examples}
Construct interacting continuum QFT examples whose higher-group backgrounds,
symmetry TQFT, defect fusion, anomaly inflow, and local-operator action are
all derived from a single regulator or reconstruction framework.
\end{openproblem}
