\chapter{Confinement, Screening, and Oblique Confinement}
\label{ch:global-confinement-screening-oblique}

\ConventionsEuclidean

Confinement is a property of gauge-invariant extended-observable data in a
specified infinite-volume or continuum limit.  Its basic inputs are external
probes, endpoint maps for dynamical charged objects, renormalized
large-distance asymptotics of line insertions, and the Hilbert-space sectors
generated by those probes.  This chapter develops the finite charge-lattice
and line-operator part of that structure.
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-phases-of-gauge-theories} then embeds it in the
broader definition of a phase of a gauge theory.

\section{Line-Charge Data}

Let \(G\) be the global form of the compact gauge group and let
\(\mathfrak g\) be its Lie algebra.  Fix a maximal torus \(T\subset G\).
Electric Wilson charges are integral weights of \(T\) that exponentiate to
representations of \(G\).  Magnetic 't Hooft charges are cocharacters
\[
  U(1)\longrightarrow T
\]
allowed by the same global form, modulo the Weyl group.  We write the
electric and magnetic charge groups as
\[
  \Gamma_{\rm e}\subset X^*(T),
  \qquad
  \Gamma_{\rm m}\subset X_*(T),
\]
and a dyonic line label as
\[
  \gamma=(\lambda_{\rm e},\lambda_{\rm m})
  \in \Gamma_{\rm e}\oplus\Gamma_{\rm m}.
\]
The unscreened center-sensitive part is often finite; for an
\(\mathfrak{su}(N)\) theory it is
\[
  \mathcal C
  =
  \mathbb Z_N^{\rm e}\oplus\mathbb Z_N^{\rm m}.
\]
The full charge lattice and the finite center-sensitive quotient should not
be confused.  The former remembers weights, cocharacters, and masses of
dynamical states; the latter remembers precisely the data detected by
center-linked topological surfaces and by the global form.

The Dirac pairing of two line labels is
\begin{equation}
  \langle \gamma,\gamma'\rangle_D
  =
  \lambda_{\rm e}(\lambda'_{\rm m})
  -
  \lambda'_{\rm e}(\lambda_{\rm m})
  \in\mathbb Z
  \label{eq:dirac-pairing-full-lattice}
\end{equation}
for simultaneously genuine lines.  On the finite
\(\mathfrak{su}(N)\) charge group this becomes the alternating pairing
\begin{equation}
  B((e,m),(e',m'))
  =
  \frac{em'-e'm}{N}
  \in\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z .
  \label{eq:finite-dirac-pairing-confinement}
\end{equation}
The phase acquired by moving one line once around the other is
\(\exp(2\pi\ii B)\).  Thus a set of line operators can be simultaneously
genuine only when the relevant pairings vanish modulo integers.

\begin{definition}[External charge system]
\label{def:external-charge-system}
For the purposes of confinement diagnostics, an external charge system is a
triple
\[
  (\mathcal C,B,S),
\]
where \(\mathcal C\) is a finite abelian group of center-sensitive line
charges, \(B:\mathcal C\times\mathcal C\to\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z\) is a
nondegenerate alternating pairing, and \(S\subset\mathcal C\) is the subgroup
of charges screened by finite-energy dynamical objects.  The quotient
\[
  \mathcal C_{\rm ext}=\mathcal C/S
\]
is the group of static external charges modulo screening.  If \(S\) is
isotropic, the residual topological charge group is
\[
  \mathcal C_{\rm top}=S^\perp/S,
  \qquad
  S^\perp=\{\gamma\in\mathcal C\mid B(\gamma,s)=0
  \text{ for every }s\in S\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The distinction between \(\mathcal C_{\rm ext}\) and
\(\mathcal C_{\rm top}\) is important.  \(\mathcal C_{\rm ext}\) classifies
external sources after allowing dynamical endpoints.  \(\mathcal C_{\rm top}\)
classifies charges with well-defined finite braiding after quotienting by
screening.

\paragraph{Pairing on the screened topological quotient.}
If \(S\subset\mathcal C\) is isotropic, then \(B\) descends to a
nondegenerate alternating pairing on \(S^\perp/S\).
The verification is the linear algebra of orthogonal quotients.  Let
\(\gamma,\eta\in S^\perp\) and change representatives by
\[
  \gamma\mapsto\gamma+s,\qquad \eta\mapsto\eta+t,
  \qquad s,t\in S .
\]
Then
\[
  B(\gamma+s,\eta+t)-B(\gamma,\eta)
  =
  B(s,\eta)+B(\gamma,t)+B(s,t).
\]
The first two terms vanish because \(\gamma,\eta\in S^\perp\), and the last
vanishes because \(S\) is isotropic.  Hence the value of \(B\) is independent
of representatives.  The descended pairing is alternating because \(B\) is.
For nondegeneracy, suppose \(\bar\gamma\in S^\perp/S\) pairs trivially with
all of \(S^\perp/S\).  Then \(\gamma\) pairs trivially with all of
\(S^\perp\), so \(\gamma\in(S^\perp)^\perp\).  Nondegeneracy of \(B\) on
\(\mathcal C\) gives \((S^\perp)^\perp=S\).  Therefore
\(\bar\gamma=0\).

\section{Renormalized Line Laws}

Let \(L_\gamma(C)\) be a Euclidean line operator with charge
\(\gamma\in\mathcal C\) supported on a closed contour \(C\).  A regulator is
part of the definition: on a lattice, \(C\) is a lattice path; in a continuum
scheme, \(C\) is a smooth embedded loop together with a short-distance
definition of the singular line insertion.  A line has self-energy and
possible curvature/contact counterterms.  We therefore write
\[
  L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C)
  =
  Z_\gamma(C,a)\,L_\gamma^{(a)}(C),
\]
where \(a\) is the cutoff and \(Z_\gamma(C,a)\) is local along the line in the
chosen scheme.  Statements about area or perimeter laws are statements about
the renormalized object and about an order of limits.

\begin{definition}[Area, perimeter, and zero-tension laws]
\label{def:line-area-perimeter-laws}
Let \(\{C_R\}_{R\ge1}\) be a family of planar loops whose linear size is
\(R\), with minimal spanning area \(A_R\) and length \(\ell_R\).  Assume
\[
  A_R\to\infty,\qquad \ell_R\to\infty,\qquad A_R/\ell_R\to\infty .
\]
For a renormalized line \(L_\gamma^{\rm ren}\):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item an area law with string tension \(\sigma_\gamma>0\) means
  \[
    -\log |\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|
    =
    \sigma_\gamma A_R+o(A_R);
  \]
  \item a perimeter law means
  \[
    -\log |\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|
    =
    \mu_\gamma \ell_R+o(\ell_R)
  \]
  for some scheme-dependent \(\mu_\gamma\);
  \item a zero-tension law means
  \[
    \lim_{R\to\infty}
    \frac{-\log |\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|}{A_R}=0.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The perimeter coefficient is not universal because it changes under local
line counterterms.  The statement \(\sigma_\gamma>0\), when defined by the
area coefficient after perimeter renormalization, is the robust asymptotic
claim.  In a gapless Coulomb regime neither the area/perimeter dichotomy nor
the word ``string tension'' is by itself a complete asymptotic description.

\begin{definition}[Static potential from rectangular lines]
\label{def:static-potential-rectangular-lines}
For a rectangular Euclidean loop \(C_{T,L}\) of time length \(T\) and spatial
separation \(L\), define
\[
  V_\gamma(L)
  =
  -\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}
  \log \langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_{T,L})\rangle
\]
when the limit exists after line renormalization.  Linear confinement of the
external charge \(\gamma\) means
\[
  V_\gamma(L)=\sigma_\gamma L+o(L),
  \qquad
  \sigma_\gamma>0 .
\]
Screening means that \(V_\gamma(L)\) remains bounded as \(L\to\infty\).
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Transfer-matrix extraction of the static potential.}
Assume a reflection-positive regulator with a transfer matrix, and assume
the rectangular line creates a nonzero vector \(\Psi_{\gamma,L}\) in the
Hilbert space with static external sources separated by \(L\).  If
\[
  \langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_{T,L})\rangle
  =
  \langle \Psi_{\gamma,L},
  e^{-T(H_{\gamma,L}-E_0)}\Psi_{\gamma,L}\rangle
\]
and the spectral measure of \(\Psi_{\gamma,L}\) has bottom
\(E_\gamma(L)\), then \(V_\gamma(L)=E_\gamma(L)\).

Let \(\rho_{\gamma,L}\) be the positive finite spectral measure of
\(H_{\gamma,L}-E_0\) in the vector \(\Psi_{\gamma,L}\).  The loop expectation
is
\[
  \int e^{-TE}\,d\rho_{\gamma,L}(E).
\]
If \(E_\gamma(L)\) is the bottom of the support of this measure, then every
interval \([E_\gamma(L),E_\gamma(L)+\varepsilon]\) has positive measure.
The elementary Laplace principle for a finite positive measure gives
\[
  -\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}
  \log\int e^{-TE}\,d\rho_{\gamma,L}(E)
  =
  E_\gamma(L).
\]
Indeed, the upper and lower bounds follow respectively from
\(E\ge E_\gamma(L)\) and from restricting the integral to
\([E_\gamma(L),E_\gamma(L)+\varepsilon]\), then letting
\(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

\section{Screening and String Breaking}

Screening is a statement about endpoint maps.  If a dynamical finite-energy
operator or state carries line charge \(s\in S\), then a line of charge
\(\gamma+s\) and a line of charge \(\gamma\) differ by an endpoint that can
be supplied from the Hilbert space.  The static probe cannot distinguish the
two charges at arbitrarily long distance.

\paragraph{Endpoint screening bound on the static energy.}
Assume a charge \(\gamma\) can be screened by a pair of dynamical particles of
finite mass \(M_{\rm scr}\), in the sense that the external-source sector at
separation \(L\) contains a trial state equal outside bounded neighborhoods
of the two sources to the vacuum, with energy at most \(2M_{\rm scr}+o(1)\)
above the vacuum.  Then
\[
  \limsup_{L\to\infty} V_\gamma(L)\le 2M_{\rm scr}.
\]
In particular this external charge cannot have a positive linear static
potential.
This is a variational consequence of the trial-state hypothesis.  In the
transfer-matrix construction, \(V_\gamma(L)\) is the infimum of the
static-source Hamiltonian spectrum in the sector with external charge
\(\gamma\) at separation \(L\).  For any normalized trial state \(\Psi_L\) in
that sector,
\[
  V_\gamma(L)\le
  \langle \Psi_L,H_{\gamma,L}\Psi_L\rangle-E_{\rm vac}.
\]
The assumed screened trial state has the right charge at the endpoints and
has energy \(2M_{\rm scr}+o(1)\) above the vacuum.  Taking the limsup gives
the displayed bound.  A function bounded along large \(L\) cannot satisfy
\(V_\gamma(L)=\sigma L+o(L)\) with \(\sigma>0\).
The nontrivial physical input is not the inequality but the construction of
the screened endpoint state in the regulated theory and its control under the
large-volume and large-\(L\) limits.

This bound gives the precise endpoint-screening content needed to
interpret Wilson loops in theories with dynamical fundamental matter.  The
closed loop may display a large intermediate string regime, while the
asymptotic static energy is bounded once the string can break by pair
creation.  Fredenhagen--Marcu type ratios, defined in
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-phases-of-gauge-theories}, measure a different
overlap after dividing out the universal flux cost.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Wilson-loop string-breaking spectral extraction
test]
\label{ca:wilson-loop-string-breaking-spectral-extraction-test}
Assume a reflection-positive regulator and a transfer matrix in the sector
with a static charge-anticharge pair of separation \(L\).  Let
\(O_{\rm F}(L)\) be a gauge-invariant operator with large overlap on an
unbroken flux-tube trial state, and let \(O_{\rm B}(L)\) be a gauge-invariant
operator whose endpoints are dressed by the dynamical screening fields.  The
measured object is then a correlator matrix,
\begin{equation}
  C_{ij}(L,T)
  =
  \langle O_i(L)^\dagger e^{-T(H_L-E_0)}O_j(L)\rangle,
  \qquad i,j\in\{{\rm F},{\rm B}\}.
  \label{eq:string-breaking-correlator-matrix}
\end{equation}
Its spectral form is
\begin{equation}
  C_{ij}(L,T)
  =
  \sum_n Z_{in}(L)\overline{Z_{jn}(L)}\,e^{-E_n(L)T},
  \qquad
  Z_{in}(L)=\langle n,L|O_i(L)|\Omega\rangle .
  \label{eq:string-breaking-spectral-matrix}
\end{equation}
In a string-breaking window the two lowest relevant energies have the
asymptotic form
\[
  E_{\rm s}(L)=\sigma L+\mu+o(1),
  \qquad
  E_{\rm b}(L)=2M_{\rm scr}+o(1).
\]
The ground static energy is the lower of these levels, with an avoided crossing
once mixing is included.  The diagonal Wilson-loop channel \(C_{{\rm F}{\rm F}}\)
need not reveal that ground state at accessible \(T\): if
\(|Z_{{\rm F}{\rm b}}|\) is small, then the flux-tube term can dominate
\(C_{{\rm F}{\rm F}}(L,T)\) over a long finite interval even when
\(E_{\rm b}(L)<E_{\rm s}(L)\).

A controlled string-breaking extraction therefore has to state more than an
area fit to one loop.  On a declared operator space it supplies a retained
two-state matrix
\begin{equation}
  C^{(0)}(L,T)
  =
  Z(L)
  \begin{pmatrix}
    e^{-E_{\rm s}(L)T} & 0\\
    0 & e^{-E_{\rm b}(L)T}
  \end{pmatrix}
  Z(L)^* ,
  \qquad |\det Z(L)|\ge d(L)>0,
  \label{eq:string-breaking-retained-two-state-matrix}
\end{equation}
plus a spectral-tail bound
\(\|C(L,T)-C^{(0)}(L,T)\|\le \tau(L,T)\) and measurement/renormalization
entry errors small compared with the rank and gap margins.  In a time-lattice
normalization the variational transfer problem
\begin{equation}
  C(L,T+a_t)v_n
  =
  \lambda_n(L,T)\,C(L,T)v_n
  \label{eq:string-breaking-gevp}
\end{equation}
then returns \(\lambda_n=e^{-a_tE_n(L)}\) for the retained matrix, with errors
controlled by the declared tail and entry budgets.  The static potential is an
eigenvalue statement in this matrix problem, not the slope of a single
finite-\(T\) Wilson-loop plateau.

This is the diagnostic distinction between center confinement and string
breaking.  If an exact center one-form symmetry forbids a dynamical endpoint of
the relevant \(N\)-ality, no broken-string state lies in the same static-source
sector and an area law can be an order parameter after the remaining infrared
hypotheses are supplied.  If dynamical fundamental matter screens that
\(N\)-ality, the broken-string channel is present; a finite-window area signal
then measures overlap with an excited flux tube unless the correlator basis and
spectral extraction actually resolve the screened ground state.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{Strong-Coupling Lattice Area Law}

Consider a hypercubic Euclidean lattice gauge theory with compact group
\(G\).  Let each plaquette Boltzmann weight be a central function with
character expansion
\[
  w(U_p)=\sum_R c_R\chi_R(U_p),
  \qquad c_{\mathbf 1}>0.
\]
Write
\[
  q_R=\frac{c_R}{c_{\mathbf 1}}.
\]
At strong coupling the nontrivial \(q_R\) are small.  The Wilson loop in an
irreducible representation \(R_0\) is
\[
  W_{R_0}(C)=\operatorname{tr}_{R_0}\prod_{\ell\in C} U_\ell .
\]
The key algebraic fact is Haar projection: an integral over a link vanishes
unless the tensor product of all representation matrices incident on that
link contains the trivial representation.

\paragraph{Surface selection by Haar projection.}
In the character expansion of a Wilson-loop expectation, every nonzero term
contains a collection of plaquettes whose oriented boundary carries the
representation flux inserted by the Wilson loop.  In particular a Wilson loop
with nonscreened charge cannot be saturated by plaquettes supported away from
a spanning surface.

Expand each plaquette weight in characters and integrate link by link.  A
link on the Wilson contour carries one matrix factor in \(R_0\) or
\(R_0^*\).  Haar integration over that link is the projection onto the
invariant subspace of the tensor product of all representation factors
incident on the link.  Unless adjacent plaquette characters supply flux whose
oriented boundary cancels the Wilson factor, the tensor product has no
invariant vector in the center-sensitive sector and the integral is zero.
Repeating this for every link on the contour forces a two-chain of plaquettes
whose boundary is \(C\), up to screened charges.  If the charge is
nonscreened, the boundary cannot be removed by local endpoints.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Area mechanism]
\label{ca:lattice-strong-coupling-area-law}
\leavevmode\par\noindent
Let \(R_0\) have nonscreened center-sensitive charge.  For a family of
planar loops \(C_R\), let \(A_{\min}(C_R)\) be the minimal lattice area of a
plaquette surface spanning \(C_R\).  A strong-coupling proof of an area
mechanism consists of the following uniform polymer-expansion estimates.
First, the Haar-projection selection rule permits no center-sensitive
contribution of area \(<A_{\min}(C_R)\).  Second, the sum of all minimal-area
contributions is nonzero and has magnitude at least
\[
  m_0\exp[-\sigma_+ A_{\min}(C_R)]
\]
for some \(m_0>0\) and finite \(\sigma_+\).  Third, the total contribution of
all larger decorated surfaces is bounded by
\[
  M_0\exp[-(\sigma_++\delta)A_{\min}(C_R)]
\]
for some \(M_0>0\) and \(\delta>0\), after possibly restricting to a
sufficiently small strong-coupling neighborhood.
Once these estimates have actually been proved for the chosen lattice action,
there are constants \(a,b>0\) such that
\[
  e^{-b A_{\min}(C_R)}
  \le
  |\langle W_{R_0}(C_R)\rangle|
  \le
  e^{-a A_{\min}(C_R)}
\]
for all sufficiently large loops in the family, up to subexponential factors.
Consequently every subsequential exponential rate
\(-A_{\min}(C_R)^{-1}\log |\langle W_{R_0}(C_R)\rangle|\) is strictly
positive and finite.

The first hypothesis is the kinematic selection part: link integration forces
the center-sensitive flux to be carried by a plaquette two-chain with boundary
\(C_R\), so no term of smaller area can occur.  The third hypothesis gives the
upper bound after choosing \(a<\sigma_+\) and absorbing the subexponential
number of local decorations into the prefactor.  For the lower bound, write
the expectation as \(M_R+E_R\), where \(M_R\) is the minimal-area sum and
\(|E_R|\le M_0e^{-(\sigma_++\delta)A_{\min}}\).  The lower estimate on
\(|M_R|\) gives
\[
  |M_R+E_R|
  \ge
  m_0e^{-\sigma_+A_{\min}}
  \left(1-\frac{M_0}{m_0}e^{-\delta A_{\min}}\right),
\]
which is at least \(\frac12m_0e^{-\sigma_+A_{\min}}\) for sufficiently large
loops.  This gives the lower exponential bound with any \(b>\sigma_+\), after
absorbing constants into the allowed subexponential factors.  The logarithmic
rate statement follows immediately.  The exact string tension is not obtained
from these estimates alone; it depends on the complete convergent polymer
sum and on the lattice action.
\end{controlledapproximation}

This becomes a theorem about a controlled lattice regime only when the
displayed area estimates are proved for the chosen action.  Its continuum
analogue requires a continuum limit, a mass gap or other infrared control,
and renormalized line operators whose asymptotics survive the limit.

\section{Three-Dimensional Monopole Gas Area Mechanism}

There is one important continuum semiclassical setting where magnetic
topological events do produce a Wilson-loop area law by a direct calculation:
compact \(U(1)\) gauge theory in three Euclidean dimensions, or the
long-distance \(U(1)\) sector of the three-dimensional Georgi--Glashow model
after \(SU(2)\) is Higgsed to \(U(1)\).  The magnetic events are
monopole-instantons, not four-dimensional BPST instantons.  Their physical
effect is not a theta-vacuum cumulant; it is a mass for the dual photon and a
domain-wall cost for an electric Wilson loop.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[3D monopole gas area law]
\label{ca:polyakov-monopole-gas-area-law}
Assume a three-dimensional compact Abelian infrared window in which the heavy
charged fields are separated from the monopole scale, primitive monopoles and
anti-monopoles have a finite fugacity \(\zeta_{\rm M}\), and the dilute
Coulomb-gas expansion is controlled on distances large compared with the
monopole core.  Let \(\varphi\sim\varphi+2\pi\) be the dual photon and choose
the local normalization
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm dual}[\varphi]
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R^3}
  \left[
    \frac{\kappa_{\rm d}}2(\partial\varphi)^2
    +
    2\zeta_{\rm M}(1-\cos\varphi)
  \right]\dd^3x .
  \label{eq:polyakov-dual-photon-action}
\end{equation}
This is the sine--Gordon representative of the monopole and anti-monopole
grand canonical sum in this normalization.  Expanding at a vacuum
\(\varphi=2\pi n\) gives the dual-photon mass
\begin{equation}
  m_\gamma^2=\frac{2\zeta_{\rm M}}{\kappa_{\rm d}} .
  \label{eq:polyakov-dual-photon-mass}
\end{equation}

For a primitive electric Wilson loop \(W(C)\) which is not screened by
dynamical endpoints in the declared low-energy theory, the dual description
imposes a \(2\pi\) discontinuity of \(\varphi\) across an oriented spanning
surface \(\Sigma\) with \(\partial\Sigma=C\).  For a large nearly planar loop,
the leading saddle is a one-dimensional sine--Gordon wall normal to
\(\Sigma\).  It satisfies
\[
  \kappa_{\rm d}\varphi''=2\zeta_{\rm M}\sin\varphi,\qquad
  \varphi(-\infty)=0,\quad \varphi(+\infty)=2\pi,
\]
or, equivalently, the first-order equation
\begin{equation}
  \varphi'=2m_\gamma\sin\frac{\varphi}{2}.
  \label{eq:polyakov-wall-first-order}
\end{equation}
The wall tension is therefore
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\rm P}
  =
  \int_0^{2\pi}
  \sqrt{2\kappa_{\rm d}\,2\zeta_{\rm M}(1-\cos\varphi)}\,\dd\varphi
  =
  8\sqrt{2\kappa_{\rm d}\zeta_{\rm M}}
  =
  8\kappa_{\rm d}m_\gamma .
  \label{eq:polyakov-wall-string-tension}
\end{equation}
After local line counterterms have removed the perimeter self-energy, the
Wilson loop has the semiclassical large-area form
\begin{equation}
  -\log |\langle W(C)\rangle|
  =
  \sigma_{\rm P}\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma_{\rm min})
  +o(\operatorname{Area})
  \label{eq:polyakov-monopole-wilson-area-law}
\end{equation}
for loop families whose curvature radii and linear sizes are large compared
with \(m_\gamma^{-1}\), provided no lower-energy screened endpoint channel is
available.

The derivation is the following finite chain of physical inputs.  The
monopole fugacity inserts \(\ee^{\ii\varphi}\), the anti-monopole fugacity
inserts \(\ee^{-\ii\varphi}\), and the dilute grand canonical sum gives the
cosine potential in
\eqref{eq:polyakov-dual-photon-action}.  The cosine pins the dual photon and
opens the mass gap \eqref{eq:polyakov-dual-photon-mass}.  The Wilson loop is
not evaluated by a monopole moduli-space volume; it changes the dual-photon
boundary condition across \(\Sigma\).  The minimum action configuration with
that discontinuity is the sine--Gordon wall, and its action per unit area is
\eqref{eq:polyakov-wall-string-tension}.

The scope of this mechanism is the three-dimensional compact-Abelian
semiclassical window.  Here the semiclassical objects live in three Euclidean
dimensions, the dual photon is a local scalar in the infrared effective
theory, and the Wilson loop is converted into a calculable wall problem.  The
dilute BPST instanton gas of Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-20} supplies
theta-dependent amplitude data in four dimensions rather than this
dual-photon wall mechanism.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{Condensation and Oblique Confinement}

The finite part of a gapped line-operator response can often be organized by
an isotropic subgroup
\[
  K\subset\mathcal C
\]
of condensed charges.  The isotropy condition
\[
  B(k,k')=0,\qquad k,k'\in K,
\]
is mutual locality of the condensed objects.  It is a consistency condition,
not a dynamical proof that such objects actually condense.

\begin{definition}[Finite confinement diagnostic from a condensate]
\label{def:finite-confinement-diagnostic}
Given a finite charge system \((\mathcal C,B)\) and an isotropic subgroup
\(K\subset\mathcal C\), define
\[
  K^\perp=\{\gamma\in\mathcal C\mid B(\gamma,k)=0
  \text{ for every }k\in K\}.
\]
The finite condensate diagnostic declares \(\gamma\) unconfined only if
\(\gamma\in K^\perp\).  Charges outside \(K^\perp\) have nontrivial finite
braiding with the condensate and are confined in this diagnostic.
\end{definition}

Assume a gapped regime in which perimeter-law lines must braid trivially with
all condensed charges in an isotropic subgroup \(K\subset\mathcal C\).  Then
only charges in \(K^\perp\) can have perimeter behavior.  If \(K\) is maximal
isotropic, then \(K^\perp=K\).
Moving a test line of charge \(\gamma\) once around a condensed object of
charge \(k\) multiplies the state by
\[
  \exp(2\pi\ii B(\gamma,k)).
\]
A perimeter-law line compatible with the condensate background must be
single-valued under this operation for every \(k\in K\).  This is exactly the
condition \(\gamma\in K^\perp\).

If \(K\subsetneq K^\perp\), choose \(\eta\in K^\perp\setminus K\).  Since the
pairing is alternating, \(B(\eta,\eta)=0\), and by definition
\(B(\eta,K)=0\).  Therefore the subgroup generated by \(K\) and \(\eta\) is
isotropic and strictly larger than \(K\), contradicting maximal isotropy.
Hence a maximal isotropic \(K\) satisfies \(K=K^\perp\).

\begin{example}[\(\mathfrak{su}(N)\) finite charges]
\label{ex:su-n-finite-oblique-confinement}
For
\[
  \mathcal C=\mathbb Z_N^{\rm e}\oplus\mathbb Z_N^{\rm m},
  \qquad
  B((e,m),(e',m'))=\frac{em'-e'm}{N},
\]
magnetic condensation is \(K=\langle(0,1)\rangle\).  A charge
\((e,m)\) lies in \(K^\perp\) exactly when
\[
  B((e,m),(0,1))=\frac{e}{N}=0\quad \text{in }\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z,
\]
so only \(e=0\) survives the finite diagnostic.  Pure electric probes with
nonzero \(N\)-ality are confined.  Electric condensation
\(K=\langle(1,0)\rangle\) gives the dual statement: purely magnetic probes
with nonzero magnetic center charge are confined.

For a dyonic condensate
\[
  K_p=\langle(p,1)\rangle ,
\]
the unconfined finite charges obey
\[
  B((e,m),(p,1))=\frac{e-pm}{N}=0,
  \qquad\text{so}\qquad e\equiv pm\pmod N .
\]
Thus the perimeter-law direction is the dyonic line generated by the
condensate.  Pure electric probes with \(e\ne0\) are confined, and pure
magnetic probes are confined unless \(pm\equiv0\pmod N\).  This is oblique
confinement: the unconfined direction is neither the electric nor the
magnetic axis.
\end{example}

\section{A Continuum Criterion from Renormalized Lines}
\label{sec:continuum-renormalized-line-confinement-criterion}

The finite pairing criterion becomes a continuum confinement statement only
after the line operators and limits have been fixed.  For a dyonic charge
\(\gamma=(\lambda_{\rm e},\lambda_{\rm m})\), write
\[
  L_\gamma(C)
\]
for the corresponding closed line: a Wilson loop when
\(\lambda_{\rm m}=0\), an 't Hooft loop when \(\lambda_{\rm e}=0\), and a
Wilson--'t Hooft loop when both components are present.  In the last case the
operator is a magnetic singularity of charge \(\lambda_{\rm m}\) along \(C\),
together with a Wilson factor for a representation of the centralizer of that
singularity, as in Chapter~\ref{ch:global-line-surface-domain-wall-operators}.

\begin{definition}[Continuum line limit and large-loop topology]
\label{def:continuum-line-limit-large-loop-topology}
Fix a UV regulator \(a\), a finite spacetime region \(\Omega\), and a
piecewise-smooth contour \(C\subset\Omega\).  A renormalized dyonic line is a
limit
\begin{equation}
  \langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C)\rangle_\Omega
  =
  \lim_{a\downarrow0}
  \exp\!\left[
    -\alpha_\gamma(a)\ell(C)
    -\sum_j\kappa_{\gamma,\theta_j}(a)
  \right]
  \langle L_{\gamma,a}(C_a)\rangle_{\Omega,a},
  \label{eq:continuum-line-renormalization-limit}
\end{equation}
where \(C_a\) approximates \(C\), \(\ell(C)\) is its length, and
\(\kappa_{\gamma,\theta_j}\) are local cusp or corner counterterms at angles
\(\theta_j\).  The thermodynamic expectation is then
\[
  \langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C)\rangle
  =
  \lim_{\Omega\uparrow\mathbb R^4}
  \langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C)\rangle_\Omega,
\]
with \(C\) kept a positive distance from \(\partial\Omega\) before the limit.
Only after these two limits are taken do we send a loop family to infinity.

A regular large-loop family \(\{C_R\}\) is a family with minimal spanning areas
\(A_R\to\infty\), perimeters \(P_R\), uniformly bounded local geometry after
rescaling, and
\[
  \frac{P_R}{A_R}\longrightarrow0 .
\]
For rectangular static loops \(C_{T,L}\), the order is UV limit, infinite
volume, \(T\to\infty\), and finally \(L\to\infty\).  The \(T\)-linear perimeter
self-energy of the two static sources is part of the line-renormalization
scheme and must be removed before reading off the linear coefficient in \(L\).
\end{definition}

This order matters.  If the large-loop limit is taken before the UV line
renormalization, the divergent self-energy can be mistaken for an area term.
If the large-\(L\) limit is taken before the transfer-matrix \(T\to\infty\)
limit, an excited flux-tube component can be mistaken for the static ground
state, as in the string-breaking discussion above.

For the criterion, start with the screened topological quotient
\[
  \mathcal C_{\rm top}=S^\perp/S
\]
from Definition~\ref{def:external-charge-system}, and let
\(\overline\gamma\) be the class of a genuine line charge
\(\gamma\in S^\perp\).  For an isotropic condensate
\(K\subset\mathcal C_{\rm top}\), define the finite pairing distance
\[
  d_K(\overline\gamma)
  =
  \max_{k\in K}
  \operatorname{dist}_{\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z}
  \bigl(B(\overline\gamma,k),0\bigr).
\]
For a finite group, \(d_K(\overline\gamma)>0\) exactly when
\(\overline\gamma\notin K^\perp\).

\begin{proposition}[Continuum line-confinement criterion]
\label{prop:continuum-line-confinement-criterion}
Assume a continuum phase has controlled line confinement data for
\((\mathcal C,B,S,K)\) in the following sense.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The renormalized Wilson, 't Hooft, and dyonic line expectations exist
  in the order of Definition~\ref{def:continuum-line-limit-large-loop-topology}.
  \item The vacuum has exponential clustering, or an explicitly stated
  replacement infrared spectral condition, for the neutral local algebra and
  for the static-source sectors generated by the declared lines.
  \item Charges in \(S\) have finite-energy endpoint operators: the static
  energy for such charges is bounded by screened trial states as in the
  endpoint-screening bound above.
  \item For every regular loop family and every
  \(\overline\gamma\notin K^\perp\), there are constants
  \(c_\gamma^\pm>0\), \(\mu_\gamma\), \(\sigma_\gamma^->0\), and
  \(\sigma_\gamma^+<\infty\), independent of \(R\), such that
  \begin{equation}
    c_\gamma^- e^{-\sigma_\gamma^+A_R-\mu_\gamma P_R}
    \le
    |\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|
    \le
    c_\gamma^+ e^{-\sigma_\gamma^-A_R+\mu_\gamma P_R}.
    \label{eq:continuum-surface-cost-bounds}
  \end{equation}
  The lower area rate satisfies
  \(\sigma_\gamma^-\ge\sigma_\ast d_K(\overline\gamma)^2\) for some
  \(\sigma_\ast>0\) on the finite set of charges under discussion.
  \item For every \(\overline\gamma\in K^\perp\), the line has zero area
  tension in the same topology:
  \begin{equation}
    \lim_{R\to\infty}
    \frac{-\log|\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|}{A_R}=0
    \label{eq:continuum-perimeter-compatible-line}
  \end{equation}
  whenever the expectation is not identically zero by a separate selection rule.
\end{enumerate}

Then:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item charges screened by \(S\) do not have an asymptotic positive linear
  static potential;
  \item a genuine charge class \(\overline\gamma\notin K^\perp\) has a positive
  area rate,
  \[
    \liminf_{R\to\infty}
    \frac{-\log|\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|}{A_R}
    \ge \sigma_\gamma^- >0 ;
  \]
  if the rate limit exists, this is an area law for \(\gamma\);
  \item a genuine charge class \(\overline\gamma\in K^\perp\) is the only kind
  compatible with perimeter behavior or zero string tension in this phase.
\end{enumerate}
For rectangular static loops, the same bounds give
\[
  V_\gamma^{\rm ren}(L)\ge \sigma_\gamma^- L+O(1)
\]
for \(\overline\gamma\notin K^\perp\), after the static source self-energy is
subtracted in the declared line scheme.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The endpoint-screened statement is the variational bound already proved above:
finite-energy endpoint trial states give
\(\limsup_{L\to\infty}V_\gamma(L)<\infty\), which is incompatible with
\(V_\gamma(L)=\sigma L+o(L)\) for \(\sigma>0\).

For a genuine nonscreened line with
\(\overline\gamma\notin K^\perp\), the upper bound in
\eqref{eq:continuum-surface-cost-bounds} gives
\[
  -\log|\langle L_\gamma^{\rm ren}(C_R)\rangle|
  \ge
  \sigma_\gamma^-A_R-\mu_\gamma P_R-\log c_\gamma^+ .
\]
Divide by \(A_R\).  Since \(P_R/A_R\to0\), the perimeter and constant terms
vanish in the large-loop topology, giving the positive lower area rate.  The
lower bound in \eqref{eq:continuum-surface-cost-bounds} prevents the same
criterion from secretly asserting an infinite tension; it keeps the response in
a finite exponential area window.  If the exponential rate has a limit, the
definition of an area law applies.

For \(\overline\gamma\in K^\perp\), the zero-tension hypothesis
\eqref{eq:continuum-perimeter-compatible-line} is precisely the assertion that
no positive area coefficient remains after line renormalization.  For
rectangles, write \(A=TL\) and \(P=2T+2L\) in
\eqref{eq:continuum-surface-cost-bounds}, take
\(-T^{-1}\log\) and then \(T\to\infty\).  The terms proportional to \(L/T\)
and \(T^{-1}\log c_\gamma^+\) vanish, while the \(2\mu_\gamma\) term is the
scheme-dependent static-source self-energy.  Subtracting it leaves the linear
coefficient \(\sigma_\gamma^-L\).
\end{proof}

The strong-coupling lattice mechanism
\ref{ca:lattice-strong-coupling-area-law} is a calibration of
the surface-cost assumptions in
Proposition~\ref{prop:continuum-line-confinement-criterion}: Haar projection
supplies the surface selection rule, and the convergent polymer expansion
supplies the finite exponential area window.  Finite cochain models and
center-charge arithmetic supply the pairing, quotient, and condensate labels;
they do not by themselves supply the continuum surface-cost bounds.

\begin{example}[\(\mathfrak{su}(N)\) continuum line classes]
\label{ex:su-n-continuum-line-criterion}
In a pure \(\mathfrak{su}(N)\) Yang--Mills phase with no dynamical fundamental
endpoints, \(S=0\) and
\(\mathcal C=\mathbb Z_N^{\rm e}\oplus\mathbb Z_N^{\rm m}\).  If the continuum
surface-cost assumptions hold with magnetic condensation
\[
  K=\langle(0,1)\rangle ,
\]
then
\[
  K^\perp=\{(0,m)\mid m\in\mathbb Z_N\}.
\]
Wilson loops of nonzero \(N\)-ality have positive electric area rate, while
pure magnetic lines are the perimeter-compatible direction.  If dynamical
fundamental matter is present, the electric generator lies in the endpoint
screening subgroup \(S\); the static Wilson charge is then screened by trial
states and the Wilson loop is not an asymptotic confinement order parameter,
even if it exhibits a long flux-tube plateau at finite Euclidean time.

For the oblique condensate
\[
  K_p=\langle(p,1)\rangle ,
\]
the perimeter-compatible classes satisfy
\[
  e\equiv pm\pmod N .
\]
The criterion therefore distinguishes electric, magnetic, and dyonic
confinement by the full line-charge class, not by a single Wilson-loop family.
The generalized-symmetry surfaces of the surrounding chapters detect the same
finite pairing \(B\); the extra dynamical input here is the continuum
surface-cost estimate that turns nontrivial pairing into a large-loop area
rate.
\end{example}

\section{Logical Status of the Diagnostic}

The finite charge-lattice criterion is kinematic plus an explicit dynamical
input.  The kinematic part is the nondegenerate Dirac pairing and the
screening quotient.  The dynamical input is the assertion that a particular
subgroup \(K\) is condensed and that nontrivial braiding with \(K\) forces an
area law.  In a lattice strong-coupling domain the dynamical input can be
verified by a convergent expansion, as in
the controlled strong-coupling estimate
\ref{ca:lattice-strong-coupling-area-law}.  In a continuum
four-dimensional Yang--Mills theory it is a major theorem-level target rather
than a definition.

The following implication is therefore the correct logical form:
\[
\begin{array}{c}
\text{regulator and continuum limit}\\
\text{renormalized line operators}\\
\text{mass gap or controlled infrared behavior}\\
\text{screening and condensate hypotheses}
\end{array}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\begin{array}{c}
\text{area/perimeter laws}\\
\text{electric, magnetic, or oblique}\\
\text{confinement diagnostics}.
\end{array}
\]
The converse is not automatic.  Observing an area law for one family of
Wilson loops does not by itself reconstruct the infrared topological sector,
the complete screening subgroup, or the full spectrum of stable flux tubes.

\begin{openproblem}[Continuum Yang--Mills surface-cost hypothesis]
\label{op:continuum-confinement-criterion}
Prove the surface-cost assumptions in
Proposition~\ref{prop:continuum-line-confinement-criterion} from a
four-dimensional continuum Yang--Mills construction, or from another continuum
regulator whose removal is controlled.  In particular, derive the surface-cost
bounds
\eqref{eq:continuum-surface-cost-bounds} and the zero-tension statement
\eqref{eq:continuum-perimeter-compatible-line} from the Lagrangian, mass gap,
cluster properties, screening endpoint spectrum, and condensate data, rather
than postulating them from the finite charge lattice.
\end{openproblem}
