\chapter{Extended Operators and Topological Defects}
\label{ch:extended-operators-topological-defects}

\ConventionsMixed

Local operators probe neighborhoods of points.  Extended operators probe the
global and higher-codimensional structure of a QFT.  Their definition is part
of the theory because it fixes allowed singularities, endpoints, and
topological sectors.

\section{Operators Supported on Submanifolds}

\begin{definition}[Extended operator by correlation data]
\label{def:extended-operator-correlation-data}
Let \(M\) be spacetime and let \(\Sigma^p\subset M\) be an embedded
\(p\)-dimensional submanifold with a specified framing or normal-bundle
structure when required.  An extended operator \(W(\Sigma)\) is a correlation
datum assigning distributions
\[
  \langle W(\Sigma)\,\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle
\]
for insertions away from \(\Sigma\), together with a prescription for
singularities as local operators approach \(\Sigma\).  The prescription may
be given by boundary conditions on a tubular neighborhood, by a defect
operator algebra, or by a regulated path integral with a specified limit.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Topological defect]
\label{def:topological-defect}
A defect is topological when its correlation functions are invariant under
isotopies of \(\Sigma\) that avoid other insertions and preserve the
background structures.
\end{definition}

Topological defects can be moved through spacetime, and their nontrivial
information appears when they cross charged operators, end on other defects,
or fuse with one another.

\section{Fusion and Defect Operators}

For two parallel topological defects \(D_1,D_2\) of the same codimension,
fusion is the limit in which their supports approach one another inside a
tubular neighborhood:
\[
  D_1\otimes D_2
  =
  \lim_{\epsilon\downarrow0}D_1(\Sigma_\epsilon)D_2(\Sigma).
\]
The limit is defined through correlation functions with external insertions
kept away from the tubular neighborhood.  The topology of this limit is part
of the defect theory: it may be finite-dimensional, distributional, or
operator-algebraic depending on the model.  In finite semisimple situations,
fusion gives a category or higher category of defects.  In nonsemisimple or
continuum situations, the target may be an analytic category of modules,
von Neumann algebras, or distributional kernels.

Endpoints and junctions are lower-dimensional defect operators.  If a line
operator can end on a local field, then that local field carries the endpoint
charge and the line is screened by the dynamical spectrum.  If no such
endpoint is present, the line defines an autonomous charged object.  This is
the operator-level distinction used in diagnosing confinement and screening.

\section{Ward Operators for Higher-Form Symmetry}

For a \(p\)-form symmetry, the topological symmetry operator
\(U_g(M^{D-p-1})\) acts on \(p\)-dimensional charged operators by linking.
The action defines a representation of the symmetry group on the space of
charged defects:
\[
  U_g(M)W_q(\Sigma)U_g(M)^{-1}
  =
  \chi_q(g)^{\operatorname{Link}(M,\Sigma)}W_q(\Sigma).
\]
Here \(\chi_q\) is the character associated to charge \(q\).  In nonabelian
or categorical generalizations, the phase is replaced by a linear functor or
natural transformation on the defect category.

\section{Defect Locality}

Extended operators obey a locality condition relative to ordinary local
operators and to one another.  If two defects have disjoint spacelike
supports in Lorentzian signature, their order in a correlation function is
fixed by a chosen braiding or commutation law.  For topological defects, this
braiding becomes a topological operation.  In gauge theories, the braiding of
Wilson and 't Hooft lines is controlled by the electric-magnetic charge
pairing and by the global form chosen in
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-forms-higher-form-symmetry}.

\begin{openproblem}[Extended operators from local reconstruction]
\label{op:extended-operators-from-local-reconstruction}
Develop reconstruction theorems that recover allowed extended operators and
their fusion categories from local observable data plus specified
superselection sectors.  The result should clarify which data are invisible
to ordinary local Wightman functions and which are forced by locality,
duality, or completeness.
\end{openproblem}
