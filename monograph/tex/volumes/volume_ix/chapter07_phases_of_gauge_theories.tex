\chapter{Phases Of Gauge Theories}
\label{ch:global-phases-of-gauge-theories}

\ConventionsMixed

A phase of a gauge theory is a statement about infinite-volume
gauge-invariant QFT data.  Gauge-variant fields, gauge-fixed expectation
values, and coordinates on a weakly coupled Lagrangian chart are not phase
invariants by themselves.  The invariant data are states on observable
algebras, spectra, local correlators, line and surface asymptotics,
background-field responses, and the realization of ordinary and higher-form
symmetries.

\section{Observable Systems And Thermodynamic Limits}

Fix a regulator class.  On a finite spatial region \(\Lambda\), let
\[
  \mathcal A_\Lambda
\]
be the algebra generated by the declared gauge-invariant observables in that
region.  Here ``observable'' means a bounded finite-support operator after the
regulator and gauge projection have been fixed.  A bounded loop or bounded
surface insertion can be an element of such an algebra.  A boundary condition,
a superselection sector, an external-source response, or a continuum
renormalized defect is a different kind of datum and is not inserted into
\(\mathcal A_\Lambda\) merely by naming it.  If \(\Lambda\subset\Lambda'\),
there is an inclusion
\[
  \iota_{\Lambda\Lambda'}:\mathcal A_\Lambda\hookrightarrow\mathcal A_{\Lambda'}.
\]
The quasi-local observable algebra is the \(C^*\)-norm completion of the
directed union
\[
  \mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}
  =
  \overline{\bigcup_\Lambda\mathcal A_\Lambda}.
\]
A thermodynamic-limit state is a positive normalized linear functional
\[
  \omega:\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}\longrightarrow\mathbb C
\]
obtained as a limit of finite-volume states along a specified boundary
condition and regulator trajectory.

The remaining phase data have separate carriers.  A sector is a representation,
localized endomorphism, field-algebra extension, or categorical object.  A
boundary condition selects a state, representation, boundary net, or limiting
prescription.  A source functional is a source-dependent family of states or
generating functionals, and a response tensor is a derivative of that family
after contact terms, normalization, and thermodynamic and frequency/momentum
limits have been specified.  Euclidean Schwinger distributions are not a
\(C^*\)-norm completion of local algebras; when they are used as Hilbert-space
phase data they require the Schwinger/Osterwalder--Schrader reconstruction
hypotheses from Volumes I and IV.

\begin{definition}[Declared phase problem]
\label{def:declared-phase-problem}
A phase problem consists of a regulator class, a parameter domain
\(\mathcal P\) of couplings and topological terms, a bounded quasi-local
observable algebra \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}\), a typed phase-data package,
a thermodynamic or continuum limit prescription with boundary conditions, fixed
symmetry/global-form/boundary conventions, and a stability criterion.  The
typed package names, separately, the selected state carrier, representation or
sector carrier, boundary nets, growing-support extended-operator families,
source functionals, response tensors, and any Euclidean/scaling-limit data.
A comparison topology is auxiliary: it specifies how transported states,
spectra, correlators, extended operators, responses, and topological-sector
data are inspected after the stability criterion has supplied an
identification of the relevant carriers.
A phase is always a phase of such a declared problem, not an absolute label
attached to a Lagrangian density.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Hamiltonian phase carrier]
\label{def:hamiltonian-phase-carrier}
Fix a parameter \(p\), boundary convention, exhausting finite-volume sequence,
and isolated finite-volume spectral band \(P_n(p)\).  Let
\(\mathcal S_p^{\rm band}\) be the weak-star compact convex set of
thermodynamic limit points of finite-volume states whose density matrices are
supported in \(P_n(p)\), restricted to the quasi-local algebra
\(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}(p)\).  A selected state carrier is a declared
weak-star closed subset
\[
  \mathfrak T_p\subseteq\mathcal S_p^{\rm band},
\]
such as the whole band-selected set, a weak-star closed face, a connected
component, or a specified family of extremal states.  A Hamiltonian phase datum
consists of this state carrier together with the finite-volume band, boundary
conditions, selector, and limiting procedure that choose it.

If \(P_n(p)\) is an arbitrary isolated band, then
\(\mathcal S_p^{\rm band}\) is only a band-supported limit set.  It is a
ground-state set only under the bottom-band hypothesis: for finite-volume
ground energies \(E_n(p)=\inf\sigma(H_n(p))\) there are
\(\delta_n\downarrow0\) and \(\gamma>0\), independent of \(n\), such that the
chosen projection is supported in
\([E_n(p),E_n(p)+\delta_n]\) and the rest of the spectrum lies above
\(E_n(p)+\delta_n+\gamma\).  An isolated excited band can be transported by
finite spectral flow, but its weak-star limits are not ground states merely
because the band is isolated.

The words naming subsets are not interchangeable.  A face
\(F\subseteq\mathcal S_p^{\rm band}\) is a convex subset such that
\(\lambda\omega_1+(1-\lambda)\omega_2\in F\), \(0<\lambda<1\), forces
\(\omega_1,\omega_2\in F\).  An extremal state is an extreme point of the
chosen convex state set; purity as a state on \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}\) is
stronger unless the ambient set is the full state space.  A factorial state is
one whose GNS von Neumann algebra has factor center.  An ergodic state is
ergodic only relative to a declared symmetry, translation, or dynamics action.
The band-selected set is a weak-star compact convex set; it is called a
simplex only when the required Choquet-decomposition hypotheses have been
stated.

Representation, sector, and boundary carriers are separate data.  A
representation carrier, denoted \(\mathfrak R_p\), is a set of GNS
quasi-equivalence classes, localized endomorphism sectors, field-algebra
extensions, or boundary-net data.  It is not a subset of
\(\mathcal S_p^{\rm band}\), although a selected state carrier can induce such
representation data.

The bare parameter value \(p\) is not the carrier.  At a symmetry-breaking or
first-order coexistence point, \(\mathcal S_p^{\rm band}\) may contain several
pure or factorial faces.  A positive gap above the union of finite-volume
ground states does not by itself select an extremal state, an ergodic
component, or a unique phase label.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Gapped regulated phase path]
\label{def:gauge-theory-phase}
\label{def:gapped-regulated-phase-equivalence}
For a regulated Hamiltonian problem, two selected Hamiltonian phase data with
state carriers \(\mathfrak T_{p_0}\) and \(\mathfrak T_{p_1}\), with
\(p_0,p_1\in\mathcal P\), are connected by a gapped regulated phase path when
they can be joined by a path
\(s\mapsto \Phi_s\), \(s\in[0,1]\), of local interactions on an exhausting
finite-volume sequence \(\Lambda_n\), with Hamiltonians
\(H_n(s)=\sum_X\Phi_{n,s}(X)\), such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the interaction range, interaction norm, overlap degree, and the
  corresponding support-cardinality bounds for \(\partial_s\Phi_{n,s}\) are
  uniform in \(n\) and \(s\), or more generally the interactions and their
  support-weighted \(s\)-derivatives obey the common summability and
  exhaustion hypotheses of
  Definition~\ref{def:uniform-summable-interaction-hypotheses};
\item the path is sufficiently regular in \(s\) in that interaction norm, with
  an admissible quasi-adiabatic filter, to apply the spectral-flow theorem;
\item the declared symmetry, global form, anomaly counterterms, boundary
  convention, quasi-local observable algebra, and typed phase-data slots are
  fixed along the path;
\item a declared finite-volume spectral projection \(P_n(s)\), with spectral
  subset \(\sigma^{\mathrm{band}}_n(s)\subset\sigma(H_n(s))\), is separated
  from the rest of the finite-volume spectrum by a volume-uniform gap
  \[
    \operatorname{dist}\bigl(\sigma^{\mathrm{band}}_n(s),
    \sigma(H_n(s))\setminus\sigma^{\mathrm{band}}_n(s)\bigr)\ge \gamma>0 ;
  \]
  when the path is used as a ground-state phase path, this projection is the
  bottom band and satisfies the \(\delta_n\downarrow0\) ground-band condition
  stated in Definition~\ref{def:hamiltonian-phase-carrier};
\item the thermodynamic limit of the quasi-local observable system, the
  finite-volume band-selected state sets \(\mathcal S_s^{\rm band}\), any
  selected state carriers \(\mathfrak T_s\), any representation or sector
  carriers \(\mathfrak R_s\), and the finite-volume restrictions of the
  interaction path exist with the stated boundary convention and satisfy the
  Cauchy/locality hypotheses of the imported automorphic-equivalence theorem.
\end{enumerate}
The definition names the path data and the selected state-space carrier.  It
does not assume the quasi-local automorphism that the theorem below constructs,
and it does not assume that independently chosen endpoint faces are transported
images of one another.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Imported automorphic equivalence for a gapped path]
\label{thm:gapped-path-automorphic-equivalence-imported}
For a gapped regulated phase path satisfying the hypotheses of
Definition~\ref{def:gapped-regulated-phase-equivalence}, the
Bachmann--Michalakis--Nachtergaele--Sims thermodynamic spectral-flow theorem,
specifically Theorem 5.4 of
\cite{BachmannMichalakisNachtergaeleSims2012Automorphic}, supplies a
spectral-flow cocycle whose thermodynamic limit is a quasi-local automorphism
\[
  \alpha_{1\leftarrow0}:\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}(p_0)
  \longrightarrow \mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}(p_1)
\]
of the quasi-local observable algebra.  The induced dual map transports the
full weak-star limit set supported on the declared band:
\[
  \mathcal S_{p_1}^{\rm band}
  =
  \bigl\{
    \omega\circ\alpha_{1\leftarrow0}^{-1}
    \;:\;
    \omega\in\mathcal S_{p_0}^{\rm band}
  \bigr\}.
\]
This is the BMNS setwise conclusion for the declared spectral interval.  When
the declared interval is the bottom band
\([E_n(s),E_n(s)+\delta_n]\), with \(\delta_n\to0\) and a uniform gap above it,
the transported set is the ground-state set used in the ground-state phase
formulation, matching Definition 5.1 and Theorem 5.4 of
\cite{NachtergaeleSimsYoung2019QuasiLocality}.  For a merely isolated excited
band the same finite projection algebra may transport band-supported states,
but the word ``ground'' is not available without the bottom-band hypothesis.
The theorem does not transport an independently chosen face, branch,
boundary-selected set, or representation class unless a separate selector
theorem or definition supplies that covariance.  It also does not say that all
states inside the transported set are phases of one another.  If the path,
observable system, and quasi-adiabatic generator are equivariant for a fixed
declared symmetry or global-form action, the automorphism intertwines that
action.  Symmetry may carry an extremal branch to another branch in the same
orbit, but equivariant transport does not merge inequivalent coexisting phase
faces.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[Selected state carriers require a covariant selector]
\label{prop:selected-state-carrier-transport-selector}
Let \(\mathfrak T_s\subseteq\mathcal S_s^{\rm band}\) be selected state
carriers along a gapped path satisfying
Theorem~\ref{thm:gapped-path-automorphic-equivalence-imported}.  The endpoint
identity
\[
  \mathfrak T_{p_1}
  =
  \bigl\{
    \omega\circ\alpha_{1\leftarrow0}^{-1}
    \;:\;
    \omega\in\mathfrak T_{p_0}
  \bigr\}
\]
holds only if the selector is spectral-flow covariant.  This may be an
additional theorem about finite-volume selected sets
\(T_n(s)\), a covariantly transported order-parameter or source selector, a
boundary-condition theorem showing that selected thermodynamic limits are
carried by the cocycle, or the explicit definition of
\(\mathfrak T_{p_1}\) as the automorphic image of \(\mathfrak T_{p_0}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The imported theorem gives an affine weak-star homeomorphism from the full
band-selected state set at \(p_0\) onto the full band-selected state set at
\(p_1\).  Restricting a homeomorphism to a subset gives the displayed endpoint
identity exactly when the endpoint subset is the image of the initial subset.
Existence of selected subsets at every \(s\) does not imply this covariance.
\end{proof}

The dual of a \(C^*\)-automorphism preserves convexity, weak-star closedness,
faces, connected components, and extreme points relative to the state set being
transported.  It preserves purity as a state on the quasi-local algebra and
factoriality of the GNS representation.  Ergodicity is preserved only for the
declared action that the automorphism intertwines.  Representation carriers
transport separately by composition of representations with the algebra
isomorphism; this is a statement about \(\mathfrak R_p\), not about subsets
of \(\mathcal S_p^{\rm band}\).

The finite constructions in
Section~\ref{sec:finite-regulator-locality-machinery-phase-stability} explain
the spectral-flow mechanism and the locality estimates that enter the
theorem, but they are not by themselves a proof of thermodynamic convergence.
The theorem is the source of the automorphism; the definition does not include
it as a hidden hypothesis.

\begin{definition}[Carriers for extended phase data]
\label{def:extended-phase-data-carriers}
Extended and topological phase diagnostics must be assigned mathematical
carriers before they can be compared along
\(\alpha_{1\leftarrow0}\).
\begin{enumerate}
\item A finite loop, finite surface, or other bounded-support gauge-invariant
  operator is an element of some local algebra \(\mathcal A_X\), hence of
  \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}\).
\item An increasing loop or surface family is a sequence
  \(O_L\in\mathcal A_{X_L}\) with \(\operatorname{diam}X_L\to\infty\), plus a
  declared topology on its renormalized expectations, such as normalized
  logarithmic area and perimeter data, and uniform estimates in \(L\).
\item A charged, disorder, or superselection sector is represented by the
  chosen sector carrier, such as cone-localized endomorphisms, a field-algebra
  extension, or a boundary-condition category, together with the topology in
  which the sector maps are compared.
\item A boundary condition is represented by the state, representation, boundary
  net, or limiting prescription it selects.  It is not a bounded local
  observable.
\item A finite-volume logical operator is a family tied to a specified
  topology and boundary condition, for example tori with noncontractible
  cycles, ground-band projections, and support growing with system size.
\item A background-source functional is a source-dependent state or generating
  functional.  A response coefficient is a derivative of such a functional,
  with its own contact terms, normalization, differentiability assumptions, and
  order of the thermodynamic, frequency, and momentum limits.
\item Boundary algebras, continuum defects after renormalization, and Euclidean
  Schwinger data require their own half-space, scaling-limit, or OS
  reconstruction maps.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Boundary of automorphic transport for extended data]
\label{prop:extended-phase-data-transport-boundary}
The automorphism in
Theorem~\ref{thm:gapped-path-automorphic-equivalence-imported} transports
fixed quasi-local observables and the selected ground-state carrier.  It
transports an extended diagnostic only after that diagnostic has been supplied
with one of the carriers in
Definition~\ref{def:extended-phase-data-carriers} and uniform estimates
showing that spectral flow acts continuously in the declared topology.  In
particular, area and perimeter laws, sector categories, topological
degeneracy, logical-operator algebras, boundary categories, source
derivatives, Kubo or Hall response tensors, susceptibilities, and
continuum-renormalized line or surface defects do not follow from pointwise
quasi-local automorphic convergence alone.  Generic responses may vary inside a
gapped phase; quantized responses require a separate stability theorem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a fixed bounded support \(X\), an observable \(O\in\mathcal A_X\) is a
single element of the quasi-local algebra, so
\(\alpha_{1\leftarrow0}(O)\) is defined by the theorem and its expectation in
transported ground states is a local phase datum.  An increasing loop family,
a noncontractible logical operator, a boundary/sector object, or a response
coefficient is not one fixed element of \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{obs}}\).  Its
comparison involves a limit over supports, topologies, sector carriers, source
derivatives, contacts, or frequency/momentum limits that are external to the
pointwise algebra automorphism.  Without a uniform estimate controlling that
extra operation, the local automorphism may be perfectly well defined while the
growing-support diagnostic or response has no transported limit or changes its
asymptotic law.  The listed extended invariants therefore require separate
hypotheses.
\end{proof}

With the declared problem fixed, gapped path equivalence is then generated by
such paths and their automorphic outputs between compatible full band-selected
state sets.  For chosen faces or branches, the equivalence relation is generated
only after a spectral-flow-covariant state selector has been supplied, as in
Proposition~\ref{prop:selected-state-carrier-transport-selector}.  Constant
paths give reflexivity, reversing a path gives symmetry, and concatenating two
uniformly controlled gapped paths gives transitivity with the smaller of the
two positive gap bounds and the composed automorphism, provided the intermediate
state selector is the same transported selector.  Representation, sector, and
boundary-net carriers require their own compatible transport statements.
Expectations of fixed local observables are transported consequences.
Susceptibilities, Kubo coefficients, Hall responses, defect responses, and
nonlocal phase data are transported only under the additional carrier-specific
derivative, contact, and limit-exchange hypotheses just stated.

\begin{definition}[Phase boundaries and gapless universality]
\label{def:phase-boundary-gapless-universality}
A gapped phase boundary is a locus where the criterion in
Definition~\ref{def:gapped-regulated-phase-equivalence} cannot be maintained.
This includes loss of a volume-uniform positive gap, divergence of the
correlation length, failure of the thermodynamic or scaling limit, change of
the declared symmetry/global-form/boundary data, or failure of the quasi-local
spectral-flow automorphism, even when finite-distance correlators or
thermodynamic functions remain pointwise continuous.

Gapless regimes require a separate declared universality notion.  The needed
data are a stable infrared description: fixed symmetry and anomaly data,
operator content and selection rules, scaling dimensions or dispersion laws
where known, topological response and extended-operator sectors, and the
allowed relevant or marginal perturbations preserving the universality class.
This chapter does not assume a general theorem turning those data into an
automorphic equivalence theorem for arbitrary gapless gauge theories.
\end{definition}

For continuum gauge theories, Definition~\ref{def:gapped-regulated-phase-equivalence}
is a target criterion.  A continuum claim needs a regulator-independent
scaling-limit map that carries the quasi-local lattice automorphism to an
equivalence of the continuum observable algebra or local net.  An analytic
lattice corridor for selected gauge-invariant local functions is therefore
evidence about declared local data, but by itself it is not a continuum phase
equivalence statement.  These definitions are intentionally conditional: they
specify what must be held fixed before statements such as ``the Higgs and
confinement regions are analytically connected'' or ``two regions are
distinct'' can be evaluated.

\section{Finite-Regulator Locality Machinery For Phase Stability}
\label{sec:finite-regulator-locality-machinery-phase-stability}

The gapped phase criterion above uses quasi-local automorphic transport rather
than continuity alone.  In a regulated Hamiltonian system that transport has
mathematical content only after locality and spectral stability are named.
This section records the finite machinery used later when a gapped lattice
regularization is treated as a proof laboratory for gauge-theory phase data.
It is a mechanism sketch and finite-regulator algebra; the thermodynamic
Cauchy convergence of the spectral-flow automorphism remains part of the
imported theorem boundary in
Theorem~\ref{thm:gapped-path-automorphic-equivalence-imported}.

Let \(\Lambda\) be a finite metric lattice and let
\[
  \mathcal A_\Lambda=\bigotimes_{x\in\Lambda}\mathcal B(\mathcal H_x)
\]
with \(\dim\mathcal H_x<\infty\).  For \(X\subset\Lambda\), write
\(\mathcal A_X\subset\mathcal A_\Lambda\) for the subalgebra supported in
\(X\).  A finite interaction is a map
\[
  \Phi:\{Z\subset\Lambda\}\longrightarrow \mathcal A_Z,
  \qquad
  \Phi(Z)=\Phi(Z)^*,
\]
with Hamiltonian
\[
  H_\Lambda=\sum_{Z\subset\Lambda}\Phi(Z).
\]
Let
\[
  \mathcal I_\Lambda=\{Z\subset\Lambda\mid \Phi(Z)\ne0\}.
\]
If \(\mathcal I_\Lambda=\varnothing\), set \(J_*=N_*=0\).  Otherwise the
finite overlap constants used below are
\[
  J_*=\sup_{Z\in\mathcal I_\Lambda}\|\Phi(Z)\|,
  \qquad
  N_*=\sup_{Z\in\mathcal I_\Lambda}
  \#\{Z'\in\mathcal I_\Lambda\mid Z'\cap Z\ne\varnothing\}.
\]
The support \(Z\) itself is included in the count defining \(N_*\), so
\(N_*\ge1\) whenever the interaction is nonzero.  On-site terms are included in
\(\mathcal I_\Lambda\); they affect the overlap graph but by themselves do not
connect disjoint metric components.
For regions \(X,Y\subset\Lambda\), define \(d_\Phi(X,Y)\) to be the smallest
integer \(n\ge1\) for which there are \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_n\in\mathcal I_\Lambda\)
with
\[
  Z_1\cap X\ne\varnothing,\qquad
  Z_i\cap Z_{i+1}\ne\varnothing,\qquad
  Z_n\cap Y\ne\varnothing .
\]
If no such chain exists, set \(d_\Phi(X,Y)=\infty\).  Also write
\[
  \partial_\Phi X=\{Z\in\mathcal I_\Lambda\mid Z\cap X\ne\varnothing\}.
\]
In sums of the form \(\sum_{n\ge d_\Phi(X,Y)}\), the value
\(d_\Phi(X,Y)=\infty\) means an empty sum.

\begin{theorem}[Finite path-count Lieb--Robinson bound]
\label{thm:finite-path-count-lieb-robinson-bound}
Let
\[
  \tau_t(A)=e^{\ii tH_\Lambda}Ae^{-\ii tH_\Lambda}.
\]
For \(A_X\in\mathcal A_X\), \(B_Y\in\mathcal A_Y\), and
\(X\cap Y=\varnothing\),
\begin{equation}
  \|[\tau_t(A_X),B_Y]\|
  \le
  2\|A_X\|\,\|B_Y\|\,|\partial_\Phi X|
  \sum_{n\ge d_\Phi(X,Y)}
  \frac{(2J_*N_*|t|)^n}{n!}.
  \label{eq:finite-path-count-lr-bound}
\end{equation}
Consequently, for every \(\mu>0\),
\begin{equation}
  \|[\tau_t(A_X),B_Y]\|
  \le
  2\|A_X\|\,\|B_Y\|\,|\partial_\Phi X|\,
  \exp\{-\mu d_\Phi(X,Y)+2J_*N_*e^\mu |t|\}.
  \label{eq:finite-path-count-lr-exponential}
\end{equation}
If the finite systems form an exhausting bounded-degree finite-range family,
with \(J_*\), \(N_*\), and the conversion from \(d_\Phi\) to the metric
distance bounded uniformly, this gives a uniform light cone for local
observables in the thermodynamic sequence.
More explicitly, if every nonzero support has metric diameter at most
\(R_\Phi>0\), then
\[
  d_\Phi(X,Y)\ge
  \left\lceil\frac{\operatorname{dist}(X,Y)}{R_\Phi}\right\rceil ,
\]
and \eqref{eq:finite-path-count-lr-exponential} implies
\[
  \|[\tau_t(A_X),B_Y]\|
  \le
  2\|A_X\|\,\|B_Y\|\,|\partial_\Phi X|\,
  \exp\!\left\{-\frac{\mu}{R_\Phi}\operatorname{dist}(X,Y)
  +2J_*N_*e^\mu |t|\right\}.
\]
Thus a family with uniform \(J_*,N_*,R_\Phi\) has, in this elementary estimate,
a metric Lieb--Robinson velocity bounded by
\[
  v_\mu\le \frac{2J_*N_*R_\Phi e^\mu}{\mu}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(\mathcal I_\Lambda=\varnothing\) or \(\partial_\Phi X=\varnothing\), the
Hamiltonian terms all commute with \(A_X\), so the commutator is zero because
\(X\cap Y=\varnothing\).  Assume henceforth that \(\mathcal I_\Lambda\ne
\varnothing\).  For a finite region \(S\subset\Lambda\), set
\[
  C_B(t,S)
  =
  \sup_{\substack{A\in\mathcal A_S\\ A\ne0}}
  \frac{\|[\tau_t(A),B_Y]\|}{\|A\|}.
\]
If \(S\cap Y\ne\varnothing\), then
\[
  C_B(t,S)\le 2\|B_Y\|
\]
by \(\|[A,B_Y]\|\le2\|A\|\,\|B_Y\|\).  For the recursion, take
\(A\in\mathcal A_S\).  Since terms disjoint from \(S\) commute with \(A\),
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd t}\tau_t(A)
  =
  \ii\sum_{Z\cap S\ne\varnothing}
  [\tau_t(\Phi(Z)),\tau_t(A)] .
\]
For \(f_A(t)=[\tau_t(A),B_Y]\), Jacobi's identity gives
\[
  \dot f_A(t)
  =
  \ii\sum_{Z\cap S\ne\varnothing}
  [\tau_t(\Phi(Z)),f_A(t)]
  +
  \ii\sum_{Z\cap S\ne\varnothing}
  [[\tau_t(\Phi(Z)),B_Y],\tau_t(A)] .
\]
The first sum is a homogeneous commutator equation and therefore only rotates
the norm.  Variation of constants and
\(\|[C,\tau_t(A)]\|\le2\|C\|\,\|A\|\) give the support-kernel recursion
\[
  C_B(t,S)
  \le
  C_B(0,S)
  +
  2\sum_{Z\cap S\ne\varnothing}\|\Phi(Z)\|
  \int_0^{|t|} C_B(u,Z)\,\dd u .
\]
Here the next argument of \(C_B\) is the single interaction support \(Z\), not
the growing cluster \(S\cup Z\).  This is the load-bearing reduction: after the
first step the recursion is indexed by one interaction support.  Applying the
same estimate with \(S=X\), where \(C_B(0,X)=0\), and then with
\(S=Z\in\mathcal I_\Lambda\), where the possible initial contact with \(Y\) is
kept, gives
\[
  C_B(t,Z)
  \le
  2\|B_Y\|\,\mathbf 1_{\{Z\cap Y\ne\varnothing\}}
  +
  2\sum_{\substack{Z'\in\mathcal I_\Lambda\\ Z'\cap Z\ne\varnothing}}
  \|\Phi(Z')\|\int_0^{|t|} C_B(u,Z')\,\dd u .
\]
Iterating these two inequalities produces only adjacent support paths
\[
  Z_1\in\partial_\Phi X,\qquad
  Z_i\cap Z_{i+1}\ne\varnothing,\qquad
  Z_n\cap Y\ne\varnothing .
\]
The ordered integration variables form the simplex
\(0\le u_n\le\cdots\le u_1\le |t|\), whose volume is \(|t|^n/n!\).  Hence
\[
  C_B(t,X)
  \le
  2\|B_Y\|
  \sum_{n\ge1}
  \frac{(2|t|)^n}{n!}
  \sum_{\substack{
    Z_1\in\partial_\Phi X,\; Z_n\cap Y\ne\varnothing\\
    Z_i\cap Z_{i+1}\ne\varnothing\ (1\le i<n)}}
  \prod_{i=1}^n\|\Phi(Z_i)\| .
\]
There are no such paths for \(n<d_\Phi(X,Y)\).  For \(n\ge1\), the first
support has at most \(|\partial_\Phi X|\) choices and, because \(N_*\) includes
self-overlap, each subsequent support has at most \(N_*\) choices.  Thus the
number of length-\(n\) adjacent paths is bounded by
\(|\partial_\Phi X|N_*^{n-1}\le|\partial_\Phi X|N_*^n\), while the product of
norms is at most \(J_*^n\).  Multiplying by \(\|A_X\|\) gives
\eqref{eq:finite-path-count-lr-bound}.  The estimate is zero when
\(d_\Phi(X,Y)=\infty\), since the path sum is empty.  Finally,
\[
  \sum_{n\ge d}\frac{a^n}{n!}
  \le
  e^{-\mu d}\sum_{n\ge0}\frac{(ae^\mu)^n}{n!}
  =
  \exp(-\mu d+ae^\mu),
\]
with \(a=2J_*N_*|t|\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:finite-path-count-lr-exponential}.
\end{proof}

The estimate is finite and quantitative.  Its thermodynamic use requires
uniform locality data.  A volume-dependent interaction norm or an interaction
graph with growing overlap degree can erase the uniform light cone.

\begin{definition}[Uniform summable interaction hypotheses]
\label{def:uniform-summable-interaction-hypotheses}
Let \(\Gamma\) be the infinite metric lattice underlying the exhaustion
\(\Lambda_n\nearrow\Gamma\), with the uniform metric growth needed for the
summable \(F\)-function estimates below.  A positive nonincreasing weight
\(\mathfrak F:[0,\infty)\to(0,\infty)\) is an interaction weight when
\[
  \|\mathfrak F\|_1
  :=
  \sup_{x\in\Gamma}\sum_{y\in\Gamma}\mathfrak F(d(x,y))<\infty
\]
and when its convolution constant
\[
  C_{\mathfrak F}
  :=
  \sup_{x,y\in\Gamma}
  \sum_{z\in\Gamma}
  \frac{\mathfrak F(d(x,z))\mathfrak F(d(z,y))}
       {\mathfrak F(d(x,y))}
\]
is finite.  For an interaction \(\Phi\), set
\[
  \|\Phi\|_{\mathfrak F}
  :=
  \sup_{x,y\in\Gamma}
  \frac{1}{\mathfrak F(d(x,y))}
  \sum_{\substack{Z\subset\Gamma\\ x,y\in Z}}\|\Phi(Z)\|.
\]
On-site terms are included by \(x=y\), or equivalently controlled by a separate
uniform one-site bound; they change local energies but do not propagate
commutators between disjoint regions without multi-site terms.

For a differentiable interaction path define the support-weighted derivative
\[
  (\partial_1\Phi_{n,s})(Z)
  :=
  |Z|\,\partial_s\Phi_{n,s}(Z).
\]
This is the derivative norm that appears in the spectral-flow theorem: BMNS
write \(\partial\Phi_X(s)=|X|\Phi'_X(s)\), and the NSY spectral-flow
assumption bounds
\(\sum_{X\ni x,y}(\|\Phi(X,s)\|+|X|\,\|\Phi'(X,s)\|)\) by an \(F\)-function
kernel.

An exhausting Hamiltonian path satisfies the uniform summable locality
hypotheses when, for some exponentially weighted
\(\mathfrak F_a(r)=e^{-ar}\mathfrak F(r)\) with \(a>0\),
\[
  \sup_{n,s}
  \bigl(
    \|\Phi_{n,s}\|_{\mathfrak F_a}
    +
    \|\partial_1\Phi_{n,s}\|_{\mathfrak F_a}
  \bigr)<\infty,
\]
the local Hilbert-space dimensions and the metric growth entering
\(\|\mathfrak F\|_1,C_{\mathfrak F}\) are uniform, and the finite-volume
interactions and their support-weighted \(s\)-derivatives converge locally in
this same norm.  Concretely, for each fixed finite \(X\) and each fixed
neighborhood radius \(R\), the restrictions of
\(\Phi_{n,s}\) and \(\partial_1\Phi_{n,s}\) to the \(R\)-neighborhood of \(X\)
are Cauchy uniformly in \(s\), and the norm-weighted contribution of all
terms that can influence \(X\) only through the boundary of \(\Lambda_n\)
tends to zero as the boundary recedes.  A bound on
\(\|\partial_s\Phi_{n,s}\|_{\mathfrak F_a}\) is equivalent only in a
finite-range setting with uniformly bounded support cardinality
\(|Z|\le K\), where
\(\|\partial_1\Phi_{n,s}\|_{\mathfrak F_a}\le
K\|\partial_s\Phi_{n,s}\|_{\mathfrak F_a}\).  Without such a cardinality
bound, the unweighted derivative norm is not the theorem hypothesis.

The gapped-path theorem also requires a volume-uniform spectral gap above the
declared band, and the ground-state phase version requires the declared band
to be the bottom band with \(\delta_n\downarrow0\).  Finally one needs an
admissible real odd quasi-adiabatic filter \(F_\gamma\) whose Fourier
transform equals \(\ii/\omega\) outside the gap and whose time tail is
summable against the Lieb--Robinson light-cone leakage.
\end{definition}

The finite-range bounded-degree setting of
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-path-count-lieb-robinson-bound} is the elementary
special case of this \(F\)-function formalism.  The thermodynamic
automorphism used in
Theorem~\ref{thm:gapped-path-automorphic-equivalence-imported} is the
BMNS Theorem 5.4 under such uniform hypotheses
\cite{BachmannMichalakisNachtergaeleSims2012Automorphic}, with the
support-weighted interaction and local \(F\)-norm convergence framework stated
explicitly in NSY Assumption 4.12, Theorem 4.14, Definition 5.1, and Theorem
5.4
\cite{NachtergaeleSims2006LiebRobinsonClustering,
NachtergaeleSimsYoung2019QuasiLocality}.  The displayed finite estimate is
one mechanism input; it is not by itself the infinite-volume Cauchy theorem.

\paragraph{Spectral transport before locality.}
Let \(s\mapsto H_\Lambda(s)\) be a \(C^1\) finite-volume Hamiltonian path and
let \(P_\Lambda(s)\) be the spectral projection onto an isolated band.  The
projection identity
\[
  P_\Lambda(s)^2=P_\Lambda(s)
\]
implies, after differentiating and multiplying by \(P_\Lambda\) on both
sides, that
\[
  P_\Lambda(\partial_sP_\Lambda)P_\Lambda=0.
\]
Define the anti-self-adjoint finite spectral generator
\[
  K_\Lambda(s)=[\partial_sP_\Lambda(s),P_\Lambda(s)].
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \partial_sP_\Lambda(s)=[K_\Lambda(s),P_\Lambda(s)].
  \label{eq:finite-projection-spectral-flow}
\end{equation}
Indeed,
\[
  [K_\Lambda,P_\Lambda]
  =
  (\partial_sP_\Lambda)P_\Lambda+P_\Lambda(\partial_sP_\Lambda)
  =
  \partial_sP_\Lambda .
\]
Thus the finite ODE
\[
  \partial_sU_\Lambda(s)=K_\Lambda(s)U_\Lambda(s),\qquad U_\Lambda(0)=1,
\]
transports the band:
\[
  P_\Lambda(s)=U_\Lambda(s)P_\Lambda(0)U_\Lambda(s)^*.
\]
This is exact spectral algebra, but the operator \(K_\Lambda(s)\) is built
from the global projection \(P_\Lambda(s)\).  It carries no locality estimate
by itself.

Quasi-adiabatic continuation is the locality replacement for this projection
generator.  Suppose the chosen band is separated from the rest of the finite
spectrum by a gap \(\gamma>0\).  Choose a real odd filter
\[
  F_\gamma:\mathbb R\longrightarrow\mathbb R
\]
whose Fourier transform, with convention
\[
  \widehat F_\gamma(\omega)=\int_{\mathbb R}e^{\ii\omega t}F_\gamma(t)\,\dd t,
\]
satisfies
\[
  \widehat F_\gamma(\omega)=\frac{\ii}{\omega},
  \qquad |\omega|\ge\gamma,
\]
and whose tail satisfies the summability condition in
Definition~\ref{def:uniform-summable-interaction-hypotheses}.  Let
\[
  V_\Lambda(s)=\partial_sH_\Lambda(s)
\]
and define the self-adjoint finite quasi-adiabatic generator
\begin{equation}
  D_\Lambda(s)=
  \int_{\mathbb R}
  F_\gamma(t)\,
  e^{\ii tH_\Lambda(s)}V_\Lambda(s)e^{-\ii tH_\Lambda(s)}
  \,\dd t .
  \label{eq:finite-quasi-adiabatic-generator}
\end{equation}
In an eigenbasis of \(H_\Lambda(s)\), with \(a\) in the band and \(b\)
outside the band,
\[
  \langle b|\partial_sP_\Lambda|a\rangle
  =
  -\frac{\langle b|V_\Lambda|a\rangle}{E_b-E_a}.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
  \langle b|D_\Lambda|a\rangle
  =
  \widehat F_\gamma(E_b-E_a)\langle b|V_\Lambda|a\rangle
  =
  \frac{\ii\,\langle b|V_\Lambda|a\rangle}{E_b-E_a}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \langle b|\ii[D_\Lambda,P_\Lambda]|a\rangle
  =
  -\frac{\langle b|V_\Lambda|a\rangle}{E_b-E_a},
\]
and the same calculation in the opposite off-diagonal block gives
\begin{equation}
  \partial_sP_\Lambda(s)=\ii[D_\Lambda(s),P_\Lambda(s)].
  \label{eq:finite-quasi-adiabatic-transports-band}
\end{equation}
The diagonal blocks are zero on both sides.  Thus the quasi-adiabatic
generator transports the isolated band exactly at finite volume.

Now assume the Hamiltonian path is local:
\[
  V_\Lambda(s)=\sum_{X\subset\Lambda}V_X(s),
  \qquad V_X(s)\in\mathcal A_X,
\]
with the uniform summability required in
Definition~\ref{def:uniform-summable-interaction-hypotheses}.  Write
\[
  D_{\Lambda,X}(s)
  =
  \int_{\mathbb R}F_\gamma(t)\,
  e^{\ii tH_\Lambda(s)}V_X(s)e^{-\ii tH_\Lambda(s)}\,\dd t
\]
for the contribution of \(V_X\) to \(D_\Lambda(s)\).  Let \(X_R\) be the
\(R\)-neighborhood of \(X\), and let \(\mathbb E_{X_R}\) be the conditional
expectation from
\(\mathcal A_\Lambda\) onto \(\mathcal A_{X_R}\) obtained by normalized
partial trace over the complement.  In finite dimension this conditional
expectation can be written as Haar averaging over unitaries \(U\) supported in
\(\Lambda\setminus X_R\):
\[
  \mathbb E_{X_R}(A)=\int UAU^*\,\dd U .
\]
Therefore
\[
  A-\mathbb E_{X_R}(A)=\int (A-UAU^*)\,\dd U,
  \qquad
  \|A-\mathbb E_{X_R}(A)\|
  \le
  \sup_U\|[A,U]\|.
\]
Apply this with \(A=e^{\ii tH_\Lambda}V_Xe^{-\ii tH_\Lambda}\).  The
unitaries \(U\) in the last supremum are supported at distance at least \(R\)
from \(X\), and the Lieb--Robinson estimate gives
\[
  \left\|
    e^{\ii tH_\Lambda}V_Xe^{-\ii tH_\Lambda}
    -
    \mathbb E_{X_R}\!\left(e^{\ii tH_\Lambda}V_Xe^{-\ii tH_\Lambda}\right)
  \right\|
  \le
  C_X\|V_X\|\,e^{-\mu(R-v|t|)}
\]
for \(R\ge v|t|\), with constants inherited from
\eqref{eq:finite-path-count-lr-exponential}.  Inserting this estimate into
\eqref{eq:finite-quasi-adiabatic-generator} gives a quasi-local tail bound:
\[
  \|D_{\Lambda,X}(s)-D_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}(s)\|
  \le
  C_X\|V_X(s)\|
  \left(
    e^{-\mu R/2}\int_{|t|\le R/(2v)}|F_\gamma(t)|\,\dd t
    +
    2\int_{|t|>R/(2v)}|F_\gamma(t)|\,\dd t
  \right),
\]
where \(D_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}(s)\in\mathcal A_{X_R}\).  The first term is the
Lieb--Robinson leakage inside the time window, and the second term is the
filter tail outside that window.  This is the mechanism behind quasi-local
spectral flow: the gap fixes the filter in frequency space, the filter tail
controls long times, and the Lieb--Robinson estimate controls the spatial
spread during the retained times.

Define
\[
  g_\gamma(R)
  =
  e^{-\mu R/2}\int_{\mathbb R}|F_\gamma(t)|\,\dd t
  +
  2\int_{|t|>R/(2v)}|F_\gamma(t)|\,\dd t .
\]
For an admissible filter this tail is summable, after replacing the exponential
weight by a slightly slower one if necessary.  The shell decomposition
\[
  \Psi_{\Lambda,X}^{(0)}=D_{\Lambda,X}^{(0)},\qquad
  \Psi_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}
  =
  D_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}-D_{\Lambda,X}^{(R-1)}
  \quad (R\ge1)
\]
writes the finite generator as
\[
  D_\Lambda(s)=\sum_{X,R}\Psi_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}(s),
  \qquad
  \Psi_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}(s)\in\mathcal A_{X_R},
\]
with
\[
  \|\Psi_{\Lambda,X}^{(R)}(s)\|
  \le
  2C_X\|V_X(s)\|\,g_\gamma(R-1)
\]
up to the harmless change of constants at \(R=0\).  Combining this shell tail
with the convolution bound for \(\mathfrak F\) gives a volume-uniform
interaction estimate
\[
  \sup_{\Lambda,s}\|\Psi_{\Lambda,s}\|_{\widetilde{\mathfrak F}}
  \le
  C_{\gamma,\mathfrak F}
  \sup_{\Lambda,s}\|\partial_1\Phi_{\Lambda,s}\|_{\mathfrak F_a}
\]
for a summable weight \(\widetilde{\mathfrak F}\) determined by the
Lieb--Robinson rate and the filter tail.  This is the finite calculation behind
the statement that the quasi-adiabatic generator is itself a uniformly
quasi-local interaction.

The support factor is essential.  In the shell decomposition a derivative term
with support \(X\) is counted through every pair of sites it can connect; BMNS
and NSY absorb this bookkeeping by estimating
\(|X|\|\partial_s\Phi(X,s)\|\).  An unweighted derivative norm controls the
right-hand side only when all nonzero derivative supports have uniformly
bounded cardinality.

Let \(\alpha_s^{(n)}\) be the finite-volume flow generated by
\(\Psi_{\Lambda_n,s}\).  For a fixed local observable \(A\in\mathcal A_X\) and
two sufficiently large volumes, Duhamel's formula for the two \(s\)-flows gives
an estimate of the form
\[
  \|\alpha_s^{(n)}(A)-\alpha_s^{(m)}(A)\|
  \le
  C_A
  \left(
    \varepsilon_{m,n}(X,R)
    +
    \sum_{r\ge R}\widetilde{\mathfrak F}(r)
  \right),
\]
uniformly for \(s\in[0,1]\).  Here \(\varepsilon_{m,n}(X,R)\) measures, inside
the \(R\)-neighborhood of \(X\), local differences in the interactions, in the
support-weighted derivatives, and in the boundary conventions.  The local
\(F\)-norm convergence hypothesis in
Definition~\ref{def:uniform-summable-interaction-hypotheses} sends
\(\varepsilon_{m,n}(X,R)\) to zero for fixed \(R\), while the summability of
\(\widetilde{\mathfrak F}\) sends the tail to zero as \(R\to\infty\).  Thus the
finite-volume spectral flows are Cauchy on every local observable, and the
same estimate gives independence of the exhaustion or boundary convention
whenever their local differences vanish in this norm.

The finite estimates just displayed are the local mechanism and Cauchy
bookkeeping used by the imported automorphic-equivalence theorem.  In an
actual thermodynamic application one must verify the uniform hypotheses of
Definition~\ref{def:uniform-summable-interaction-hypotheses}; the cited theorem
then turns the finite-volume cocycles into a quasi-local automorphism of the
infinite-volume observable algebra.  That automorphism is the regulated
mathematical object that transports local phase data.  It is distinct from both
physical time evolution and from an arbitrary finite-dimensional unitary
interpolation of ground spaces.

\section{Lieb--Schultz--Mattis--Oshikawa--Hastings Constraints}
\label{sec:lsmoh-lattice-qft-constraints}

The locality machinery above has a sharper use than phase stability along a
chosen path: it can obstruct the existence of the path's endpoint.  The
Lieb--Schultz--Mattis--Oshikawa--Hastings family of results
\cite{LiebSchultzMattis1961,Oshikawa2000Commensurability,Hastings2004LSMHigher}
connects microscopic translation and charge data to infrared phase
alternatives.  The physics statement is not ``fractional filling implies
gapless.''  It is that a symmetric, unique, gapped, short-range-entangled
infrared phase is incompatible with certain unit-cell data.  The allowed
outcomes are symmetry breaking, gapless excitations, or intrinsic topological
order.

Start with the \(U(1)\)-filling form.  Let
\[
  \Lambda_L=\prod_{a=1}^d\mathbb Z/L_a\mathbb Z
\]
be a finite spatial torus with finite-dimensional on-site Hilbert spaces, a
finite-range translation-invariant Hamiltonian \(H_L\), translations \(T_a\),
and an integer-valued conserved charge
\[
  Q=\sum_{x\in\Lambda_L} q_x,\qquad [H_L,Q]=0 .
\]
The filling along a thermodynamic sequence is
\[
  \nu=\lim_{L\to\infty}\frac{N_L}{|\Lambda_L|},
  \qquad N_L=\langle Q\rangle
\]
when the finite states have a fixed charge sector or charge density.  A
rational filling will be written
\[
  \nu=\frac pq,\qquad (p,q)=1.
\]
The theorem is a statement about a sequence of finite tori and boundary twists;
the order of limits is part of the datum.  One first works at finite \(L\),
then takes a thermodynamic sequence with the filling and the transverse-volume
arithmetic specified.  A continuum effective action with no map from these
finite charge and translation operators is not enough data.

Insert \(2\pi\) units of background \(U(1)\) flux through the first cycle.  In a
lattice gauge where the flux is spread uniformly along that cycle, returning
the Hamiltonian to the original zero-flux gauge uses the large gauge
transformation
\begin{equation}
  G_1
  =
  \exp\!\left[
    -\frac{2\pi i}{L_1}
    \sum_{x\in\Lambda_L}x_1 q_x
  \right],
  \qquad x_1\in\{0,\ldots,L_1-1\}.
  \label{eq:lsmoh-large-gauge-transformation}
\end{equation}
For the translation convention \(T_1q_xT_1^{-1}=q_{x+\widehat 1}\), integer
charge eigenvalues give
\begin{equation}
  T_1G_1T_1^{-1}
  =
  \exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi iQ}{L_1}\right)G_1 .
  \label{eq:lsmoh-translation-large-gauge-commutator}
\end{equation}
Indeed, translating the coordinate \(x_1\) to \(x_1-1\) shifts
\(\sum x_1q_x\) by \(-Q\), while the boundary site contributes
\(\exp(-2\pi i q_{(0,x_\perp)})=1\).  Hence if an eigenstate transported around
the flux cycle has charge \(N_L\) and initial crystal momentum \(P_1\), the
large-gauge-returned state has
\begin{equation}
  \Delta P_1
  =
  \frac{2\pi N_L}{L_1}
  =
  2\pi\nu\,L_\perp
  \quad(\operatorname{mod}2\pi),
  \qquad
  L_\perp=\prod_{a=2}^d L_a .
  \label{eq:lsmoh-flux-momentum-shift}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:lsmoh-flux-momentum-shift} is exact finite-regulator
bookkeeping.  The theorem begins only when one asks whether the flux insertion
can be implemented inside a single uniformly gapped, symmetry-preserving,
short-range-entangled ground-state sector.

\begin{theorem}[LSMOH obstruction, theorem-boundary form]
\label{thm:lsmoh-obstruction-theorem-boundary}
Consider an exhausting sequence of finite \(d\)-dimensional tori with uniformly
finite-range interactions, bounded local Hilbert spaces, uniformly bounded
interaction norms and degrees, translation symmetry, and a conserved integer
\(U(1)\) charge.  Suppose the filling tends to \(p/q\notin\mathbb Z\).  Choose a
subsequence for which \(pL_\perp/q\notin\mathbb Z\).  Then the sequence cannot
have all of the following properties simultaneously:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item a unique symmetric ground sector: one translation- and
  \(U(1)\)-invariant ground state on the torus;
\item a uniform flux gap: a nonzero gap above that sector for all
  \(\phi\in[0,2\pi]\);
\item a short-range-entangled return: quasi-adiabatic flux insertion returns the
  same local phase sector.
\end{enumerate}
Thus the infrared must realize at least one of the following alternatives:
translation or internal-symmetry breaking, gapless states in the thermodynamic
limit, or intrinsic topological order/long-range entanglement whose sectors can
absorb the momentum shift.
\end{theorem}

The finite calculation above supplies the obstruction visible in
\eqref{eq:lsmoh-flux-momentum-shift}.  The nontrivial analytic input is the
controlled adiabatic statement.  In one dimension this is the
Lieb--Schultz--Mattis/Oshikawa low-energy-state argument.  In higher dimensions
the Hastings proof uses Lieb--Robinson bounds and quasi-adiabatic continuation:
the flux derivative is supported near the cut, the filter is fixed by the
assumed gap, and the tails are controlled by estimates of the kind displayed in
Section~\ref{sec:finite-regulator-locality-machinery-phase-stability}.  The
finite-size errors are not the slogan ``there is a finite-volume level
crossing.''  They are the quasi-local leakage and filter-tail errors, together
with the thermodynamic choice of transverse volumes.  This is why
Theorem~\ref{thm:lsmoh-obstruction-theorem-boundary} is stated as a
theorem-boundary result rather than as a proof extracted only from
\eqref{eq:lsmoh-translation-large-gauge-commutator}.

The spin formulation replaces fractional \(U(1)\) filling by a projective
representation per unit cell.  If each unit cell carries a nontrivial projective
class for an internal symmetry \(G_0\), for example the spin-\(1/2\) class of
\(SO(3)\), then translation ties that projective class to the whole infrared
Hilbert space.  A symmetric short-range-entangled state would trivialize the
projective class in every large cell, but a one-cell translation shifts the
cell decomposition by one nontrivial projective factor.  Equivalently, after
coupling to flat \(G_0\) backgrounds, translation and background-defect motion
act projectively on the twisted Hilbert spaces.  This is the mixed-anomaly
interpretation developed in modern treatments
\cite{MetlitskiThorngren2018Anomalies,ElseThorngren2020LSMTopological,
ChengSeiberg2023LSMLuttingerAnomaly}; it is a reformulation of the microscopic
constraint, while the finite-regulator proof remains the source of the
microscopic obstruction.

For a spin-\(1/2\) chain with \(U(1)\) spin rotations and zero magnetization,
set
\[
  n_x=S_x^z+\frac12 .
\]
Then \(n_x\) has eigenvalues \(0,1\), and the filling is
\[
  \nu=\frac{1}{L}\sum_x\langle n_x\rangle=\frac12 .
\]
Equation~\eqref{eq:lsmoh-flux-momentum-shift} gives
\[
  \Delta P=\pi\quad(\operatorname{mod}2\pi).
\]
A unique symmetric gapped short-range-entangled ground state would have to
return to itself after \(2\pi\) spin flux, but the returned state has opposite
crystal momentum.  The familiar physical resolutions fit the theorem precisely:
the Heisenberg spin-\(1/2\) chain is gapless; a dimerized chain is gapped but
breaks one-site translation; in higher dimensions a symmetric gapped spin
liquid may instead carry intrinsic topological order.

For a lattice boson or fermion system at filling \(p/q\), take a one-dimensional
sequence with \(L\) a multiple of \(q\) and \(N=pL/q\).  The same finite
calculation gives
\[
  \Delta P=\frac{2\pi p}{q}\quad(\operatorname{mod}2\pi).
\]
An ordinary band or Mott insulator with one symmetric short-range-entangled
ground state is therefore impossible at fractional filling under the stated
hypotheses.  A charge-density wave can evade the conclusion by enlarging the
unit cell by \(q\); a Luttinger liquid evades it by being gapless; a fractional
quantum Hall or other fractionalized phase evades it by having topological
sectors whose symmetry fractionalization carries the missing momentum.

The anomaly language is useful only after its input map is explicit.  The
finite operator identity
\[
  T_1G_1T_1^{-1}G_1^{-1}
  =
  \exp(2\pi i Q/L_1)
\]
is a projective commutation relation between translation and a large
background-gauge transformation.  In an infrared topological description it can
be encoded as an anomaly line or symmetry-TQFT datum involving translation
defects and \(U(1)\) background holonomy, in the sense of
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-anomaly-inflow-invertible-field-theories} and
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-higher-group-symmetry-tft}.  What is gained is a
matching principle: every candidate continuum or topological infrared theory
must reproduce the twisted-sector momentum and defect algebra.  What is not
proved is the existence of a continuum QFT, the size of the gap, or the
emergence of a particular topological order.  Those require the scaling maps of
Chapter~\ref{ch:constructive-lattice-continuum-local-qft}, the finite-cutoff
validation discipline of
Chapter~\ref{ch:efts-without-poincare-uv-completion}, and the topological-sector
embedding data described below in
Section~\ref{sec:finite-one-form-laboratory-z2-code}.

\section{Finite Flux-Torus Hall Response}
\label{sec:finite-flux-torus-hall-response}

Hall response in an interacting system is not a single-particle Chern number
unless the theory has first been reduced to a free one-particle problem.  The
finite many-body object is a family of Hamiltonians over a flux torus.  This
formulation is the appropriate finite-regulator laboratory for quantized
topological response, and it is also the place where the order of statements
must be kept straight: curvature over the finite flux torus is exact;
thermodynamic Hall conductance is obtained only after a separate locality,
gap, and limit argument.

Let \(\Lambda\) be a finite two-dimensional torus with a conserved
\(U(1)\) charge.  Choose two oriented cuts, one dual to each primitive cycle,
and introduce dimensionless fluxes
\[
  \phi=(\phi_x,\phi_y)\in\mathbb T^2=(\mathbb R/2\pi\mathbb Z)^2 .
\]
The finite flux Hamiltonian
\[
  H_\Lambda(\phi)
\]
is obtained by coupling charged terms crossing the \(a\)-cut to the flat
background holonomy \(e^{\ii \mathfrak q\phi_a}\), with \(\mathfrak q\) the
\(U(1)\) charge of the term crossing the cut.  Equivalently,
\(H_\Lambda(\phi)\) is a \(C^2\) family of self-adjoint matrices which is
periodic in \(\phi\), or periodic up to a large background-gauge
transformation.  In the latter case the formulas below are read as formulas
for the spectral vector bundle over \(\mathbb T^2\) with the corresponding
transition functions.  The flux-current operators in this normalization are
\[
  I_a(\phi)=\partial_{\phi_a}H_\Lambda(\phi),
  \qquad a=x,y .
\]
If a physical electric field is produced by a time-dependent flux, its sign
relative to \(I_a\) is fixed by the convention used for
\(\phi_a(t)\); the finite curvature formula below is independent of that
conversion convention.

\begin{definition}[Finite flux-torus response structure]
\label{def:finite-flux-torus-response-datum}
A finite flux-torus response structure consists of:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
  \text{finite Hilbert space} & \mathcal H_\Lambda,\\[1mm]
  \text{flux Hamiltonian} & H_\Lambda:\mathbb T^2\to
  \operatorname{End}(\mathcal H_\Lambda)_{\rm sa},\\[1mm]
  \text{isolated band} & P_\Lambda(\phi)\text{ of constant rank }r_\Lambda,\\[1mm]
  \text{uniform finite gap} &
  \operatorname{dist}\bigl(\operatorname{Spec}H_\Lambda|_{P_\Lambda},
  \operatorname{Spec}H_\Lambda|_{1-P_\Lambda}\bigr)\ge\gamma_\Lambda>0 .
\end{array}
\]
The band curvature is the two-form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal F_{xy,\Lambda}(\phi)\,
  \dd\phi_x\wedge\dd\phi_y
  =
  \ii\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_\Lambda}
  \bigl(P_\Lambda[\partial_{\phi_x}P_\Lambda,
  \partial_{\phi_y}P_\Lambda]\bigr)
  \dd\phi_x\wedge\dd\phi_y .
  \label{eq:finite-flux-curvature}
\end{equation}
The flux-averaged dimensionless Hall coefficient of this finite structure is
\begin{equation}
  \overline\kappa_{xy,\Lambda}
  =
  \frac{1}{r_\Lambda(2\pi)^2}
  \int_{\mathbb T^2}\mathcal F_{xy,\Lambda}(\phi)\,
  \dd\phi_x\,\dd\phi_y .
  \label{eq:finite-flux-averaged-hall-coefficient}
\end{equation}
In units \(e=\hbar=1\), the corresponding physical Hall conductivity uses
the same number when the flux/electric-field convention is the one stated
above; restoring units multiplies by \(e^2/\hbar\) and the conventional
\(2\pi\) normalization is already contained in
\eqref{eq:finite-flux-averaged-hall-coefficient}.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Finite Hall curvature theorem]
\label{thm:finite-hall-curvature-theorem}
Let \(H_\Lambda(\phi)\) and \(P_\Lambda(\phi)\) be a finite flux-torus
response structure.  Then
\begin{equation}
  C_\Lambda
  =
  \frac{1}{2\pi}
  \int_{\mathbb T^2}\mathcal F_{xy,\Lambda}(\phi)\,
  \dd\phi_x\,\dd\phi_y
  \label{eq:finite-many-body-chern-number}
\end{equation}
is an integer depending only on the spectral vector bundle
\(\operatorname{Ran}P_\Lambda\to\mathbb T^2\).  It is unchanged under every
\(C^1\) deformation of the finite structure for which the rank of
\(P_\Lambda\) and the spectral gap remain fixed.

If, in addition, the isolated band is an exact \(r_\Lambda\)-fold degenerate
eigenvalue,
\[
  H_\Lambda(\phi)P_\Lambda(\phi)=E_0(\phi)P_\Lambda(\phi),
\]
and
\[
  Q_\Lambda=1-P_\Lambda,\qquad
  R_\Lambda(\phi)=
  Q_\Lambda(\phi)\bigl(H_\Lambda(\phi)-E_0(\phi)\bigr)^{-1}Q_\Lambda(\phi),
\]
then the same curvature has the finite Kubo form
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal F_{xy,\Lambda}
  =
  \ii\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(
    P_\Lambda I_x R_\Lambda^2 I_y P_\Lambda
    -
    P_\Lambda I_y R_\Lambda^2 I_x P_\Lambda
  \right).
  \label{eq:finite-hall-kubo-curvature}
\end{equation}
Thus
\[
  \overline\kappa_{xy,\Lambda}=\frac{C_\Lambda}{2\pi r_\Lambda}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The integrality statement is the Chern-Weil formula for the determinant line
of the finite-rank bundle \(\operatorname{Ran}P_\Lambda\), and in this finite
setting the proof can be written explicitly.  On a coordinate patch
\(U_\alpha\subset\mathbb T^2\), choose a smooth orthonormal frame
\[
  \Psi_\alpha=(\psi_{\alpha,1},\ldots,\psi_{\alpha,r_\Lambda})
\]
for \(\operatorname{Ran}P_\Lambda\).  The trace Berry connection in this
frame is
\[
  A_\alpha
  =
  \ii\operatorname{Tr}\bigl(\Psi_\alpha^*\dd\Psi_\alpha\bigr).
\]
Using \(P_\Lambda=\Psi_\alpha\Psi_\alpha^*\) and
\(\Psi_\alpha^*\Psi_\alpha=1_q\), one obtains
\[
  \dd A_\alpha
  =
  \ii\operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(P_\Lambda\,\dd P_\Lambda\wedge\dd P_\Lambda\bigr),
\]
whose \(xy\)-component is
\eqref{eq:finite-flux-curvature}.  On an overlap
\(U_\alpha\cap U_\beta\), the two frames obey
\[
  \Psi_\beta=\Psi_\alpha g_{\alpha\beta},
  \qquad g_{\alpha\beta}:U_\alpha\cap U_\beta\to U(r_\Lambda).
\]
Therefore
\[
  A_\beta-A_\alpha
  =
  \ii\operatorname{Tr}(g_{\alpha\beta}^{-1}\dd g_{\alpha\beta})
  =
  \ii\,\dd\log\det g_{\alpha\beta}.
\]
Integrating \(\dd A_\alpha\) over a fundamental polygon for the torus and
using Stokes' theorem reduces the integral to the winding of the transition
function \(\det g_{\alpha\beta}:S^1\to U(1)\).  Hence
\[
  \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\mathbb T^2}
  \ii\operatorname{Tr}(P_\Lambda\,\dd P_\Lambda\wedge\dd P_\Lambda)
  \in\mathbb Z .
\]
The same argument applies when the flux family is only covariantly periodic:
the large-gauge transition unitaries are precisely the transition functions
of the bundle.  Constancy under a gapped deformation follows because the
integer \(C_\Lambda(s)\) depends continuously on \(s\) while the projection
bundle is defined throughout the deformation.

It remains to prove the Kubo expression.  Differentiate
\[
  H_\Lambda P_\Lambda=E_0P_\Lambda
\]
and multiply by \(Q_\Lambda\) on the left and \(P_\Lambda\) on the right.
Since \(Q_\Lambda P_\Lambda=0\), this gives
\[
  Q_\Lambda(\partial_aP_\Lambda)P_\Lambda
  =
  -R_\Lambda Q_\Lambda I_aP_\Lambda,
  \qquad a=x,y.
\]
Taking adjoints gives
\[
  P_\Lambda(\partial_aP_\Lambda)Q_\Lambda
  =
  -P_\Lambda I_aQ_\Lambda R_\Lambda .
\]
The diagonal identities \(P_\Lambda(\partial_aP_\Lambda)P_\Lambda=0\),
obtained from \(P_\Lambda^2=P_\Lambda\), imply
\[
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(P_\Lambda[\partial_xP_\Lambda,\partial_yP_\Lambda]\bigr)
  =
  \operatorname{Tr}
  \bigl(P_\Lambda(\partial_xP_\Lambda)Q_\Lambda(\partial_yP_\Lambda)P_\Lambda
  -
  P_\Lambda(\partial_yP_\Lambda)Q_\Lambda(\partial_xP_\Lambda)P_\Lambda
  \bigr).
\]
Substituting the two off-diagonal formulas above gives
\eqref{eq:finite-hall-kubo-curvature}.  The final displayed equality follows
by inserting \eqref{eq:finite-many-body-chern-number} into
\eqref{eq:finite-flux-averaged-hall-coefficient}.
\end{proof}

For a finite interacting system the coefficient at a fixed flux value need
not be quantized.  The quantized object in
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-hall-curvature-theorem} is the flux average of the
band curvature.  A thermodynamic Hall theorem requires additional hypotheses:
the flux family must be local in the sense that \(I_a=\partial_{\phi_a}H\)
is supported near the corresponding cut up to uniformly local tails; the gap
above the relevant band must remain uniform over \(\phi\) and over the
volume sequence; the quasi-adiabatic flow from
Section~\ref{sec:finite-regulator-locality-machinery-phase-stability} must
control the change of local current response under moving the cuts; and the
thermodynamic limit of the response must exist.  Under such hypotheses the
fixed-flux response becomes independent of \(\phi\) up to errors vanishing in
the thermodynamic limit, and the finite integer \(C_\Lambda\) becomes the
phase-stable Hall datum.  Without these hypotheses, writing a continuum
integer Hall conductance directly from a finite Kubo formula hides the
substance of the theorem.

\section{Gauge Invariance And Elitzur Averaging}
\label{sec:gauge-invariance-elitzur-averaging}

Gauge-variant local fields cannot be order parameters for a gauge theory
unless a gauge has been fixed and the residual meaning of the statement has
been specified.  The finite-regulator reason is elementary.

\paragraph{Finite-volume gauge averaging.}
Let a finite lattice gauge theory have compact gauge group \(G\), gauge
invariant action, and gauge invariant measure.  Let \(O\) be an observable
which transforms nontrivially under the gauge transformation at one site
\(x\).  Then
\[
  \langle O\rangle_\Lambda=0
\]
in finite volume with gauge-invariant boundary conditions.

Average over gauge transformations supported at \(x\).  Gauge invariance of
the measure and action gives
\[
  \langle O\rangle_\Lambda
  =
  \int_G dh\,\langle h\cdot O\rangle_\Lambda .
\]
If \(O\) transforms in a representation \(\rho\) with no invariant vector in
the component being averaged, Haar orthogonality gives
\[
  \int_G dh\,\rho(h)=0
\]
on that component.  Therefore the group average of \(O\) is zero, and so is
its expectation value.  For a finite collection of gauge-variant insertions,
perform the Haar average independently at each site touched by the product.
The integral at a given site projects the tensor product of the local
representation factors onto its invariant subspace.  The expectation can
survive only if this projection is nonzero at every site.
The load-bearing input here is the finite-regulator Haar projection with
gauge-invariant boundary conditions; the calculation is the local averaging
step used in Elitzur-type statements.  Continuum phase statements require the
separate limiting data specified in
Definition~\ref{def:declared-phase-problem}.

Consequently a Higgs regime must be described by gauge-invariant data:
screening of external charges, massive vector states in gauge-invariant
channels, boundary-condition response, or nonlocal diagnostics.  A gauge-fixed
scalar expectation value may be a useful coordinate in a weakly coupled chart,
but it is not the invariant phase datum.

\section{Static Potentials From Transfer Matrices}

Let \(W_R(C)\) be a renormalized Wilson loop in representation \(R\).  For a
rectangle \(C_{T,L}\) with temporal length \(T\) and spatial separation \(L\),
define the static potential by
\begin{equation}
  V_R(L)=
  -\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\log
  \left\langle W_R(C_{T,L})\right\rangle_{\mathrm{ren}},
  \label{eq:static-potential-definition}
\end{equation}
when the limit exists.  The subscript indicates that the line self-energy has
been subtracted or divided out in a specified line-renormalization scheme.

Reflection positivity gives a Hilbert-space interpretation.  In a finite
spatial box, a pair of static external sources separated by \(L\) defines a
sector Hilbert space \(\mathcal H_{R,\bar R;L}\) or, equivalently, a state
\(\Psi_{R,L}\) in the transfer-matrix construction with a Wilson transporter
between the two sources.  If \(H_{R,L}\) is the Hamiltonian in this sector and
\(E_0\) is the vacuum energy, then
\[
  \left\langle W_R(C_{T,L})\right\rangle_{\mathrm{ren}}
  =
  \langle\Psi_{R,L},
  e^{-T(H_{R,L}-E_0)}
  \Psi_{R,L}\rangle .
\]
By the spectral theorem this has the form
\[
  \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-TE}\,d\rho_{R,L}(E)
\]
for a positive finite spectral measure \(\rho_{R,L}\).

\paragraph{Large-time spectral extraction.}
The static-potential limit uses the elementary Laplace principle for a
positive spectral measure.  Let \(\rho\) be a nonzero finite positive measure
on \([E_\ast,\infty)\), and assume every interval
\([E_\ast,E_\ast+\varepsilon]\) has positive \(\rho\)-measure.  Then
\[
  -\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac1T
  \log\int e^{-TE}\,d\rho(E)
  =
  E_\ast .
\]
Since \(E\geq E_\ast\),
\[
  \int e^{-TE}\,d\rho(E)
  \leq
  \rho([E_\ast,\infty))e^{-TE_\ast}.
\]
Thus the liminf of the left side is at least \(E_\ast\).  For every
\(\varepsilon>0\),
\[
  \int e^{-TE}\,d\rho(E)
  \geq
  \rho([E_\ast,E_\ast+\varepsilon])e^{-T(E_\ast+\varepsilon)}.
\]
The measure factor is positive and independent of \(T\), so the limsup is at
most \(E_\ast+\varepsilon\).  Letting \(\varepsilon\to0\) proves the claim.
This calculation supplies only the last step after a reflection-positive
transfer-matrix sector and a renormalized Wilson-loop spectral measure have
already been constructed.

Thus \(V_R(L)\) is the bottom of the static-source spectrum in the chosen
sector.  Its large-\(L\) behavior gives several distinct responses:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
  \text{confining} &
  V_R(L)=\sigma_R L+o(L),\quad \sigma_R>0,\\[1mm]
  \text{screened} &
  V_R(L)\text{ is bounded as }L\to\infty,\\[1mm]
  \text{Coulombic in four spacetime dimensions} &
  V_R(L)=V_\infty-\kappa_R/L+o(L^{-1}).
\end{array}
\]
These are responses of the chosen external probe.  A theory with dynamical
fundamental matter can screen the fundamental Wilson charge even when other
probes or boundary conditions still diagnose nontrivial long-distance
structure.

\section{Loop Laws And Higher-Form Symmetry}

For a closed contour \(C\), let \(L_\gamma(C)\) be a renormalized line
operator with finite charge \(\gamma\).  For a sequence of large planar loops,
an area law means
\[
  -\log\langle L_\gamma(C)\rangle
  =
  \sigma_\gamma\,\operatorname{Area}_{\min}(C)
  +o(\operatorname{Area}_{\min}(C)),
  \qquad \sigma_\gamma>0,
\]
after perimeter renormalization.  A perimeter law means
\[
  -\log\langle L_\gamma(C)\rangle
  =
  \mu_\gamma\,\operatorname{Length}(C)
  +o(\operatorname{Length}(C)).
\]
In a gapless Coulomb regime, logarithmic and power corrections can replace
both pure laws; the phase datum is then the full asymptotic class, not a
binary area/perimeter label.

Let \(A\) be a finite one-form symmetry and let
\[
  U_a(\Sigma),\qquad a\in A,
\]
be its topological symmetry surface on a closed codimension-two surface
\(\Sigma\).  If a line \(L_\chi(C)\) carries character
\[
  \chi\in \widehat A=\operatorname{Hom}(A,U(1)),
\]
then linking gives
\[
  U_a(\Sigma)L_\chi(C)
  =
  \chi(a)^{\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma,C)}
  L_\chi(C).
\]
In a gapped phase with a homogeneous vacuum and no boundary contribution, the
unbroken realization of a finite electric one-form symmetry is diagnosed by
area laws for charged Wilson lines.  A perimeter law for a charged line is
the characteristic response of a broken one-form symmetry or a deconfined
topological sector.  This statement requires the line to be genuine and
charged under an actual one-form symmetry; if dynamical matter lets the line
end, the one-form symmetry is explicitly absent or reduced.

\section{Screening, Condensation, And Oblique Confinement}

Let \(\mathcal C\) be the finite abelian group of center-sensitive dyonic line
charges, with nondegenerate alternating pairing
\[
  B:\mathcal C\times\mathcal C\longrightarrow\mathbb Q/\mathbb Z.
\]
For \(\mathfrak{su}(N)\) this is
\[
  \mathcal C=\mathbb Z_N^{\mathrm e}\oplus\mathbb Z_N^{\mathrm m},
  \qquad
  B((e,m),(e',m'))=\frac{em'-e'm}{N}.
\]
Let \(S\subset\mathcal C\) be the subgroup screened by dynamical finite-energy
excitations: a line whose charge lies in \(S\) can end on a dynamical
operator or state.  The asymptotic external-probe labels are then identified
modulo screened charges,
\[
  \mathcal C_{\mathrm{ext}}=\mathcal C/S
\]
for questions about static sources.  The finite braiding form does not, in
general, descend to all of this quotient.  If the screened subgroup is
isotropic, then the genuine residual finite topological charges are
\[
  \mathcal C_{\mathrm{top}}=S^\perp/S,\qquad
  S^\perp=\{\gamma\in\mathcal C\mid B(\gamma,s)=0
  \text{ for every }s\in S\},
\]
and the pairing descends to \(\mathcal C_{\mathrm{top}}\) because changing a
representative by \(s\in S\) shifts \(B(\gamma,\gamma')\) by
\(B(s,\gamma')=0\) whenever \(\gamma'\in S^\perp\).

A condensate of line or particle charges is encoded, at the finite
center-sensitive level, by an isotropic subgroup
\[
  K\subset\mathcal C,\qquad B|_{K\times K}=0.
\]
The isotropy condition is mutual locality of condensed charges.  The charges
with trivial finite braiding against the condensate form
\[
  K^\perp=\{\gamma\in\mathcal C\mid B(\gamma,k)=0
  \text{ for every }k\in K\}.
\]

Assume a gapped regime in which the long-distance condensate is described by
an isotropic finite subgroup \(K\subset\mathcal C\), and assume a line of
charge \(\gamma\) has perimeter behavior only if it has trivial braiding with
every condensed charge.  Then the unconfined finite charges lie in
\[
  K^\perp,
\]
and every \(\gamma\notin K^\perp\) is confined in this finite-charge
diagnostic.  If \(K\) is maximal isotropic, then \(K^\perp=K\).
Moving a test line of charge \(\gamma\) around a condensed object of charge
\(k\) multiplies the state by
\[
  \exp(2\pi\ii B(\gamma,k)).
\]
A perimeter-law line in a condensate background must have a nonzero overlap
with the condensate sector without introducing a branch-dependent phase.  By
the hypothesis this requires the displayed phase to be one for every
\(k\in K\), which is exactly \(\gamma\in K^\perp\).  If
\(K\subsetneq K^\perp\), an element \(\eta\in K^\perp\setminus K\) would
generate with \(K\) a strictly larger isotropic subgroup, because
\(B(\eta,K)=0\) and \(B(\eta,\eta)=0\).  Hence a maximal isotropic subgroup
obeys \(K=K^\perp\).

Electric confinement corresponds to magnetic condensation: purely electric
charges pair nontrivially with the magnetic condensate and acquire area law.
A Higgs response from electric condensation screens electric probes and
confines appropriate magnetic probes.  Oblique confinement is the same
criterion with a dyonic condensate
\[
  K=\langle(e_c,m_c)\rangle
\]
whose generator has both electric and magnetic components.  The word
``oblique'' is therefore not a new mechanism; it is the finite Dirac pairing
applied to a dyonic condensate.

\section{Gauge--Higgs Analytic Connectivity}
\label{sec:gauge-higgs-analytic-connectivity}

Gauge--Higgs complementarity is a theorem-boundary statement, rather than a
consequence of finite-volume analyticity alone.  The finite partition function
of a compact lattice model is an entire function of the bare couplings, and a
finite-volume local expectation is analytic at real positive couplings because
the partition function is strictly positive there.  Lee--Yang zeros can still
accumulate as the volume grows.  The real content of the
Fradkin--Shenker/Osterwalder--Seiler theorem is a uniform thermodynamic
cluster expansion in a connected region that reaches both a confinement-like
corner and a Higgs-like corner for the declared local gauge-invariant
observable algebra \cite{OsterwalderSeiler1978GaugeLattice,
FradkinShenker1979GaugeHiggs}.

\begin{definition}[Fixed-length \(\mathbb Z_2\) gauge--Higgs datum]
\label{def:z2-gauge-higgs-fs-datum}
Let \(\Lambda\subset\mathbb Z^d\), \(d\geq2\), be a finite box or torus.  Put
link variables \(\sigma_e\in\{\pm1\}\) on oriented nearest-neighbor edges and
fixed-length Higgs variables \(\varphi_x\in\{\pm1\}\) on sites.  For an
oriented plaquette \(p\) and edge \(e=(xy)\), define
\[
  B_p=\prod_{e\subset\partial p}\sigma_e,\qquad
  H_e=\varphi_x\sigma_e\varphi_y .
\]
The finite-volume partition function is
\begin{equation}
  Z_\Lambda(\beta,\kappa)
  =
  2^{-|V|-|E|}
  \sum_{\varphi,\sigma}
  \exp\!\left[
    \beta\sum_{p} B_p+\kappa\sum_e H_e
  \right],
  \qquad \beta,\kappa\geq0 .
  \label{eq:z2-gauge-higgs-fixed-length-partition}
\end{equation}
The gauge transformation
\[
  \varphi_x\longmapsto \eta_x\varphi_x,\qquad
  \sigma_{xy}\longmapsto \eta_x\sigma_{xy}\eta_y,\qquad
  \eta_x\in\{\pm1\},
\]
leaves \(B_p\) and \(H_e\) invariant.  The local observable algebra
\(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}^{\mathrm{inv}}\) is generated by finite products
of plaquettes and by finite open-line composites
\[
  M_\gamma
  =
  \varphi_x
  \left(\prod_{e\in\gamma}\sigma_e\right)
  \varphi_y ,
  \qquad \partial\gamma=y-x,
\]
together with finite products and translates of these observables.
\end{definition}

This is the smallest completely explicit laboratory for the issue.  The gauge
group is the compact group \(\mathbb Z_2\), the Higgs field has charge one,
and the radial mode is absent.  The \(SU(2)\) fixed-length fundamental Higgs
model is the Lie-group analogue: \(U_e\in SU(2)\), a unit doublet or
group-valued fixed-length Higgs field on sites, a plaquette action
\(\beta\sum_p \operatorname{Re}\operatorname{tr}U_p\), and a hopping action
\(\kappa\sum_e\operatorname{Re}(\varphi_x^\dagger U_e\varphi_y)\).  The
\(\mathbb Z_2\) model keeps the same logical distinction between local
gauge-invariant analyticity and extended-probe phase data, while making the
finite algebra transparent.

\begin{quotedtheorem}[Fradkin--Shenker/Osterwalder--Seiler corridor]
\label{qthm:fradkin-shenker-os-corridor}
For the fixed-length gauge--Higgs models above, with matter in a representation
that screens the fundamental center charge, there is a connected open domain
\(\mathcal C_{\mathrm{FS}}\) in the nonnegative coupling plane, with a complex
neighborhood for the expansion variables, such that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item \(\mathcal C_{\mathrm{FS}}\) contains a strong-gauge/strong-matter corner
  and a Higgs corner reached at large hopping coupling;
\item for every \(O_1,\ldots,O_n\in\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}^{\mathrm{inv}}\),
  the infinite-volume connected correlator
  \[
    \lim_{\Lambda\nearrow\mathbb Z^d}
    \langle O_1\cdots O_n\rangle_{\Lambda,c}
  \]
  exists uniformly on compact subsets of \(\mathcal C_{\mathrm{FS}}\), is
  analytic there, and has exponential clustering with constants uniform on
  compact subsets;
\item along paths inside \(\mathcal C_{\mathrm{FS}}\), local gauge-invariant
  thermodynamic functions have absent phase singularities.
\end{enumerate}
The corridor and the uniform estimates are the hard theorem input of
Osterwalder--Seiler and Fradkin--Shenker, rather than a consequence of
finite-volume analyticity.
\end{quotedtheorem}

The polymer coordinates identify what the theorem proves.  For
Definition~\ref{def:z2-gauge-higgs-fs-datum}, write
\[
  u=\tanh\beta,\qquad v=\tanh\kappa,\qquad r=e^{-2\kappa}.
\]
The high-temperature identity
\[
  e^{\beta B_p}=\cosh\beta\,(1+uB_p),\qquad
  e^{\kappa H_e}=\cosh\kappa\,(1+vH_e)
\]
turns \(Z_\Lambda\), up to the harmless product of \(\cosh\)-factors, into a
finite sum over plaquette subsets and edge subsets:
\[
  \sum_{P\subset\mathrm{Plaquettes}}\sum_{\Gamma\subset E}
  u^{|P|}v^{|\Gamma|}
  \sum_{\varphi,\sigma}
  \prod_{p\in P}B_p\prod_{e\in\Gamma}H_e .
\]
The spin sum kills every monomial in which any site variable or link variable
appears with odd parity.  Surviving monomials are surfaces whose boundaries
are matter lines.  Connected clusters carry activities \(\zeta(X)\), and a
standard Koteck\'y--Preiss type norm is
\begin{equation}
  \|\zeta\|_a
  =
  \sup_b
  \sum_{X\ni b} |\zeta(X)|e^{a|X|},
  \label{eq:fradkin-shenker-polymer-norm}
\end{equation}
where \(b\) ranges over sites, links, and plaquettes.  The strong-coupling
cluster expansion is controlled by a bound of the form
\[
  \|\zeta_{\mathrm{sc}}\|_a
  \leq C_{d,a}(|u|+|v|).
\]
This gives a uniform local expansion near \((\beta,\kappa)=(0,0)\).

The Higgs-side expansion uses unitary gauge, \(\varphi_x=1\), as a calculation
coordinate on gauge-invariant observables.  Defect links with
\(\sigma_e=-1\) have activity \(r=e^{-2\kappa}\), while plaquettes are still
organized by \(u\).  Its polymer norm has the theorem-boundary bound
\[
  \|\zeta_{\mathrm{H}}\|_a
  \leq C'_{d,a}(|u|+r).
\]
The bridge expansion is the crucial corridor mechanism: at \(\beta=0\) the
unitary-gauge link variables are independent for every \(\kappa\), and small
\(|u|\) plaquette polymers perturb this product measure with a bound
\[
  \|\zeta_{\mathrm{br}}\|_a
  \leq C''_{d,a}|u|,
\]
uniformly in \(\kappa\).  Thus the imported theorem supplies constants for
open domains of the form
\[
  \mathcal D_{\mathrm{sc}}:\ C_{d,a}(|u|+|v|)<1,\qquad
  \mathcal D_{\mathrm{br}}:\ C''_{d,a}|u|<1,\qquad
  \mathcal D_{\mathrm{H}}:\ C'_{d,a}(|u|+r)<1,
\]
whose union contains a connected path from the confinement-like corner to the
Higgs-like corner.  The displayed inequalities are the mechanism map; the
external theorem supplies the actual constants, analyticity neighborhoods, and
volume-uniform estimates.

This theorem is intentionally about a declared observable system.  Charge-one
matter explicitly screens the fundamental Wilson charge: the open composite
\(M_\gamma\) is gauge invariant, so a Wilson line can end on dynamical matter.
Equivalently, the center one-form diagnostic that separates pure
\(\mathbb Z_2\) gauge theory or pure \(SU(N)\) Yang--Mills is explicitly
removed by fundamental matter.  Adjoint or center-neutral matter changes the
problem.  A fundamental Wilson line then carries an exact center charge, the
corresponding one-form symmetry can survive, and a genuine deconfined,
confining, or topologically ordered distinction may remain visible to
extended operators even when some local correlators are analytic.

The theorem also keeps Elitzur averaging and heavy-probe diagnostics in their
proper places.  Unitary gauge is a coordinate for the expansion, while
Section~\ref{sec:gauge-invariance-elitzur-averaging} supplies the finite Haar
projection explaining why a gauge-variant Higgs expectation is absent from the
gauge-invariant finite-volume theory.  The local analytic corridor concerns
correlators in \(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}^{\mathrm{inv}}\).  It leaves
Wilson- and 't Hooft-line asymptotics, Fredenhagen--Marcu ratios,
boundary-condition sectors, topological Hilbert spaces, unscreened heavy
probes, and continuum scaling limits as separate declared data
\cite{FredenhagenMarcu1983ChargedZ2,CaudyGreensite2008GaugeAmbiguity}.
Likewise, the exponential clustering supplied by the Euclidean polymer theorem
is a local statistical estimate; a Hamiltonian mass gap or continuum particle
spectrum requires the additional transfer-matrix and scaling-limit data named
in Definition~\ref{def:declared-phase-problem}.  Consequently the theorem
gives local lattice gauge--Higgs complementarity for the specified model and
corridor, while continuum Yang--Mills phase equivalence would require a
separate scaling limit datum.

\section{Fredenhagen--Marcu Type Ratios}

When dynamical matter screens a Wilson charge, the Wilson loop itself need
not distinguish a confinement-like region from a Higgs-like region.  A more
refined gauge-invariant probe uses an open transporter with charged matter
fields at its endpoints.  Let \(\phi\) be a matter field in representation
\(R\), let \(\gamma_{xy}\) be a path from \(x\) to \(y\), and define the
gauge-invariant open-line correlator
\[
  G_R(\gamma_{xy})
  =
  \left\langle
    \phi^\dagger(x)\,
    U_R(\gamma_{xy})\,
    \phi(y)
  \right\rangle .
\]
Let \(C_{\gamma}\) be a closed contour built from \(\gamma_{xy}\) and a
reference return path of comparable size.  A Fredenhagen--Marcu type ratio is
\[
  \rho_R(\gamma)
  =
  \frac{G_R(\gamma)}
  {\sqrt{\langle W_R(C_\gamma)\rangle_{\mathrm{ren}}}} ,
\]
with the regulator, endpoint renormalization, and order of limits specified.
The denominator removes the bulk string contribution associated with the
closed flux sheet.  The resulting ratio asks whether the open charged
configuration has nonzero overlap with the vacuum or with a charged
superselection sector after the universal flux cost has been divided out.

\paragraph{Exponential scaling of the ratio.}
Suppose a family of paths has size \(R\), and suppose
\[
  -\log |G_R(\gamma_R)|=aR+o(R),
  \qquad
  -\log \langle W_R(C_{\gamma_R})\rangle_{\mathrm{ren}}=bR+o(R)
\]
with \(b\geq0\).  Then
\[
  -\log |\rho_R(\gamma_R)|
  =
  \left(a-\frac b2\right)R+o(R).
\]
In particular the ratio has a nonzero exponential limit only when the
endpoint-open-line decay matches one half of the closed-loop decay at leading
order.
This is the logarithm of the definition:
\[
  -\log|\rho_R|
  =
  -\log|G_R|+\frac12\log\langle W_R\rangle_{\mathrm{ren}}.
\]
Substituting the two assumed asymptotic formulas gives
\[
  aR+o(R)-\frac b2R+o(R),
\]
which is the displayed expression.

The ratio is therefore not a slogan for ``Higgs'' or ``confinement'' without
the accompanying limit definition.  Its interpretation depends on the matter
representation, the boundary conditions, the renormalization of the endpoints,
and the sector whose overlap is being measured.  Its virtue is that all of
these choices are gauge-invariant and can be stated at finite regulator.

\section{Topological Order And Deconfined Gauge Theory}

A gapped gauge theory can have a finite-dimensional Hilbert space on spatial
manifolds with nontrivial topology, nontrivial line-operator braiding, and
surface operators whose correlation functions depend on homology classes.
These data form the long-distance topological sector.  In continuum language
they are encoded by an infrared TQFT together with a map
\[
  \Phi_{\mathrm{IR}}:
  \begin{array}{c}
  \{\text{microscopic topological}\\
  \text{extended operators}\}
  \end{array}
  \longrightarrow
  \begin{array}{c}
  \{\text{topological operators}\\
  \text{of the IR TQFT}\}
  \end{array}.
\]
The map is part of the phase definition.  Two microscopic theories may have
the same emergent TQFT but differ in massive local spectra, symmetry
fractionalization, anomaly inflow, or the embedding of microscopic line
operators into the topological category.

\section{Finite One-Form Laboratory: The \texorpdfstring{\(\mathbb Z_2\)}{Z2} Gauge Code}
\label{sec:finite-one-form-laboratory-z2-code}

The preceding paragraph is abstract because a continuum gapped phase rarely
comes with an exactly known Hilbert space on every spatial manifold.  A finite
commuting-projector gauge code supplies a useful laboratory, provided its
logical status is kept clear.  It is not a definition of continuum QFT and it
does not replace the infinite-volume phase problem.  It gives an exact
operator algebra in which one-form charge, topological line operators, local
indistinguishability, and energy-barrier limitations can be checked without
asymptotic or semiclassical language.

Let \(\Lambda\) be an \(L_x\times L_y\) square cellulation of the spatial
torus, with \(L_x,L_y\geq 3\).  Let \(V,E,P\) be its vertices, edges, and
plaquettes, and put a two-dimensional Hilbert space
\[
  \mathcal H_\Lambda=\bigotimes_{e\in E}\mathbb C^2_e
\]
on the edges.  Write \(X_e,Z_e\) for Pauli operators on the edge \(e\).  The
star and plaquette stabilizers are
\begin{equation}
  A_v=\prod_{e\ni v}X_e,\qquad
  B_p=\prod_{e\subset\partial p}Z_e .
  \label{eq:z2-code-star-plaquette-stabilizers}
\end{equation}
The orientation of \(\partial p\) is irrelevant because all coefficients are
in \(\mathbb Z_2\).  Each \(A_v\) and \(B_p\) squares to one, and all
stabilizers commute: a star and a plaquette share either no edge or two edges,
so the two \(XZ=-ZX\) signs cancel.  The Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
  H_\Lambda=-J\sum_{v\in V}A_v-J\sum_{p\in P}B_p,\qquad J>0,
  \label{eq:z2-code-hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
has ground projector
\begin{equation}
  P_0=
  \prod_{v\in V}\frac{1+A_v}{2}
  \prod_{p\in P}\frac{1+B_p}{2}.
  \label{eq:z2-code-ground-projector}
\end{equation}
The two products of all stars and all plaquettes are identities,
\[
  \prod_{v\in V}A_v=1,\qquad \prod_{p\in P}B_p=1,
\]
and these are the only stabilizer relations on the connected torus.  Hence
the number of independent stabilizer constraints is
\[
  (|V|-1)+(|P|-1).
\]
Since \(|E|-|V|-|P|=0\) for the square torus, the ground space has dimension
\[
  2^{|E|-(|V|-1)-(|P|-1)}=4.
\]
Equivalently, the two encoded qubits are the two \(\mathbb Z_2\)-homology
cycles of the torus.

It is useful to express the logical algebra in chain-complex notation.  For a
\(\mathbb Z_2\) edge chain \(c\in C_1(\Lambda;\mathbb Z_2)\) and an edge
cochain \(\eta\in C^1(\Lambda;\mathbb Z_2)\), define
\begin{equation}
  Z(c)=\prod_{e:c_e=1}Z_e,\qquad
  X(\eta)=\prod_{e:\eta(e)=1}X_e .
  \label{eq:z2-code-chain-operators}
\end{equation}
Their commutation relation is the finite intersection pairing
\begin{equation}
  X(\eta)Z(c)
  =
  (-1)^{\langle \eta,c\rangle}Z(c)X(\eta),
  \qquad
  \langle \eta,c\rangle=\sum_{e\in E}\eta(e)c_e\pmod 2 .
  \label{eq:z2-code-intersection-sign}
\end{equation}
The operator \(Z(c)\) commutes with every star iff \(\partial c=0\).  If
\(c=\partial s\) is the boundary of a plaquette two-chain \(s\), then
\[
  Z(c)=\prod_{p:s_p=1}B_p
\]
and \(Z(c)\) acts as the identity on \(P_0\mathcal H_\Lambda\).  Thus
nontrivial \(Z\)-type logical lines are represented by
\[
  [c]\in H_1(T^2;\mathbb Z_2).
\]
Dually, \(X(\eta)\) commutes with every plaquette iff \(\delta\eta=0\); if
\(\eta=\delta f\) is exact, then \(X(\eta)\) is a product of star
stabilizers and is trivial on the ground space.  The nontrivial \(X\)-type
logical lines are represented by
\[
  [\eta]\in H^1(T^2;\mathbb Z_2).
\]
Equation~\eqref{eq:z2-code-intersection-sign} is then the exact finite
version of the one-form linking phase: a primal \(Z\)-loop and a dual
\(X\)-loop anticommute precisely when their mod-two intersection number is
one.

\paragraph{Local indistinguishability.}
Let \(R\subset E\) be an edge set contained in a contractible disk of the
cellulation, and let \(O_R\) be any operator supported on
\(\bigotimes_{e\in R}\mathbb C^2_e\).  Expanding \(O_R\) in the Pauli basis,
it is enough to consider a monomial
\[
  W=i^\ell X(\eta)Z(c),\qquad \operatorname{supp}(\eta,c)\subset R.
\]
If \(\partial c\neq0\), then \(Z(c)\) anticommutes with at least one star
stabilizer; if \(\delta\eta\neq0\), then \(X(\eta)\) anticommutes with at
least one plaquette stabilizer.  In either case
\[
  P_0 W P_0=0,
\]
because the anticommuting stabilizer \(S\) obeys
\[
  P_0WP_0=P_0SWP_0=-P_0WSP_0=-P_0WP_0 .
\]
If \(\partial c=0\) and \(\delta\eta=0\), contractibility of \(R\) implies
that \(c\) is a plaquette boundary and \(\eta\) is a star coboundary inside
the same disk.  Therefore \(W\) is a phase times a product of stabilizers, and
\[
  P_0 W P_0=\lambda(W)P_0,\qquad \lambda(W)\in\{0,\pm1,\pm\ii\}.
\]
By linearity,
\begin{equation}
  P_0 O_R P_0=c(O_R)P_0
  \label{eq:z2-code-local-indistinguishability}
\end{equation}
for every operator supported in a contractible region \(R\).  This is the
finite local-indistinguishability mechanism.  It is stronger than saying that
particular expectation values agree: the full local operator algebra acts
projectively as scalars after projection to the ground sector.  It is also
weaker than a continuum phase theorem: stability under perturbations and
passage to an infinite-volume gapped phase require separate locality and gap
input, such as Lieb--Robinson and quasi-adiabatic-continuation estimates.

\paragraph{Open strings and the finite energy barrier.}
For an open chain \(c\), the boundary \(\partial c\) is the set of star
violations created by \(Z(c)\).  More precisely, for a ground vector
\(\psi\),
\begin{equation}
  \langle Z(c)\psi,H_\Lambda Z(c)\psi\rangle
  -\langle \psi,H_\Lambda\psi\rangle
  =
  2J\,|\partial c|.
  \label{eq:z2-code-open-string-energy}
\end{equation}
A simple partial string has two endpoints, so the energy cost is \(4J\),
independent of its length.  When the string closes into a noncontractible
cycle, the endpoints disappear and the operator becomes a logical line inside
the ground sector.  The same statement holds for dual \(X\)-strings with
plaquette violations.  Thus this exact laboratory simultaneously exhibits
topological logical lines and fixes the two-dimensional \(\mathbb Z_2\)
gauge-code barrier scale at order \(J\), independent of system size.  Any
finite-temperature memory statement must additionally specify the bath, rates,
decoder or absence of decoder, and the limiting notion of lifetime.

The lesson for gauge theory is the logical separation.  The finite stabilizer
model makes the microscopic line algebra and the one-form intersection phase
completely explicit.  A continuum deconfined gauge theory may have the same
long-distance topological sector when a regulator family, phase
classification, and infrared limit connect the microscopic model to that
sector.

\begin{openproblem}[Complete phase invariant for gauge theories]
\label{op:complete-phase-invariant-gauge-theories}
Define a tractable invariant of gauge-theory phases that includes local
correlators, extended-operator categories, higher-form symmetry realization,
background responses, boundary-condition sectors, and massive spectra, and
prove its stability under finite local deformations inside a gapped phase.
\end{openproblem}
