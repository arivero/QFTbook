\chapter{Boundaries And Defects}
\label{ch:global-boundaries-and-defects}

\ConventionsMixed

Boundaries and defects are QFT data supported on submanifolds.  They modify
operator algebras, symmetry realization, anomaly inflow, and phase structure.
This chapter formulates them as local data rather than as afterthoughts added
to a bulk theory.  The examples below are elementary enough that their fusion
and anomaly data can be checked directly.

\section{Boundary QFT Datum}

\begin{definition}[Boundary QFT datum]
\label{def:boundary-qft-datum}
Let \(M\) be a spacetime manifold with boundary \(\partial M\).  A boundary
condition for a local QFT assigns the following data:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{bulk observables} & \mathcal A(U),\quad U\subset M^\circ,\\
\text{boundary observables} & \mathcal B(V),\quad V\subset\partial M,\\
\text{bulk-to-boundary maps} & \mathcal A(U)\to\mathcal B(V)
\text{ for }U\to V,\\
\text{state or functional} & \omega_{\partial}
\text{ on the combined algebra}.
\end{array}
\]
Locality requires that observables with spacelike separated supports commute
or graded-commute after their supports are interpreted in the manifold with
boundary.
\end{definition}

The bulk-to-boundary maps in Definition~\ref{def:boundary-qft-datum} are not
optional.  They specify what happens when a bulk operator is brought to the
boundary and therefore determine boundary operator expansions, boundary Ward
identities, and the possible endpoints of bulk extended operators.

\section{Boundary Operator Expansion}

\begin{definition}[Boundary operator expansion]
\label{def:boundary-operator-expansion}
For a scalar bulk local operator \(\mathcal O(x_\perp,y)\), with
\(x_\perp\geq0\) the distance to the boundary and \(y\in\partial M\), the
boundary expansion has the form
\[
  \mathcal O(x_\perp,y)
  \sim
  \sum_\alpha
  c_{\mathcal O}^{\ \alpha}(x_\perp)\,\widehat{\mathcal O}_\alpha(y),
\]
where \(\widehat{\mathcal O}_\alpha\) are boundary local operators.  The
symbol means convergence inside the declared correlation-function topology
after smearing away from coincident boundary insertions.  Boundary critical
exponents and boundary anomalies are properties of this expansion and of the
boundary stress tensor.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Free scalar half-space boundary expansion]
\label{ex:free-scalar-half-space-boe}
Let \(\phi\) be a free massless scalar field on the Euclidean half-space
\(\mathbb R_{\ge0}\times\mathbb R^{D-1}\), with coordinates
\((x_\perp,y)\).  For Neumann boundary condition
\(\partial_\perp\phi|_{x_\perp=0}=0\), the method of images gives
\[
  \langle\phi(x_\perp,y)\phi(x'_\perp,y')\rangle_N
  =
  G_D(x_\perp-x'_\perp,y-y')
  +
  G_D(x_\perp+x'_\perp,y-y').
\]
For Dirichlet boundary condition \(\phi|_{x_\perp=0}=0\), the sign of the
image term is reversed.  Consequently the leading boundary operator in the
Neumann case is \(\widehat\phi(y)=\phi(0,y)\), whereas in the Dirichlet case
the leading nonzero boundary operator is
\(\widehat{\partial_\perp\phi}(y)=\partial_\perp\phi(0,y)\).  This example
shows that the boundary operator expansion is a statement about a boundary
condition, not merely a formal expansion near a hypersurface.
\end{example}

\section{Interfaces And Domain Walls}

\begin{definition}[Interface and domain wall]
\label{def:interface-domain-wall}
An interface between two QFTs \(\mathcal T_-\) and \(\mathcal T_+\) is a
codimension-one defect whose local neighborhoods have \(\mathcal T_-\) on one
side and \(\mathcal T_+\) on the other.  A domain wall is an interface between
two phases or vacua of the same microscopic theory.  Topological interfaces
can be moved without changing separated correlation functions, up to contact
terms specified on the wall.  Non-topological interfaces carry ordinary
localized excitations and have their own stress-tensor Ward identities.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Chern--Simons boundary and chiral WZW currents]
\label{ex:cs-boundary-wzw-global-chapter}
For Chern--Simons theory on a three-manifold with boundary, the variation of
the bulk action contains the boundary one-form
\[
  \frac{k}{4\pi}\int_{\partial M}\operatorname{tr}(A\wedge\delta A).
\]
After choosing the holomorphic polarization on the boundary surface
\(\Sigma\), the boundary field \(g:\Sigma\to G\) has the chiral
Wess--Zumino--Witten action displayed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:tqft-chern-simons-theory}.  The boundary current
\[
  J(z)=-\frac{k}{2\pi}\partial_zg\,g^{-1}
\]
obeys the affine current algebra at level \(k\).  Thus the boundary condition
does not simply remove a boundary term; it creates a boundary operator
algebra whose representation theory is the state-space input for Wilson-line
endpoints and boundary conformal blocks.
\end{example}

\section{Fusion}

\begin{definition}[Renormalized defect fusion]
\label{def:renormalized-defect-fusion}
Let \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) be parallel defects separated by distance \(\ell\).
Fusion asks for the limit
\[
  D_2\circ_\ell D_1\quad \text{as}\quad \ell\to0 .
\]
The limit is a defect \(D_2\star D_1\) only if correlation functions with
separated external insertions converge after renormalizing defect-local
operators.  In a topological sector, fusion gives a monoidal category of
defects.  In a general QFT, fusion is a renormalization problem for
codimension-one local data.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Ising topological defect fusion]
\label{ex:ising-defect-fusion-global-boundaries}
At the critical two-dimensional Ising point, the basic topological defects
are
\[
  \mathbf 1,\qquad \eta,\qquad \mathcal N,
\]
where \(\eta\) is the invertible spin-flip defect and \(\mathcal N\) is the
Kramers--Wannier duality defect.  Their fusion rules are
\[
  \eta\otimes\eta\simeq\mathbf 1,\qquad
  \eta\otimes\mathcal N\simeq\mathcal N\otimes\eta\simeq\mathcal N,
  \qquad
  \mathcal N\otimes\mathcal N\simeq\mathbf 1\oplus\eta .
\]
The last equation is the first test that the word ``duality'' is being used
as a defect statement rather than as a slogan: \(\mathcal N\) has no inverse,
because its square is a direct sum.  Chapter~\ref{ch:global-categorical-symmetry-defect-fusion}
derives the corresponding action on primary sectors from the Ising modular
\(S\)-matrix.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Fusion detects noninvertibility]
\label{prop:ising-defect-noninvertible}
In the fusion algebra of Example~\ref{ex:ising-defect-fusion-global-boundaries},
the defect \(\mathcal N\) is not invertible and has Frobenius--Perron
dimension \(\sqrt2\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(\mathcal N\) had an inverse \(X\), then
\(\mathcal N\otimes X\simeq\mathbf 1\) would be simple.  But fusing
\(\mathcal N\otimes\mathcal N\simeq\mathbf 1\oplus\eta\) with \(X\) gives
\[
  \mathcal N\simeq X\oplus(\eta\otimes X),
\]
which expresses the simple defect \(\mathcal N\) as a direct sum of two
nonzero summands, a contradiction in the semisimple defect category.  The
positive dimension homomorphism satisfies
\[
  d_{\mathcal N}^2=d_{\mathbf 1}+d_\eta=2,
\]
because \(d_{\mathbf 1}=d_\eta=1\).  Hence
\(d_{\mathcal N}=\sqrt2\).
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Electric-magnetic duality surface in three-dimensional BF]
\label{ex:three-d-bf-duality-surface}
The \(\mathbb Z_N\) topological gauge theory can be written in continuum
notation as the \(U(1)\times U(1)\) BF action
\[
  S_{\rm BF}
  =
  \frac{N}{2\pi}\int B\wedge dA,
\]
with compact one-form gauge fields \(A\) and \(B\).  Its simple line labels
are pairs \((e,m)\in\mathbb Z_N\oplus\mathbb Z_N\), represented by
\[
  W_{e,m}(\gamma)
  =
  \exp\!\left(\ii e\int_\gamma A+\ii m\int_\gamma B\right).
\]
The codimension-one surface defect \(S\) that exchanges \(A\) and \(B\) acts
by
\[
  S:\ (e,m)\longmapsto(m,e).
\]
It is topological because it preserves the BF action and the braiding pairing
\[
  \langle(e,m),(e',m')\rangle
  =
  \exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi\ii}{N}(em'+me')\right).
\]
Fusion satisfies \(S\otimes S\simeq\mathbf 1\).  This is an invertible
surface defect; unlike the Ising \(\mathcal N\) defect, it is an ordinary
duality wall because the induced map on the finite line-label group is an
automorphism.
\end{example}

\section{Anomalies And Inflow}

\begin{definition}[Anomaly line of a boundary or defect]
\label{def:defect-anomaly-line}
A boundary or defect can carry an anomaly line for background fields
restricted to its support.  If \(A_D\) denotes those restricted background
fields, then a defect partition function with anomaly is a section
\[
  Z_D(A_D)\in\Gamma(\mathcal L_D),
\]
where \(\mathcal L_D\) is a complex line over the background-field space.
\end{definition}

An invertible bulk theory with boundary \(D\) supplies a section of
\(\mathcal L_D^{-1}\).  The product is then a complex number.  If a boundary
condition preserves a global symmetry, its boundary anomaly must match the
restriction of the bulk inflow.  If it breaks the symmetry, the broken
background fields are not part of the boundary datum.

\begin{proposition}[Inflow cancellation as line trivialization]
\label{prop:inflow-line-trivialization}
The combined bulk-defect amplitude is an ordinary complex-valued functional
of background fields if and only if the tensor product of the defect anomaly
line and the bulk inflow line is canonically trivialized.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(Z_D(A_D)\) is a section of \(\mathcal L_D\) and the bulk inflow
amplitude \(Z_{\rm bulk}(A)\) is a section of \(\mathcal L_{\rm bulk}\), then
their product is a section of
\(\mathcal L_D\otimes\mathcal L_{\rm bulk}\).  It is a number precisely after
choosing a natural isomorphism
\[
  \mathcal L_D\otimes\mathcal L_{\rm bulk}\cong\underline{\C}.
\]
Conversely, if the product is a complex-valued functional for all background
fields and is compatible with gluing of backgrounds, it defines exactly such
a trivialization of the tensor product line.
\end{proof}

\section{Gauge-Theory Boundaries}

For gauge theories, a boundary condition must specify which gauge
transformations are redundancies at the boundary and which become global
symmetries acting on boundary operators.  Electric boundary conditions fix
the tangential connection or electric flux according to the chosen
Hamiltonian polarization.  Magnetic boundary conditions fix the dual data.
Line operators may end on the boundary precisely when boundary degrees of
freedom carry the corresponding charge and cancel the endpoint gauge
variation.

\begin{example}[Endpoint gauge variation of a Wilson line]
\label{ex:wilson-line-endpoint-boundary}
Let \(\gamma\) be an oriented path from \(p\) to \(q\), and let
\[
  U_\gamma(A)=\operatorname{Pexp}\int_\gamma A
\]
be parallel transport in a representation \(R\).  Under a gauge
transformation \(g\),
\[
  U_\gamma(A^g)=R(g(p))\,U_\gamma(A)\,R(g(q))^{-1}.
\]
Thus an open Wilson line is not gauge invariant by itself.  It can end on a
boundary only if boundary operators at \(p\) and \(q\) transform in the dual
representations needed to contract these endpoint factors, or if the
boundary condition converts the relevant gauge transformations into fixed
global symmetry transformations and the line is interpreted as a charged
operator under that boundary symmetry.
\end{example}

\section{Defects And Phase Data}

Boundary and defect categories refine the phase invariant of
Chapter~\ref{ch:global-phases-of-gauge-theories}.  A gapped phase includes
the fusion category of topological line defects, higher-codimension defect
operators, boundary conditions, and their anomaly responses.  Two theories
with the same local massive spectrum can be distinguished by these extended
data.

\begin{openproblem}[Defect-complete phase classification]
\label{op:defect-complete-phase-classification}
Construct a phase invariant that includes boundary conditions, defect fusion,
endpoint maps for line and surface operators, anomaly inflow, and local
operator data, and prove stability of this invariant under finite gapped
deformations.
\end{openproblem}
