\chapter{O(N), Gross--Neveu, And Sigma-Model Families}
\label{ch:integrable-on-gross-neveu-sigma-model-families}

\ConventionsMixedCFT

This chapter introduces asymptotically free two-dimensional model families
whose exact scattering data carry nonabelian internal symmetry.  The aim is to
make explicit which pieces of the exact description are consequences of
symmetry, analyticity, and factorization, and which pieces require a
nonperturbative construction of the underlying local QFT.

\section{Internal-Symmetry Scattering Data}

Let \(G\) be a compact Lie group and let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional
unitary representation.  A massive particle multiplet with rapidity
\(\theta\) has one-particle space
\[
  \Hilb_1=L^2(\R,\dd\theta)\otimes V .
\]
A factorized two-particle scattering operator is a meromorphic family
\[
  S_{VV}(\theta):V\otimes V\longrightarrow V\otimes V,
\]
where \(\theta=\theta_1-\theta_2\).  \(G\)-invariance means
\[
  S_{VV}(\theta)\,(\rho(g)\otimes\rho(g))
  =
  (\rho(g)\otimes\rho(g))\,S_{VV}(\theta)
\]
for every \(g\in G\).  If
\[
  V\otimes V\simeq \bigoplus_\lambda m_\lambda V_\lambda ,
\]
then Schur decomposition gives
\[
  S_{VV}(\theta)
  =
  \bigoplus_\lambda S_\lambda(\theta)\otimes I_{V_\lambda}
\]
whenever the multiplicities \(m_\lambda\) are one.  With multiplicities, the
coefficient \(S_\lambda(\theta)\) is an endomorphism of the multiplicity
space.  Thus symmetry reduces the scattering problem to scalar or finite
matrix meromorphic functions, but unitarity, crossing, pole residues, and the
Yang--Baxter equation still constrain them.

\section{O(N)-Invariant Two-Particle Form}

For the vector representation \(V=\R^N\) of \(O(N)\), an invariant
two-particle amplitude has the index form
\[
  S_{ij}^{kl}(\theta)
  =
  \sigma_1(\theta)\delta_{ij}\delta^{kl}
  +\sigma_2(\theta)\delta_i^{\,k}\delta_j^{\,l}
  +\sigma_3(\theta)\delta_i^{\,l}\delta_j^{\,k}.
\]
Equivalently, decompose
\[
  V\otimes V
  =
  \mathbf 1\oplus \operatorname{Sym}^2_0(V)\oplus \wedge^2V .
\]
Let \(P_0,P_S,P_A\) be the corresponding projectors.  Then
\[
  S(\theta)=S_0(\theta)P_0+S_S(\theta)P_S+S_A(\theta)P_A .
\]
The two descriptions are related by applying the projectors to the index
formula.  For example,
\[
  P_0{}_{ij}^{kl}=\frac1N\delta_{ij}\delta^{kl},\qquad
  P_A{}_{ij}^{kl}=\frac12(\delta_i^{\,k}\delta_j^{\,l}
  -\delta_i^{\,l}\delta_j^{\,k}).
\]
The amplitude is unitary on the real rapidity axis when
\[
  S_R(\theta)S_R(-\theta)=1,\qquad
  R\in\{0,S,A\},
\]
after diagonalization in these channels.  Crossing identifies the particle
with its conjugate and relates \(S_{ij}^{kl}(\theta)\) to the amplitude at
\(\ii\pi-\theta\) with one external index crossed.

\section{Exact O(N) Sigma-Model Vector S-Matrix}

For \(N>2\), set
\[
  \Delta=\frac1{N-2},
  \qquad
  z=-\frac{\ii\theta}{2\pi}.
\]
The minimal \(O(N)\)-invariant vector S-matrix of the massive nonlinear
sigma-model family is
\begin{equation}
  S_{ij}^{kl}(\theta)
  =
  \sigma_1(\theta)\delta_{ij}\delta^{kl}
  +\sigma_2(\theta)\delta_i^{\,k}\delta_j^{\,l}
  +\sigma_3(\theta)\delta_i^{\,l}\delta_j^{\,k},
  \label{eq:on-sigma-exact-smatrix-index}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_2(\theta)
  =
  \frac{
    \Gamma(\Delta+z)\Gamma(\frac12+z)
    \Gamma(\frac12+\Delta-z)\Gamma(1-z)}
  {
    \Gamma(\frac12+\Delta+z)\Gamma(z)
    \Gamma(1+\Delta-z)\Gamma(\frac12-z)}
  ,
  \label{eq:on-sigma-sigma2}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_1(\theta)
  =
  -\frac{2\pi\ii}{N-2}
  \frac{\sigma_2(\theta)}{\ii\pi-\theta},
  \qquad
  \sigma_3(\theta)=\sigma_1(\ii\pi-\theta)
  =
  -\frac{2\pi\ii}{N-2}
  \frac{\sigma_2(\theta)}{\theta}.
  \label{eq:on-sigma-sigma1-sigma3}
\end{equation}
The word minimal means that no CDD factor, unitary and crossing-symmetric and
analytic without zeros or poles in the physical strip, has been multiplied
into the displayed solution.  The formula is a scattering datum.  Identifying
it with a continuum local sigma-model QFT additionally requires the
nonperturbative construction and matching problem stated at the end of the
chapter.

The tensor in \eqref{eq:on-sigma-exact-smatrix-index} is written in the
Zamolodchikov--Faddeev component convention: it obeys
\[
  S_{12}(\theta)S_{13}(\theta+\phi)S_{23}(\phi)
  =
  S_{23}(\phi)S_{13}(\theta+\phi)S_{12}(\theta)
\]
as a meromorphic identity on \(V^{\otimes3}\).  The common scalar factor
\(\sigma_2(\theta)\sigma_2(\theta+\phi)\sigma_2(\phi)\) cancels from this
identity, leaving the rational \(O(N)\)-invariant tensor
\[
  I-\frac{\ii a}{\theta}P-\frac{\ii a}{\ii\pi-\theta}K,\qquad
  a=\frac{2\pi}{N-2},
\]
where \(P_{ij}^{kl}=\delta_i^{\,l}\delta_j^{\,k}\) and
\(K_{ij}^{kl}=\delta_{ij}\delta^{kl}\).  Direct expansion on the basis
\(e_i\otimes e_j\otimes e_k\) reduces the component equality to rational
identities in \(\theta,\phi\) and the contraction relation \(K^2=NK\);
the accompanying calculation check enumerates finite-dimensional instances of
this tensor identity together with the scalar unitarity and crossing checks.

\begin{proposition}[Channel unitarity and crossing]
\label{prop:on-sigma-smatrix-unitarity-crossing}
Let
\[
  S_0=N\sigma_1+\sigma_2+\sigma_3,\qquad
  S_S=\sigma_2+\sigma_3,\qquad
  S_A=\sigma_2-\sigma_3
\]
be the singlet, symmetric-traceless, and antisymmetric channel eigenvalues of
\eqref{eq:on-sigma-exact-smatrix-index}.  For real rapidity \(\theta\neq0\),
\[
  S_R(\theta)S_R(-\theta)=1,\qquad R\in\{0,S,A\}.
\]
The coefficient functions satisfy the crossing relations
\[
  \sigma_2(\theta)=\sigma_2(\ii\pi-\theta),
  \qquad
  \sigma_1(\theta)=\sigma_3(\ii\pi-\theta),
  \qquad
  \sigma_3(\theta)=\sigma_1(\ii\pi-\theta).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The projector decomposition gives the displayed channel eigenvalues because
\[
  Q_{ij}^{kl}=\delta_{ij}\delta^{kl},\qquad
  I_{ij}^{kl}=\delta_i^{\,k}\delta_j^{\,l},\qquad
  F_{ij}^{kl}=\delta_i^{\,l}\delta_j^{\,k}
\]
act on \(V\otimes V\) as \(Q=NP_0\), \(I=P_0+P_S+P_A\), and
\(F=P_0+P_S-P_A\).  Thus
\[
  \sigma_1Q+\sigma_2I+\sigma_3F
  =
  S_0P_0+S_SP_S+S_AP_A .
\]

For real \(\theta\), the gamma-function expression obeys
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_2(\theta)\sigma_2(-\theta)
  =
  \frac{\theta^2}
       {\theta^2+\left(\frac{2\pi}{N-2}\right)^2}.
  \label{eq:on-sigma-sigma2-unitarity-factor}
\end{equation}
This follows by multiplying \eqref{eq:on-sigma-sigma2} at \(z\) and \(-z\)
and cancelling adjacent gamma factors with \(\Gamma(1+z)=z\Gamma(z)\) and
\(\Gamma(1-z)=(-z)\Gamma(-z)\).  Set
\[
  a=\frac{2\pi}{N-2}.
\]
The symmetric-traceless and antisymmetric rational factors are
\[
  \frac{S_S(\theta)}{\sigma_2(\theta)}
  =
  1-\frac{\ii a}{\theta},
  \qquad
  \frac{S_A(\theta)}{\sigma_2(\theta)}
  =
  1+\frac{\ii a}{\theta}.
\]
Multiplying these factors at \(\theta\) and \(-\theta\) gives
\[
  1+\frac{a^2}{\theta^2},
\]
which cancels the denominator in
\eqref{eq:on-sigma-sigma2-unitarity-factor}.  For the singlet channel,
\[
  \frac{S_0(\theta)}{\sigma_2(\theta)}
  =
  1-\frac{\ii a}{\theta}
  -\frac{\ii Na}{\ii\pi-\theta}.
\]
The elementary identity
\[
  \left(
    1-\frac{\ii a}{\theta}
    -\frac{\ii Na}{\ii\pi-\theta}
  \right)
  \left(
    1+\frac{\ii a}{\theta}
    -\frac{\ii Na}{\ii\pi+\theta}
  \right)
  =
  1+\frac{a^2}{\theta^2}
\]
again cancels \eqref{eq:on-sigma-sigma2-unitarity-factor}.  This proves
channel unitarity.

Crossing for \(\sigma_2\) is immediate from
\[
  -\frac{\ii(\ii\pi-\theta)}{2\pi}
  =
  \frac12-z,
\]
which interchanges the two numerator gamma factors with the two denominator
gamma factors in \eqref{eq:on-sigma-sigma2} exactly as displayed.  The
relations between \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_3\) then follow from their
definitions in \eqref{eq:on-sigma-sigma1-sigma3}.
\end{proof}

\section{Sigma-Model Ultraviolet Datum}

The Euclidean \(O(N)\) nonlinear sigma model is defined with field
\[
  n:\Sigma\longrightarrow S^{N-1}\subset\R^N,\qquad n\cdot n=1,
\]
and action
\[
  S_{\sigma}[n]
  =
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma \dd^2x\,\partial_\mu n\cdot\partial_\mu n .
\]
The parameter \(g\) is the coefficient of the kinetic term in the displayed
regularized path integral.  Perturbation theory around a local coordinate
chart on \(S^{N-1}\) gives the one-loop running
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd g}{\dd\mu}
  =
  -\frac{N-2}{2\pi}g^3+O(g^5).
\]
The sign follows from the Ricci tensor of the round target:
\[
  R_{ab}=(N-2)h_{ab}.
\]
The corresponding sigma-model beta tensor has leading term
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd h_{ab}}{\dd\mu}
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}R_{ab}+O(R^2),
\]
and \(h_{ab}=g^{-2}h^{\mathrm{round}}_{ab}\) yields the displayed scalar
beta function.  A nonperturbative massive QFT construction must then provide
the Hilbert space, local fields or local algebras, and scattering states whose
two-particle data match the factorized \(O(N)\)-invariant amplitude.

\section{Large-N Sigma-Model Gap Equation}

The large-\(N\) saddle gives a direct derivation of dimensional transmutation.
Write the constraint with a Lagrange multiplier \(M^2\) and integrate the
\(N\) components of \(n\) at a translation-invariant saddle.  With Euclidean
momentum cutoff \(\Lambda\), and with the 't Hooft coupling
\[
  \lambda=N g^2
\]
held fixed, the saddle equation is
\[
  \frac1{\lambda}
  =
  \int^\Lambda\frac{\dd^2p}{(2\pi)^2}\frac1{p^2+M^2}
  =
  \frac1{4\pi}\log\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2+M^2}{M^2}\right).
\]
Solving gives
\[
  M
  =
  \frac{\Lambda}{\sqrt{\exp(4\pi/\lambda)-1}}
  =
  \Lambda\exp\!\left(-\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\right)
  \left(1+O(e^{-4\pi/\lambda})\right)
\]
in the weak-coupling scaling regime.  If the physical mass \(M\) is held
fixed while the subtraction scale \(\mu\) is changed, the running coupling
\[
  \lambda(\mu)=\frac{2\pi}{\log(\mu/M)}
\]
satisfies
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd\lambda}{\dd\mu}
  =
  -\frac{\lambda^2}{2\pi}
  +O(1/N).
\]
This is the large-\(N\) form of asymptotic freedom.  The finite-\(N\)
one-loop coefficient replaces \(N\) by \(N-2\) in the beta function for the
unscaled coupling, as in the Ricci-tensor calculation above.

\section{Projective Sigma Models}
\label{sec:integrable-cpn-sigma-models}

The next basic asymptotically free family is the projective model with target
\(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\).  A convenient ultraviolet coordinate uses a complex
\(N\)-component field \(z\), a nondynamical \(U(1)\) connection \(A_\mu\), and
the local redundancy
\[
  z(x)\mapsto \ee^{\ii\alpha(x)}z(x),\qquad
  A_\mu(x)\mapsto A_\mu(x)+\partial_\mu\alpha(x).
\]
The constraint and covariant derivative are
\[
  z^\dagger z=1,\qquad D_\mu z=(\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu)z,
\]
and the Euclidean action is
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}}[z,A]
  =
  \frac1{g^2}\int_\Sigma\dd^2x\, (D_\mu z)^\dagger D_\mu z
  +\ii\frac{\theta}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma F_A .
  \label{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge}
\end{equation}
The \(U(1)\) field is part of the quotient description, not an additional
microscopic photon.  On a closed Euclidean surface, for a smooth line bundle,
\[
  \frac1{2\pi}\int_\Sigma F_A\in\mathbb Z,
\]
so the coefficient \(\theta\) is \(2\pi\)-periodic in the continuum
topological sectors.  A lattice or other ultraviolet definition must specify
how this integer is represented before the \(\theta\)-dependent path integral
is a mathematical object.

The projector
\[
  P=z z^\dagger,\qquad P^2=P,\qquad \operatorname{tr}P=1,
\]
is gauge invariant and gives an intrinsic coordinate on
\(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\).  The \(U(1)\) connection in
\eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge} can be eliminated at the classical
level.  Varying with respect to \(A_\mu\), while imposing \(z^\dagger z=1\),
gives
\begin{equation}
  A_\mu=-\ii z^\dagger\partial_\mu z,
  \qquad
  D_\mu z=(1-P)\partial_\mu z .
  \label{eq:cpn-auxiliary-connection-eliminated}
\end{equation}
This formula is invariant under \(z\mapsto \ee^{\ii\alpha}z\) together with
\(A\mapsto A+\dd\alpha\), as required by the quotient description.

\begin{proposition}[Projector form of the kinetic term and charge]
\label{prop:cpn-projector-kinetic-topological-charge}
For a smooth representative \(z\) of a map into \(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\), with
\(A=-\ii z^\dagger\dd z\) and \(P=zz^\dagger\),
\begin{align}
  \operatorname{tr}(\partial_\mu P\,\partial_\mu P)
  &=2(D_\mu z)^\dagger D_\mu z,
  \label{eq:cpn-projector-kinetic-identity}\\
  F_A&=-\ii\,\operatorname{tr}(P\,\dd P\wedge\dd P).
  \label{eq:cpn-projector-curvature-identity}
\end{align}
Consequently the action in \eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge} is
equivalently
\[
  S_{\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}}[P]
  =
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma\dd^2x\,
  \operatorname{tr}(\partial_\mu P\,\partial_\mu P)
  +\ii\theta Q[P],
  \qquad
  Q[P]=-\frac{\ii}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma
  \operatorname{tr}(P\,\dd P\wedge\dd P).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The stationarity equation for \(A_\mu\) follows from
\[
  (D_\mu z)^\dagger D_\mu z
  =
  \partial_\mu z^\dagger\partial_\mu z
  +A_\mu^2
  +2\ii A_\mu z^\dagger\partial_\mu z ,
\]
where \(\partial_\mu z^\dagger z=-z^\dagger\partial_\mu z\).  Hence
\(A_\mu=-\ii z^\dagger\partial_\mu z\), and then
\[
  D_\mu z
  =
  \partial_\mu z-z(z^\dagger\partial_\mu z)
  =
  (1-P)\partial_\mu z .
\]
Differentiating \(P=zz^\dagger\) and using \(z^\dagger D_\mu z=0\) gives
\[
  \partial_\mu P=(D_\mu z)z^\dagger+z(D_\mu z)^\dagger .
\]
Squaring and tracing gives
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(\partial_\mu P\,\partial_\mu P)
  =
  2(D_\mu z)^\dagger D_\mu z,
\]
because \(z^\dagger D_\mu z=0\) and \(z^\dagger z=1\).  Finally
\[
  F_A=\dd A=-\ii\,\dd z^\dagger\wedge\dd z .
\]
The same differentiated-projector identity gives
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(P\,\dd P\wedge\dd P)
  =
  \dd z^\dagger\wedge\dd z,
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:cpn-projector-curvature-identity}.  If the map is a
section of a nontrivial line bundle, the computation is local and the
two-form patches globally; its integral is the first Chern number of that
line bundle.
\end{proof}

On an oriented Euclidean surface with local coordinates \(x^1,x^2\), write
\(F_{12}\dd x^1\wedge\dd x^2=F_A\).  The algebraic identity
\[
  \bigl|D_1z\pm\ii D_2z\bigr|^2
  =
  |D_1z|^2+|D_2z|^2\mp F_{12}
\]
gives the instanton bound, in the normalization of
\eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge},
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathbb{CP}^{N-1},\theta=0}
  \ge
  \frac{2\pi}{g^2}|Q[P]|.
  \label{eq:cpn-instanton-bound}
\end{equation}
For \(Q>0\), equality is attained by \(D_1z+\ii D_2z=0\), equivalently by a
holomorphic map to projective space in the induced complex structure.  For
\(Q<0\), equality is attained by the anti-holomorphic equation.  This is a
finite-action Euclidean saddle statement in the continuum sigma-model
functional.  A real-time local QFT construction additionally supplies the
Hilbert space, local observables, reflection-positive Euclidean data, and
analytic continuation needed to interpret these sectors dynamically.

The one-loop beta tensor is again the Ricci tensor of the target; with the
Fubini--Study metric normalized so that
\[
  \operatorname{Ric}=N\,h_{\rm FS},
\]
the model is asymptotically free.  If \(t=g^2\) denotes the scalar coupling in
\eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge}, the leading running has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mu\frac{\dd t}{\dd\mu}
  =
  -\frac{N}{2\pi}t^2+\text{higher powers of }t,
  \label{eq:cpn-leading-beta}
\end{equation}
in this normalization.

At large \(N\) with \(\lambda=Ng^2\) fixed, introduce a Lagrange multiplier
enforcing \(z^\dagger z=1\).  A translation-invariant saddle gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac1{\lambda}
  =
  \int^\Lambda\frac{\dd^2p}{(2\pi)^2}\frac1{p^2+m^2}
  =
  \frac1{4\pi}\log\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2+m^2}{m^2}\right),
  \label{eq:cpn-large-n-gap}
\end{equation}
up to the convention-dependent factor already fixed in the action.  Thus the
projective family has the same dimensional-transmutation structure as the
round \(O(N)\) model.  What differs is the global topology, the \(U(1)\)
quotient, the \(\theta\)-angle, and the particle multiplet structure.

\subsection{The Projective Line and the Exact-Scattering Status}
\label{subsec:cp1-o3-and-projective-status}

The case \(N=2\) is a useful normalization check because
\(\mathbb{CP}^1\) is the two-sphere.  Let \(\sigma_a\),
\(a=1,2,3\), be Pauli matrices with
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(\sigma_a\sigma_b)=2\delta_{ab},\qquad
  \operatorname{tr}(\sigma_a\sigma_b\sigma_c)=2\ii\epsilon_{abc}.
\]
For a unit vector \(n:\Sigma\to S^2\subset\R^3\), set
\begin{equation}
  P_n=\frac12(I+n_a\sigma_a).
  \label{eq:cp1-projector-pauli}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[\(\mathbb{CP}^1\) as the \(O(3)\) sigma model]
\label{prop:cp1-o3-normalization-ledger}
The map \(n\mapsto P_n\) identifies \(S^2\) with the rank-one Hermitian
projectors in \(\mathbb C^2\) and obeys
\begin{align}
  \operatorname{tr}(\partial_\mu P_n\,\partial_\mu P_n)
  &=
  \frac12\,\partial_\mu n\cdot\partial_\mu n,
  \label{eq:cp1-o3-kinetic-ledger}\\
  -\ii\,\operatorname{tr}(P_n\,\dd P_n\wedge\dd P_n)
  &=
  \frac12\,\epsilon_{abc}n_a\,\dd n_b\wedge\dd n_c .
  \label{eq:cp1-o3-charge-ledger}
\end{align}
Consequently the \(\mathbb{CP}^1\) action
\[
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma\operatorname{tr}(\partial_\mu P\,\partial_\mu P)
  +\ii\theta Q[P]
\]
is the \(O(3)\) action
\[
  \frac1{2g_{O(3)}^2}\int_\Sigma
  \partial_\mu n\cdot\partial_\mu n+\ii\theta\,\deg(n),
  \qquad
  g_{O(3)}^2=2g^2,
  \label{eq:cp1-o3-coupling-match}
\]
where
\[
  \deg(n)=\frac1{8\pi}\int_\Sigma
  \epsilon_{abc}n_a\,\dd n_b\wedge\dd n_c .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The matrix \(P_n\) is Hermitian and
\[
  P_n^2=\frac14(I+2n_a\sigma_a+n_an_b\sigma_a\sigma_b)
  =\frac12(I+n_a\sigma_a)=P_n,
\]
because \(n_an_b\epsilon_{abc}=0\) and \(n_an_a=1\).  Its trace is one.
Conversely every rank-one Hermitian projector in \(\mathbb C^2\) has the form
\eqref{eq:cp1-projector-pauli}, since Hermitian \(2\times2\) matrices are
spanned by \(I,\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\sigma_3\), the trace-one condition fixes the
coefficient of \(I\), and \(P^2=P\) fixes \(n\cdot n=1\).

Differentiating gives \(\partial_\mu P_n=\frac12(\partial_\mu n_a)\sigma_a\).
Thus
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(\partial_\mu P_n\,\partial_\mu P_n)
  =
  \frac14\,\partial_\mu n_a\partial_\mu n_b
  \operatorname{tr}(\sigma_a\sigma_b)
  =
  \frac12\,\partial_\mu n\cdot\partial_\mu n.
  \]
For the curvature form,
\[
  \operatorname{tr}(P_n\,\dd P_n\wedge\dd P_n)
  =
  \frac18 n_a\,\dd n_b\wedge\dd n_c\,
  \operatorname{tr}(\sigma_a\sigma_b\sigma_c),
\]
because the term with \(\operatorname{tr}(\sigma_b\sigma_c)\) is symmetric in
\((b,c)\) and hence vanishes after wedging.  The Pauli trace identity gives
\eqref{eq:cp1-o3-charge-ledger}.  Substituting
\eqref{eq:cp1-o3-kinetic-ledger} into the projector action gives the coupling
match, and substituting \eqref{eq:cp1-o3-charge-ledger} into \(Q[P]\) gives
the degree formula.
\end{proof}

For \(N=2\), the exact massive scattering problem is therefore the \(O(3)\)
case of the vector sigma-model scattering problem described earlier in this
chapter.
For \(N>2\), one must keep a sharper distinction.  The classical
\(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\) target is a compact symmetric space and has a classical
zero-curvature description.  A quantum factorization theorem for the bosonic
projective model requires additional data: quantum conserved charges or an
equivalent factorizing construction, the particle spectrum, the scalar
normalization, the crossed particle-antiparticle amplitudes, and the matching
map from the regulator-defined model
\eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge} to the bootstrap mass coordinate.

The \(N=(2,2)\) supersymmetric \(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\) model is a different
local QFT.  Its scattering proposal factorizes an \(SU(N)\) chiral
Gross--Neveu tensor part with an additional supersymmetric minimal factor and
has an antisymmetric bound-state tower.  Those statements use the extra
fermionic fields and supercharges of the supersymmetric theory; the bosonic
projective action \eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge} requires its own
factorization and matching data.

\subsection{Projective-Model Crossing Data}
\label{subsec:projective-model-crossing-data}

The minimal scattering language for the massive projective family contains
particles in the defining representation \(V=\mathbb C^N\) of \(SU(N)\) and
antiparticles in the dual representation \(V^\vee\).  Fix a basis
\(\{e_i\}_{i=1}^N\) of \(V\) and the dual basis \(\{e^i\}_{i=1}^N\) of
\(V^\vee\).  On \(V\otimes V\), define
\[
  I_{VV}(e_i\otimes e_j)=e_i\otimes e_j,\qquad
  P_{VV}(e_i\otimes e_j)=e_j\otimes e_i .
\]
On \(V\otimes V^\vee\), define
\[
  I_{V\bar V}(e_i\otimes e^j)=e_i\otimes e^j,\qquad
  E_{V\bar V}(e_i\otimes e^j)
  =
  \delta_i^j\Omega,\qquad
  \Omega=\sum_{m=1}^N e_m\otimes e^m .
  \label{eq:projective-annihilation-operator}
\]
The operator \(E_{V\bar V}\) is the invariant cap-cup tensor; it obeys
\[
  E_{V\bar V}^2=N E_{V\bar V}.
  \label{eq:projective-annihilation-idempotent}
\]
Thus \(N^{-1}E_{V\bar V}\) projects onto the singlet line
\(\mathbb C\Omega\), while
\[
  \Pi_{\rm adj}=I_{V\bar V}-\frac1N E_{V\bar V}
  \label{eq:projective-adjoint-projector}
\]
projects onto the adjoint summand in \(V\otimes V^\vee\).

\begin{definition}[Projective crossing datum]
\label{def:projective-crossing-datum}
A rank-one projective bootstrap datum consists of meromorphic functions
\(A(\theta)\), \(B(\theta)\), \(C(\theta)\), and \(D(\theta)\), together with
the operators
\begin{align}
  S_{VV}(\theta)&=A(\theta)I_{VV}+B(\theta)P_{VV},
  \label{eq:projective-vv-smatrix}\\
  S_{V\bar V}(\theta)&=C(\theta)I_{V\bar V}+D(\theta)E_{V\bar V}.
  \label{eq:projective-vbarv-smatrix}
\end{align}
The chosen charge-conjugation pairing identifies \(V^\vee\) with the crossed
outgoing \(V\)-leg.  In this convention, crossing in the second leg is the
meromorphic relation
\begin{equation}
  C(\theta)=A(\ii\pi-\theta),\qquad
  D(\theta)=B(\ii\pi-\theta).
  \label{eq:projective-crossing-functions}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Crossing of the invariant tensors]
\label{prop:projective-crossing-tensor-ledger}
With the convention of Definition~\ref{def:projective-crossing-datum}, the
crossed tensor of \(S_{VV}\) is exactly \(S_{V\bar V}\) with coefficients
\eqref{eq:projective-crossing-functions}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write matrix elements of \(S_{VV}\) as
\[
  (S_{VV})_{ij}^{k\ell}(\theta)
  =
  A(\theta)\delta_i^k\delta_j^\ell
  +
  B(\theta)\delta_i^\ell\delta_j^k .
  \label{eq:projective-vv-components}
\]
Crossing the second outgoing leg to an incoming antiparticle replaces the pair
\((j,\ell)\) by \((\ell,j)\) and evaluates the rapidity at \(\ii\pi-\theta\):
\[
  (S_{V\bar V})_{i\bar j}^{k\bar\ell}(\theta)
  =
  (S_{VV})_{i\ell}^{k j}(\ii\pi-\theta).
  \label{eq:projective-crossing-component-rule}
\]
Substitution into \eqref{eq:projective-vv-components} gives
\[
  A(\ii\pi-\theta)\delta_i^k\delta_\ell^j
  +
  B(\ii\pi-\theta)\delta_i^j\delta_\ell^k .
\]
The first tensor is \(I_{V\bar V}\), and the second tensor is
\(E_{V\bar V}\), with the component convention in
\eqref{eq:projective-annihilation-operator}.  This proves
\eqref{eq:projective-crossing-functions}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Projective-channel unitarity ledger]
\label{prop:projective-channel-unitarity-ledger}
Let
\[
  S_\pm(\theta)=A(\theta)\pm B(\theta),\qquad
  S_{\rm adj}(\theta)=C(\theta),\qquad
  S_{\rm sing}(\theta)=C(\theta)+N D(\theta).
  \label{eq:projective-channel-eigenvalues}
\]
Then braiding unitarity on \(V\otimes V\) is equivalent to
\[
  S_+(\theta)S_+(-\theta)=1,\qquad
  S_-(\theta)S_-(-\theta)=1,
  \label{eq:projective-vv-unitarity-eigenvalues}
\]
and braiding unitarity on \(V\otimes V^\vee\) is equivalent to
\[
  S_{\rm adj}(\theta)S_{\rm adj}(-\theta)=1,\qquad
  S_{\rm sing}(\theta)S_{\rm sing}(-\theta)=1.
  \label{eq:projective-vbarv-unitarity-eigenvalues}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The flip \(P_{VV}\) has eigenvalue \(+1\) on \(\operatorname{Sym}^2 V\) and
eigenvalue \(-1\) on \(\wedge^2 V\).  Therefore \(S_{VV}\) has eigenvalues
\(S_+\) and \(S_-\) on the two irreducible summands, and unitarity reduces to
\eqref{eq:projective-vv-unitarity-eigenvalues}.  For \(V\otimes V^\vee\),
\eqref{eq:projective-annihilation-idempotent} gives eigenvalue \(N\) on
\(\mathbb C\Omega\) and eigenvalue \(0\) on the adjoint summand.  The two
eigenvalues of \(S_{V\bar V}\) are therefore \(S_{\rm sing}\) and
\(S_{\rm adj}\), giving \eqref{eq:projective-vbarv-unitarity-eigenvalues}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Pole assignment in the projective rank-one block]
\label{prop:projective-pole-assignment-ledger}
Suppose the \(VV\) tensor part is the rational block
\(\mathcal R_N(\theta)\) of \eqref{eq:su-n-rational-r-block}, multiplied by a
scalar factor \(X_{\mathbb{CP}}(\theta)\).  If the projective bootstrap datum
assigns no stable direct-channel particle in \(\wedge^2 V\), then
\[
  X_{\mathbb{CP}}(\theta)
\]
has a zero at \(\theta=2\pi\ii/N\) of at least the order of the pole of
\(\mathcal R_N\) there.  If instead that pole is assigned to a listed stable
bound state, the residue must be projected onto \(\wedge^2 V\) and must obey
the positive-residue convention of the Hilbert-space normalization.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The symmetric and antisymmetric eigenvalues of \(\mathcal R_N\) are
\[
  1,\qquad
  \frac{\theta+\frac{2\pi\ii}{N}}{\theta-\frac{2\pi\ii}{N}} .
\]
The second eigenvalue has a simple pole at \(\theta=2\pi\ii/N\), inside the
physical strip for \(N>2\), and the corresponding eigenspace is
\(\wedge^2V\).  A meromorphic scattering amplitude can contain such a pole in
the physical strip only as declared scattering data.  If the spectrum assigns
no stable direct-channel state to that tensor channel, the pole of the matrix
block must be canceled by a zero of the scalar factor.  If the pole is kept,
its residue is precisely the projection onto the stated bound-state channel,
with its normalization fixed by the Hilbert-space inner product.
\end{proof}

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Projective scattering status]
\label{ca:cpn-exact-scattering-status}
The preceding tensor constructions give finite-dimensional bootstrap
infrastructure for the projective family.  They specify the invariant channels,
their crossing relation, and the pole-assignment ledger before any scalar
factor is chosen.  A completed projective-model \(S\)-matrix further specifies
the scalar functions, their physical-strip singularities, the CDD
normalization, and the map from the regulator-defined model
\eqref{eq:cpn-action-auxiliary-gauge} to the bootstrap mass coordinate.  The
\(\theta=\pi\) infrared behavior is a separate dynamical question; for some
families it is expected to land on a level-one WZW fixed point, but that
statement belongs to a specified continuum limit and cannot be read off from
the perturbative beta function alone.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{Principal Chiral Models and WZW Endpoints}
\label{sec:integrable-pcm-wzw-family}

Let \(G\) be a compact simple Lie group with invariant positive form
\(\kappa\) on its Lie algebra \(\mathfrak g\).  The principal chiral model has
field \(U:\Sigma\to G\), current \(j_\mu=U^{-1}\partial_\mu U\), and action
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm PCM}[U]
  =
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma\dd^2x\,\kappa(j_\mu,j_\mu).
  \label{eq:pcm-action}
\end{equation}
The global symmetry is \(G_L\times G_R\), acting by
\[
  U\mapsto g_L\,U\,g_R^{-1}.
\]
The equations of motion and Maurer--Cartan identity in light-cone coordinates
are
\begin{equation}
  \partial_+j_-+\partial_-j_+=0,
  \qquad
  \partial_+j_- -\partial_-j_+ +[j_+,j_-]=0 .
  \label{eq:pcm-eom-maurer-cartan}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Principal-chiral Lax equivalence]
\label{prop:pcm-lax-equivalence}
Define
\begin{equation}
  L_+(\zeta)=\frac{j_+}{1-\zeta},\qquad
  L_-(\zeta)=\frac{j_-}{1+\zeta}.
  \label{eq:pcm-lax-pair}
\end{equation}
Then the flatness condition
\[
  \partial_+L_-(\zeta)-\partial_-L_+(\zeta)+[L_+(\zeta),L_-(\zeta)]=0
\]
for all complex \(\zeta\) away from \(\pm1\) is equivalent to the two
relations in \eqref{eq:pcm-eom-maurer-cartan}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Multiplying the curvature by \(1-\zeta^2\) gives
\[
  (1-\zeta)\partial_+j_-
  -(1+\zeta)\partial_-j_+
  +[j_+,j_-]
  =
  \bigl(\partial_+j_- -\partial_-j_+ +[j_+,j_-]\bigr)
  -\zeta(\partial_+j_-+\partial_-j_+).
\]
Vanishing for all \(\zeta\) is therefore equivalent to the vanishing of the
constant and linear coefficients, which are precisely the Maurer--Cartan
identity and the equation of motion.
\end{proof}

The Wess--Zumino deformation is defined by extending \(U\) to a three-manifold
\(B\) with \(\partial B=\Sigma\) and setting
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma[U]
  =
  \frac1{24\pi}\int_B
  \kappa(U^{-1}\dd U,[U^{-1}\dd U,U^{-1}\dd U]).
  \label{eq:wz-functional}
\end{equation}
The quantum functional
\begin{equation}
  S_{k,\lambda}[U]
  =
  \frac1{2\lambda^2}\int_\Sigma\kappa(j_\mu,j_\mu)
  +\ii k\,\Gamma[U]
  \label{eq:pcm-wz-action}
\end{equation}
is single-valued only when the normalization of \(\kappa\) and the integer
level \(k\) make the change of \(k\Gamma\) under a different extension a
multiple of \(2\pi\).

\begin{proposition}[Polyakov--Wiegmann identity in the chapter normalization]
\label{prop:polyakov-wiegmann-identity}
Let \(U,V:\Sigma\to G\) be smooth maps that admit extensions to a common
three-manifold \(B\) with \(\partial B=\Sigma\).  Put
\[
  \Theta_U=U^{-1}\dd U,\qquad
  \overline\Theta_V=\dd V\,V^{-1}.
\]
With \(\Gamma\) normalized as in \eqref{eq:wz-functional},
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma[UV]
  =
  \Gamma[U]+\Gamma[V]
  -\frac1{4\pi}\int_\Sigma \kappa(\Theta_U,\overline\Theta_V),
  \label{eq:polyakov-wiegmann-identity}
\end{equation}
up to the integral periods already fixed by the level quantization of the WZ
term.  Here \(\kappa(\Theta_U,\overline\Theta_V)\) denotes the scalar
two-form obtained by wedging the one-forms and pairing their Lie-algebra
values by \(\kappa\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let
\[
  H(g)
  =
  \frac1{24\pi}\kappa(g^{-1}\dd g,[g^{-1}\dd g,g^{-1}\dd g])
\]
be the closed three-form integrated in \(\Gamma[g]\).  The bracket of
Lie-algebra-valued one-forms is the wedge bracket, so that the displayed
normalization agrees with \eqref{eq:wz-functional}.  Since
\[
  (UV)^{-1}\dd(UV)
  =
  V^{-1}\Theta_UV+V^{-1}\dd V ,
\]
invariance of \(\kappa\) and the Maurer--Cartan equations
\[
  \dd\Theta_U+\frac12[\Theta_U,\Theta_U]=0,
  \qquad
  \dd\overline\Theta_V-\frac12[\overline\Theta_V,\overline\Theta_V]=0
\]
give the differential identity
\begin{equation}
  H(UV)
  =
  H(U)+H(V)
  -\frac1{4\pi}\dd\,\kappa(\Theta_U,\overline\Theta_V).
  \label{eq:polyakov-wiegmann-three-form-identity}
\end{equation}
To see the coefficient, expand the cubic expression for \(H(UV)\): the pure
\(\Theta_U\) and pure \(V^{-1}\dd V\) terms give \(H(U)\) and \(H(V)\), and
the three mixed cyclic placements combine with the two Maurer--Cartan
equations into the exterior derivative in
\eqref{eq:polyakov-wiegmann-three-form-identity}.  Integrating this identity
over \(B\) and applying Stokes' theorem gives
\eqref{eq:polyakov-wiegmann-identity}.  Changing the extensions shifts the
three bulk terms by integral periods; the quantization condition on \(k\) is
exactly the condition that \(\exp(-S_{k,\lambda})\) be independent of that
choice.
\end{proof}

At the conformal WZW point the chiral currents generate
the affine algebra \(\widehat{\mathfrak g}_k\), and the Sugawara stress tensor
has central charge
\begin{equation}
  c=\frac{k\,\dim\mathfrak g}{k+h^\vee},
  \label{eq:wzw-central-charge-crosslink}
\end{equation}
where \(h^\vee\) is the dual Coxeter number.  Volume~V develops the affine
and VOA side.  The role here is to identify WZW models as fixed points and
endpoints in the sigma-model family, not as isolated algebraic examples.

\section{Nonabelian Bosonization and Chiral Gross--Neveu Bridges}
\label{sec:integrable-nonabelian-bosonization-gn-bridges}

The relation between sigma models, WZW models, and fermionic Gross--Neveu
models should be stated at the level of current algebras, not as a slogan
about replacing fermions by bosons.  Let
\[
  \psi_{L\,a}^{\ i}(x^+),\qquad
  \psi_{R\,a}^{\ i}(x^-),
  \qquad
  a=1,\ldots,N_c,\quad i=1,\ldots,N_f,
\]
be free complex chiral fermions.  The index \(a\) is acted on by
\(SU(N_c)\), the index \(i\) by \(SU(N_f)\).  The left-moving bilinears
\[
  J_{c}^{A}=:\psi_{L\,a}^{\ i\,\dagger}(T_c^A)^a{}_{b}\psi_{L\,b}^{\ i}:,
  \qquad
  J_{f}^{B}=:\psi_{L\,a}^{\ i\,\dagger}(T_f^B)^i{}_{j}\psi_{L\,a}^{\ j}:,
  \qquad
  J_{u}=:\psi_{L\,a}^{\ i\,\dagger}\psi_{L\,a}^{\ i}:
\]
generate affine
\[
  \widehat{\mathfrak{su}}(N_c)_{N_f}\oplus
  \widehat{\mathfrak{su}}(N_f)_{N_c}\oplus \widehat{\mathfrak u}(1)
\]
inside the free-fermion chiral algebra.  The level is the number of spectator
fermion flavors in the trace of the short-distance contraction: for example,
the color-current two-point singularity receives \(N_f\) identical flavor
contractions and hence has level \(N_f\).

\begin{proposition}[Central-charge check for nonabelian bosonization]
\label{prop:nonabelian-bosonization-central-charge}
With the current normalization in which
\[
  c\bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak{su}}(N)_k\bigr)
  =
  \frac{k(N^2-1)}{k+N},
\]
the chiral algebra generated by the bilinears above has the same central
charge as \(N_cN_f\) free complex left-moving fermions:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{N_f(N_c^2-1)}{N_f+N_c}
  +
  \frac{N_c(N_f^2-1)}{N_c+N_f}
  +1
  =
  N_cN_f .
  \label{eq:nonabelian-bosonization-central-charge-identity}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Bring the two nonabelian terms to the common denominator \(N_c+N_f\):
\[
  \frac{
    N_fN_c^2-N_f+N_cN_f^2-N_c
  }{N_c+N_f}
  =
  \frac{N_cN_f(N_c+N_f)-(N_c+N_f)}{N_c+N_f}
  =
  N_cN_f-1 .
\]
Adding the \(\widehat{\mathfrak u}(1)\) boson gives \(N_cN_f\).  The identity
is only the central-charge compatibility check; the operator equivalence also
requires equality of the affine OPEs, the stress tensor, and the compact
\(U(1)\) radius.
\end{proof}

For \(N_f=1\) the flavor affine factor is absent and a set of \(N_c\) complex
fermions decomposes into \(SU(N_c)_1\) WZW data plus a compact \(U(1)\) boson.
Perturbing the WZW model by the marginal current-current operator
\begin{equation}
  \delta S
  =
  \lambda\int\dd^2x\,
  \sum_A J_L^A(x^+)J_R^A(x^-)
  \label{eq:nonabelian-current-current-gn-perturbation}
\end{equation}
is the bosonic current-algebra presentation of the chiral Gross--Neveu
family, once the \(U(1)\) sector and discrete quotient data have been fixed.
The exact massive \(SU(N)\) chiral Gross--Neveu scattering theory is then a
bootstrap datum for the current-current perturbation, while the WZW model is
the ultraviolet fixed point described by the affine algebra.  The statement is
not that every fermionic model is identical to a principal chiral model; the
dictionary is a statement about local current algebras and perturbations with
specified global quotients.  In the abelian rank-one subcase this reduces to
the sine-Gordon/massive-Thirring bridge of
Chapter~\ref{ch:integrable-sine-gordon-thirring-affine-toda}; in the
nonabelian case the replacement of the compact boson by a WZW field is the
essential new datum.

\section{SU(N) Bootstrap Spectrum and Matrix Blocks}
\label{sec:su-n-bootstrap-spectrum-pcm-gn}

The exact scattering descriptions of the \(SU(N)\) chiral Gross--Neveu and
principal-chiral families contain a common representation-theoretic skeleton.
Let \(V=\mathbb C^N\) be the defining representation of \(SU(N)\), and write
\[
  V_a=\wedge^a V,\qquad a=1,\ldots,N-1 .
\]
The conjugate of \(V_a\) is \(V_{N-a}\).  A bootstrap spectrum datum of
sine-law type assigns masses
\begin{equation}
  m_a=M\,\frac{\sin(\pi a/N)}{\sin(\pi/N)},
  \qquad
  a=1,\ldots,N-1 ,
  \label{eq:su-n-sine-mass-spectrum}
\end{equation}
for a single positive mass scale \(M=m_1\).  In the chiral Gross--Neveu
family these multiplets are the antisymmetric tensor particles.  In the
principal chiral model the corresponding particles carry left and right
indices, with one common convention taking the rank-\(a\) multiplet to
transform in \(V_a\otimes V_a^\ast\) under \(SU(N)_L\times SU(N)_R\).  The
choice of the global form of the symmetry and the quotient by common centers
is part of the family datum; it affects which line operators and twisted
sectors exist, although not the local sine-mass formula itself.

\begin{proposition}[Fusing-angle consistency of the sine spectrum]
\label{prop:su-n-sine-mass-fusing-angle}
For \(1\le a,b\) and \(a+b<N\), the mass formula
\eqref{eq:su-n-sine-mass-spectrum} is compatible with a direct-channel bound
state \(V_a\otimes V_b\to V_{a+b}\) at rapidity
\[
  \theta=\ii u_{ab}^{a+b},\qquad
  u_{ab}^{a+b}=\frac{\pi(a+b)}{N}.
  \label{eq:su-n-fusing-angle}
\]
Indeed,
\begin{equation}
  m_{a+b}^2
  =
  m_a^2+m_b^2+2m_am_b\cos u_{ab}^{a+b}.
  \label{eq:su-n-sine-mass-fusion-identity}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Set \(A=\pi a/N\), \(B=\pi b/N\), and suppress the common positive factor
\(M/\sin(\pi/N)\).  The required identity is
\[
  \sin^2(A+B)
  =
  \sin^2A+\sin^2B+2\sin A\sin B\cos(A+B).
  \label{eq:su-n-trig-fusion-identity}
\]
Using \(\cos(A+B)=\cos A\cos B-\sin A\sin B\), the right-hand side becomes
\[
  \sin^2A+\sin^2B+2\sin A\sin B\cos A\cos B
  -2\sin^2A\sin^2B .
\]
Since
\[
  \sin^2(A+B)
  =
  \sin^2A\cos^2B+\cos^2A\sin^2B
  +2\sin A\sin B\cos A\cos B ,
\]
and \(\cos^2A=1-\sin^2A\), \(\cos^2B=1-\sin^2B\), the two expressions agree.
The restriction \(a+b<N\) selects the antisymmetric tensor before conjugation;
fusion channels with \(a+b\ge N\) are equivalently described after replacing
\(V_c^\ast\) by \(V_{N-c}\) and keeping track of crossed-channel conventions.
\end{proof}

The fundamental rational \(SU(N)\) matrix block acts on \(V\otimes V\).  Let
\[
  P(v\otimes w)=w\otimes v,\qquad
  \lambda_N=\frac{2\pi}{N},
\]
and define
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal R_N(\theta)
  =
  \frac{\theta\,I-\ii\lambda_N P}{\theta-\ii\lambda_N}.
  \label{eq:su-n-rational-r-block}
\end{equation}
This normalization makes the symmetric-channel eigenvalue equal to \(1\), and
the antisymmetric-channel eigenvalue equal to
\[
  \frac{\theta+\ii\lambda_N}{\theta-\ii\lambda_N}.
\]
Thus the pole of the matrix block at \(\theta=\ii\lambda_N\) lies in the
antisymmetric channel, matching the rank-two entry of
\eqref{eq:su-n-sine-mass-spectrum}.  The scalar factor multiplying
\(\mathcal R_N\) is not optional: it fixes crossing, removes or adds CDD
ambiguities, and supplies the complete pole-residue normalization.  The
matrix block records the tensor part of the exact S-matrix; the scalar
minimality and the regulator-to-bootstrap matching are additional data.

\begin{proposition}[Rational-block unitarity and Yang--Baxter equation]
\label{prop:su-n-rational-block-ybe}
The block \(\mathcal R_N\) obeys
\[
  \mathcal R_N(\theta)\mathcal R_N(-\theta)=I
\]
as an operator on \(V\otimes V\).  Its unnormalized numerator
\[
  R_N(\theta)=\theta I-\ii\lambda_NP
\]
obeys the Yang--Baxter equation
\[
  R_{12}(\theta)R_{13}(\theta+\phi)R_{23}(\phi)
  =
  R_{23}(\phi)R_{13}(\theta+\phi)R_{12}(\theta)
  \label{eq:su-n-rational-ybe}
\]
on \(V^{\otimes3}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Unitarity is immediate from \(P^2=I\):
\[
  (\theta I-\ii\lambda_NP)(-\theta I-\ii\lambda_NP)
  =
  -(\theta^2+\lambda_N^2)I
  =
  (\theta-\ii\lambda_N)(-\theta-\ii\lambda_N)I .
\]
For the Yang--Baxter equation, expand both sides of
\eqref{eq:su-n-rational-ybe} as polynomials in \(\theta,\phi\) and
\(\lambda_N\).  The constant, linear, and quadratic terms reduce to the
relations of the symmetric group acting on tensor factors:
\[
  P_{12}^2=P_{23}^2=I,\qquad
  P_{12}P_{23}P_{12}=P_{23}P_{12}P_{23}.
\]
The mixed \(P_{13}\) terms are written as
\(P_{13}=P_{12}P_{23}P_{12}=P_{23}P_{12}P_{23}\).  After this substitution the
two expanded polynomials coincide coefficient by coefficient.  The
calculation companion checks this equality by finite-dimensional matrix
multiplication for representative \(N\) and rapidities.
\end{proof}

\section{SU(N) Scalar Factors and CDD Conventions}
\label{sec:su-n-scalar-factors-cdd}

The rational matrix block is only the tensor part of the scattering datum.
The scalar factor fixes the analytic normalization.  Let
\[
  \mathcal P=\{\theta\in\mathbb C:0<\operatorname{Im}\theta<\pi\}
\]
be the physical rapidity strip for equal-mass relativistic scattering.  In a
scalar channel, unitarity is the meromorphic identity
\[
  X(\theta)X(-\theta)=1,
\]
and self-conjugate crossing is the identity \(X(\theta)=X(\ii\pi-\theta)\).
For non-self-conjugate multiplets, crossing relates a particle-particle
amplitude to a particle-antiparticle amplitude; the scalar factor must be
specified together with the charge-conjugation convention.

\begin{definition}[CDD factor in one scalar channel]
\label{def:cdd-factor-one-channel}
A scalar CDD factor is a meromorphic function \(C(\theta)\), nonzero on the
real rapidity axis, such that
\[
  C(\theta)C(-\theta)=1
\]
and such that the appropriate crossing equation of the channel is preserved.
It is an allowed ambiguity only after its zeros and poles in
\(\mathcal P\) have been declared either absent or assigned to specified
bound states, Coleman--Thun singularities, or other explicitly stated
on-shell mechanisms.  A scalar factor is called minimal relative to a listed
bootstrap spectrum when it has no additional zeros or poles in
\(\mathcal P\) beyond those listed.
\end{definition}

The elementary self-conjugate block
\begin{equation}
  C_\alpha(\theta)
  =
  \frac{\sinh\theta+\ii\sin\alpha}
       {\sinh\theta-\ii\sin\alpha}
  \label{eq:elementary-cdd-block}
\end{equation}
obeys \(C_\alpha(\theta)C_\alpha(-\theta)=1\) and
\(C_\alpha(\ii\pi-\theta)=C_\alpha(\theta)\).  Its denominator vanishes at
\(\theta=\ii u\) when \(\sin u=\sin\alpha\).  Thus, for
\(0<\alpha<\pi\), it has physical-strip singularities at
\(u=\alpha\) and \(u=\pi-\alpha\), with coincidences at
\(\alpha=\pi/2\).  Such a block is therefore not a harmless convention unless
those singularities have been assigned.  This is the reason that ``multiply
by a CDD factor'' is not a mathematical conclusion; it is new scattering
data.

For the \(SU(N)\) chiral Gross--Neveu family, \(N\ge3\), put
\[
  x=\frac{\theta}{2\pi\ii},\qquad \lambda_N=\frac{2\pi}{N},
\]
and define the rank-one scalar transmission eigenvalue
\begin{equation}
  A_N(\theta)
  =
  -
  \frac{
    \Gamma(1-x)\Gamma(1-\frac1N+x)}
  {
    \Gamma(1+x)\Gamma(1-\frac1N-x)}
  .
  \label{eq:su-n-cgn-a-scalar}
\end{equation}
In the Zamolodchikov--Faddeev component convention with outgoing order
reversed,
\begin{equation}
  S_{\alpha\beta}^{\delta\gamma}(\theta)
  =
  \delta_\alpha^\gamma\delta_\beta^\delta\,b_N(\theta)
  +
  \delta_\alpha^\delta\delta_\beta^\gamma\,c_N(\theta),
  \qquad
  c_N(\theta)=-\frac{\ii\lambda_N}{\theta}b_N(\theta),
  \label{eq:su-n-cgn-component-smatrix}
\end{equation}
with
\[
  A_N(\theta)=b_N(\theta)+c_N(\theta),
  \qquad
  b_N(\theta)=\frac{A_N(\theta)}{1-\ii\lambda_N/\theta}.
\]
Equivalently, if the outgoing permutation is pulled out explicitly, this is
\[
  S_{\rm CGN}^{(1,1)}(\theta)
  =
  A_N(\theta)\,P\,\mathcal R_N(\theta),
  \label{eq:chiral-gn-fundamental-smatrix-form}
\]
with \(\mathcal R_N\) defined in \eqref{eq:su-n-rational-r-block}.  The
matrix pole at \(\theta=\ii\lambda_N\) is then the first
antisymmetric bound-state pole, while \(A_N\) itself supplies the minimal
gamma-function normalization in this convention.

\begin{proposition}[Gross--Neveu scalar unitarity ledger]
\label{prop:su-n-cgn-scalar-unitarity-ledger}
As meromorphic identities in \(\theta\),
\[
  A_N(\theta)A_N(-\theta)=1,
\]
and the component matrix \eqref{eq:su-n-cgn-component-smatrix} obeys
\[
  S(\theta)S(-\theta)=I
\]
on \(V\otimes V\), away from its poles.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first identity follows immediately from
\eqref{eq:su-n-cgn-a-scalar}: replacing \(x\) by \(-x\) interchanges the two
gamma-function products, while the two overall signs multiply to \(1\).
For the component matrix, write \(S=bP+cI\) with
\(c=-(\ii\lambda_N/\theta)b\).  At \(-\theta\),
\[
  c(-\theta)=\frac{\ii\lambda_N}{\theta}b(-\theta).
\]
Since \(A=b(1-\ii\lambda_N/\theta)\) and
\[
  A(-\theta)=b(-\theta)(1+\ii\lambda_N/\theta),
\]
the scalar identity gives
\[
  b(\theta)b(-\theta)\left(1+\frac{\lambda_N^2}{\theta^2}\right)=1.
\]
Using \(P^2=I\),
\[
  (bP+cI)(b_-P+c_-I)
  =
  (bb_-+cc_-)I+(bc_-+cb_-)P,
\]
where \(b_-=b(-\theta)\) and \(c_-=c(-\theta)\).  The \(P\)-coefficient
vanishes, and the \(I\)-coefficient is the scalar displayed above.  This
proves matrix unitarity.
\end{proof}

For the \(SU(N)\) principal chiral model, the fundamental particle carries
left and right indices.  It is convenient to distinguish the normalized block
\(\mathcal R_N\) from the unnormalized rational block
\[
  \widetilde{\mathcal R}_N(\theta)
  =
  I-\frac{\ii\lambda_N}{\theta}P
  =
  \frac{\theta-\ii\lambda_N}{\theta}\,\mathcal R_N(\theta).
\]
One standard particle-particle bootstrap convention is
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm PCM}^{(1,1)}(\theta)
  =
  W_N(\theta)\,
  \widetilde{\mathcal R}_{N,L}(\theta)\otimes
  \widetilde{\mathcal R}_{N,R}(\theta),
  \label{eq:pcm-fundamental-smatrix-unnormalized}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  W_N(\theta)
  =
  \frac{\sinh(\theta/2-\pi\ii/N)}
       {\sinh(\theta/2+\pi\ii/N)}
  \left[
  \frac{\Gamma(1-x)\Gamma(x-\frac1N)}
       {\Gamma(1-x-\frac1N)\Gamma(x)}
  \right]^2,
  \qquad x=\frac{\theta}{2\pi\ii}.
  \label{eq:pcm-wiegmann-scalar}
\end{equation}
In the normalized block convention used in
\eqref{eq:pcm-fundamental-smatrix-matrix-part} below, the scalar factor is
\begin{equation}
  X_{\rm PCM}(\theta)
  =
  W_N(\theta)\left(\frac{\theta-\ii\lambda_N}{\theta}\right)^2.
  \label{eq:pcm-normalized-scalar}
\end{equation}
Thus
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm PCM}^{(1,1)}(\theta)
  =
  X_{\rm PCM}(\theta)\,
  \mathcal R_{N,L}(\theta)\otimes\mathcal R_{N,R}(\theta).
  \label{eq:pcm-fundamental-smatrix-matrix-part}
\end{equation}
The sine-law tower \eqref{eq:su-n-sine-mass-spectrum} is generated by fusion
at \eqref{eq:su-n-fusing-angle}; higher-rank scattering is obtained by
projecting tensor products onto the corresponding antisymmetric
representations and taking residues at the listed poles.  The CDD convention
is part of the statement: multiplying \(W_N\) or \(X_{\rm PCM}\) by any
additional factor satisfying Definition~\ref{def:cdd-factor-one-channel}
changes the bootstrap theory unless the extra singularity data are also
specified.

\begin{proposition}[Principal-chiral scalar unitarity ledger]
\label{prop:pcm-scalar-unitarity-ledger}
The scalar factor \eqref{eq:pcm-wiegmann-scalar} obeys
\begin{equation}
  W_N(\theta)W_N(-\theta)
  =
  \left(1+\frac{\lambda_N^2}{\theta^2}\right)^{-2}.
  \label{eq:pcm-wiegmann-unitarity-ledger}
\end{equation}
Consequently \(X_{\rm PCM}(\theta)X_{\rm PCM}(-\theta)=1\), and
\eqref{eq:pcm-fundamental-smatrix-matrix-part} is unitary on the real
rapidity axis away from poles.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(a=1/N\) and \(x=\theta/(2\pi\ii)\).  The hyperbolic prefactor in
\eqref{eq:pcm-wiegmann-scalar} has product \(1\) under
\(\theta\mapsto-\theta\), because \(\sinh\) is odd.  For the gamma ratio
\[
  G(x)=
  \frac{\Gamma(1-x)\Gamma(x-a)}
       {\Gamma(1-x-a)\Gamma(x)},
\]
use \(\Gamma(1+x)=x\Gamma(x)\),
\(\Gamma(1-x)=-x\Gamma(-x)\),
\(\Gamma(1-x-a)=(-x-a)\Gamma(-x-a)\), and
\(\Gamma(1+x-a)=(x-a)\Gamma(x-a)\).  These identities give
\[
  G(x)G(-x)=\frac{x^2}{(x-a)(x+a)}.
\]
Since \(\lambda_N=2\pi a\) and \(\theta=2\pi\ii x\),
\[
  1+\frac{\lambda_N^2}{\theta^2}
  =
  1-\frac{a^2}{x^2}
  =
  \frac{(x-a)(x+a)}{x^2}.
\]
Squaring the gamma-ratio product proves
\eqref{eq:pcm-wiegmann-unitarity-ledger}.  Multiplying by
\[
  \left(\frac{\theta-\ii\lambda_N}{\theta}\right)^2
  \left(\frac{-\theta-\ii\lambda_N}{-\theta}\right)^2
  =
  \left(1+\frac{\lambda_N^2}{\theta^2}\right)^2
\]
then gives \(X_{\rm PCM}(\theta)X_{\rm PCM}(-\theta)=1\).  The normalized
matrix blocks are unitary by
Proposition~\ref{prop:su-n-rational-block-ybe}, so the full
particle-particle PCM matrix is unitary.
\end{proof}

Equations \eqref{eq:chiral-gn-fundamental-smatrix-form} and
\eqref{eq:pcm-fundamental-smatrix-matrix-part} are exact bootstrap data with
the displayed scalar convention.  They become statements about the continuum
sigma models only after the ultraviolet definitions of the preceding sections
are matched to the factorizing QFT: one must construct the Hilbert space,
local or graded-local fields, conserved currents, Haag--Ruelle scattering
states, and the map between the regulator mass scale and the bootstrap mass
coordinate.

\section{Symmetric-Space and Deformed-Sphere Families}
\label{sec:integrable-symmetric-super-sausage}

Many integrable sigma models are organized by a homogeneous target \(G/H\).
Let \(\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\oplus\mathfrak m\) be a reductive split and
write
\[
  g^{-1}\dd g=A+P,\qquad A\in\mathfrak h,\quad P\in\mathfrak m .
\]
The bosonic coset action is
\begin{equation}
  S_{G/H}[g]
  =
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma\kappa(P_\mu,P_\mu),
  \label{eq:coset-sigma-action}
\end{equation}
with local \(H\) redundancy \(g(x)\mapsto g(x)h(x)\).  If \(G/H\) is a
symmetric space,
\[
  [\mathfrak h,\mathfrak h]\subset\mathfrak h,\qquad
  [\mathfrak h,\mathfrak m]\subset\mathfrak m,\qquad
  [\mathfrak m,\mathfrak m]\subset\mathfrak h,
\]
the equations admit a Lax representation that is slightly different from the
principal-chiral one because the \(H\) gauge connection must remain in the
auxiliary connection.  Write \(A_\pm\) and \(P_\pm\) for the light-cone
components of \(A\) and \(P\), and let
\[
  D_\pm X=\partial_\pm X+[A_\pm,X]
\]
be the \(H\)-covariant derivative on \(\mathfrak m\)-valued fields.

\begin{proposition}[Symmetric-space Lax equivalence]
\label{prop:symmetric-space-lax-equivalence}
For the bosonic symmetric-space action \eqref{eq:coset-sigma-action}, the
Euler--Lagrange equation together with the two \(\mathfrak h\)- and
\(\mathfrak m\)-components of the Maurer--Cartan identity is equivalent to
flatness, for every \(\zeta\in\mathbb C^\times\), of the connection
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal L_+(\zeta)=A_+ +\zeta P_+,\qquad
  \mathcal L_-(\zeta)=A_- +\zeta^{-1}P_- .
  \label{eq:symmetric-space-lax-pair}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Maurer--Cartan equation for \(g^{-1}\dd g=A+P\) splits, using the
symmetric-space brackets, into
\begin{align}
  F_{+-}(A)+[P_+,P_-]&=0,
  \label{eq:symmetric-space-mc-h}\\
  D_+P_- -D_-P_+&=0 .
  \label{eq:symmetric-space-mc-m}
\end{align}
Varying \(g\) by a compactly supported \(\mathfrak m\)-valued field gives the
field equation
\begin{equation}
  D_+P_-+D_-P_+=0 .
  \label{eq:symmetric-space-eom}
\end{equation}
Conversely, \eqref{eq:symmetric-space-mc-m} and
\eqref{eq:symmetric-space-eom} are equivalent to
\[
  D_+P_-=0,\qquad D_-P_+=0 .
\]
The curvature of \eqref{eq:symmetric-space-lax-pair} is
\[
  \partial_+\mathcal L_- -\partial_-\mathcal L_+
  +[\mathcal L_+,\mathcal L_-]
  =
  F_{+-}(A)+[P_+,P_-]
  +\zeta^{-1}D_+P_- -\zeta D_-P_+ .
\]
If \eqref{eq:symmetric-space-mc-h}, \eqref{eq:symmetric-space-mc-m}, and
\eqref{eq:symmetric-space-eom} hold, this curvature vanishes for all
\(\zeta\).  If the curvature vanishes for all
\(\zeta\in\mathbb C^\times\), the coefficients of \(\zeta^{-1}\),
\(\zeta^0\), and \(\zeta\) give \(D_+P_-=0\),
\(F_{+-}(A)+[P_+,P_-]=0\), and \(D_-P_+=0\), which imply the
Maurer--Cartan identities and the equation of motion.  This proves the
equivalence.
\end{proof}

Projective space is the example
\[
  \mathbb{CP}^{N-1}=SU(N)/S(U(N-1)\times U(1)).
\]

\section{Supergroup and Supersphere Sigma-Model Families}
\label{sec:supergroup-supersphere-sigma-models}

Supergroup sigma models require the same locally super-ringed-space
discipline as fermionic path integrals.  A supertarget is not a set of
ordinary maps.  Locally it is described by even coordinate functions and odd
coordinate generators in a structure sheaf; the odd coordinates are integrated
by Berezin rules, not by a Borel measure.  This distinction matters in
sigma-model examples because cancellations in beta functions are supertrace
cancellations, not cancellations between two positive measures.

Let \(E=E_{\bar0}\oplus E_{\bar1}\) be a finite-dimensional real super vector
space, with
\[
  \operatorname{sdim}E=\dim E_{\bar0}-\dim E_{\bar1}.
\]
For \(m,n\ge0\), the orthosymplectic supersphere
\[
  S^{m-1|2n}
  =
  OSp(m|2n)/OSp(m-1|2n)
\]
is the supermanifold cut out in \(\mathbb R^{m|2n}\) by
\[
  X\cdot X=1.
\]
Here \(X\cdot X\) uses a positive quadratic form on the even coordinates and
a nondegenerate symplectic pairing on the \(2n\) odd coordinates.  The
Euclidean sigma-model functional is
\begin{equation}
  S_{S^{m-1|2n}}[X]
  =
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma\dd^2x\,
  \partial_\mu X\cdot\partial_\mu X ,
  \qquad X\cdot X=1,
  \label{eq:supersphere-sigma-action}
\end{equation}
understood as a local expression in the supercoordinate sheaf.  The ordinary
round sphere is recovered at \(n=0\), \(m=N\).

\begin{proposition}[One-loop supersphere curvature ledger]
\label{prop:supersphere-one-loop-curvature-ledger}
In the standard constant-curvature normalization of
\eqref{eq:supersphere-sigma-action}, the Ricci tensor entering the one-loop
sigma-model beta tensor is
\[
  \operatorname{Ric}_{ab}
  =
  (m-2n-2)h_{ab}.
  \label{eq:supersphere-ricci-coefficient}
\]
Thus the scalar coupling has leading perturbative running
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd g}{\dd\mu}
  =
  -\frac{m-2n-2}{2\pi}g^3+O(g^5).
  \label{eq:supersphere-leading-beta}
\]
The family \(S^{2n+1|2n}=OSp(2n+2|2n)/OSp(2n+1|2n)\) has vanishing one-loop
coefficient.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a bosonic round sphere \(S^{m-1}\), the constant-curvature tensor gives
\(\operatorname{Ric}=(m-2)h\): the tangent space has \(m-1\) dimensions and
the Ricci contraction counts all tangent directions except the distinguished
one in the two-plane used by the curvature operator.  In the supersphere,
the same normal-coordinate computation uses the graded contraction
\[
  \operatorname{Ric}_{ab}
  =
  (-1)^{|c|}R^{c}{}_{acb},
  \label{eq:graded-ricci-contraction}
\]
where \(|c|=0\) on even tangent directions and \(|c|=1\) on odd tangent
directions.  The tangent superdimension is
\[
  \operatorname{sdim}T S^{m-1|2n}=m-1-2n.
\]
The same constant-curvature contraction therefore replaces the bosonic count
\((m-1)-1\) by the graded count
\[
  (m-1-2n)-1=m-2n-2,
\]
which gives \eqref{eq:supersphere-ricci-coefficient}.  Substitution into the
one-loop sigma-model beta tensor
\[
  \mu\frac{\dd h_{ab}}{\dd\mu}
  =
  \frac1{2\pi}\operatorname{Ric}_{ab}+O(R^2)
\]
and \(h_{ab}=g^{-2}h^{\rm unit}_{ab}\) gives
\eqref{eq:supersphere-leading-beta}.  Setting \(m=2n+2\) gives zero.
\end{proof}

The projective supermodel is the quotient analogue.  Let
\[
  Z=(z^1,\ldots,z^{p+1};\eta^1,\ldots,\eta^q)
\]
be a complex supervector, with \(z^i\) even and \(\eta^\alpha\) odd.  The
superprojective target
\[
  \mathbb{CP}^{p|q}
\]
is obtained by imposing a nonzero homogeneous coordinate and quotienting by
the even \(\mathbb C^\times\) rescaling.  In the unitary coordinate used for
the sigma model, one writes a \(U(1)\) redundancy
\[
  Z(x)\mapsto \ee^{\ii\alpha(x)}Z(x),\qquad
  A_\mu\mapsto A_\mu+\partial_\mu\alpha,
\]
and
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathbb{CP}^{p|q}}[Z,A]
  =
  \frac1{g^2}\int_\Sigma\dd^2x\, (D_\mu Z)^\dagger D_\mu Z
  +\ii\frac{\theta}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma F_A ,
  \qquad
  D_\mu Z=(\partial_\mu-\ii A_\mu)Z,
  \label{eq:projective-supermodel-action}
\end{equation}
with the constraint \(Z^\dagger Z=1\) interpreted in the superalgebra of
local functions.  The gauge-invariant superprojector is
\[
  \mathcal P=Z Z^\dagger,\qquad
  \mathcal P^2=\mathcal P,\qquad
  \operatorname{str}\mathcal P=1,
\]
where \(\operatorname{str}\) is the supertrace.

\begin{proposition}[Projective-supermodel one-loop ledger]
\label{prop:projective-supermodel-one-loop-ledger}
For the Fubini--Study supermetric on \(\mathbb{CP}^{p|q}\), the one-loop
Ricci coefficient is
\[
  \operatorname{Ric}=(p+1-q)\,h_{\rm FS}.
  \label{eq:projective-super-ricci-coefficient}
\]
Consequently \(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1|N}\) has zero one-loop beta coefficient in
this normalization, while the ordinary \(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\) coefficient is
\(N\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In a holomorphic coordinate patch, the Fubini--Study Kähler potential is the
even function
\[
  K=\log(1+w^\dagger w+\eta^\dagger\eta),
\]
where \(w\) has \(p\) even components and \(\eta\) has \(q\) odd components.
The Ricci form of a Kähler supermetric is computed by the Berezinian:
\[
  \rho=-\ii\,\partial\bar\partial\log\operatorname{Ber}(g_{A\bar B}).
  \label{eq:super-kahler-ricci-berezinian}
\]
For the Fubini--Study metric, the Berezinian is proportional to
\[
  (1+w^\dagger w+\eta^\dagger\eta)^{-(p+1-q)}.
\]
The exponent is the ordinary projective homogeneous-coordinate count
\(p+1\) minus the number \(q\) of odd homogeneous coordinates, because the
odd block enters the Berezinian inversely.  Applying
\(-\ii\partial\bar\partial\log\) gives
\eqref{eq:projective-super-ricci-coefficient}.  The two special cases follow
by setting \(q=0\), \(p=N-1\), and by setting \(p=N-1\), \(q=N\).
\end{proof}

Finally, let \(G\) be a Lie supergroup whose Lie superalgebra
\(\mathfrak g\) carries a nondegenerate even invariant bilinear form
\(\kappa\).  The principal supergroup sigma-model action is
\begin{equation}
  S_{\rm PSG}[U]
  =
  \frac1{2g^2}\int_\Sigma\dd^2x\,
  \kappa(U^{-1}\partial_\mu U,U^{-1}\partial_\mu U).
  \label{eq:principal-supergroup-action}
\end{equation}
The one-loop counterterm is proportional to the Killing form
\[
  K(X,Y)=\operatorname{str}_{\mathfrak g}
  (\operatorname{ad}_X\operatorname{ad}_Y).
  \label{eq:supergroup-killing-form}
\]
Thus supergroups with \(K=0\), such as the projective special linear family
\(\mathfrak{psl}(N|N)\) and the dual-Coxeter-zero orthosymplectic family
\(\mathfrak{osp}(2n+2|2n)\), are distinguished perturbatively.  This is a
statement about the first local counterterm in a regularized superfield
path integral.  Construction of the Hilbert space, locality, and exact
factorized scattering theory requires additional data.

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Supercoset status]
\label{ca:supercoset-status}
The supersphere actions, projective supermodels, and principal supergroup
actions above are ultraviolet family data.  Their perturbative cancellations
explain why \(OSp(2n+2|2n)\), \(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1|N}\), and
\(\mathfrak{psl}(N|N)\)-type examples recur in the integrable and logarithmic
two-dimensional literature.  A theorem-level identification with a local QFT
requires the same ingredients demanded in
Open Problem~\ref{op:constructive-factorizing-on-gn}: a regulator, Hilbert
space or graded state space, local observables, conserved currents, and
matching between ultraviolet and bootstrap coordinates.  For supertargets,
one must additionally specify the real form, the positivity or graded
replacement of positivity, and the interpretation of logarithmic sectors if
Jordan blocks appear.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\section{Deformed-Sphere and Sausage Family}
\label{sec:deformed-sphere-sausage-family}

The sausage family should be treated as a controlled deformation of the round
\(S^2\) sigma model with a stated metric and RG trajectory before any
comparison to cigar or parafermion cosets is made.  The sign of the
deformation parameter is part of the datum: the elongated physical sausage is
the negative branch of the real parameter used below.

\begin{definition}[Sausage metric datum]
\label{def:sausage-metric-datum}
The two-dimensional deformed-sphere, or sausage, target used in the
semiclassical description is the \(U(1)\)-invariant Riemannian metric
\begin{equation}
  \dd s^2_{h,q}
  =
  h\left[
    \frac{\dd r^2}{(1-r^2)(1+q r^2)}
    +
    \frac{1-r^2}{1+q r^2}\,\dd\phi^2
  \right],
  \qquad
  -1<r<1,\quad \phi\sim\phi+2\pi ,
  \label{eq:sausage-metric}
\end{equation}
with \(h>0\) and \(q>-1\).  The undeformed value
\(q=0\) is the round sphere of radius \(\sqrt h\): setting
\(r=\cos\vartheta\) gives
\[
  \dd s^2_{h,0}=h(\dd\vartheta^2+\sin^2\vartheta\,\dd\phi^2).
\]
For \(q\ne0\) the metric preserves only the axial \(U(1)\) isometry.  The
entry \((h,q)\) is therefore a metric-coordinate datum for the sigma model;
an exact quantum \(S\)-matrix proposal for the sausage family is a separate
bootstrap datum that must be matched to this coordinate choice.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Curvature of the sausage target]
\label{prop:sausage-curvature}
For the metric \eqref{eq:sausage-metric}, the scalar curvature is
\begin{equation}
  R_{h,q}(r)
  =
  \frac{2(1+q)(1-q r^2)}
       {h(1+q r^2)} .
  \label{eq:sausage-scalar-curvature}
\end{equation}
In particular \(R_{h,0}=2/h\), as required for the round two-sphere of radius
\(\sqrt h\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write the metric as \(E(r)\dd r^2+G(r)\dd\phi^2\), where
\[
  E(r)=\frac{h}{(1-r^2)(1+qr^2)},
  \qquad
  G(r)=h\frac{1-r^2}{1+qr^2}.
\]
For any diagonal two-dimensional metric of this form, direct computation from
the Christoffel symbols gives
\begin{equation}
  R
  =
  -\frac{G''}{EG}
  +\frac{(G')^2}{2EG^2}
  +\frac{E'G'}{2E^2G}.
  \label{eq:diagonal-rotational-curvature-formula}
\end{equation}
Substitution of \(E\) and \(G\) into
\eqref{eq:diagonal-rotational-curvature-formula} gives
\eqref{eq:sausage-scalar-curvature}.  At \(q=0\), the formula becomes the
constant \(2/h\), agreeing with the standard scalar curvature of the round
sphere.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Closure under one-loop Ricci flow]
\label{prop:sausage-ricci-flow-closure}
Let \(g_{h,q}\) denote the metric \eqref{eq:sausage-metric}.  Under the
geometric Ricci flow
\[
  \partial_\tau g_{ij}=-\operatorname{Ric}_{ij},
  \label{eq:geometric-ricci-flow}
\]
the family \(g_{h,q}\) is closed.  The induced parameter equations are
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd h}{\dd\tau}=-(1+q),
  \qquad
  \frac{\dd q}{\dd\tau}=-\frac{2q(1+q)}{h}.
  \label{eq:sausage-ricci-flow-hq}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, for the physical sausage branch \(q=-\kappa^2\), \(0\le\kappa<1\),
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd h}{\dd\tau}=-(1-\kappa^2),
  \qquad
  \frac{\dd\kappa}{\dd\tau}=-\frac{\kappa(1-\kappa^2)}{h}.
  \label{eq:sausage-ricci-flow-kappa}
\end{equation}
The ratio \(q/h^2\) is constant along \eqref{eq:sausage-ricci-flow-hq}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In two dimensions \(\operatorname{Ric}_{ij}=\frac12 R g_{ij}\).  Write
\[
  g_{rr}=\frac{h}{(1-r^2)(1+qr^2)},
  \qquad
  g_{\phi\phi}=h\frac{1-r^2}{1+qr^2}.
\]
The logarithmic variation of either component under a parameter change is
\[
  \frac{\partial_\tau g_{ij}}{g_{ij}}
  =
  \frac{\dot h}{h}-\frac{\dot q\,r^2}{1+qr^2}.
  \label{eq:sausage-log-variation}
\]
The right side of \eqref{eq:geometric-ricci-flow}, using
\eqref{eq:sausage-scalar-curvature}, gives the common logarithmic factor
\[
  -\frac12 R_{h,q}(r)
  =
  -\frac{(1+q)(1-qr^2)}{h(1+qr^2)}.
  \label{eq:sausage-ricci-log-factor}
\]
Equating \eqref{eq:sausage-log-variation} and
\eqref{eq:sausage-ricci-log-factor} as rational functions of \(r^2\) gives
the constant term
\[
  \dot h=-(1+q)
\]
and the coefficient of \(r^2\),
\[
  q\frac{\dot h}{h}-\dot q=\frac{q(1+q)}{h}.
\]
Substituting \(\dot h=-(1+q)\) gives
\(\dot q=-2q(1+q)/h\).  The \(\kappa\)-form follows from
\(q=-\kappa^2\).  Finally,
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd\tau}\left(\frac q{h^2}\right)
  =
  \frac{\dot q}{h^2}-\frac{2q\dot h}{h^3}=0.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Integrated sausage Ricci trajectory]
\label{prop:sausage-integrated-ricci-trajectory}
Fix a solution of \eqref{eq:sausage-ricci-flow-hq} with initial value
\((h_0,q_0)\) at \(\tau=\tau_0\).  If \(q_0=0\), then
\[
  h(\tau)=h_0-(\tau-\tau_0),\qquad q(\tau)=0 .
  \label{eq:sausage-round-integrated-flow}
\]
On the physical sausage branch \(q<0\), write the invariant as
\[
  \frac{q}{h^2}=-\alpha^2,\qquad \alpha>0,
  \label{eq:sausage-alpha-invariant}
\]
so that \(\kappa(\tau)=\alpha h(\tau)\).  While \(0<\alpha h(\tau)<1\),
\begin{equation}
  h(\tau)
  =
  \frac1\alpha
  \tanh\!\left(
    \operatorname{arctanh}(\alpha h_0)-\alpha(\tau-\tau_0)
  \right),
  \qquad
  q(\tau)=-\alpha^2h(\tau)^2 .
  \label{eq:sausage-physical-integrated-flow}
\end{equation}
On the positive branch \(q>0\), write \(q/h^2=\beta^2\), \(\beta>0\).  Then
\begin{equation}
  h(\tau)
  =
  \frac1\beta
  \tan\!\left(
    \arctan(\beta h_0)-\beta(\tau-\tau_0)
  \right),
  \qquad
  q(\tau)=\beta^2h(\tau)^2 ,
  \label{eq:sausage-positive-integrated-flow}
\end{equation}
up to the first zero or pole of the tangent chart.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Proposition~\ref{prop:sausage-ricci-flow-closure} proves that \(q/h^2\) is
constant.  For \(q_0=0\), the equation for \(h\) reduces to \(\dot h=-1\),
which gives \eqref{eq:sausage-round-integrated-flow}.

On the negative branch, substitute \(q=-\alpha^2h^2\) into
\eqref{eq:sausage-ricci-flow-hq}.  The scalar equation is
\[
  \dot h=-(1-\alpha^2h^2).
  \label{eq:sausage-negative-h-ode}
\]
Since
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd h}\operatorname{arctanh}(\alpha h)
  =
  \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha^2h^2},
\]
\eqref{eq:sausage-negative-h-ode} is equivalent to
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd\tau}\operatorname{arctanh}(\alpha h(\tau))=-\alpha .
\]
Integrating from \(\tau_0\) to \(\tau\) gives
\eqref{eq:sausage-physical-integrated-flow}.  The relation
\(q=-\alpha^2h^2\) then supplies the second coordinate.  The condition
\(\alpha h<1\) is exactly \(q>-1\), the Riemannian domain of
Definition~\ref{def:sausage-metric-datum}.

On the positive branch, substitute \(q=\beta^2h^2\).  Then
\[
  \dot h=-(1+\beta^2h^2),
\]
and
\[
  \frac{\dd}{\dd h}\arctan(\beta h)=\frac{\beta}{1+\beta^2h^2}.
\]
Thus \(\frac{\dd}{\dd\tau}\arctan(\beta h(\tau))=-\beta\), and integration
gives \eqref{eq:sausage-positive-integrated-flow}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Round and cylindrical endpoints of the metric family]
\label{prop:sausage-metric-endpoints}
The branch \(q=-\kappa^2\), \(0\le\kappa<1\), interpolates at the metric level
between the round two-sphere and a flat cylinder.  More precisely,
\(\kappa=0\) gives \(S^2\) of radius \(\sqrt h\), while the limiting metric
as \(\kappa\to1\) is
\[
  \dd s^2_{h,-1}=h(\dd\rho^2+\dd\phi^2),
  \qquad r=\tanh\rho,\quad \rho\in\mathbb R .
  \label{eq:sausage-cylinder-limit}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The case \(\kappa=0\) is the calculation already displayed in
Definition~\ref{def:sausage-metric-datum}.  For \(\kappa\to1\), set
\(q=-1\) in \eqref{eq:sausage-metric}.  Then
\[
  \frac{\dd r^2}{(1-r^2)^2}
  +
  \dd\phi^2
\]
is obtained.  The change of variable \(r=\tanh\rho\) gives
\(\dd r=(1-\tanh^2\rho)\dd\rho=(1-r^2)\dd\rho\), hence
\(\dd r^2/(1-r^2)^2=\dd\rho^2\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:sausage-cylinder-limit}.
\end{proof}

\begin{controlledapproximation}[Sausage bootstrap matching]
\label{ca:sausage-bootstrap-matching}
Equations \eqref{eq:sausage-metric}, \eqref{eq:sausage-ricci-flow-hq}, and
\eqref{eq:sausage-cylinder-limit} are metric and one-loop RG data.  An exact
factorized \(S\)-matrix description of the sausage model requires a separate
bootstrap datum: particle labels, physical-strip pole assignments, CDD
normalization, and a matching map from the regulator-defined sigma model to
those rapidity-space coordinates.  This matching is especially delicate near
the cylindrical endpoint, where the target develops an asymptotic circle and
the relation to compact parafermion or noncompact cigar descriptions depends
on the precise scaling limit.
\end{controlledapproximation}

\begin{definition}[Sigma-model family datum]
\label{def:sigma-model-family-datum}
A sigma-model family datum consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a target, quotient, or supertarget \(X\), including its global and
  local redundancy groups;
  \item a regulator definition of maps \(\Sigma\to X\) and of the functional
  measure or Berezin functional;
  \item coupling coordinates in the metric, \(B\)-field, Wess--Zumino, and
  \(\theta\)-angle sectors;
  \item classical integrability data, such as a Lax connection, when present;
  \item ultraviolet beta functions or a nonperturbative mass-generation
  calculation;
  \item a proposed bootstrap spectrum and S-matrix, with CDD choices and bound
  state poles specified;
  \item a matching statement between the regulator and bootstrap data.
\end{enumerate}
The families discussed in this chapter are the round \(O(N)\) model,
\(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\), principal chiral models, WZW endpoints, symmetric-space
cosets, supergroup variants, and deformed-sphere trajectories.
\end{definition}

\section{Gross--Neveu Ultraviolet Datum}

Let \(\psi^i\), \(i=1,\ldots,N\), be Majorana fermions in two Lorentzian
dimensions.  The \(O(N)\) Gross--Neveu action is
\[
  S_{\mathrm{GN}}
  =
  \int\dd^2x\,
  \left[
    \frac{\ii}{2}\psi^i\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi^i
    +\frac{g^2}{8}(\bar\psi^i\psi^i)^2
  \right].
\]
Introducing an auxiliary scalar \(\sigma\) gives the equivalent finite-cutoff
functional
\[
  S_{\mathrm{GN}}[\psi,\sigma]
  =
  \int\dd^2x\,
  \left[
    \frac{\ii}{2}\psi^i\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi^i
    -\frac12\sigma\,\bar\psi^i\psi^i
    -\frac1{2g^2}\sigma^2
  \right],
\]
because the Gaussian equation of motion is
\[
  \sigma=-\frac{g^2}{2}\bar\psi^i\psi^i .
\]
Substitution returns the quartic interaction.  The auxiliary-field form makes
the large-\(N\) mass gap equation explicit: for a constant saddle
\(\sigma=m\),
\[
  \frac{m}{g^2}
  =
  Nm\int^\Lambda\frac{\dd^2p_E}{(2\pi)^2}\frac1{p_E^2+m^2}.
\]
For \(m\ne0\),
\[
  \frac1{Ng^2}
  =
  \frac1{4\pi}\log\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2+m^2}{m^2}\right).
\]
Thus a fixed physical mass \(m\) requires logarithmic dependence of \(g\) on
the regulator scale.  Equivalently, with \(\lambda_{\mathrm{GN}}=Ng^2\),
\[
  m
  =
  \frac{\Lambda}{\sqrt{\exp(4\pi/\lambda_{\mathrm{GN}})-1}}
  \sim
  \Lambda\exp\!\left(-\frac{2\pi}{\lambda_{\mathrm{GN}}}\right).
\]
This derivation is a saddle calculation in a specified regularized functional
integral; the exact finite-\(N\) factorized QFT is a separate construction.

\section{Bootstrap Matching Conditions}

For an asymptotically free model family, exact scattering data must match at
least four layers:
\[
  (G,V)\quad
  \longleftrightarrow\quad
  \text{particle multiplets},
\]
\[
  \beta(g)<0\quad
  \longleftrightarrow\quad
  \text{dynamically generated mass scale},
\]
\[
  \text{local currents and stress tensor}\quad
  \longleftrightarrow\quad
  \text{form-factor residues},
\]
\[
  \text{finite-volume spectrum}\quad
  \longleftrightarrow\quad
  \text{TBA kernels from }-\ii\partial_\theta\log S_R(\theta).
\]
The last line is checked by inserting the channel eigenvalues into the TBA
kernel
\[
  K_R(\theta)=\frac1{2\pi\ii}\partial_\theta\log S_R(\theta).
\]
The ultraviolet central charge extracted from the small-volume TBA must agree
with the short-distance fields that define the regulated model.  This is an
equality of two constructed objects only after the regulator-to-continuum map
and the scattering construction have been specified.

\begin{openproblem}[Constructive factorizing examples]
\label{op:constructive-factorizing-on-gn}
Construct the \(O(N)\) sigma model and Gross--Neveu factorizing QFTs as local
QFTs with Hilbert space, local algebras, conserved currents, mass gap,
Haag--Ruelle scattering states, and exact S-matrix, in one framework where the
ultraviolet regulator data and the bootstrap data are proved to match.
\end{openproblem}
