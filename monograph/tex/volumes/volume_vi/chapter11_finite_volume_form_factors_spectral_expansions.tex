\chapter{Finite-Volume Form Factors And Spectral Expansions}
\label{ch:integrable-finite-volume-form-factors}

\ConventionsLorentzian

Exact scattering data become QFT data only after they are connected to local
operator matrix elements and to Hilbert-space traces or correlation
functions.  This chapter develops the finite-volume bridge.  The finite
circumference \(L\) is a regulator, not an infrared decoration: it turns
rapidity states into discrete vectors, supplies a controlled normalization of
matrix elements, and gives a testable route from bootstrap form factors to
correlators.

\section{Bethe--Yang States}

Let a massive integrable two-dimensional QFT have particle species
\(a=1,\ldots,N\), masses \(m_a\), and two-body scattering eigenvalues
\[
  S_{ab}(\theta)=\ee^{i\delta_{ab}(\theta)}
\]
in a diagonal sector.  On a circle of circumference \(L\), an \(n\)-particle
state with rapidities \(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n\) satisfies the Bethe--Yang
equations
\[
  Q_j(\theta)
  =
  m_{a_j}L\sinh\theta_j
  +
  \sum_{k\ne j}\delta_{a_j a_k}(\theta_j-\theta_k)
  =
  2\pi I_j ,
  \qquad j=1,\ldots,n .
\]
The integers or half-integers \(I_j\) specify the state once a branch of
\(\delta\) and an ordering convention are fixed.  The finite-volume energy
and momentum at this order are
\[
  E_L(\theta)=\sum_{j=1}^n m_{a_j}\cosh\theta_j ,
  \qquad
  P_L(\theta)=\sum_{j=1}^n m_{a_j}\sinh\theta_j .
\]
Corrections are exponentially small in \(mL\) when no massless channel or
nearby singularity invalidates the contour deformation used in the derivation.

\section{Gaudin Density}

The density of Bethe--Yang states is the Jacobian
\[
  \rho_n(\theta)
  =
  \det_{1\le i,j\le n}
  \frac{\partial Q_i}{\partial\theta_j}.
\]
For one particle,
\[
  \rho_1(\theta)=m_aL\cosh\theta .
\]
For two particles in a diagonal channel,
\[
  \rho_2(\theta_1,\theta_2)
  =
  m_{a_1}m_{a_2}L^2\cosh\theta_1\cosh\theta_2
  +
  \varphi_{a_1a_2}(\theta_{12})
  L\bigl(
    m_{a_1}\cosh\theta_1+m_{a_2}\cosh\theta_2
  \bigr),
\]
where \(\theta_{12}=\theta_1-\theta_2\) and
\[
  \varphi_{ab}(\theta)=\partial_\theta\delta_{ab}(\theta).
\]
The determinant is the finite-volume normalization factor that converts
infinite-volume delta-normalized rapidity states into unit vectors.

\section{Off-Diagonal Matrix Elements}

Let \(\mathcal O\) be a local scalar operator and let
\[
  F^{\mathcal O}_{b_1\ldots b_m,a_1\ldots a_n}
  (\theta'_1+i\pi,\ldots,\theta'_m+i\pi,\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)
\]
be the infinite-volume crossed form factor in the branch specified by the
ordering of particles.  If the incoming and outgoing finite-volume states have
no coincident rapidities after crossing, the leading finite-volume matrix
element is
\[
  {}_L\langle J_1,\ldots,J_m|\mathcal O(0)|I_1,\ldots,I_n\rangle_L
  =
  \frac{
    F^{\mathcal O}_{b_1\ldots b_m,a_1\ldots a_n}
    (\theta'_1+i\pi,\ldots,\theta'_m+i\pi,\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)
  }{
    \sqrt{\rho_m(\theta')\rho_n(\theta)}
  }
  +
  O(\ee^{-\mu L}) .
\]
The constant \(\mu\) is determined by the lightest particle and by the closest
singularity crossed in moving virtual particles around the circle.  The
formula is a theorem only after the analytic strip, residue prescriptions,
and finite-volume Hilbert-space construction have been stated.

\section{Diagonal Matrix Elements}

Diagonal matrix elements require subtracting kinematic poles.  Let
\[
  F^{\mathcal O}_{2n,c}
  (\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)
\]
denote the connected diagonal form factor obtained from the finite part of
\[
  F^{\mathcal O}_{\bar a_n\ldots\bar a_1a_1\ldots a_n}
  (\theta_n+i\pi+\epsilon_n,\ldots,\theta_1+i\pi+\epsilon_1,
  \theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)
\]
after removing all pole terms as \(\epsilon_j\to0\).  The finite-volume
expectation has the expansion
\[
  {}_L\langle I_1,\ldots,I_n|\mathcal O|I_1,\ldots,I_n\rangle_L
  =
  \frac{1}{\rho_n(\theta)}
  \sum_{A\subset\{1,\ldots,n\}}
  F^{\mathcal O}_{2|A|,c}(\theta_A)\,
  \rho_{n-|A|}(\theta_{\bar A})
  +
  O(\ee^{-\mu L}) .
\]
Here \(\rho_0=1\), and \(\theta_A\) is the rapidity list indexed by \(A\).
This identity is a precise statement of how contact terms from coincident
crossed particles are replaced by connected diagonal form factors.

\section{Spectral Expansion Of Correlators}

For a zero-temperature two-point function in finite volume,
\[
  C_L(x,t)=
  \langle\Omega_L|\mathcal O(x,t)\mathcal O(0,0)|\Omega_L\rangle ,
\]
inserting a complete orthonormal basis gives
\[
  C_L(x,t)
  =
  \sum_\alpha
  \bigl|
    \langle\Omega_L|\mathcal O(0)|\alpha,L\rangle
  \bigr|^2
  \ee^{-iE_\alpha t+iP_\alpha x}.
\]
The infinite-volume expansion is obtained by replacing sums over Bethe
integers by rapidity integrals with the Gaudin density.  For a scalar operator
in a diagonal theory,
\[
  C(x,t)
  =
  \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}
  \frac{1}{n!}
  \sum_{a_1,\ldots,a_n}
  \int_{\mathbb R^n}
  \prod_{j=1}^n\frac{d\theta_j}{2\pi}\,
  \bigl|
    F^{\mathcal O}_{a_1\ldots a_n}(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)
  \bigr|^2
  \ee^{-i\sum_j m_{a_j}t\cosh\theta_j
      +i\sum_j m_{a_j}x\sinh\theta_j}.
\]
Convergence is part of the statement.  It requires bounds on form factors at
large rapidity, control of particle-number growth, and a domain for
\((x,t)\) or its Euclidean continuation.

\section{Thermal Traces And Leclair--Mussardo Type Series}

At finite temperature the Gibbs trace is not obtained by simply inserting the
zero-temperature spectral sum with Boltzmann weights.  The thermodynamic
limit must be taken at fixed rapidity density.  A thermal expectation of a
local operator has the candidate series
\[
  \langle\mathcal O\rangle_R
  =
  \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n!}
  \sum_{a_1,\ldots,a_n}
  \int\prod_{j=1}^n\frac{d\theta_j}{2\pi}\,
  \prod_{j=1}^n n_{a_j}(\theta_j)\,
  F^{\mathcal O}_{2n,c}(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n),
\]
where \(R\) is inverse temperature and
\[
  n_a(\theta)=\frac{1}{1+\ee^{\varepsilon_a(\theta)}}
\]
for fermionic Bethe quantization conventions.  Other statistics conventions
change the occupation factor and the definition of the pseudoenergy.  The
series is tied to the TBA equations; it is meaningful only with the same
scattering kernel, branch conventions, and connected-diagonal prescription.

\section{Reconstruction Boundary}

Finite-volume form-factor formulae are not a replacement for local QFT
construction.  They provide a computable comparison system:
\[
  \text{bootstrap } S
  \longrightarrow
  \text{form factors}
  \longrightarrow
  \text{finite-volume matrix elements}
  \longrightarrow
  \text{correlators}.
\]
To obtain a local QFT, the correlators must satisfy positivity, covariance,
locality, clustering, and reconstruction hypotheses in the intended
framework.  The finite-volume regulator is valuable because every step before
the infinite-volume limit has a declared Hilbert space and normalizable
vectors.

\begin{openproblem}[Form-factor construction]
\label{op:form-factor-construction}
Construct an interacting factorizing QFT from bootstrap scattering and
form-factor data by proving convergence of the spectral expansions, locality
of reconstructed fields or algebras, and agreement of finite-volume matrix
elements with the Hilbert-space limit.
\end{openproblem}
