\section{Field-Theoretic Data}

Let \(M=\mathbb R^{1,D-1}\) be \(D\)-dimensional Minkowski space with
translation coordinates \(x^\mu\) and metric
\(\eta=\operatorname{diag}(-1,1,\ldots,1)\).  The proper orthochronous
Poincare group is denoted by
\[
  \mathcal P_+^\uparrow=\mathbb R^D\rtimes SO^+(1,D-1).
\]
The Wightman framework describes a QFT by Hilbert-space data together with
local operator-valued distributions.  The basic data are:
\[
  (\Hilb,\vac,U,\mathcal D,\{\Phi_a\}_{a\in A}).
\]
Here \(\Hilb\) is a Hilbert space, \(\vac\in\Hilb\) is a unit vector, \(U\) is a
strongly continuous unitary representation of \(\mathcal P_+^\uparrow\),
\(\mathcal D\subset\Hilb\) is a dense invariant domain, and each \(\Phi_a\) is
an operator-valued tempered distribution on \(M\) with common domain
\(\mathcal D\).  For a Schwartz test function \(f\in\mathcal S(M)\),
\[
  \Phi_a(f)=\int_M \dd^D x\, f(x)\Phi_a(x)
\]
denotes the smeared operator.  This integral notation means distributional
pairing, and all products of fields below are products of smeared operators on
the common domain.

The vacuum is invariant:
\[
  U(g)\vac=\vac,\qquad g\in\mathcal P_+^\uparrow .
\]
Translations are written \(U(a,1)=\ee^{\ii a_\mu P^\mu}\).  The spectrum
condition is
\[
  \operatorname{Spec}(P)\subset \overline V_+
  =
  \{p\in\mathbb R^D\mid p^0\ge0,\ p^2\le0\}.
\]
If \(\Phi_a\) transforms in a finite-dimensional Lorentz representation
\(S(\Lambda)\), covariance is the identity
\[
  U(a,\Lambda)\Phi_a(f)U(a,\Lambda)^{-1}
  =
  S(\Lambda)_a{}^b\,
  \Phi_b(f_{a,\Lambda}),
\]
where
\[
  f_{a,\Lambda}(x)=f\bigl(\Lambda^{-1}(x-a)\bigr).
\]
The precise spin indices are carried by the label \(a\).

Locality is imposed after smearing.  If the supports of \(f\) and \(g\) are
spacelike separated, then
\[
  [\Phi_a(f),\Phi_b(g)]_\pm=0
\]
on \(\mathcal D\), with the anticommutator used when both fields are fermionic.
The vacuum is cyclic for the polynomial field algebra:
\[
  \operatorname{span}\{
    \Phi_{a_1}(f_1)\cdots\Phi_{a_n}(f_n)\vac
    \mid n\ge0
  \}
  \quad\text{is dense in }\Hilb .
\]

\section{Wightman Functions}

The vacuum \(n\)-point distributions are
\begin{equation}
  W_{a_1\cdots a_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
  =
  \bra{\vac}
  \Phi_{a_1}(x_1)\cdots\Phi_{a_n}(x_n)
  \ket{\vac}.
  \label{eq:wightman-functions}
\end{equation}
They are tempered distributions on \(M^n\).  Translation invariance allows one
to pass to difference variables
\[
  \xi_j=x_{j+1}-x_j,\qquad j=1,\ldots,n-1.
\]
The spectral condition implies that the Fourier transform in the difference
variables has support in \(\overline V_+^{\,n-1}\).  This is the same positive
spectral input that led in Chapter \ref{ch:kallen-lehmann} to the
Kallen--Lehmann representation of two-point functions.

The Wightman hierarchy satisfies four structural conditions that are directly
read from the Hilbert-space data:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
  \text{covariance:} &
  W\text{ transforms under } \mathcal P_+^\uparrow \text{ with the field
  representations},\\[0.25em]
  \text{spectrum:} &
  \operatorname{supp}\widehat W_n\subset \overline V_+^{\,n-1}
  \text{ in difference variables},\\[0.25em]
  \text{locality:} &
  W_n\text{ has the graded permutation property at spacelike separated
  adjacent points},\\[0.25em]
  \text{positivity:} &
  \sum_{m,n}\overline c_m c_n\,
  W_{m+n}(f_m^\dagger\otimes f_n)\ge0 .
\end{array}
\]
In the positivity line, \(f_n\) denotes a compactly supported or Schwartz test
function valued in the appropriate finite direct sum of field labels, and
\(f_m^\dagger\) reverses order and complex conjugates the test functions
with the field adjunction included.

\section{Reconstruction From Correlation Functions}

The Wightman reconstruction theorem says that the correlation hierarchy is
itself a complete encoding of the field theory in this framework, provided the
hierarchy satisfies the Wightman conditions with the required regularity.

\begin{theorem}[Wightman reconstruction]
Let \(\{W_n\}_{n\ge0}\) be a hierarchy of tempered distributions with field
indices, with \(W_0=1\).  Suppose the hierarchy satisfies Poincare covariance,
the spectral support condition, Hermiticity, locality, and positivity.  Then
there exists Wightman field data
\[
  (\Hilb,\vac,U,\mathcal D,\{\Phi_a\})
\]
whose vacuum correlation functions are exactly \(\{W_n\}\).  The resulting
data are unique up to a unitary equivalence preserving the vacuum, the
Poincare representation, and all smeared fields.
\end{theorem}

The construction begins with formal vectors
\[
  [a_1,f_1;\ldots ;a_n,f_n]
\]
and defines an inner product by the Wightman distributions.  Positivity makes
this a positive semidefinite form.  Dividing by zero-norm vectors and
completing gives \(\Hilb\).  Fields act by left multiplication on the dense
subspace of finite sequences, and the Poincare action is induced by covariance
of the hierarchy.

\section{Analytic Continuation From Spectral Positivity}

For scalar fields, write the Wightman function in difference variables as
\[
  W_n(\xi_1,\ldots,\xi_{n-1}).
\]
The support condition for the Fourier transform implies that \(W_n\) is the
boundary value of a holomorphic function on the forward tube
\[
  T_{n-1}
  =
  \{z_j=\xi_j+\ii\eta_j\in\mathbb C^D
  \mid \eta_j\in V_+,\ j=1,\ldots,n-1\}.
\]
Lorentz covariance enlarges the domain to the extended tube obtained by the
complex Lorentz group.  Locality then identifies the analytic continuations
associated with permutations at Jost configurations, where all relevant real
linear combinations are spacelike.

The Euclidean Schwinger functions are restrictions of these analytic functions
to noncoincident imaginary-time configurations.  The converse passage from
Euclidean data to Lorentzian Wightman data requires reflection positivity and
growth hypotheses.  That converse is the subject of the next chapter.

\section{Gauge Theories and Observable Fields}

The Wightman framework is most direct for gauge-invariant local fields acting
on a positive Hilbert space.  Gauge potentials before quotienting by gauge
redundancy usually require additional structure: an indefinite inner product,
BRST cohomology, a local algebra of gauge-invariant observables, or a
combination of these.  The logically invariant statement is that physical
matrix elements and local gauge-invariant observables must be represented on a
positive physical Hilbert space.  The framework comparison records which
version of this statement has been established in a given construction.

Thus the Wightman framework supplies precise theorems for local fields,
spectral positivity, analytic continuation, and reconstruction.  Its output is
Hilbert-space QFT data with operator-valued distributions and a distinguished
vacuum representation.
