\section{Vacuum Net and Isolated Mass Shell}

Let \(M=\mathbb M^D\) be Minkowski spacetime, and let
\[
  \mathcal O\longmapsto \mathcal R(\mathcal O)\subset\mathcal B(\Hilb)
\]
be a Poincare-covariant vacuum net of von Neumann algebras.  The standing
data are a Hilbert space \(\Hilb\), a unit vector \(\vac\), and a strongly
continuous unitary representation \(U\) of the proper orthochronous Poincare
group such that
\[
  U(a,\Lambda)\mathcal R(\mathcal O)U(a,\Lambda)^{-1}
  =
  \mathcal R(\Lambda\mathcal O+a),
  \qquad
  U(a,\Lambda)\vac=\vac .
\]
For translations alone we write
\[
  U(a)=U(a,\mathbf 1)=\exp(\ii a_\mu P^\mu),
\]
where \(P^\mu\) are the commuting self-adjoint energy-momentum generators.
The scalar product in Fourier transforms is the same contraction
\(q\cdot x=q_\mu x^\mu\).  This sign convention is part of the standing data:
with the opposite convention for \(U(a)\), every spectral-transfer formula
below would contain \(-\operatorname{supp}\widehat f\) instead of
\(\operatorname{supp}\widehat f\).
The translation generators obey the spectrum condition
\[
  \operatorname{Spec}(P)\subset \overline V_+ .
\]
Let \(E(\Delta)\) denote the joint spectral projection of \(P^\mu\) for a
Borel set \(\Delta\subset\mathbb R^D\).

Fix \(m>0\).  The positive mass shell is
\[
  \Sigma_m^+
  =
  \{p\in\mathbb R^D:p^0=\omega_{\vec p},\ p^2=-m^2\},
  \qquad
  \omega_{\vec p}=\sqrt{\vec p^{\,2}+m^2}.
\]
The one-particle subspace for this stable species is
\[
  \Hilb_1=E(\Sigma_m^+)\Hilb .
\]
This notation carries an existence hypothesis: \(\Hilb_1\ne\{0\}\).  The
projection onto the Borel set \(\Sigma_m^+\) may be zero in a perfectly
consistent Hilbert-space theory, or it may fail to be isolated from continuous
spectral support.  In either case the ordinary massive Haag--Ruelle theorem
does not produce scattering states for that putative species.  The relation
between a one-particle shell and an atom in a K\"all\'en--Lehmann measure was
spelled out in
Remark~\ref{rem:kallen-lehmann-mass-shell-existence-status}.
The Haag--Ruelle construction assumes that the shell is isolated in the
energy-momentum spectrum: there is an open neighborhood \(N\) of
\(\Sigma_m^+\) such that
\[
  \operatorname{Spec}(P)\cap N=\Sigma_m^+ .
\]
This isolation is the spectral form of stability.  The formulas below are
written for a single bosonic species.  If several stable species, spin
multiplets, or fermionic particles are present, \(\Hilb_1\) is replaced by the
appropriate direct sum of isolated one-particle representations and
\(\mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)\) by the corresponding graded asymptotic Fock space.

For a scalar multiplicity-one species, the one-particle Hilbert space is
realized by the mass-shell spectral representation
\[
  W_1:L^2(\Sigma_m^+,\dd\mu_m)\overset{\sim}{\longrightarrow}\Hilb_1,
  \qquad
  \dd\mu_m(p)=\frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}2\omega_{\vec p}},
\]
with
\[
  W_1 f=\int_{\Sigma_m^+}\dd\mu_m(p)\,f(p)|p\rangle,
  \qquad
  U(a,\mathbf 1)W_1f
  =
  W_1\bigl(p\mapsto \ee^{\ii a_\mu p^\mu}f(p)\bigr).
\]
The bosonic asymptotic Fock space is then the Hilbert direct sum
\[
  \mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)
  \simeq
  \mathbb C\Omega_F
  \oplus
  \bigoplus_{n\ge1}
  L^2_{\mathrm{sym}}\bigl((\Sigma_m^+)^n,\dd\mu_m^{\otimes n}\bigr).
\]
Thus the wave operators below start from a constructed free many-particle
Hilbert space whose momentum labels are coordinates in the spectral model.

\section{Almost-Local Operators and Spectral Transfer}

Almost-locality is the norm-approximation condition of
Definition~\ref{def:almost-local-bounded-operator}: an operator
\(B\in\mathcal B(\Hilb)\) is approximated by bounded local operators \(B_R\)
localized in double cones of radius \(R\) such that, for every \(N\ge1\), some
\(C_N<\infty\) obeys
\[
  \|B-B_R\|\le C_N R^{-N}.
\]
Almost locality is the operator-algebraic substitute for using smeared local
fields whose support is not strictly compact after energy-momentum
filtering.

For a bounded local operator \(A\), set
\[
  A(x)=U(x)AU(x)^{-1},\qquad x\in\mathbb R^D .
\]
For \(f\in\mathcal S(\mathbb R^D)\), define
\[
  A(f)=\int\dd^D x\,f(x)A(x),
  \qquad
  \widehat f(q)=\int\dd^D x\,f(x)\ee^{\ii q\cdot x}.
\]
With this convention, the energy-momentum transfer of \(A(f)\) is contained
in \(\operatorname{supp}\widehat f\):
\begin{equation}
  E(\Delta_1)A(f)E(\Delta_2)=0
  \quad\text{if}\quad
  \Delta_1\cap
  \bigl(\Delta_2+\operatorname{supp}\widehat f\bigr)=\emptyset .
  \label{eq:haag-ruelle-spectral-transfer}
\end{equation}
Indeed, by the joint spectral theorem,
\[
  E(\dd p)\,A(f)\,E(\dd r)
  =
  \left(\int \dd^D x\,f(x)\ee^{\ii x_\mu(p^\mu-r^\mu)}\right)
  E(\dd p)\,A\,E(\dd r)
  =
  \widehat f(p-r)\,E(\dd p)\,A\,E(\dd r).
\]
After integrating \(p\in\Delta_1\) and \(r\in\Delta_2\), the operator can be
nonzero only if \(p-r\in\operatorname{supp}\widehat f\), equivalently
\(\Delta_1\cap(\Delta_2+\operatorname{supp}\widehat f)\ne\emptyset\).
Equation \eqref{eq:haag-ruelle-spectral-transfer} is the precise meaning of
selecting a particle shell by smearing in spacetime.

Choose a compact set \(K\subset\Sigma_m^+\).  Let \(\chi\in\mathcal S(\mathbb R^D)\)
have \(\operatorname{supp}\widehat\chi\subset N\), with
\(\widehat\chi=1\) on a smaller neighborhood of \(K\).  For a local \(A\),
set
\[
  B=A(\chi).
\]
Then \(B\) is almost local.  Since the vacuum has momentum \(0\),
\[
  B\vac=E(\operatorname{supp}\widehat\chi)B\vac=P_1B\vac,
  \qquad P_1=E(\Sigma_m^+),
\]
provided the support of \(\widehat\chi\) meets the spectrum only on the
isolated shell.  If \(-\operatorname{supp}\widehat\chi\) is disjoint from the
forward spectrum and \(\operatorname{supp}\widehat\chi\) avoids \(0\), then
\[
  B^*\vac=0 .
\]
An almost-local operator satisfying these spectral support properties and
\(B\vac\ne0\) is a regular Haag--Ruelle creator for the shell.

\section{Wave Packets and Velocity Localization}

Let \(B\) be a regular creator.  For
\[
  h\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^{D-1}),
\]
define the positive-energy Klein--Gordon packet
\[
  h_t(\vec x)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  h(\vec p)\,
  \ee^{\ii\omega_{\vec p}t-\ii\vec p\cdot\vec x}
\]
and the time-dependent operator
\[
  B_t(h)=\int\dd^{D-1}\vec x\,h_t(\vec x)B(t,\vec x).
\]
The one-particle vector
\[
  \psi_h=P_1B_t(h)\vac
\]
is independent of \(t\); the phase in \(h_t\) cancels the free time evolution
on the mass shell.

The velocity support of \(h\) is
\[
  V(h)=
  \left\{
    \frac{\vec p}{\omega_{\vec p}}:
    \vec p\in\operatorname{supp}h
  \right\}.
\]
Stationary phase says that \(h_t(\vec x)\) is rapidly decreasing away from
the equal-time tube
\[
  \vec x\in t\,V(h)+O(|t|^0).
\]
Thus packets with disjoint velocity supports become spacelike separated at
large \(|t|\).  Combining this geometric separation with almost locality
gives, for every \(N\ge1\),
\begin{equation}
  \|[B_{i,t}(h_i),B_{j,t}(h_j)]\|
  \le C_N(1+|t|)^{-N}
  \label{eq:algebraic-haag-ruelle-commutator}
\end{equation}
whenever \(V(h_i)\cap V(h_j)=\emptyset\).

\section{Haag--Ruelle Limits}

Let \(B_1,\ldots,B_n\) be regular creators and let
\[
  h_1,\ldots,h_n\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^{D-1})
\]
have pairwise disjoint velocity supports.  Define
\[
  \Psi_t=
  B_{1,t}(h_1)\cdots B_{n,t}(h_n)\vac .
\]

\begin{theorem}[Haag--Ruelle scattering for a vacuum net]
\label{thm:algebraic-haag-ruelle-scattering}
Assume locality, Poincare covariance, the spectrum condition, a unique
vacuum, an isolated massive one-particle shell, and enough regular creators
to generate a dense subspace of \(\Hilb_1\).  Then the limits
\[
  \Psi_{\mathrm{out}}=\lim_{t\to+\infty}\Psi_t,
  \qquad
  \Psi_{\mathrm{in}}=\lim_{t\to-\infty}\Psi_t
\]
exist in Hilbert norm.  They depend only on the one-particle vectors
\(\psi_{h_j}=P_1B_{j,t}(h_j)\vac\), and they define isometric wave
operators from the symmetric Fock space \(\mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)\) into
\(\Hilb\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Physical scope of the isolated-shell hypothesis]
\label{rem:haag-ruelle-isolated-shell-scope}
The nonzero isolated-shell assumption is a spectral theorem about the
particular nonperturbative model, not a generic consequence of writing a formal
Lagrangian.  It is satisfied by free massive fields.  It is also available in
the massive stable sectors of constructive low-dimensional scalar models, such
as \(P(\phi)_2\) and massive \(\lambda\phi^4_3\), in regimes where the
Wightman theory, mass gap, and one-particle spectral gap have been established.
For a four-dimensional \(\lambda\phi^4\) Lagrangian treated only
perturbatively, an isolated real pole in the formal two-point function is
evidence for the intended particle interpretation, but it is not a
nonperturbative theorem constructing an isolated shell in a Hilbert-space
spectrum.

A K\"all\'en--Lehmann measure with no atom at \(m^2\), even if its continuous
support starts at \(m^2\), supplies no normalizable one-particle vector of mass
\(m\) for the corresponding local interpolating field.  Haag--Ruelle then has
no external particle of that species to scatter.  The theorem exactly
distinguishes a sharp stable particle from threshold spectral weight.

The hypothesis fails, or is the wrong hypothesis, in several central physical
settings.  In QED with long-range electromagnetic fields, charged sectors are
expected to be infraparticle sectors: the energy-momentum spectrum has
continuous soft-photon dressing attached to the charged state rather than a
sharp Wigner mass hyperboloid.  The photon is massless and belongs to a theory
without the massive spectral gap used by the theorem.  In QCD, colored quark
and gluon shells are not expected to occur in the gauge-invariant observable
Hilbert space; stable hadrons, when present below strong-decay thresholds, are
the candidate isolated shells.  Unstable particles and resonances do not give
external Haag--Ruelle particles, because their spectral singularities are
resonance poles on analytically continued sheets rather than point spectrum on
the physical mass shell.  Each of these cases requires a different asymptotic
framework or a separate spectral theorem.  The corresponding
asymptotic-completeness problems for charged QED and physical QCD are stated
as Open Problems~\ref{op:qed-charged-asymptotic-completeness}
and~\ref{op:qcd-asymptotic-completeness}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Cook Estimate and the Fock Inner Product}

We now spell out the proof in a form that separates the analytic estimates
from the algebraic conclusion.  The estimates below are precisely the
Haag--Ruelle estimates supplied by almost locality, spectral isolation, and
stationary phase.  Once they are known, the construction of scattering states
and the Fock inner product is an elementary Hilbert-space argument.

\begin{definition}[Haag--Ruelle estimate package]
\label{def:haag-ruelle-estimate-package}
For a finite family of regular creators \(B_j\) and smooth compactly
supported packets \(h_j\), write
\[
  B_{j,t}=B_{j,t}(h_j),
  \qquad
  \psi_j=B_{j,t}\vac\in\Hilb_1 .
\]
The family is said to satisfy the Haag--Ruelle estimate package on
\(|t|\ge T_0\) if the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The vectors \(B_{j,t}\vac=\psi_j\) are independent of \(t\), and
  \(B_{j,t}^*\vac=0\).
  \item For every \(N\ge1\) and every pair with disjoint velocity supports,
  \[
    \|[B_{i,t}^{\#},B_{j,t}^{\#}]\|
    \le C_N(1+|t|)^{-N},
    \qquad B^\#\in\{B,B^*\}.
    \label{eq:hr-estimate-package-commutator}
  \]
  The same estimate holds on the finite-energy vectors obtained from the
  family when one entry is replaced by its strong time derivative.  This
  derivative is the derivative of the smooth translation-smearing parameter;
  no norm differentiability of an unsmeared local operator is assumed.
  \item For every pair of regular creators \(B,C\) with packets \(h,g\),
  \[
    B_t(h)^*C_t(g)\vac
    =
    \langle B_t(h)\vac,C_t(g)\vac\rangle\,\vac
    +
    r_t(B,h;C,g),
    \label{eq:hr-estimate-package-contraction}
  \]
  where for every \(N\ge1\),
  \(\|r_t(B,h;C,g)\|\le C_N(1+|t|)^{-N}\).
  \item For two velocity-separated lists
  \(B_1,\ldots,B_n\) and \(C_1,\ldots,C_m\), the scalar products obey the
  recursive contraction estimate
  \[
  \begin{aligned}
  &
  \left\langle
    B_{1,t}\cdots B_{n,t}\vac,
    C_{1,t}\cdots C_{m,t}\vac
  \right\rangle
  \\
  &\quad =
  \sum_{\ell=1}^{m}
  \langle\psi_1,\varphi_\ell\rangle
  \left\langle
    B_{2,t}\cdots B_{n,t}\vac,
    C_{1,t}\cdots \widehat{C_{\ell,t}}\cdots C_{m,t}\vac
  \right\rangle
  +O_N((1+|t|)^{-N})
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:hr-estimate-package-recursive-contraction}
  \]
  for every \(N\ge1\), with the convention that the sum is empty when
  \(m=0\).
  \item For each velocity-separated product
  \(\Psi_t=B_{1,t}\cdots B_{n,t}\vac\), there is an integrable nonnegative
  function \(R(t)\) on \([T_0,\infty)\) and on
  \((-\infty,-T_0]\) such that
  \[
    \left\|\frac{\dd}{\dd t}\Psi_t\right\|\le R(t)
    \label{eq:hr-estimate-package-cook}
  \]
  on the finite vector domain under discussion.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Estimate package implies Haag--Ruelle limits]
\label{prop:estimate-package-implies-haag-ruelle}
Assume the estimate package
Definition~\ref{def:haag-ruelle-estimate-package} for every finite
velocity-separated family of regular creators.  Then the limits in
Theorem~\ref{thm:algebraic-haag-ruelle-scattering} exist, depend only on the
one-particle vectors, and satisfy the bosonic Fock inner product formula
\[
\begin{aligned}
&
\left\langle
  \Psi^{\rm out}(\psi_1,\ldots,\psi_n),
  \Phi^{\rm out}(\varphi_1,\ldots,\varphi_m)
\right\rangle
\\
&\qquad
=
\delta_{nm}
\sum_{\sigma\in S_n}
\prod_{j=1}^{n}
\langle\psi_j,\varphi_{\sigma(j)}\rangle_{\Hilb_1}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:hr-fock-inner-product-from-estimates}
\]
The same statement holds for incoming limits.  Hence the wave operators are
isometries from \(\mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)\) into \(\Hilb\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We give the outgoing proof.  The incoming proof is the same with
\(t\to-\infty\).

First, Cook's criterion gives existence.  For \(t'>t\ge T_0\),
\[
  \|\Psi_{t'}-\Psi_t\|
  \le
  \int_t^{t'}\left\|\frac{\dd}{\dd s}\Psi_s\right\|\,\dd s
  \le
  \int_t^{t'}R(s)\,\dd s .
\]
Since \(R\in L^1([T_0,\infty))\), the right side tends to zero as
\(t,t'\to+\infty\).  Thus \(\Psi_t\) is Cauchy in the Hilbert norm and has an
outgoing limit.

Second, compute the limiting scalar products.  Let
\[
  \Psi_t=B_{1,t}\cdots B_{n,t}\vac,
  \qquad
  \Phi_t=C_{1,t}\cdots C_{m,t}\vac ,
\]
with both lists velocity-separated.  Then
\[
  \langle\Psi_t,\Phi_t\rangle
  =
  \langle\vac,
  B_{n,t}^*\cdots B_{1,t}^*
  C_{1,t}\cdots C_{m,t}\vac\rangle .
\]
The recursive contraction estimate
\eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-recursive-contraction} gives
\[
  \langle\Psi_t,\Phi_t\rangle
  =
  \sum_{\ell=1}^{m}
  \langle\psi_1,\varphi_\ell\rangle\,
  \langle
    B_{2,t}\cdots B_{n,t}\vac,
    C_{1,t}\cdots \widehat{C_{\ell,t}}\cdots C_{m,t}\vac
  \rangle
  +O_N(t^{-N}).
\]
Induction on \(n+m\), with the base cases following from
\(\langle\vac,\vac\rangle=1\) and \(B_t^*\vac=0\), gives
\eqref{eq:hr-fock-inner-product-from-estimates}.  If \(n\ne m\), the
induction eventually leaves an annihilator acting on the vacuum, and the
limit is zero.

The scalar product formula shows at once that changing an interpolating
creator while keeping the one-particle vector fixed changes the limiting
state by a vector of zero norm.  It also identifies the norm of every finite
linear combination of outgoing limits with the norm of the corresponding
finite symmetric tensor in \(\mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)\).  Therefore the map from
the algebraic velocity-separated Fock domain to \(\Hilb\) is isometric and
extends uniquely to the Fock completion.  Density of regular creators in
\(\Hilb_1\) gives the stated domain of the wave operator.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Stationary-phase velocity localization]
\label{lem:hr-stationary-phase-velocity-localization}
Let \(h\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^{D-1})\) and let
\[
  h_t(\vec x)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^{D-1}\vec p}{(2\pi)^{D-1}}\,
  h(\vec p)\,
  \ee^{\ii\omega_{\vec p}t-\ii\vec p\cdot\vec x}.
\]
Let \(V(h)=\{\vec p/\omega_{\vec p}:\vec p\in\operatorname{supp}h\}\).  If
\(U\subset\mathbb R^{D-1}\) is an open neighborhood of \(V(h)\), then for
every \(N\ge1\) there is \(C_N\) such that
\[
  |h_t(\vec x)|\le C_N(1+|t|+|\vec x|)^{-N}
  \qquad
  \text{whenever }\vec x/t\notin U,\quad |t|\ge1 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
On the compact support of \(h\), the phase is
\[
  \Phi_t(\vec p;\vec x)
  =
  \omega_{\vec p}t-\vec p\cdot\vec x .
\]
Its gradient is
\[
  \nabla_{\vec p}\Phi_t
  =
  t\,\frac{\vec p}{\omega_{\vec p}}-\vec x .
\]
If \(\vec x/t\notin U\), compactness gives
\(|\nabla_{\vec p}\Phi_t|\ge c|t|\) on \(\operatorname{supp}h\) for some
\(c>0\).  Apply repeatedly the integration-by-parts operator
\[
  L
  =
  \frac{1}{\ii|\nabla_{\vec p}\Phi_t|^2}
  \nabla_{\vec p}\Phi_t\cdot\nabla_{\vec p},
  \qquad
  L\ee^{\ii\Phi_t}=\ee^{\ii\Phi_t}.
\]
Each integration by parts gains a factor \(O(|t|^{-1})\), while derivatives
of \(h\) and of the coefficients of \(L\) are bounded on the compact support.
This gives \(O(|t|^{-N})\).  In the region \(|\vec x|\gg |t|\), the same
lower bound is \(c(|t|+|\vec x|)\), giving the displayed estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Almost locality gives the commutator estimate]
\label{prop:almost-locality-gives-hr-commutator}
Let \(B_i\) and \(B_j\) be almost-local regular creators and let
\(h_i,h_j\) have disjoint velocity supports.  Then
\[
  \|[B_{i,t}(h_i),B_{j,t}(h_j)]\|
  \le C_N(1+|t|)^{-N}
  \qquad (N\ge1),
\]
and the same estimate holds with either operator replaced by its adjoint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Choose disjoint open neighborhoods \(U_i,U_j\) of the velocity supports.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:hr-stationary-phase-velocity-localization}, the parts of
the wave packets outside the tubes \(\vec x\in tU_i\) and \(\vec y\in tU_j\)
give \(O_N(|t|^{-N})\) contributions in operator norm after integration
against the bounded translates of \(B_i\) and \(B_j\).

Inside the two tubes, the equal-time points \((t,\vec x)\) and
\((t,\vec y)\) have spatial separation at least \(c|t|\).  Let
\(B_{i,R}\) and \(B_{j,R}\) be local approximants with
\(\|B_\ell-B_{\ell,R}\|\le C_N R^{-N}\), and choose
\(R=c|t|/4\).  The translated local approximants are localized in double
cones whose radii are \(R\) around the two tube points; for large \(|t|\)
these double cones are spacelike separated.  Locality gives zero commutator
for the approximants.  The difference between the original operators and the
approximants contributes at most \(O_N(|t|^{-N})\).  Combining this with the
rapidly decreasing tube-tail contributions proves the estimate.  The adjoint
case follows because almost locality and localization are preserved by
adjoints.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Origin of the remaining Haag--Ruelle estimates]
\label{prop:origin-of-remaining-haag-ruelle-estimates}
For regular creators obtained by smooth spectral transfer inside the
isolating neighborhood \(N\), the one-particle contraction estimate
\eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-contraction}, the recursive contraction
estimate \eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-recursive-contraction}, and the Cook
derivative estimate \eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-cook} follow from spectral
isolation of \(\Sigma_m^+\), the transfer relation
\eqref{eq:haag-ruelle-spectral-transfer}, and the commutator estimate of
Proposition~\ref{prop:almost-locality-gives-hr-commutator}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The support of the spectral transfer of a regular creator meets the spectrum,
when acting on the vacuum, only on \(\Sigma_m^+\).  Therefore
\(B_t(h)\vac\) is exactly the one-particle vector determined by the mass-shell
packet and is independent of \(t\), while \(B_t(h)^*\vac=0\) because the
reflected transfer support is disjoint from \(\overline V_+\).  Acting with
\(B_t(h)^*C_t(g)\) on the vacuum, spectral transfer permits a vacuum component
and separates all nonvacuum components from the isolated shell by a positive
spectral distance fixed by the supports.  Choose a smooth cutoff \(\rho(P)\)
that is \(0\) on a neighbourhood of the vacuum point and \(1\) on the
separated nonvacuum spectral support.  The oscillatory factors left after
subtracting the vacuum component then have no stationary point on the relevant
compact spectral support.  Repeated integration by parts in the spectral
variables, written equivalently as commuting powers of smooth functions of
\(P\) through the translated smearing, gives the rapidly decreasing remainder
in \eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-contraction}.  The estimate is a strong
finite-energy estimate; it is not a statement about an operator norm
derivative of a pointlike field.
Iterating this contraction after decomposing packet supports into finitely
many small velocity cells gives
\eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-recursive-contraction}; terms in which two
uncontracted cells have separated velocity supports are estimated by
Proposition~\ref{prop:almost-locality-gives-hr-commutator}, and the finite
partition is removed by summing the finitely many cells.

For the Cook estimate, differentiate the product on the finite vector domain
spanned by the vectors obtained by applying finitely many translated,
spectrally smeared creators to the vacuum.
The derivative of the \(k\)-th factor, after commuting it to the far right,
annihilates the vacuum because the one-particle packet has been chosen to
solve the free mass-shell evolution.  Every commutation needed to move this
derivative through later factors is controlled by the derivative version of
Proposition~\ref{prop:almost-locality-gives-hr-commutator}; the derivative
version is obtained by applying
Lemma~\ref{lem:hr-stationary-phase-velocity-localization} to the differentiated
packet.  The differentiated packet only multiplies the compact momentum
amplitude by a smooth polynomial expression in \(p\), so it has the same
velocity localization and rapid tube-tail estimates.
The resulting bound is \(C_N(1+|t|)^{-N}\) for arbitrary \(N\), hence
integrable.  This is \eqref{eq:hr-estimate-package-cook}.
\end{proof}

Together,
Propositions~\ref{prop:estimate-package-implies-haag-ruelle},
\ref{prop:almost-locality-gives-hr-commutator}, and
\ref{prop:origin-of-remaining-haag-ruelle-estimates} give the ordinary
massive Haag--Ruelle theorem under the displayed vacuum-net, isolated-shell,
and regular-creator hypotheses.  No perturbative pole expansion or
Lagrangian formalism enters this construction.

\section{Gauge Charges and Nonlocal Charged Creators}

The ordinary theorem uses local creators \(B\in\Obs(\mathcal O)\) whose
vacuum vector \(B\vac\) has nonzero projection to an isolated one-particle
shell.  In a gauge theory with an unscreened Gauss-law charge, the existence
of such a creator is a further dynamical and localization hypothesis.  It is
false for charged particles if the available local algebra is the
gauge-invariant observable algebra.

\begin{theorem}[Gauss-law obstruction to local charged creators]
\label{thm:gauss-law-obstruction-local-charged-creators}
Let \(\Obs\) be a gauge-invariant local observable net in a vacuum
representation.  Assume that an abelian charge \(Q\) is represented on a
common invariant domain by the large-sphere Gauss-law limit
\[
  Q=\lim_{R\to\infty}Q_R,\qquad
  Q_R=\int_{S_R} n_i E^i\,\dd S ,
\]
and that locality holds between \(\Obs(\mathcal O)\) and electric-field
operators localized spacelike to \(\mathcal O\).  If the vacuum is neutral,
\(Q\vac=0\), then every local gauge-invariant observable \(A\in\Obs(\mathcal O)\)
creates a neutral vector:
\[
  Q\,A\vac=0 .
\]
Consequently a charged one-particle vector cannot be obtained by applying a
bounded-region gauge-invariant observable to the vacuum.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose \(R\) so large that the sphere \(S_R\) is spacelike separated from the
bounded region \(\mathcal O\) at the time used to define \(Q_R\), after the
usual smooth smearing of the surface integral.  Locality gives
\[
  [Q_R,A]=0
\]
on the common domain.  Taking the Gauss-law limit gives \([Q,A]=0\) on
vectors for which the limit defining \(Q\) exists.  Therefore
\[
  Q A\vac=AQ\vac=0 .
\]
If \(\psi\) has charge \(q\ne0\), so that \(Q\psi=q\psi\), it cannot equal
\(A\vac\) for such an \(A\).
\end{proof}

This obstruction concerns localization and Gauss-law charge.  It is distinct
from the infraparticle obstruction in massless QED.  The latter states that a
charged sector with long-range electromagnetic flux does not contain an
ordinary sharp Wigner mass-shell vector.  The present theorem applies before
asking whether the charged spectral distribution is an isolated shell or a
soft-photon continuum: a bounded gauge-invariant observable cannot change the
surface charge at infinity.

Charged fields can be introduced in a larger field algebra, but they are not
gauge-invariant local observables.  A gauge-invariant charged insertion must
carry flux to infinity.  In an abelian convention
\[
  A\longmapsto A+\dd\alpha,\qquad
  \psi_q(x)\longmapsto \ee^{\ii q\alpha(x)}\psi_q(x),
\]
a path \(\gamma\) from \(x\) to spatial infinity gives the formal dressing
\[
  \Psi_{q,\gamma}(x)
  =
  \psi_q(x)\,
  \exp\!\left\{\ii q\int_\gamma A\right\}.
  \label{eq:charged-wilson-line-dressed-field}
\]
For gauge functions with \(\alpha(\infty)=0\), the exponential transforms by
\(\ee^{-\ii q\alpha(x)}\), so \(\Psi_{q,\gamma}(x)\) is gauge invariant.  For
a gauge function with nonzero boundary value, it transforms by the boundary
phase and therefore carries the global charge.  The price is noncompact
localization: the localization region includes the curve \(\gamma\) reaching
infinity, or a Coulombic dressing with equivalent asymptotic flux.

\begin{definition}[Dressed charged LSZ problem]
\label{def:dressed-charged-lsz-problem}
A dressed charged LSZ datum consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a gauge-invariant observable net and a charged sector carrying a
Gauss-law surface charge;
\item a family of gauge-invariant noncompact charged creators
\(\Psi_{q,\gamma}(x)\), or Coulombic variants with the same asymptotic flux;
\item a specification of the dressing geometry \(\gamma\) in terms of the
asymptotic velocity or momentum labels;
\item a spectral statement for the vectors \(\Psi_{q,\gamma}(f)\vac\), either
an isolated massive shell in an infrared-regulated or screened model, or the
corresponding charged infraparticle threshold datum in massless QED;
\item a reduction prescription for Fourier-transformed dressed correlators
\[
  \langle\vac|
  T\{\Psi_{q_1,\gamma_1}(x_1)\cdots
      \Psi^\dagger_{q_n,\gamma_n}(y_n)\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_k\}
  |\vac\rangle
\]
which extracts the same matrix elements as the large-time dressed wave
operators.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

This definition identifies the replacement for ordinary local-field LSZ in a
charged gauge sector.  The external residue, when an isolated charged shell is
available after a regulator or screening deformation, is the residue of the
dressed two-point function.  It includes the short-distance renormalization of
the matter field and the endpoint or cusp renormalization of the Wilson-line
dressing.  Different dressing geometries define different charged operators;
showing that a physical on-shell answer is independent of the unobserved
geometric choices is part of the reduction theorem, not an assumption inside
the scalar LSZ formula.

\subsection{Wilson-Line Covariance and Boundary Charge}

The sign and orientation in
\eqref{eq:charged-wilson-line-dressed-field} are fixed by gauge covariance.
Let \(\gamma_{x,\infty}:[0,\infty)\to M\) be a regulated curve with
\(\gamma(0)=x\) and endpoint tending to spatial infinity.  With
\[
  A\mapsto A+\dd\alpha,\qquad
  \psi_q(x)\mapsto \ee^{\ii q\alpha(x)}\psi_q(x),
\]
the abelian Wilson factor
\[
  W_{q,\gamma}(x)
  =
  \exp\!\left\{\ii q\int_{\gamma_{x,\infty}} A\right\}
\]
transforms as
\[
  W_{q,\gamma}(x)
  \longmapsto
  \ee^{\ii q(\alpha(\infty)-\alpha(x))}W_{q,\gamma}(x).
\]
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \Psi_{q,\gamma}(x)=\psi_q(x)W_{q,\gamma}(x)
  \longmapsto
  \ee^{\ii q\alpha(\infty)}\Psi_{q,\gamma}(x).
  \label{eq:charged-wilson-line-boundary-transformation}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:charged-wilson-line-boundary-transformation} is the
precise sense in which the operator is invariant under gauge transformations
trivial at infinity and carries charge \(q\) under the boundary gauge
transformation.  The boundary phase is not a gauge redundancy; it is the
global charge action in the charged sector.

For a nonabelian gauge field \(A=A_\mu^a t^a_R\dd x^\mu\) and a matter field
\(\psi_R(x)\) in representation \(R\), choose the parallel transporter
\[
  U_R(\infty,x;\gamma)
  =
  P\exp\!\left\{\ii\int_{\gamma_{x,\infty}} A_\mu^a t^a_R\,\dd x^\mu\right\}.
\]
Under a gauge transformation \(g\),
\[
  U_R(\infty,x;\gamma)
  \longmapsto
  g(\infty)U_R(\infty,x;\gamma)g(x)^{-1}.
\]
Thus \(U_R(\infty,x;\gamma)\psi_R(x)\) transforms only by \(g(\infty)\).  In
a sector where the boundary gauge group is treated as a global symmetry, this
is a charged operator; in a confining theory with no colored asymptotic
sectors, it is a noncompact string-like excitation rather than an external
particle creator.  This distinction is dynamical and cannot be read off from
the formal gauge covariance alone.

\subsection{Ray Dressings as Gauge-Slice Coordinates}

The preceding covariance statement has a second, more computational,
interpretation.  A Wilson-line dressed charged coordinate is the ordinary
charged coordinate written on a gauge slice adapted to the same ray.  The
statement below is elementary in abelian gauge theory, but it is a useful
guardrail for perturbative calculations of dressed correlators.\footnote{A
recent perturbative study of finite-distance dressed observables in
electrodynamics and perturbative gravity is C.~Cheung,
A.~Sivaramakrishnan, J.~Wilson-Gerow, and L.~Zhou, \emph{On Perturbatively
Dressed Observables}, \href{https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26077}
{arXiv:2605.26077}.  The use made here is only the gauge-slice viewpoint; the
abelian identities and denominators in this subsection are derived directly.}

Let \(u^\mu\) be a fixed nonzero vector and let the half-line from \(x\) be
\[
  \gamma_{x,u}(\tau)=x+\tau u,\qquad \tau\ge0 .
\]
Work on a class of gauge potentials \(A\) and gauge functions \(\alpha\) for
which \(u^\mu A_\mu(x+\tau u)\) and
\(u^\mu\partial_\mu\alpha(x+\tau u)\) are absolutely integrable on
\([0,\infty)\) and for which
\[
  \alpha(x+\tau u)\longrightarrow0,
  \qquad
  u^\mu A_\mu(x+\tau u)\longrightarrow0
  \qquad(\tau\to\infty)
\]
along every ray under consideration.  Define the line functional
\begin{equation}
  \chi_u[A](x)
  =
  \int_0^\infty \dd\tau\,u^\mu A_\mu(x+\tau u).
  \label{eq:ray-dressing-line-functional}
\end{equation}
For the abelian gauge convention
\[
  A_\mu\longmapsto A_\mu+\partial_\mu\alpha,
  \qquad
  \psi_q\longmapsto \ee^{\ii q\alpha}\psi_q ,
\]
one has
\[
  \chi_u[A+\dd\alpha](x)
  =
  \chi_u[A](x)-\alpha(x).
\]
Hence
\begin{equation}
  \Psi_{q,u}(x)
  =
  \ee^{\ii q\chi_u[A](x)}\psi_q(x)
  \label{eq:ray-dressed-charged-coordinate}
\end{equation}
is invariant under gauge transformations that are trivial at the ray endpoint.

\begin{proposition}[Ray dressing and axial gauge]
\label{prop:ray-dressing-axial-gauge-coordinate}
For every \(A\) in the class above, set
\[
  A_\mu^{[u]}(x)=A_\mu(x)+\partial_\mu\chi_u[A](x),
  \qquad
  \psi_q^{[u]}(x)=\ee^{\ii q\chi_u[A](x)}\psi_q(x).
\]
Then
\[
  u^\mu A_\mu^{[u]}(x)=0,
  \qquad
  \psi_q^{[u]}(x)=\Psi_{q,u}(x).
  \label{eq:ray-dressing-axial-coordinate-equality}
\]
Thus a dressed charged insertion may be computed, in any finite regularized
perturbative path integral for which this gauge slice and the corresponding
change of variables have been defined with their regulator and
Faddeev--Popov factors, as the ordinary charged field inserted in the gauge
\(u^\mu A_\mu=0\) with the same endpoint prescription.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate \eqref{eq:ray-dressing-line-functional} in the ray direction:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  u^\nu\partial_\nu\chi_u[A](x)
  &=
  \int_0^\infty\dd\tau\,
  \frac{d}{d\tau}\bigl(u^\mu A_\mu(x+\tau u)\bigr)
  \\
  &=
  \lim_{T\to\infty}u^\mu A_\mu(x+Tu)-u^\mu A_\mu(x)
  =
  -u^\mu A_\mu(x).
\end{aligned}
\]
Therefore
\[
  u^\mu A_\mu^{[u]}(x)
  =
  u^\mu A_\mu(x)+u^\mu\partial_\mu\chi_u[A](x)
  =
  0 .
\]
The second identity in
\eqref{eq:ray-dressing-axial-coordinate-equality} is the definition of
\(\psi_q^{[u]}\) together with \eqref{eq:ray-dressed-charged-coordinate}.
The preceding transformation law for \(\chi_u\) shows that this construction
is constant on gauge orbits once the boundary gauge value has been fixed.
\end{proof}

The perturbative singularities produced by the ray are also fixed at this
elementary level.  Insert an endpoint regulator
\[
  \chi_{u,\epsilon}[A](x)
  =
  \int_0^\infty\dd\tau\,\ee^{-\epsilon\tau}
  u^\mu A_\mu(x+\tau u),
  \qquad \epsilon>0,
\]
and use the Fourier convention
\[
  A_\mu(y)=\int\frac{\dd^D k}{(2\pi)^D}\,
  \ee^{-\ii k\cdot y}\widehat A_\mu(k).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \chi_{u,\epsilon}[A](x)
  =
  -\ii
  \int\frac{\dd^D k}{(2\pi)^D}\,
  \ee^{-\ii k\cdot x}
  \frac{u^\mu\widehat A_\mu(k)}{k\cdot u-\ii\epsilon}.
  \label{eq:finite-ray-dressing-eikonal-denominator}
\end{equation}
Indeed, substituting the Fourier transform gives
\[
  \chi_{u,\epsilon}[A](x)
  =
  \int\frac{\dd^D k}{(2\pi)^D}
  \ee^{-\ii k\cdot x}u^\mu\widehat A_\mu(k)
  \int_0^\infty\dd\tau\,
  \ee^{-(\epsilon+\ii k\cdot u)\tau},
\]
and the last integral is
\[
  \frac{1}{\epsilon+\ii k\cdot u}
  =
  -\frac{\ii}{k\cdot u-\ii\epsilon}.
\]
Expanding the exponential in
\eqref{eq:ray-dressed-charged-coordinate} therefore inserts the distribution
\((k\cdot u-\ii0)^{-1}\) for every photon emitted from the line.  These
linear denominators are part of the nonlocal composite operator and of the
corresponding axial-gauge coordinate system.  They must be treated as
additional singular hypersurfaces in loop integrals involving dressed
finite-distance correlators.  They do not, by themselves, supply the
large-time Haag--Ruelle estimate or the infrared representation theory
required for charged scattering; those are the separate problems formulated
below and in Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-infrared-inclusive-qed}.

\subsection{The Missing Haag--Ruelle Estimate}

Ordinary Haag--Ruelle theory uses two facts at large time: the one-particle
part of \(B_t(h)\vac\) is time independent, and commutators of creators with
disjoint velocity supports are rapidly decreasing because the creators are
almost local.  A Wilson-line charged creator has noncompact localization, so
the second fact must be replaced rather than reused.

A charged dressing class \(\mathcal D_{\rm dress}\) suitable for a
Haag--Ruelle-type
construction would have to provide, for each charged momentum packet \(h\),
operators \(\Psi_{\Gamma,t}(h)\) with dressing datum
\(\Gamma=(q,\gamma,\text{asymptotic flux})\) such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the vectors \(\Psi_{\Gamma,t}(h)\vac\) have a controlled one-particle
or threshold spectral component with the desired charge and velocity support;
\item the commutator of two velocity-separated dressed creators has an
explicit large-time form
\[
  [\Psi_{\Gamma_i,t}(h_i),\Psi_{\Gamma_j,t}(h_j)]
  =
  \ii\,\Theta_{ij}(t)\,
  \Psi_{\Gamma_j,t}(h_j)\Psi_{\Gamma_i,t}(h_i)
  +R_{ij}(t),
  \qquad
  \int^\infty \|R_{ij}(t)\|\,\dd t<\infty ,
  \label{eq:dressed-hr-commutator-template}
\]
or a mathematically equivalent Dollard/Faddeev--Kulish modification of the
large-time dynamics;
\item the phase \(\Theta_{ij}(t)\), when present, is determined by the
asymptotic flux data and is included in the comparison dynamics, not left as
an error term;
\item changes of dressing that preserve the asymptotic flux act as changes of
interpolating coordinates, whereas changes of asymptotic flux change the
charged superselection sector.
\end{enumerate}
Condition~\eqref{eq:dressed-hr-commutator-template} is deliberately written
as a requirement, not as a theorem.  It is the point at which the proof of
ordinary Haag--Ruelle theory uses almost locality, and it is the point that a
charged-sector replacement must supply.  If the gauge field is screened or
massive, the long-range phase may be absent and ordinary massive scattering
estimates can be recovered for gauge-invariant composite charged operators.
For an unscreened Gauss-law charge, the flux tail is part of the asymptotic
datum.

\subsection{Finite-Regulator Dressed LSZ}

There is nevertheless a precise finite-regulator statement.  It is not the
full massless charged scattering theorem; it is the part of the desired theory
that follows from the usual LSZ argument once a dressed charged creator and an
isolated charged shell have been supplied.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-regulator dressed LSZ]
\label{thm:finite-regulator-dressed-charged-lsz}
Fix a gauge theory with a regulator or screening deformation for which:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the physical Hilbert space contains an isolated massive charged
one-particle shell \(\Sigma_m^+\) in a charged sector;
\item for each external species there is a translation-covariant
gauge-invariant dressed operator \(\Psi_a(x)\) of the form
\eqref{eq:charged-wilson-line-dressed-field}, or a Coulombic variant, whose
smearing is defined on a common dense domain;
\item the dressed two-point distribution has, in a neighborhood of
\(\Sigma_m^+\), the pole decomposition
\[
  \widehat G_{ab}(p)
  =
  \int \dd^D x\,\ee^{\ii p\cdot x}
  \langle\vac|T\{\Psi_a(x)\Psi_b^\dagger(0)\}|\vac\rangle
  =
  \frac{\ii Z_{ab}}{p^2+m^2-\ii0}
  +\widehat G^{\rm reg}_{ab}(p),
  \label{eq:dressed-two-point-pole}
\]
where \(Z=(Z_{ab})\) is a positive matrix on the one-particle multiplicity
space and \(\widehat G^{\rm reg}\) is less singular at the shell;
\item the large-time wave operators built from these dressed creators exist,
and time-ordered dressed correlators have the usual boundary-value and
wave-packet regularity needed for the LSZ integration by parts.
\end{enumerate}
Let \(Z^{-1/2}\) be the positive inverse square root on
\(\operatorname{Ran}Z\).  Then the simultaneous external residue of the dressed
time-ordered \((m+n)\)-point function equals the matrix element of the
scattering operator between the dressed asymptotic charged states:
\[
\begin{aligned}
&\prod_{r=1}^m
  \bigl[(Z^{-1/2})_{a'_r b'_r}(p'_r{}^2+m_r^2)\bigr]
 \prod_{s=1}^n
  \bigl[(p_s^2+m_s^2)(Z^{-1/2})_{b_s a_s}\bigr]
  \,\widehat G^{\,b'_1\cdots b'_m}{}_{b_1\cdots b_n}
     (p'_1,\ldots;p_1,\ldots)
\\
&\qquad\longrightarrow
  \langle
    p'_1,a'_1;\ldots;p'_m,a'_m,{\rm out}
  |S|
    p_1,a_1;\ldots;p_n,a_n,{\rm in}
  \rangle_{\rm conn},
  \label{eq:finite-regulator-dressed-lsz-formula}
\end{aligned}
\]
after wave-packet smearing and removal of the total momentum-conservation
delta distribution, with the same connected/disconnected convention as in the
ordinary LSZ chapter.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof is the LSZ proof with \(\Psi_a\) as the interpolating operator.
The pole decomposition \eqref{eq:dressed-two-point-pole} identifies the
one-particle projection:
\[
  P_1\Psi_a(f)\vac
  =
  \sum_b (Z^{1/2})_a{}^b\,|f,b\rangle
\]
in the mass-shell spectral representation, after restricting the Fourier
support of \(f\) to the isolating neighborhood.  Multiplication by
\((p^2+m^2)\) in momentum space is the Fourier transform of applying the
Klein--Gordon operator to the external coordinate.  In position space,
integrating this differential operator against a positive-frequency
wave-packet converts the time-ordered derivative into boundary terms at
large positive or negative times; interior terms either cancel against the
equation of motion away from contact supports or are regular at the external
mass shell and vanish after multiplication by \((p^2+m^2)\).  The assumed
large-time limits identify the boundary terms with the incoming and outgoing
dressed wave operators.  Finally \(Z^{-1/2}\) changes from the dressed
interpolating coordinates to orthonormal one-particle coordinates.  This gives
\eqref{eq:finite-regulator-dressed-lsz-formula}.  Locality of bounded-region
observables is not used to turn \(\Psi_a\) into an ordinary local observable;
all nonlocal dressing information is carried by the hypotheses on the dressed
wave operators and correlators.
\end{proof}

The theorem is useful because it isolates what is known from what remains
open.  At finite infrared regulator or in a screened phase, the formal
dressed-correlator residue has a precise Hilbert-space meaning if the listed
hypotheses hold.  Removing the regulator in four-dimensional QED is a
different operation: the pole in \eqref{eq:dressed-two-point-pole} is expected
to be replaced by charged threshold behavior, and the asymptotic Hilbert space
must remember the angular electric-flux sector.

\subsection{Dressing Dependence as Coordinate Dependence}

Path dependence has two different meanings.  A deformation that changes the
field at infinity changes the charged sector.  A compact deformation, or a
replacement of one short-distance dressing by another with the same
asymptotic flux, changes the interpolating coordinate used to reach the same
charged state.

\begin{proposition}[Compact dressing changes and LSZ coordinates]
\label{prop:compact-dressing-change-lsz-coordinate}
In the finite-regulator setting of
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-regulator-dressed-charged-lsz}, let
\(\Psi_a\) and \(\Psi'_a\) be two families of dressed charged interpolating
operators with the same charge, spin, mass, and asymptotic flux.  Suppose
their one-particle matrix elements are related by an invertible matrix \(M\),
\[
  P_1\Psi'_a(f)\vac
  =
  M_a{}^b\,P_1\Psi_b(f)\vac
  +\text{terms orthogonal to }\Hilb_1
\]
for all wave packets \(f\) supported near the shell.  Then the LSZ residues
computed with \(\Psi'\) give the same scattering matrix after the external
normalization matrix \(Z'^{-1/2}\) is used.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The singular part of the two-point function transforms as
\[
  Z' = M Z M^\dagger .
\]
The amputated external coordinates obtained from \(\Psi'\) are therefore
related to those obtained from \(\Psi\) by the unitary polar part of
\(MZ^{1/2}\) acting inside the one-particle multiplicity space.  LSZ computes
matrix elements between orthonormal one-particle vectors, not between the raw
operator labels.  After multiplying by \(Z'^{-1/2}\), this coordinate change
is removed on every external leg.  Contributions orthogonal to \(\Hilb_1\)
are regular at the isolated shell and vanish in the external residue.
\end{proof}

For Wilson lines, a compact path deformation with fixed endpoints multiplies
the operator by a neutral Wilson loop spanning the narrow surface between the
two paths, up to regulator choices.  Proposition
\ref{prop:compact-dressing-change-lsz-coordinate} explains the limited sense
in which such a deformation can leave a finite-regulator \(S\)-matrix
unchanged.  It does not prove independence under changes of the asymptotic
ray, because those changes modify the electric flux at infinity.  In massless
QED the asymptotic ray or Coulomb profile is tied to the charged velocity, and
changing it changes the infrared representation rather than merely the
interpolating coordinate.

\subsection{Asymptotic Wilson Lines, Flux, and Eikonal Data}

The preceding definitions identify a charged dressing as part of the
asymptotic datum.  We now spell out the elementary calculation behind this
statement.  Work in four spacetime dimensions with an abelian gauge field, and
let a massive charged particle have charge \(q\), momentum \(p^\mu\), mass
\(m>0\), and four-velocity
\[
  u^\mu=\frac{p^\mu}{m},
  \qquad u_\mu u^\mu=-1,
  \qquad p^0>0 .
\]
The future-directed straight dressing from \(x\) is represented, before the
infrared limit is taken, by the regulated current
\begin{equation}
  J_{q,u,\epsilon}^\mu(y;x)
  =
  q\int_0^\infty \dd\tau\,
  \ee^{-\epsilon\tau}u^\mu
  \delta^{(4)}(y-x-u\tau),
  \qquad \epsilon>0 .
  \label{eq:regulated-asymptotic-worldline-current}
\end{equation}
The corresponding Wilson factor is the exponential of
\(\ii\int A_\mu J^\mu\).  The factor \(\epsilon\) is not a physical photon
mass; it is an adiabatic endpoint regulator used to define the half-line
Fourier transform.  The endpoint at \(\tau=0\) is the charged field insertion,
and the endpoint at \(\tau=\infty\) is the boundary charge recorded by
\eqref{eq:charged-wilson-line-boundary-transformation}.

\begin{proposition}[Worldline dressing gives the soft eikonal denominator]
\label{prop:worldline-dressing-eikonal-denominator}
With the Fourier convention
\[
  \widehat J^\mu(k)=\int\dd^4y\,\ee^{\ii k\cdot y}J^\mu(y),
\]
the regulated current \eqref{eq:regulated-asymptotic-worldline-current} has
\[
  \widehat J_{q,u,\epsilon}^\mu(k;x)
  =
  q\,u^\mu\,\ee^{\ii k\cdot x}
  \frac{1}{\epsilon-\ii k\cdot u}.
  \label{eq:worldline-current-fourier-transform}
\]
For an on-shell soft photon of momentum \(k\) and physical polarization
\(e_h(k)\), the homogeneous soft factor carried by the dressing is therefore
\begin{equation}
  f_{p,\epsilon}^h(k)
  =
  q\,\frac{p\cdot e_h^*(k)}{\epsilon m-\ii p\cdot k}\,
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot x}.
  \label{eq:charged-dressed-fk-profile}
\end{equation}
After the endpoint prescription is fixed, this is the same eikonal
denominator that appears in the leading Faddeev--Kulish soft cloud and in
the leading soft-photon theorem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Substituting \eqref{eq:regulated-asymptotic-worldline-current} gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \widehat J_{q,u,\epsilon}^\mu(k;x)
  &=
  q u^\mu
  \int_0^\infty\dd\tau\,
  \ee^{-\epsilon\tau}
  \ee^{\ii k\cdot(x+u\tau)}
  \\
  &=
  q u^\mu\ee^{\ii k\cdot x}
  \int_0^\infty\dd\tau\,
  \ee^{-(\epsilon-\ii k\cdot u)\tau}
  =
  q u^\mu\ee^{\ii k\cdot x}
  \frac{1}{\epsilon-\ii k\cdot u}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Since \(p^\mu=mu^\mu\), contraction with \(e_h^*(k)\) gives
\eqref{eq:charged-dressed-fk-profile}.  The sign of the infinitesimal
imaginary part depends on whether the half-line is future or past directed
and on the incoming/outgoing convention.  The load-bearing structure is the
homogeneous denominator \(p\cdot k\), not the regulator sign.
\end{proof}

The same charged sector can be described at spacelike infinity by its
electric flux.  If \(\mathbf v=\mathbf p/p^0\) and
\(\mathbf n\in S^2\), the boosted Coulomb radial flux density is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal E_{q,\mathbf v}(\mathbf n)
  =
  \frac{q}{4\pi}
  \frac{1-|\mathbf v|^2}{(1-\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf n)^2}.
  \label{eq:charged-boosted-coulomb-flux-density-hr}
\end{equation}
It defines the value of the limiting flux functional
\[
  \Phi_\infty(f)=
  \int_{S^2}\dd\Omega(\mathbf n)\,
  f(\mathbf n)\mathcal E_{q,\mathbf v}(\mathbf n)
\]
on a one-particle charged asymptotic datum with velocity \(\mathbf v\).

\begin{proposition}[Velocity-labelled flux sectors]
\label{prop:velocity-labelled-flux-sectors}
For \(|\mathbf v|<1\),
\[
  \int_{S^2}\dd\Omega(\mathbf n)\,
  \mathcal E_{q,\mathbf v}(\mathbf n)
  =
  q .
  \label{eq:boosted-coulomb-flux-integral}
\]
If \(q\ne0\), the function
\(\mathcal E_{q,\mathbf v}(\mathbf n)\) determines \(\mathbf v\).  Thus
different charged velocities carry different sharp angular flux data.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Rotate coordinates so that \(\mathbf v=\beta\mathbf e_3\).  Then
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \int_{S^2}\dd\Omega\,
  \frac{1-\beta^2}{(1-\beta\cos\theta)^2}
  &=
  2\pi(1-\beta^2)
  \int_{-1}^{1}\frac{\dd z}{(1-\beta z)^2}
  \\
  &=
  2\pi(1-\beta^2)
  \left[\frac{1}{\beta(1-\beta z)}\right]_{z=-1}^{z=1}
  =
  4\pi ,
\end{aligned}
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:boosted-coulomb-flux-integral}.  For \(q\ne0\), the
maximum of \(\mathcal E_{q,\mathbf v}\) occurs in the direction of
\(\mathbf v\) when \(\mathbf v\ne0\), while the ratio of the maximum to the
minimum is
\[
  \left(\frac{1+|\mathbf v|}{1-|\mathbf v|}\right)^2 .
\]
This ratio determines \(|\mathbf v|\), and the direction of the maximum
determines the direction of \(\mathbf v\).  The case \(\mathbf v=0\) is the
unique rotationally invariant density.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Soft Coherent Norm and Velocity Superselection}

The preceding flux density is visible already in the finite-cutoff soft
photon Fock calculation.  The calculation is not a construction of massless
QED scattering; it is a precise diagnostic of why a single ordinary photon
Fock representation cannot contain sharp charged particles of all velocities
as normal vectors after the infrared cutoff is removed.

Fix \(0<\lambda<\Lambda<\infty\).  The finite soft-photon one-particle space
is
\[
  \mathfrak h_{\lambda,\Lambda}
  =
  L^2\!\left(
    [\lambda,\Lambda]\times S^2,
    \frac{r\,\dd r\,\dd\Omega(\mathbf n)}{2(2\pi)^3};
    \mathbb C^2
  \right),
\]
where \(k=(r,r\mathbf n)\) is null and the two complex components label a
measurable orthonormal transverse polarization frame
\(\epsilon_h(\mathbf n)\), \(h=1,2\), satisfying
\(\mathbf n\cdot\epsilon_h(\mathbf n)=0\).  The polarization frame is a
coordinate choice; the sum over \(h\) below is the transverse projector
\[
  P_{\mathbf n}^{ij}=\delta^{ij}-n^in^j .
\]
For a massive charge \(q\) with velocity \(\mathbf v\), \(|\mathbf v|<1\), the
homogeneous soft part of the worldline dressing
\eqref{eq:charged-dressed-fk-profile} is represented by
\begin{equation}
  F_{q,\mathbf v,\lambda,\Lambda}^{h}(r,\mathbf n)
  =
  -q\,\frac{\mathbf v\cdot\epsilon_h(\mathbf n)}
           {r(1-\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf n)}
  \mathbf 1_{\lambda<r<\Lambda}.
  \label{eq:charged-soft-coherent-profile-velocity}
\end{equation}
The mass and Lorentz factor cancel because
\[
  \frac{p\cdot e_h(k)}{p\cdot k}
  =
  -\frac{\mathbf v\cdot\epsilon_h(\mathbf n)}
          {r(1-\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf n)}
\]
for \(e_h^0=0\), \(p^\mu=m\gamma(1,\mathbf v)\), and the mostly-plus
Minkowski product.

\begin{proposition}[Velocity separation in the soft coherent norm]
\label{prop:soft-coherent-velocity-separation}
For two velocities \(\mathbf v,\mathbf w\in B_1(0)\subset\mathbb R^3\) and the
same nonzero charge \(q\),
\[
  \left\|
    F_{q,\mathbf v,\lambda,\Lambda}
    -
    F_{q,\mathbf w,\lambda,\Lambda}
  \right\|_{\mathfrak h_{\lambda,\Lambda}}^2
  =
  \frac{q^2}{2(2\pi)^3}
  \log\frac{\Lambda}{\lambda}\,
  \mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w),
  \label{eq:soft-coherent-velocity-log-norm}
\]
where
\[
  \mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w)
  =
  \int_{S^2}\dd\Omega(\mathbf n)\,
  \left|
    P_{\mathbf n}
    \left(
      \frac{\mathbf v}{1-\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf n}
      -
      \frac{\mathbf w}{1-\mathbf w\cdot\mathbf n}
    \right)
  \right|^2 .
  \label{eq:soft-coherent-angular-coefficient}
\]
The coefficient obeys \(\mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w)\ge0\), and
\(\mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w)=0\) if and only if
\(\mathbf v=\mathbf w\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The radial dependence of
\eqref{eq:charged-soft-coherent-profile-velocity} is \(r^{-1}\).  Therefore
the measure \(r\,\dd r\,\dd\Omega/(2(2\pi)^3)\) gives the logarithm
\[
  \int_\lambda^\Lambda \frac{\dd r}{r}=\log\frac{\Lambda}{\lambda}.
\]
After summing over the two physical polarizations,
\[
  \sum_{h=1}^2
  \left|
    \left(
      \frac{\mathbf v}{1-\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf n}
      -
      \frac{\mathbf w}{1-\mathbf w\cdot\mathbf n}
    \right)
    \cdot\epsilon_h(\mathbf n)
  \right|^2
  =
  \left|
    P_{\mathbf n}
    \left(
      \frac{\mathbf v}{1-\mathbf v\cdot\mathbf n}
      -
      \frac{\mathbf w}{1-\mathbf w\cdot\mathbf n}
    \right)
  \right|^2 ,
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:soft-coherent-velocity-log-norm}.  Nonnegativity is
immediate from the displayed square.

It remains to characterize the zero set.  If
\(\mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w)=0\), continuity implies that the displayed
transverse projection vanishes for every \(\mathbf n\).  Suppose first that
\(\mathbf v\ne0\).  Put
\(\mathbf n=\mathbf v/|\mathbf v|\).  The projection of \(\mathbf v\) onto
the plane orthogonal to \(\mathbf n\) is zero, hence the projection of
\(\mathbf w\) onto that plane must also vanish; \(\mathbf w\) is parallel to
\(\mathbf v\).  Writing
\(\mathbf v=\beta\mathbf e_3\) and
\(\mathbf w=\alpha\mathbf e_3\), and choosing any
\(\mathbf n\) not parallel to \(\mathbf e_3\), the vanishing condition becomes
\[
  \frac{\beta}{1-\beta n_3}
  =
  \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha n_3}.
\]
Thus \(\beta=\alpha\).  If \(\mathbf v=0\), the same condition says that the
transverse projection of \(\mathbf w\) vanishes for every \(\mathbf n\); this
forces \(\mathbf w=0\).  Hence the coefficient vanishes exactly on the
diagonal \(\mathbf v=\mathbf w\).
\end{proof}

Let \(\mathcal E(F)\) denote the normalized bosonic coherent vector in the
finite-cutoff photon Fock space,
\[
  \mathcal E(F)
  =
  \ee^{-\|F\|^2/2}
  \bigoplus_{n\ge0}\frac{F^{\otimes_s n}}{\sqrt{n!}} .
\]
Summing the Fock inner products gives
\[
  \left|
    \langle \mathcal E(F),\mathcal E(G)\rangle
  \right|
  =
  \exp\!\left\{-\frac12\|F-G\|^2\right\}.
\]
Therefore Proposition~\ref{prop:soft-coherent-velocity-separation} implies
that, for \(\mathbf v\ne\mathbf w\),
\[
  \left|
    \left\langle
      \mathcal E(F_{q,\mathbf v,\lambda,\Lambda}),
      \mathcal E(F_{q,\mathbf w,\lambda,\Lambda})
    \right\rangle
  \right|
  =
  \exp\!\left\{
    -\frac{q^2}{4(2\pi)^3}
    \log\frac{\Lambda}{\lambda}\,
    \mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w)
  \right\}
  \longrightarrow0
  \qquad(\lambda\downarrow0).
\]
This is the finite-Fock-space calculation behind velocity-labelled charged
sectors.  It is stronger than saying that a bare electron emits many soft
photons: different charged velocities determine different infrared coherent
directions.  A Haag--Ruelle replacement for massless charged sectors must
therefore build its asymptotic representation with the charged velocity and
flux label included from the start.

\subsection{Weyl Implementation and the Failure of a Common Photon Fock Space}

The preceding norm calculation has an operator-algebraic meaning.  For
\(0<\Lambda<\infty\), let \(\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\) be the photon
one-particle space obtained by replacing the interval
\([\lambda,\Lambda]\) in \(\mathfrak h_{\lambda,\Lambda}\) by
\((0,\Lambda]\).  For each \(\lambda>0\),
\(\mathfrak h_{\lambda,\Lambda}\) is a closed subspace of
\(\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\) by extension by zero on \((0,\lambda]\).  Let
\[
  \mathcal F_{0,\Lambda}=\mathcal F_s(\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda})
\]
be the corresponding fixed photon Fock space, and let
\[
  W(f)=\exp\!\left(a^\dagger(f)-a(f)\right),
  \qquad f\in\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda},
\]
be the Weyl operator.  With the real symplectic form
\[
  \sigma(f,g)=2\,\operatorname{Im}\langle f,g\rangle_{\mathfrak h},
\]
the Weyl relations are
\begin{equation}
  W(f)W(g)=
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{\ii}{2}\sigma(f,g)\right)W(f+g),
  \qquad
  W(f)^*=W(-f),
  \label{eq:charged-sector-weyl-relations}
\end{equation}
and the Fock vacuum state is
\[
  \omega_0(W(f))=\langle\Omega,W(f)\Omega\rangle
  =
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|f\|_{\mathfrak h}^2\right).
\]
The coherent state with soft profile \(F\) is
\[
  \omega_F(A)=
  \langle \mathcal E(F),A\mathcal E(F)\rangle
  =
  \langle W(F)\Omega,A W(F)\Omega\rangle .
\]
On Weyl generators this gives
\begin{equation}
  \omega_F(W(f))
  =
  \exp\!\left(\ii\sigma(F,f)\right)
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|f\|_{\mathfrak h}^2\right).
  \label{eq:coherent-weyl-state-characteristic-functional}
\end{equation}
Thus the soft cloud changes the representation by adding a linear phase on
the Weyl algebra.  At finite cutoff this phase is inner, implemented by the
unitary \(W(F)\).  The infrared question is whether the implementing unitaries
have a limit on one fixed Fock representation as \(\lambda\downarrow0\).

\begin{proposition}[No common Fock implementer for distinct charged velocities]
\label{prop:no-common-fock-implementer-distinct-velocities}
Fix \(q\ne0\), \(0<\Lambda<\infty\), and two distinct velocities
\(\mathbf v,\mathbf w\in B_1(0)\).  Put
\[
  D_{\lambda,\Lambda}
  =
  F_{q,\mathbf v,\lambda,\Lambda}
  -
  F_{q,\mathbf w,\lambda,\Lambda}.
\]
Then \(\|D_{\lambda,\Lambda}\|\to\infty\) as
\(\lambda\downarrow0\).  Consequently the finite-cutoff Weyl implementers
\(W(D_{\lambda,\Lambda})\) have no strong operator limit, and no nonzero weak
operator limit, on the fixed photon Fock representation.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The divergence of \(\|D_{\lambda,\Lambda}\|\) is exactly
Proposition~\ref{prop:soft-coherent-velocity-separation}: for
\(\mathbf v\ne\mathbf w\),
\[
  \|D_{\lambda,\Lambda}\|^2
  =
  \frac{q^2}{2(2\pi)^3}
  \log\frac{\Lambda}{\lambda}\,
  \mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w),
  \qquad
  \mathcal A(\mathbf v,\mathbf w)>0 .
\]
It remains to translate this divergence into an operator statement.  Let
\(\mathcal D_{\rm fin}\) be the dense subspace of finite-particle vectors.
Matrix elements of \(W(D)\) between vectors in
\(\mathcal D_{\rm fin}\) are finite sums of polynomials in the finitely many
inner products \(\langle f_i,D\rangle\) and their complex conjugates,
multiplied by the vacuum Gaussian factor
\[
  \langle\Omega,W(D)\Omega\rangle
  =
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|D\|^2\right).
\]
This follows by commuting \(a(f)\) and \(a^\dagger(f)\) through \(W(D)\), or
equivalently by differentiating the Weyl relation
\eqref{eq:charged-sector-weyl-relations} with respect to finite test
directions.  Polynomial growth cannot compensate the Gaussian decay.  Hence
for every \(\xi,\eta\in\mathcal D_{\rm fin}\),
\[
  \langle\xi,W(D_{\lambda,\Lambda})\eta\rangle
  \longrightarrow0
  \qquad(\lambda\downarrow0).
\]
The operators \(W(D_{\lambda,\Lambda})\) are unitary and therefore uniformly
bounded, so density of \(\mathcal D_{\rm fin}\) gives weak convergence to zero
on all Fock vectors.  A strong limit of unitaries, if it existed, would have
the same weak limit and would therefore be the zero operator; but a strong
limit of unitaries is an isometry on every vector whose norm limit exists:
\[
  \|U\eta\|=\lim_{\lambda\downarrow0}
  \|W(D_{\lambda,\Lambda})\eta\|=\|\eta\|.
\]
The zero operator is not an isometry.  Thus no strong limit exists.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Hilbert-equivalent soft dressings]
\label{def:hilbert-equivalent-soft-dressings}
Fix the ultraviolet cutoff \(\Lambda\) and use the photon one-particle space
\(\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\) introduced above.  Let
\[
  F_{\lambda},G_{\lambda}\in\mathfrak h_{\lambda,\Lambda}
  \subset\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda},
  \qquad 0<\lambda<\Lambda,
\]
be two infrared-regulated soft profiles for the same charged insertion.  They
are called Hilbert-equivalent at the cutoff \(\Lambda\) if there exists a
vector \(\Delta\in\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\) such that
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{\lambda\downarrow0}
  \|G_{\lambda}-F_{\lambda}-\Delta\|_{\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}}
  =
  0 .
  \label{eq:soft-profile-hilbert-equivalence}
\end{equation}
The vector \(\Delta\) is the finite soft-coordinate change between the two
dressings.  This definition is deliberately a Hilbert-space definition: it
does not identify dressings whose difference changes the limiting Gauss-law
flux, because such a difference is not represented by a vector of
\(\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Hilbert soft changes are inner Weyl coordinate changes]
\label{prop:hilbert-soft-changes-inner-weyl-coordinate}
Let \(F_{\lambda}\) and \(G_{\lambda}\) be Hilbert-equivalent soft profiles in
the sense of Definition~\ref{def:hilbert-equivalent-soft-dressings}, and put
\[
  \Delta_{\lambda}=G_{\lambda}-F_{\lambda}.
\]
For every finite Weyl polynomial \(A\) in the generators \(W(f)\),
\(f\in\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\),
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{G_{\lambda}}(A)
  -
  \omega_{F_{\lambda}}\!\left(
    W(\Delta_{\lambda})^* A W(\Delta_{\lambda})
  \right)
  =
  0 ,
  \label{eq:finite-cutoff-soft-coordinate-inner-equality}
\end{equation}
and the implementing unitaries have the strong limit
\[
  W(\Delta_{\lambda})
  \longrightarrow
  W(\Delta)
  \qquad\text{on }\mathcal F_{0,\Lambda}.
\]
Consequently
\[
  |\langle\mathcal E(F_{\lambda}),\mathcal E(G_{\lambda})\rangle|
  =
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|\Delta_{\lambda}\|^2\right)
  \longrightarrow
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|\Delta\|^2\right)>0 .
\]
Thus a Hilbert-equivalent soft change is a finite reparametrization of the
same infrared representation, not a change of charged sector.  The velocity
change in
Proposition~\ref{prop:no-common-fock-implementer-distinct-velocities} fails
this criterion because
\(\|F_{q,\mathbf v,\lambda,\Lambda}
-F_{q,\mathbf w,\lambda,\Lambda}\|\to\infty\) for
\(\mathbf v\ne\mathbf w\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The equality \eqref{eq:finite-cutoff-soft-coordinate-inner-equality} is first
checked on one Weyl generator.  The Weyl relations give
\[
  W(\Delta_{\lambda})^*W(f)W(\Delta_{\lambda})
  =
  \exp\!\left(\ii\sigma(\Delta_{\lambda},f)\right)W(f),
\]
and hence, using
\eqref{eq:coherent-weyl-state-characteristic-functional},
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \omega_{F_{\lambda}}\!\left(
    W(\Delta_{\lambda})^*W(f)W(\Delta_{\lambda})
  \right)
  &=
  \exp\!\left(\ii\sigma(\Delta_{\lambda},f)\right)
  \exp\!\left(\ii\sigma(F_{\lambda},f)\right)
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|f\|^2\right)
\\
  &=
  \exp\!\left(\ii\sigma(G_{\lambda},f)\right)
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|f\|^2\right)
  =
  \omega_{G_{\lambda}}(W(f)).
\end{aligned}
\]
Linearity and multiplication by the Weyl relations extend this identity to
finite Weyl polynomials.

It remains to prove the strong convergence of the implementers.  The coherent
vectors
\[
  \mathcal E(K)
  =
  \ee^{-\|K\|^2/2}
  \bigoplus_{n\ge0}\frac{K^{\otimes_s n}}{\sqrt{n!}},
  \qquad K\in\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda},
\]
span a dense subspace of \(\mathcal F_{0,\Lambda}\).  On such vectors,
\[
  W(H)\mathcal E(K)
  =
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{\ii}{2}\sigma(H,K)\right)
  \mathcal E(K+H),
\]
because \(\mathcal E(K)=W(K)\Omega\) and
\eqref{eq:charged-sector-weyl-relations} gives
\(W(H)W(K)=\exp[-\ii\sigma(H,K)/2]W(H+K)\).  If \(H_n\to H\) in
\(\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\), the displayed formula and the coherent-vector
inner product
\[
  \langle\mathcal E(K_1),\mathcal E(K_2)\rangle
  =
  \exp\!\left(
    -\frac12\|K_1\|^2-\frac12\|K_2\|^2+\langle K_1,K_2\rangle
  \right)
\]
show that \(W(H_n)\mathcal E(K)\to W(H)\mathcal E(K)\) in norm.  Since all
\(W(H_n)\) and \(W(H)\) are unitary, density upgrades convergence on coherent
vectors to strong convergence on the whole Fock space.  Applying this to
\(H_n=\Delta_{\lambda_n}\) along every sequence
\(\lambda_n\downarrow0\) proves
\[
  W(\Delta_{\lambda})\to W(\Delta)
  \quad\text{strongly}.
\]
The overlap formula follows by summing the Fock inner products of coherent
vectors, and the limiting overlap is nonzero because
\(\Delta\in\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\) has finite norm.  The last assertion is
then exactly
Proposition~\ref{prop:no-common-fock-implementer-distinct-velocities}.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:no-common-fock-implementer-distinct-velocities} is a
precise version of the representation-theoretic obstruction.  At every
positive infrared cutoff, changing the soft cloud is an ordinary Weyl
displacement on Fock space.  Removing the cutoff separates two cases.  A
Hilbert-equivalent change remains an inner coordinate change by
Proposition~\ref{prop:hilbert-soft-changes-inner-weyl-coordinate}.  A change
of charged velocity escapes to infinity in the one-particle norm and is not
implemented on the original Fock representation.  Therefore a charged
asymptotic Hilbert space must be organized as a direct integral or measurable
field of infrared representations labelled by the limiting flux data, not as
a single photon Fock space tensored with a Wigner one-particle electron
space.  This is still not the missing Haag--Ruelle theorem, but it is one of
the structural inputs that theorem must contain.

The flux statement and the eikonal statement are two aspects of the same
nonlocal charged datum.  The Wilson line fixes how the charged insertion
transforms under boundary gauge transformations; the boosted Coulomb field
records the corresponding Gauss-law flux at spacelike infinity; the
half-line Fourier transform gives the soft denominator used by perturbative
dressed-state constructions.  A local observable cannot change
\(\Phi_\infty(f)\), so a local observable cannot replace this noncompact
dressing in a Haag--Ruelle construction.  Conversely, specifying only the
formal denominator \(p\cdot k\) is not yet a scattering theorem: a full
charged-sector Haag--Ruelle theory must still construct large-time limits in
the representation labelled by the flux density
\eqref{eq:charged-boosted-coulomb-flux-density-hr}.

\subsection{Velocity-Fibered Soft Representations}

The preceding propositions rule out one tempting but incorrect construction:
one cannot keep a single photon Fock representation and implement all charged
velocity changes by limiting Weyl unitaries.  The minimal replacement is a
fibered infrared representation.  A scattering theory must still construct
large-time limits, while this representation gives the correct
representation-theoretic target for such a construction in a
nonconfining charged sector.

This qualification is essential.  The charged-sector construction discussed
here is not a proposed scattering theory for colored quarks in a confining
four-dimensional Yang--Mills theory.  It applies only when the theory has
physical charged superselection sectors with charged asymptotic data: for
example electric charge in a nonconfining abelian gauge theory, or suitable
unconfined charges on a Coulomb branch or in a deconfined regime after the
infrared and representation-theoretic hypotheses have been stated.  In a
confining theory the gauge-invariant Wilson-line operator ending at infinity
creates a state with a macroscopic flux string or fails to have a
finite-energy charged-particle limit; the physical massive one-particle
states, if present, are instead neutral hadrons or glueballs created by local
gauge-invariant operators.  Ordinary Haag--Ruelle theory is then the correct
starting point for those neutral particles.

Fix \(q\ne0\) and \(0<\Lambda<\infty\).  Let
\(\mathfrak h_{\rm reg}\subset\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}\) be a complex linear
subspace of infrared-regular photon test functions such that, for every
\(\mathbf v\in B_1(0)\), the limit
\begin{equation}
  \ell_{\mathbf v}(f)
  =
  \lim_{\lambda\downarrow0}
  \sigma(F_{q,\mathbf v,\lambda,\Lambda},f)
  \label{eq:velocity-soft-character-limit}
\end{equation}
exists for all \(f\in\mathfrak h_{\rm reg}\).  For example, the condition is
satisfied on smooth compactly supported one-photon wave packets whose radial
support is bounded away from \(r=0\).  Larger infrared test spaces require an
explicit condition on the radial behavior of \(f\); the definition above
records exactly the condition needed for the soft phase to be a finite
functional.

Let \(\mathfrak W_{\rm reg}\) be the Weyl \(*\)-algebra generated by
\(W(f)\), \(f\in\mathfrak h_{\rm reg}\), with relations
\eqref{eq:charged-sector-weyl-relations}.  The charged velocity
\(\mathbf v\) defines the state
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{\mathbf v}(W(f))
  =
  \exp\!\left(\ii\ell_{\mathbf v}(f)\right)
  \exp\!\left(-\frac12\|f\|^2_{\mathfrak h_{0,\Lambda}}\right),
  \qquad f\in\mathfrak h_{\rm reg}.
  \label{eq:velocity-fiber-soft-state}
\end{equation}
Let \((\mathcal H_{\mathbf v},\pi_{\mathbf v},\Omega_{\mathbf v})\) be the
GNS representation of \(\omega_{\mathbf v}\).  If \(\nu\) is a Borel measure
on \(B_1(0)\) for which the field
\(\mathbf v\mapsto(\mathcal H_{\mathbf v},\pi_{\mathbf v})\) has been given a
measurable structure, define
\[
  \mathcal H_{q,\nu}^{\rm soft}
  =
  \int_{B_1(0)}^{\oplus}\mathcal H_{\mathbf v}\,\dd\nu(\mathbf v),
  \qquad
  \bigl(\pi_{q,\nu}(A)\Psi\bigr)(\mathbf v)
  =
  \pi_{\mathbf v}(A)\Psi(\mathbf v).
  \label{eq:charged-soft-direct-integral-representation}
\]

\begin{proposition}[Soft photon algebra preserves the velocity fibers]
\label{prop:soft-weyl-algebra-preserves-velocity-fibers}
In the direct-integral representation
\eqref{eq:charged-soft-direct-integral-representation}, let
\(P_E\) be multiplication by the characteristic function of a Borel set
\(E\subset B_1(0)\).  Then
\[
  [P_E,\pi_{q,\nu}(A)]=0
  \qquad
  \text{for all }A\in\mathfrak W_{\rm reg}.
  \label{eq:velocity-projection-commutes-soft-algebra}
\]
Consequently, if \(E\cap E'=\varnothing\), then
\[
  \langle P_E\xi,\pi_{q,\nu}(A)P_{E'}\eta\rangle=0
  \qquad
  \text{for all }\xi,\eta\in\mathcal H_{q,\nu}^{\rm soft},
  \quad A\in\mathfrak W_{\rm reg}.
  \label{eq:soft-algebra-no-velocity-transition}
\]
Thus the soft photon Weyl algebra is diagonal in the charged velocity label.
Any charged scattering operator that changes the massive particle momentum
must include additional charged-sector dynamics; it is not an operator of the
soft photon algebra alone.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(\Psi\in\mathcal H_{q,\nu}^{\rm soft}\),
\[
  (P_E\pi_{q,\nu}(A)\Psi)(\mathbf v)
  =
  \mathbf 1_E(\mathbf v)\pi_{\mathbf v}(A)\Psi(\mathbf v)
  =
  (\pi_{q,\nu}(A)P_E\Psi)(\mathbf v)
\]
for \(\nu\)-almost every \(\mathbf v\).  This proves
\eqref{eq:velocity-projection-commutes-soft-algebra}.  If
\(E\cap E'=\varnothing\), then \(P_EP_{E'}=0\), and
\[
  \langle P_E\xi,\pi_{q,\nu}(A)P_{E'}\eta\rangle
  =
  \langle \xi,P_E\pi_{q,\nu}(A)P_{E'}\eta\rangle
  =
  \langle \xi,\pi_{q,\nu}(A)P_EP_{E'}\eta\rangle
  =
  0 .
  \]
\end{proof}

This fibered construction is the algebraic form of the velocity
superselection conclusion.  It also states a precise target for the still
missing charged Haag--Ruelle theorem: the theorem must construct incoming and
outgoing charged wave operators whose asymptotic photon part lives in a
measurable field of infrared representations labelled by the Gauss-law flux,
and whose hard charged part supplies the off-diagonal momentum-changing
dynamics.  Treating the charged soft cloud as a removable unitary dressing on
one Fock space would erase exactly the sector information proved above.

\begin{openproblem}[Haag--Ruelle and LSZ for nonconfining dressed charged sectors]
\label{op:haag-ruelle-lsz-dressed-charged-sectors}
Construct a large-time scattering theory for nonconfining gauge-theory
charged sectors using noncompact gauge-invariant charged creators.  The
construction should prove the analogue of Haag--Ruelle limits for the chosen
dressing class, identify the asymptotic flux labels, and prove a
dressed-correlator reduction formula whose external factors are the residues
or threshold coefficients of the corresponding dressed two-point functions.
The hypothesis ``nonconfining'' means that the charged sector is a genuine
finite-energy physical sector with asymptotic charged data; it excludes
colored charges in a confining phase, for which the physical scattering
problem is the neutral hadron or glueball scattering problem.  In massless QED
this problem must be combined with the infraparticle flux-sector analysis of
Chapter~\ref{ch:volume-ii-infrared-inclusive-qed}.
\end{openproblem}

\section{Wave Operators, Scattering Operator, and Completeness}

Let
\[
  \Omega_{\mathrm{in}},\Omega_{\mathrm{out}}:
  \mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)\longrightarrow\Hilb
\]
be the Haag--Ruelle wave operators, in the terminology introduced in
Chapter~\ref{ch:haag-ruelle}, produced by Haag--Ruelle limits.  They are
isometric maps.  Their construction has two equivalent topological
descriptions.  First, for each finite velocity-separated Fock vector
\[
  F=\psi_1\odot\cdots\odot\psi_n
\]
represented by regular creators \(B_j\) and packets \(h_j\), the vector family
\[
  \Omega_{{\rm out},t}^{\rm HR}F
  =
  B_{1,t}(h_1)\cdots B_{n,t}(h_n)\vac
\]
converges in the Hilbert norm of \(\Hilb\).  Second, after these vector limits
are packaged as maps from the algebraic velocity-separated Fock domain to
\(\Hilb\), the same assertion is strong convergence of the approximating maps:
\[
  \Omega_{{\rm out},t}^{\rm HR}F
  \longrightarrow
  \Omega_{\rm out}F
  \quad\text{in }\Hilb
  \quad\text{for every fixed }F.
  \label{eq:algebraic-haag-ruelle-strong-operator-limit}
\]
The incoming map is obtained by the same statement with \(t\to-\infty\).
Weak convergence of matrix elements follows from this norm convergence for
each \(F\), while weak convergence alone would not determine the isometric
identification.  No operator-norm limit over the unit sphere of
\(\mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)\) is part of the theorem.  Their ranges
\[
  \Hilb_{\mathrm{in}}=\operatorname{Ran}\Omega_{\mathrm{in}},
  \qquad
  \Hilb_{\mathrm{out}}=\operatorname{Ran}\Omega_{\mathrm{out}}
\]
are the incoming and outgoing scattering subspaces.  The comparison operator
\[
  S=\Omega_{\mathrm{out}}^*\Omega_{\mathrm{in}}
  \quad\text{on}\quad \mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)
\]
is unitary on the sector when \(\Hilb_{\mathrm{in}}=\Hilb_{\mathrm{out}}\).  Its
wave-packet matrix elements are
\[
\begin{aligned}
&\left\langle
  \varphi_1\odot\cdots\odot\varphi_m,\,
  S(\psi_1\odot\cdots\odot\psi_n)
  \right\rangle_{\mathcal F_s(\Hilb_1)}
\\
&\qquad =
  \left\langle
  \Omega_{\mathrm{out}}(\varphi_1\odot\cdots\odot\varphi_m),\,
  \Omega_{\mathrm{in}}(\psi_1\odot\cdots\odot\psi_n)
  \right\rangle_{\Hilb}.
\end{aligned}
\]
This is the nonperturbative definition of the scattering matrix element in
the massive isolated-shell sector.

Asymptotic completeness is a further assertion:
\[
  \Hilb_{\rm sector}=\Hilb_{\mathrm{in}}=\Hilb_{\mathrm{out}}.
\]
It is a dynamical property of the model or sector.  For nontrivial interacting
massive local Wightman theories in four spacetime dimensions, full asymptotic
completeness is presently an open problem rather than a theorem following from
the axioms.  Existing proofs are confined to restricted model classes, notably
constructive or integrable models in lower spacetime dimensions and related
weakened-locality settings.  Bound states require additional isolated shells.
Confinement can remove charged or colored one-particle shells from the
observable spectrum while retaining hadronic shells.  Long-range gauge forces
and infraparticle spectra require modified asymptotic state spaces or detector
observables; the charged-QED and physical-QCD versions are the open
asymptotic-completeness problems formulated in
Open Problems~\ref{op:qed-charged-asymptotic-completeness}
and~\ref{op:qcd-asymptotic-completeness}.  Each case changes the large-time
construction rather than the meaning of the massive theorem above.

\section{Interface With LSZ and Perturbation Theory}

The Haag--Ruelle construction defines the Hilbert-space target of LSZ.  If a
local field or local observable has a nonzero vacuum-to-one-particle matrix
element on the isolated shell, then its two-point function contains the
corresponding one-particle pole or spectral atom.  LSZ extracts those external
singularities from time-ordered correlation functions and thereby computes
matrix elements of the scattering operator already defined by wave operators.

The logical dependencies are
\[
\begin{gathered}
  \text{local net and spectrum}
  \longrightarrow
  \text{Haag--Ruelle wave operators}
  \longrightarrow S,
  \\[0.35em]
  \text{time-ordered Green functions}
  \xrightarrow{\ \text{LSZ external residues}\ }
  \langle{\mathrm{out}}|S|{\mathrm{in}}\rangle,
  \\[0.35em]
  \text{renormalized perturbation theory}
  \longrightarrow
  \text{Green-function expansion}
  \longrightarrow
  \text{expansion of } \langle{\mathrm{out}}|S|{\mathrm{in}}\rangle .
\end{gathered}
\]
Thus Feynman graphs for Green functions acquire a scattering interpretation
only after the asymptotic Hilbert-space data and the reduction theorem are in
place.  The same order applies whether the theory is presented by a
Lagrangian, a Euclidean construction satisfying reconstruction hypotheses, a
local net, or another nonperturbative definition.
